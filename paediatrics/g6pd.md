# G6PD Deficiency (葡萄糖六磷酸去氫酵素缺乏症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of G6PD Deficiency
* **G6PD Deficiency (葡萄糖六磷酸去氫酵素缺乏症)**: An **X-linked recessive** enzymopathy → RBCs lack protection against oxidative stress → episodic **Haemolytic Anaemia (溶血性貧血)**.
* Most common human enzyme defect globally.

### Local Hong Kong Epidemiology for G6PD Deficiency
* Highly prevalent in Southern Chinese populations.
* **Prevalence in HK**: ~4.5% of males, ~0.3% of females.
* **HK Department of Health (DH) Protocol**: Universal neonatal screening implemented in HK since the 1980s via cord blood sampling.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics

### Genetic Inheritance of G6PD Deficiency
* **Inheritance**: X-linked recessive (X染色體隱性遺傳).
* Primarily affects males (hemizygotes).
* Females are typically carriers but can be symptomatic due to **skewed X-inactivation (Lyonization)** or homozygosity.

### Pathophysiological Mechanism of Haemolysis
* G6PD enzyme defect → `↓` **NADPH** production in RBCs.
* `↓` NADPH → `↓` **Reduced Glutathione (GSH)**.
* `↓` GSH → inability to neutralize reactive oxygen species (ROS).
* Oxidative stress (from drugs/infection/food) → oxidation of **Haemoglobin (血紅蛋白)**.
* Oxidized Hb precipitates as **Heinz Bodies (海因氏小體)**.
* Heinz bodies damage RBC membrane → splenic macrophages bite out inclusions → **Bite Cells (咬痕細胞)**.
* Result: **Intravascular & Extravascular Haemolysis (血管內外溶血)**.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Neonatal Presentation of G6PD Deficiency
* **Neonatal Jaundice (新生兒黃疸)**: Often presents on day 2-3 of life.
* Can rapidly progress to **Kernicterus (核黃疸)** if unmanaged.
* Lethargy, poor feeding, hypotonia.

### Acute Haemolytic Crisis Presentation
* Triggered 1-3 days post-exposure to oxidative stress.
* **Sudden Pallor (蒼白)** & fatigue.
* **Jaundice (黃疸)** & scleral icterus.
* **Tea-coloured/Dark Urine (茶色尿/血尿)** secondary to haemoglobinuria.
* Acute back or abdominal pain.
* Tachycardia & tachypnoea (compensatory).

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)

### Distinguishing Factors for Acute G6PD Crisis
* **Positive Exposure History**: Recent ingestion of fava beans, specific drugs, or exposure to mothballs.
* **Sudden Onset**: Hyperacute haemolysis following a recognizable trigger.
* **Absence of Splenomegaly**: Unlike hereditary spherocytosis or thalassemia, massive splenomegaly is rare in acute G6PD crises.
* **Negative Coombs Test (抗人球蛋白試驗)**: Distinguishes from Autoimmune Haemolytic Anaemia (AIHA).

## 5. Differential Diagnosis

### Differential Diagnosis of Neonatal Jaundice & Acute Haemolysis
| Condition | Differentiating Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Autoimmune Haemolytic Anaemia (AIHA)** | +ve Direct Coombs Test (DAT), spherocytes on smear, no specific oxidative trigger. |
| **Hereditary Spherocytosis (遺傳性球形紅血球症)** | Autosomal dominant, +ve family history, abundant spherocytes, abnormal osmotic fragility, splenomegaly. |
| **Thalassaemia (地中海貧血)** | Microcytic hypochromic anaemia, target cells, abnormal Hb pattern on electrophoresis. |
| **ABO/Rh Incompatibility** | Seen in neonates, +ve DAT, maternal-fetal blood group mismatch. |
| **Biliary Atresia (膽道閉鎖)** | Conjugated (direct) hyperbilirubinaemia, pale stools, dark urine, occurs weeks after birth. |

## 6. Tiered Investigations

### Acute Crisis Investigations
| Investigation | Expected Finding in Acute G6PD Crisis |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Complete Blood Count (CBC)** | `↓` Hb (normocytic/macrocytic), `↑` WBC (stress response). |
| **Reticulocyte Count** | `↑` (compensatory bone marrow response). |
| **Peripheral Blood Smear** | **Bite cells (咬痕細胞)**, blister cells, **Heinz bodies** (requires supravital stain like methylene blue). |
| **Haemolysis Screen** | `↑` Unconjugated bilirubin, `↑` LDH, `↓` Haptoglobin. |
| **Urinalysis** | +ve for Haemoglobin/Urobilinogen, -ve for intact RBCs (Haemoglobinuria). |
| **Direct Antiglobulin Test (DAT/Coombs)** | -ve (rules out immune-mediated haemolysis). |

### Definitive Diagnostic Investigations
* **Quantitative G6PD Enzyme Assay**: Confirms diagnosis. 
* **Crucial Timing Rule**: Must be repeated 2-3 months *after* an acute crisis resolves (reticulocytes have normal/high G6PD levels, potentially causing false-negative results during acute haemolysis).
* **Newborn Screening**: Fluorescent spot test from cord blood or heel prick.

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)

### Acute Crisis Management
* **Stop Trigger**: Immediately cease suspected drug or remove offending agent.
* **Hyperhydration**: IV fluids to maintain high urine output and prevent acute kidney injury secondary to haemoglobinuria.
* **Blood Transfusion**: Packed RBCs if severe symptomatic anaemia or cardiovascular compromise occurs.
* **Neonates**: Intensive phototherapy (照燈); exchange transfusion (換血) if bilirubin reaches kernicterus threshold.

### Chronic Management & Prevention
* **Absolute Avoidance**: Strict avoidance of oxidative triggers.
* No routine medications or supplements required if baseline Hb is normal.

### HK Hospital Authority (HA) & Department of Health (DH) Protocols
* All affected infants receive a **DH G6PD Alert Card (G6PD缺乏症提示卡)**.
* **Strict Avoidance of Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM)**:
  * *Rhizoma Coptidis* (黃連 / Chuen Lin)
  * *Flos Lonicerae* (金銀花 / Kam Yin Fa)
  * *Radix Polygoni Multiflori* (何首烏 / Ho Shou Wu)
  * *Calculus Bovis* (牛黃 / Ngau Wong)
  * *Margarita* (珍珠末 / Chun Chu Mut)
* **Household Avoidance**: Naphthalene-containing mothballs (臭丸).
* **Dietary Avoidance**: Fava beans (蠶豆).

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care

### Multidisciplinary Team Involvement
* **Paediatrician**: Acute management and diagnostic confirmation.
* **Clinical Genetic Service (DH)**: Counseling and newborn screening results notification.
* **Maternal & Child Health Centre (MCHC)**: Routine monitoring of neonatal jaundice and parental education.
* **Pharmacist**: Education on avoiding contraindicated over-the-counter medications (e.g., aspirin, nitrofurantoin, primaquine).

### Psychosocial Care for Families
* Reassurance that children can lead a completely normal life with normal life expectancy if triggers are avoided.
* Empowering parents to inform schools, caretakers, and all medical providers of the child's status.

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags

### Accidental Exposure & Cultural Safeguarding
* Accidental exposure is common in HK due to extended family caretaking.
* Grandparents may unknowingly administer "cooling" herbal teas (廿四味/涼茶) or place mothballs (臭丸) in clothing drawers.
* **Action**: Ensure culturally sensitive but firm education reaches the *entire* household, not just the primary parents. 

## 10. Complications & Prognosis

### Complications of Severe Haemolysis
* **Neonatal Kernicterus (核黃疸)**: Bilirubin encephalopathy → permanent sensorineural hearing loss, athetoid cerebral palsy, intellectual disability.
* **Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)**: Secondary to massive haemoglobinuria leading to acute tubular necrosis.
* **Severe Anaemic Shock**: Cardiovascular collapse in hyperacute presentations (rare but fatal).

### Prognosis for G6PD Deficiency
* **Excellent**. Asymptomatic between crises.
* Normal growth, development, and lifespan provided oxidative triggers are strictly avoided.

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: Why must the quantitative G6PD assay be delayed for 2-3 months after an acute haemolytic crisis? Answer: During a crisis, older RBCs (which lack G6PD) are destroyed. The bone marrow responds by releasing young reticulocytes, which have high G6PD activity. Testing during this period can yield a false-normal result.
* Trap: Assuming that only males can suffer from symptomatic G6PD deficiency. Pearl: Females can present with severe acute haemolysis due to skewed X-chromosome inactivation (Lyonization) or if they are homozygous for the mutation.
* Pitfall: Discharging a jaundiced neonate with a family history of G6PD deficiency without strict follow-up. Always counsel local Hong Kong families extensively on the dangers of proprietary Chinese medicines (like Chuen Lin / 黃連) and camphor/mothballs (臭丸), which are common household items that can trigger fatal haemolysis.
