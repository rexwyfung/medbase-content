
# Acute Leukaemia (ALL & AML) (急性白血病)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Malignant clonal proliferation of haematopoietic stem cells in the bone marrow, leading to bone marrow failure and infiltration of other organs.
- **Local Epidemiology**: The most common childhood cancer in HK (~30% of all paediatric malignancies).
  - **Acute Lymphoblastic Leukaemia (ALL)**: Accounts for ~80% of cases. Peak age 2-5 years.
  - **Acute Myeloid Leukaemia (AML)**: Accounts for ~20% of cases.
- Managed at the Hong Kong Children's Hospital (HKCH) Paediatric Oncology Centre.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Mechanism**: Genetic mutations in a single lymphoid or myeloid progenitor cell cause it to arrest in development and proliferate uncontrollably (blasts). These blasts crowd out normal haematopoiesis in the marrow, causing pancytopenia.
- **Genetics**:
  - *Favourable ALL*: Hyperdiploidy (>50 chromosomes), t(12;21) [ETV6-RUNX1].
  - *Unfavourable ALL*: Hypodiploidy, t(9;22) [Philadelphia chromosome - BCR-ABL1], MLL gene rearrangements (infants).
  - *Down Syndrome*: 10-20x increased risk of ALL and AML (specifically Acute Megakaryoblastic Leukaemia, AML M7).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- See below.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Bone Marrow Failure**:
  - *Anaemia*: Pallor, lethargy, tachycardia.
  - *Thrombocytopenia*: Bruising, petechiae, mucosal bleeding.
  - *Neutropenia*: Recurrent or severe infections, unexplained fever.
- **Tissue Infiltration**:
  - **Bone pain**: Often severe, waking the child at night, causing a limp or refusal to walk (due to marrow expansion).
  - **Hepatosplenomegaly**: Very common.
  - **Lymphadenopathy**: Generalised, non-tender.
  - **CNS involvement**: Headache, vomiting, cranial nerve palsies (more common in ALL).
  - **Testicular enlargement**: Painless, unilateral (a sanctuary site for ALL).
## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **ITP**: Presents with bruising/petechiae, but the child is otherwise completely well, with NO hepatosplenomegaly, NO bone pain, and normal Hb/WBC.
- **Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA)**: Can present with a limp and joint pain, but leukaemia pain is often non-articular (bone pain) and associated with abnormal blood counts.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Most Likely**:
  - Acute Leukaemia (ALL > AML).
- **Must-Not-Miss**:
  - **Oncological Emergencies**:
    - **Tumour Lysis Syndrome (TLS)**: Hyperkalaemia, hyperuricaemia, hyperphosphatemia, hypocalcaemia -> Acute Kidney Injury and arrhythmias.
    - **Neutropenic Sepsis**: Life-threatening.
    - **Hyperleukocytosis**: WBC > 100 x 10^9/L. Causes leukostasis (sludging of blood in microcirculation) -> stroke, pulmonary oedema.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**:
  - **Complete Blood Count (CBC)**: Typically shows pancytopenia (or bi-cytopenia). WBC can be low, normal, or extremely high.
  - **Peripheral Blood Smear**: The crucial initial test. Shows **circulating blast cells** (large cells with high nuclear:cytoplasmic ratio, prominent nucleoli).
  - **Coagulation Profile**: To rule out DIC (especially in APML - Acute Promyelocytic Leukaemia).
  - **U&E, Uric Acid, Calcium, Phosphate**: To screen for Tumour Lysis Syndrome.
- **Gold Standard**:
  - **Bone Marrow Aspiration & Trephine Biopsy**: Definitive diagnosis requires >20% blasts in the marrow.
  - *Flow Cytometry (Immunophenotyping)*: Distinguishes ALL (B-cell or T-cell) from AML.
  - *Cytogenetics/Molecular*: Identifies specific translocations (e.g., Philadelphia chromosome) for risk stratification.
  - **Lumbar Puncture**: To assess for CNS involvement (requires intrathecal chemotherapy).
- **Monitoring**:
  - Minimal Residual Disease (MRD) monitoring via flow cytometry or PCR during treatment to assess response and guide therapy intensity.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**:
  - **Neutropenic Sepsis**: Immediate IV broad-spectrum antibiotics (e.g., Tazocin) within 1 hour.
  - **Tumour Lysis Syndrome**: Hyperhydration (IV fluids without potassium), Allopurinol or Rasburicase (recombinant urate oxidase).
  - **Anaemia/Bleeding**: Judicious transfusion of packed red cells and platelets.
- **Chronic/Definitive**:
  - **Chemotherapy**: The mainstay of treatment. Protocols are complex and risk-adapted.
  - *Phases*: Induction (to achieve remission), Consolidation, Maintenance (prolonged low-dose chemo, usually 2-3 years for ALL).
  - *CNS Directed Therapy*: Intrathecal Methotrexate/Cytarabine to prevent/treat CNS relapse.
  - **Targeted Therapy**: e.g., Imatinib (Tyrosine Kinase Inhibitor) added for Philadelphia chromosome-positive ALL.
  - **Haematopoietic Stem Cell Transplant (HSCT)**: Reserved for high-risk ALL, relapsed ALL, and many cases of AML.
  - **CAR-T Cell Therapy**: Available at HKCH for relapsed/refractory B-cell ALL.
## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Comprehensive support at HKCH: Play therapists, clinical psychologists, hospital school (Red Cross), and the Children's Cancer Foundation (CCF).
## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- **Safety Netting**: Parents MUST bring the child to A&E immediately for any fever > 38.0°C while neutropenic.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Prognosis**: Dramatic improvement over decades. Current 5-year survival for childhood ALL is >90%. AML is lower (~60-70%).
- **Late Effects**: Cardiotoxicity (Anthracyclines), secondary malignancies, endocrine dysfunction, infertility, neurocognitive deficits.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Assuming a child with a limp has a musculoskeletal problem without checking a CBC. *Pearl*: Leukaemia is the most common cancer in children and frequently presents with bone pain/limp. Always do a CBC and film.
- **Viva Question**: "What are the metabolic derangements in Tumour Lysis Syndrome?" *Answer*: Hyperkalaemia, Hyperuricaemia, Hyperphosphatemia, and secondary Hypocalcaemia (due to calcium-phosphate precipitation).
