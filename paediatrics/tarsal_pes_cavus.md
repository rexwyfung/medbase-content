# Tarsal Coalition & Pes Cavus (跗骨聯合及高弓足)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Tarsal Coalition**: A congenital failure of segmentation where two or more tarsal bones fuse together with a bridge (fibrous, cartilaginous, or bony), restricting normal foot mechanics.
- **Pes Cavus**: A structurally unflattenable high arch of the foot that does NOT flatten on weight-bearing.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Tarsal Coalition**: Mesenchymal tissue in the fetal foot fails to cleave properly. The most common bridges are **Calcaneonavicular** and **Talocalcaneal**. As the child reaches early adolescence, these cartilaginous bridges ossify into rigid bone, eliminating joint motion and causing pain/spasms.
- **Pes Cavus**: In the vast majority of cases, it is the result of a **neuromuscular imbalance** (weakness of intrinsic foot muscles against strong extrinsic muscles), causing the toes to claw and the arch to elevate. 

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- **Tarsal Coalition**: A 12-year-old boy presents with aching midfoot pain after playing basketball and states he keeps twisting his ankle. 
- **Pes Cavus**: A 10-year-old girl is brought in because she has very high arches, her toes are starting to claw, and she trips over her own feet due to foot drop.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **TARSAL COALITION**:
  - **Rigid Flatfoot**: The foot is flat while standing. When you ask them to stand on their tiptoes, the arch does *not* reconstitute (unlike flexible flat foot, where the arch reappears beautifully on tiptoes).
  - Lack of subtalar joint motion (cannot invert/evert the heel).
  - "Peroneal Spastic Flatfoot": The peroneal muscles visibly spasm as a reflex to protect the painful rigid joint.
- **PES CAVUS**:
  - Extremely high medial longitudinal arch.
  - Clawing of the toes.
  - **Peek-a-boo heel sign**: Looking at the patient from the front, you can see the heel pad peeking out medially.
  - MUST meticulously check for neurology (muscle wasting in calves, absent reflexes, loss of proprioception/vibration).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from Flexible Flat Foot (Pes Planus): Flexible flat foot is normal in childhood; the arch drops when standing (due to lax ligaments) but reconstitutes when sitting or on tiptoes. It is painless and requires no treatment. Tarsal coalition is rigid, unyielding, and painful.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Pes Cavus Differential**:
  - Charcot-Marie-Tooth Disease (CMT; Hereditary Motor Sensory Neuropathy) - accounts for >50% of bilateral pes cavus.
  - Friedreich Ataxia.
  - Spinal dysraphism / Tethered Cord.
  - Cerebral Palsy.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Tarsal Coalition**: 
  - X-Rays: May show the "Anteater Nose Sign" (calcaneonavicular coalition) or the "C-Sign" (talocalcaneal).
  - **CT Scan**: The absolute gold standard to clearly define the bony bridge size and location before surgery.
- **Pes Cavus**: 
  - Complete neurological exam.
  - **Nerve Conduction Studies / EMG**: To confirm CMT or other neuropathies.
  - **MRI Spine**: To rule out spinal cord tethering or lesions.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Tarsal Coalition**:
  - Conservative: Casting or a rigid walking boot for 6 weeks to immobilize the joint and calm the inflammation, followed by orthotics.
  - Surgical: Excision of the coalition bridge and placing fat/muscle in the gap to prevent it fusing again.
- **Pes Cavus**:
  - Treat the underlying neurological disease if possible.
  - Custom orthotics to distribute the extreme pressure off the metatarsal heads.
  - Tendon transfers or osteotomies to flatten the foot if progressing.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Untreated coalition leads to severe, early degenerative osteoarthritis of the hindfoot.
- Pes Cavus leads to painful severe calluses, metatarsalgia, and chronic ankle instability.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Treating an adolescent with new, progressive bilateral Pes Cavus with "better supportive shoes" and missing the underlying Charcot-Marie-Tooth neurodegeneration. Unilateral cavus is often trauma; bilateral cavus is neurological until proven otherwise!
- **Viva Q**: "A 13-year-old has painless flat feet. When you ask him to stand on his tiptoes, his medial arch cleanly re-appears. Does he need special orthopedic shoes?" Answer: No. This is flexible, physiological pes planus. It requires zero intervention. Special shoes do not build the arch; they merely waste parents' money.
