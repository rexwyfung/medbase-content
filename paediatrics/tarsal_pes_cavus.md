# Tarsal Coalition & Pes Cavus (跗骨聯合及高弓足)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Tarsal Coalition Definition
* **Tarsal Coalition (跗骨聯合):** A congenital failure of segmentation between ≥2 tarsal bones resulting in an abnormal fibrous, cartilaginous, or bony bridge. This restricts normal foot mechanics and subtalar joint motion.
* **Local Epidemiology:** Affects ~1-2% of the general population. Often bilateral (~50%).

### Pes Cavus Definition
* **Pes Cavus (高弓足):** A structurally elevated medial longitudinal arch that **does NOT flatten** on weight-bearing.
* **Local Epidemiology:** Less common than pes planus (flat foot). In Hong Kong Hospital Authority (HA) clinics, bilateral pes cavus in a child/adolescent is a major red flag for underlying neuromuscular disease.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Tarsal Coalition Pathophysiology
* **Embryology:** Failure of mesenchymal cleavage in the fetal foot (weeks 6-8 of gestation).
* **Progression:** The bridge is initially cartilaginous (asymptomatic in early childhood) → **ossifies into rigid bone** during early adolescence (ages 8-16) → restricts subtalar motion → alters biomechanics → microtrauma, joint irritability, and reflex muscle spasm.
* **Most Common Types:** * **Calcaneonavicular (跟舟骨聯合):** Ossifies age 8-12.
  * **Talocalcaneal (距跟骨聯合):** Ossifies age 12-16.

### Pes Cavus Pathophysiology
* **Neuromuscular Imbalance:** Weakness of intrinsic foot muscles + unopposed strong pull of extrinsic muscles (e.g., tibialis posterior & peroneus longus).
* **Mechanical Result:** Forefoot drops (plantarflexes), the arch elevates severely, and toes buckle into a clawed position. 
* **Genetics:** Strongly linked to inherited neuropathies (e.g., Charcot-Marie-Tooth disease involves genes like *PMP22* duplication).

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Tarsal Coalition Clinical Presentation
* **Demographics:** Typically an active 8-16 year old (often presenting after minor trauma/sports).
* **Pain:** Aching deep midfoot/hindfoot pain, exacerbated by uneven surfaces or sports.
* **Rigid Flatfoot:** The foot is flat on weight-bearing AND remains flat when standing on tiptoes (arch fails to reconstitute).
* **Subtalar Rigidity:** Markedly ↓ inversion/eversion of the heel.
* **Peroneal Spastic Flatfoot:** Reflex spasm of peroneal muscles (visible tightening) to splint and protect the painful subtalar joint.

### Pes Cavus Clinical Presentation
* **Arch & Toes:** Extremely high medial arch + **Claw Toes (爪狀趾)**.
* **Peek-a-boo Heel Sign:** Viewed from the front, the medial border of the heel pad is visible due to severe hindfoot varus. * **Callosities:** Thick painful calluses under the 1st metatarsal head and heel due to altered pressure distribution.
* **Neurological Signs (MUST EXAMINE):** Muscle wasting (e.g., "stork leg" or "inverted champagne bottle" calves in CMT), areflexia, ↓ proprioception/vibration, foot drop, clumsiness/frequent tripping.

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Flexible Pes Planus vs. Tarsal Coalition
* **Flexible Pes Planus (生理性扁平足):** Arch is absent on standing but **reappears beautifully** on tiptoes or when sitting (Jack's Test +ve). Painless. Normal subtalar motion. 
* **Tarsal Coalition:** Arch is absent standing and **remains absent** on tiptoes. Painful. Rigid subtalar motion.

### Unilateral vs. Bilateral Pes Cavus
* **Unilateral Pes Cavus:** Often due to focal trauma, tethered cord syndrome, or previous polio.
* **Bilateral Pes Cavus:** **Neuromuscular until proven otherwise!** (e.g., Charcot-Marie-Tooth, Friedreich Ataxia).

## 5. Differential Diagnosis
### Differentials for Rigid Flatfoot
| Condition | Differentiating Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Tarsal Coalition** | Adolescent onset, rigid subtalar joint, peroneal spasm. |
| **Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA)** | Morning stiffness, multiple joints involved, ↑ ESR/CRP. |
| **Septic Arthritis** | Acute severe pain, fever, inability to weight-bear, toxic child. |
| **Trauma/Fracture** | Clear history of injury, acute localized swelling. |

### Differentials for Pes Cavus
| Neuromuscular Condition | Key Clinical Clues |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Charcot-Marie-Tooth (CMT)** | Hereditary Motor Sensory Neuropathy. >50% of bilateral cavus. Distal muscle wasting, absent ankle jerks, family history. |
| **Friedreich Ataxia** | Ataxia, dysarthria, absent reflexes, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy. |
| **Spinal Dysraphism / Tethered Cord** | Sacral dimple/hair tuft, lower limb neurological deficits, incontinence. |
| **Cerebral Palsy (CP)** | Spasticity, hyperreflexia, upgoing plantars, history of perinatal hypoxia. |

## 6. Tiered Investigations
### Tarsal Coalition Investigations
* **X-Rays (Weight-bearing AP, Lateral, Oblique):**
  * **Calcaneonavicular:** Best seen on 45° internal oblique view → **"Anteater Nose Sign" (食蟻獸鼻徵)** (tubular prolongation of the anterior calcaneus).   * **Talocalcaneal:** Best seen on lateral view → **"C-Sign" (C字徵)** (bony outline between talar dome and sustentaculum tali).
* **CT Scan (Gold Standard):** Exquisitely defines the precise location, size, and extent of the bony bridge. Essential for surgical planning.
* **MRI:** Useful if early (cartilaginous or fibrous coalition before ossification).

### Pes Cavus Investigations
* **X-Rays (Weight-bearing):** ↑ calcaneal pitch, Meary's angle disruption.
* **Nerve Conduction Studies (NCS) / EMG:** Confirms peripheral neuropathy (e.g., demyelinating ↓ velocity in CMT type 1).
* **MRI Spine:** Urgent if red flags (unilateral, rapidly progressive, bladder/bowel symptoms) to rule out tethered cord or spinal tumour.
* **Genetic Testing:** CMT gene panels (e.g., *PMP22*).

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Tarsal Coalition Management
* **Conservative (1st Line):** * Immobilization: Short leg walking cast or rigid CAM boot for 4-6 weeks to rest the joint and break the pain-spasm cycle.
  * Followed by custom medial arch supports/orthotics + NSAIDs.
* **Surgical (If conservative fails):**
  * **Resection:** Excision of the coalition bridge + interposition of fat or muscle (e.g., extensor digitorum brevis) to prevent refusion.
  * **Arthrodesis (Triple fusion):** Salvage procedure if severe secondary degenerative osteoarthritis is already present.

### Pes Cavus Management
* **Conservative:** * Custom orthotics / AFOs (Ankle-Foot Orthoses) with metatarsal domes to offload high-pressure areas and prevent calluses.
  * Physiotherapy (stretching plantar fascia/Achilles).
* **Surgical:**
  * **Soft Tissue:** Plantar fascia release (Steindler stripping), tendon transfers.
  * **Bony:** Metatarsal osteotomies, calcaneal osteotomies, or triple arthrodesis for rigid/severe deformity.
* **Treat Underlying Cause:** Neurology referral for definitive management of CMT or tethered cord.

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Multidisciplinary Approach
* **Orthotist / Podiatrist:** Crucial for manufacturing custom insoles/AFOs.
* **Physiotherapist:** Gait retraining, stretching tight structures, balance exercises.
* **Neurologist / Geneticist:** For progressive pes cavus (CMT requires genetic counselling for the family).

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Non-Accidental Injury (NAI) Considerations
* **Delayed Presentation:** A child with persistent limp, rigid foot, or bruising where parents delay seeking medical attention or provide an inconsistent history.
* **Safeguarding Action:** If NAI is suspected alongside foot trauma, admit for observation, notify MSW (Medical Social Worker) and consult HA pediatric safeguarding guidelines.

## 10. Complications & Prognosis
### Tarsal Coalition Complications
* **Early Osteoarthritis:** Untreated rigid coalition inevitably leads to early, severe degenerative osteoarthritis of the hindfoot/subtalar joints.
* **Chronic Pain:** Long-term inability to participate in sports.

### Pes Cavus Complications
* **Skin Breakdown:** Severe callosities and diabetic-like foot ulcers under metatarsal heads.
* **Joint Instability:** Chronic recurrent ankle sprains due to fixed varus hindfoot.
* **Progressive Disability:** If underlying neuromuscular condition (CMT) is unrecognized.

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Viva Question: A 13-year-old boy presents with a painful, rigid flatfoot and visible spasm of the lateral leg muscles. What is the diagnosis? Answer: Tarsal Coalition presenting as peroneal spastic flatfoot.
* Trap: Reassuring an adolescent with new, progressive bilateral Pes Cavus and simply prescribing "better supportive shoes." Pearl: Bilateral pes cavus is a hallmark of neuromuscular disease (e.g., Charcot-Marie-Tooth) until proven otherwise! You MUST perform a full neurological exam.
* Pitfall: Recommending expensive orthopedic shoes for a 4-year-old with painless flexible flat feet. Flexible pes planus is physiological and requires zero intervention; special shoes do not "build" the arch, they merely waste parents' money.
* Viva Question: What specific X-ray sign is associated with a calcaneonavicular coalition? Answer: The "Anteater Nose Sign" on a 45-degree internal oblique view.
