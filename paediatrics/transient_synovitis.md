# Transient Synovitis of the Hip (Irritable Hip) (短暫性髖關節滑膜炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition
* Acute, self-limiting, sterile inflammation of the synovial lining of the hip joint.
* Most common cause of acute hip pain and limping in childhood.

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
* Extremely common presentation in Hong Kong Hospital Authority (HA) Paediatric Emergency Departments (AED).
* Peak incidence: 3–8 years of age.
* Gender: Male predominance (M:F ≈ 2:1).
* Seasonal variation: ↑ incidence during local viral upper respiratory or enterovirus seasons.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Pathogenesis
* Exact aetiology is unknown; widely considered a **post-infectious reactive process**.
* Frequently follows a recent viral upper respiratory tract infection (URTI) or gastroenteritis by 1–2 weeks.

### Articular Mechanics
* Inflammation → hyperaemia & synovial hypertrophy.
* Extravasation of fluid → sterile joint effusion.
* ↑ Intra-articular pressure → stretches capsule → severe pain & limitation of movement.

### Genetic Predisposition
* No specific Mendelian genetic inheritance identified.
* Occasional familial clustering suggests potential HLA-associated hyper-reactive immune responses to viral antigens.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Classic Clinical Presentation
* **Acute onset** of unilateral hip, groin, or anterior thigh pain.
* **Limp**: Antalgic gait (↓ stance phase weight-bearing on affected side).
* **Pain referral**: Frequently referred to the **knee (膝部)** or medial thigh via the obturator nerve.
* Systemic symptoms: Well-appearing child. Afebrile or low-grade fever (< 38.5°C).

### Physical Examination Findings
* **Position of comfort**: Hip held in slight flexion, abduction, and external rotation (maximizes capsular volume, ↓ intra-articular pressure).
* **Range of Motion (ROM)**: Mild-to-moderate restriction.
* **Internal rotation (內旋)** & **abduction (外展)** are most severely limited & painful.
* **Log roll test**: +ve (pain on gentle internal/external rotation of extended leg).
* **No rest pain**: Pain strictly provoked by movement/weight-bearing.

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Differentiating Transient Synovitis vs. Septic Arthritis
* **Septic Arthritis (化膿性關節炎)** is the critical "must-not-miss" diagnosis.

| Feature | Transient Synovitis | Septic Arthritis |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Systemic State** | Well, non-toxic | Toxic, irritable, ill-appearing |
| **Fever** | Afebrile or < 38.5°C | High-grade > 38.5°C |
| **Weight Bearing** | Can usually bear some weight | Absolute refusal (Pseudoparalysis) |
| **Rest Pain** | Absent | Severe, constant |
| **Kocher Score** | Typically 0-1 | Typically 3-4 |

## 5. Differential Diagnosis
### Common Paediatric Hip Differentials
| Diagnosis | Differentiating Clinical Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Septic Arthritis** | Toxic, high fever, severe rest pain, absolute refusal to bear weight, ↑↑ CRP/WBC. |
| **Perthes Disease (Legg-Calvé-Perthes)** | Insidious onset (weeks-months), older age (4-10y), painless limp, avascular necrosis on X-ray. |
| **SCFE (Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis)** | Older/obese child (10-15y), restricted internal rotation, "ice cream slipping off cone" on X-ray. |
| **Osteomyelitis (Proximal Femur/Pelvis)** | Bone point tenderness, ↑ inflammatory markers, positive MRI/Bone scan. |
| **Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA)** | Chronic (>6 weeks), morning stiffness, multiple joints involved. |
| **Leukaemia / Malignancy** | Night pain, bone pain out of proportion, pallor, hepatosplenomegaly, abnormal blood smear. |

## 6. Tiered Investigations
### Clinical Scoring: Kocher Criteria
* Used to differentiate Septic Arthritis (SA) vs Transient Synovitis (TS).
* 1 point for each of the following 4 variables:

| Kocher Criteria Variable | Cut-off Value |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Fever** | > 38.5°C |
| **Non-weight bearing** | Absolute refusal on affected side |
| **ESR** | > 40 mm/hr |
| **WBC** | > 12.0 x 10^9/L |

* **Risk of Septic Arthritis based on score**:
    * 0/4: < 0.2%
    * 1/4: 3%
    * 2/4: 40%
    * 3/4: 93%
    * 4/4: 99.6%
* *Note: Caird’s modification adds CRP > 20 mg/L as a 5th independent predictor.*

### Bedside & Blood Investigations
* **FBC**: WBC usually normal or marginally ↑.
* **ESR & CRP**: Normal or mildly ↑ (CRP typically < 20 mg/L).
* **Blood Culture**: Indicated if fever > 38.5°C or toxic-appearing to rule out SA.

### Imaging Modalities
* **Ultrasound Hip (Gold Standard for Effusion)**: Highly sensitive for detecting joint effusion. Cannot reliably distinguish sterile (TS) from purulent (SA) fluid.
* **X-ray Pelvis (AP & Frog-leg Lateral)**: Usually normal in TS. Essential to exclude Perthes disease, SCFE, fractures, or bony tumors.
* **MRI**: Only indicated if suspecting early osteomyelitis, Perthes, or atypical presentation with prolonged symptoms.

### Special Procedures
* **Ultrasound-Guided Joint Aspiration**: Mandatory if Kocher score ≥ 2, high clinical suspicion of SA, or no improvement after 48 hours. Synovial fluid sent for Gram stain, cell count (>50,000 WBCs/mm³ suggests SA), and culture.

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Acute Management & HA Protocols
* **Reassurance**: Explain benign, self-limiting nature to parents.
* **Bed Rest**: Strict avoidance of weight-bearing activities until pain subsides.
* **Analgesia**: **NSAIDs (e.g., Ibuprofen 10 mg/kg/dose TDS)** are the 1st line treatment of choice. Provide analgesia & ↓ synovial inflammation.
* **Skin Traction**: Rarely used in modern HA protocols, but historically applied for severe muscle spasm.

### Outpatient Follow-up Protocol
* **Strict 48-72 Hour Review**: Patient MUST be reviewed in AED or paediatric outpatient clinic.
* **Safety Netting**: Advise immediate return if ↑ fever, ↑ pain, or complete inability to bear weight develops (red flags for evolving Septic Arthritis).

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Multidisciplinary Team Involvement
* **Paediatricians / Emergency Physicians**: Primary management and follow-up.
* **Orthopaedic Surgery**: Urgent consultation required if Septic Arthritis cannot be confidently excluded (e.g., Kocher ≥ 2 or toxic).
* **Physiotherapy**: Generally not acutely required. May be involved later if mild gait asymmetry or muscle tightness persists after pain resolves.

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Non-Accidental Injury (NAI) Considerations
* TS is non-traumatic. If presentation includes unexplained bruising, inconsistent history, or delayed presentation, consider NAI.
* Skeletal survey and Medical Social Worker (MSW) involvement indicated if multiple unexplained fractures or injuries are identified.

## 10. Complications & Prognosis
### Disease Prognosis
* **Excellent**: Complete resolution of symptoms usually occurs within 1 to 2 weeks without any long-term sequelae.
* **Recurrence**: Can recur in 4-17% of children during subsequent viral illnesses.

### Potential Complications
* **Coxa Magna**: Mild, asymptomatic enlargement of femoral head (rare, benign).
* **Misdiagnosis**: The major complication is missing an early Septic Arthritis, leading to rapid irreversible joint destruction and avascular necrosis.

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Viva Question: A 5-year-old boy presents with a 1-day history of right hip pain and a limp. He is afebrile and his CRP is normal. What is the most likely diagnosis, and what is the most critical condition you must exclude? Answer: Most likely Transient Synovitis. You must absolutely exclude Septic Arthritis of the hip.
* Viva Question: What are the four components of the Kocher criteria used to differentiate septic arthritis from transient synovitis? Answer: Fever > 38.5°C, Non-weight bearing, ESR > 40 mm/hr, WBC > 12 x 10^9/L.
* Viva Question: A 6-year-old boy presents with acute knee pain but his knee examination is entirely normal. What joint MUST you examine? Answer: The hip joint, as hip pathology (Transient Synovitis, Perthes, SCFE) frequently refers pain to the knee via the obturator nerve.
* Trap: Assuming a joint effusion on ultrasound confirms Transient Synovitis. Pearl: Ultrasound cannot differentiate between sterile fluid (Transient Synovitis) and purulent pus (Septic Arthritis). Urgent joint aspiration is required if clinical suspicion for SA is high.
* Pitfall: Discharging a child with a presumed diagnosis of transient synovitis without arranging a clear clinical review in 48-72 hours. An early or partially treated septic arthritis can initially mimic transient synovitis.
* Pitfall: Forgetting to perform a Frog-leg lateral X-ray in a limping child. An AP pelvis view alone can easily miss a subtle Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE).
