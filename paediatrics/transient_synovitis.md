# Transient Synovitis of the Hip (Irritable Hip) (短暫性髖關節滑膜炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: An acute, self-limiting, sterile inflammation of the synovial lining of the hip joint. It is the most common cause of acute hip pain and limping in childhood.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Extremely common in Hong Kong paediatric emergency departments and outpatient clinics. Peak incidence is between 3 and 8 years of age. More common in boys.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Pathophysiology**: The exact aetiology is unknown, but it is widely considered to be a post-infectious reactive process. 
- It frequently follows a recent viral upper respiratory tract infection (URTI) or gastroenteritis by 1-2 weeks.
- The inflammation causes a sterile effusion (fluid accumulation) within the hip joint capsule, leading to increased intra-articular pressure and pain.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- A previously healthy 3-to-8-year-old child presents with a sudden onset of a painful limp or refusal to walk.
- The parents often report that the child had a cold or mild fever a week ago.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Pain**: Localized to the groin, anterior thigh, or referred to the **knee**.
- **Limp**: Antalgic gait (spending less time weight-bearing on the painful leg).
- **Position of Comfort**: The child typically holds the hip in slight **flexion, abduction, and external rotation**. This position maximizes the volume of the joint capsule, reducing the pressure from the effusion and minimizing pain.
- **Examination**:
  - Mild to moderate restriction of hip movements, particularly **internal rotation and abduction**.
  - *Crucially*, the child is usually **afebrile** (or has a very low-grade fever) and does NOT look systemically toxic.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Septic Arthritis**: This is the critical "must-not-miss" diagnosis. In septic arthritis, the child is toxic, has a high fever (>38.5°C), severe pain with *any* movement of the hip (pseudoparalysis), and refuses to bear weight entirely. Transient synovitis is milder, the child is well, and they can usually bear some weight.
- **Perthes Disease**: Has an insidious (slow) onset over weeks to months, whereas transient synovitis is acute.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Septic Arthritis** (The most important differential to exclude).
- Perthes Disease (Legg-Calvé-Perthes).
- Osteomyelitis of the proximal femur or pelvis.
- Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE) - usually in older, obese children.
- Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA).
- Leukaemia (can present with bone/joint pain).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **The Kocher Criteria**: Used to differentiate Transient Synovitis from Septic Arthritis.
  1. Non-weight bearing on the affected side.
  2. ESR > 40 mm/hr.
  3. Fever > 38.5°C.
  4. WBC count > 12 x 10^9/L.
  *(If 3 or 4 criteria are met, the probability of septic arthritis is >93%, and urgent joint aspiration is required. If 0 or 1 criterion is met, it is highly likely to be transient synovitis).*
- **Bloods**: FBC, ESR, CRP. In transient synovitis, these are typically normal or only marginally elevated.
- **Ultrasound of the Hip**: Highly sensitive for detecting a joint effusion. However, it *cannot* distinguish between a sterile effusion (synovitis) and a purulent effusion (septic arthritis).
- **X-ray of the Pelvis (AP and Frog-leg lateral)**: Usually normal in transient synovitis. Essential to rule out Perthes disease, SCFE, or fractures.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Reassurance**: The most important step once septic arthritis is excluded. Explain to parents that it is a benign, self-limiting condition.
- **Rest**: Bed rest and avoidance of weight-bearing activities until the pain subsides.
- **Analgesia**: **NSAIDs (e.g., Ibuprofen)** are the treatment of choice. They provide pain relief and reduce synovial inflammation.
- **Follow-up**: The child MUST be reviewed within 48-72 hours. If symptoms are worsening or the child develops a high fever, they must return immediately to rule out evolving septic arthritis.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Generally managed by Paediatricians or Emergency Physicians. Orthopaedic consultation is only required if septic arthritis cannot be confidently excluded.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- N/A for this condition.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Prognosis**: Excellent. Complete resolution of symptoms usually occurs within 1 to 2 weeks without any long-term sequelae.
- **Recurrence**: Can recur in a small percentage of children with subsequent viral illnesses.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "A 5-year-old boy presents with a 1-day history of right hip pain and a limp. He is afebrile and his CRP is normal. What is the most likely diagnosis, and what is the most critical condition you must exclude?" (Most likely Transient Synovitis. You must absolutely exclude Septic Arthritis of the hip).
- *Viva*: "What are the four components of the Kocher criteria used to differentiate septic arthritis from transient synovitis?" (Fever >38.5C, Non-weight bearing, ESR >40, WBC >12).
- *Pitfall*: Discharging a child with a presumed diagnosis of transient synovitis without arranging a clear clinical review in 48 hours. A partially treated or early septic arthritis can initially mimic transient synovitis.
