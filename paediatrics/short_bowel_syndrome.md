# Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS) (短腸綜合症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A state of massive malabsorption following extensive surgical resection of the small intestine, leading to an inability to maintain protein-energy, fluid, electrolyte, or micronutrient balances on a normal diet.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Most often occurs in the neonatal ICU following massive resections for Necrotizing Enterocolitis (NEC) or Midgut Volvulus.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Loss of jejunum (primary site of macronutrient absorption) or ileum (primary site of Vitamin B12 and bile salt absorption).
- Loss of the ileocecal valve results in massively accelerated transit time and small bowel bacterial overgrowth (SIBO).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Neonates failing to tolerate enteral feeds post-operatively, requiring complete reliance on Total Parenteral Nutrition (TPN) via a central venous catheter.
- Presents with catastrophic diarrhea, dehydration, and electrolyte derangement if enteral feeds are increased too rapidly.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- High-volume, watery stoma output or diarrhea.
- Poor weight gain / falling crossing centiles.
- Signs of specific micronutrient deficiencies (e.g., zinc deficiency causing acrodermatitis enteropathica-like rash around mouth/anus).
- Central line in situ.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from purely functional malabsorption (like severe Coeliac) as the anatomical loss is documented surgically. SBS is defined anatomy.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Congenital specific malabsorption disorders (e.g., Glucose-galactose malabsorption)
- Intestinal Failure due to dysmotility (e.g., total colonic aganglionosis in Hirschsprung's, pseudo-obstruction).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bloods**: Liver function tests (monitor for Intestinal Failure Associated Liver Disease - IFALD, caused by long-term TPN). Full micronutrient screens (Vit ADEK, B12, Zinc, Selenium).
- **Imaging**: Contrast follow-through to assess exact remaining bowel length and calibre.
- **Special**: Breath tests for SIBO. Liver biopsy if unexplained severe jaundice.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Medical**: Long-term TPN is life-saving. Loperamide to slow transit. Probiotics/Antibiotics (Rifaximin) rotating to treat SIBO. Ursodeoxycholic acid for cholestasis.
- **Nutritional Rehabilitation**: Continuous slow enteral feeds via NG/NJ tube. The bowel undergoes *adaptation* over 1-2 years, increasing villous height to absorb more.
- **Surgical**: STEP procedure (Serial Transverse Enteroplasty) to lengthen and taper dilated bowel. Liver-Bowel transplantation is the ultimate cure if IFALD progresses to end-stage cirrhosis.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Massive burden on the family (managing TPN pumps at home at night). Extensive Home-TPN training by specialized nurses. Continuous MSW and psychologist support.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Central line infections (CLABSI) are deadly. The parents must maintain absolute sterility at home. Non-compliance is highly dangerous.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Intestinal Failure Associated Liver Disease (IFALD) leading to cirrhosis.
- Central Line Associated Bloodstream Infections (CLABSI) and loss of venous access (running out of veins for the central line).
- Eventual survival relies on the amount of terminal ileum and whether the ileocecal valve was preserved.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Viva Q**: "Why is the loss of the ileum worse than the loss of the jejunum?" Answer: The ileum can adapt to take over jejunal functions (absorbing carbs/proteins), but the jejunum CANNOT adapt to absorb Vitamin B12 or reabsorb bile salts. Loss of bile salts leads to fat malabsorption and severe secretory diarrhea in the colon.
