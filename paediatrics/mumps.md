# Mumps (流行性腮腺炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: An acute viral infection caused by the Mumps virus (a paramyxovirus). It is characterized by painful swelling of the salivary glands, most commonly the parotid glands.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Incidence in Hong Kong is very low due to the universal two-dose MMR (Measles, Mumps, Rubella) vaccination programme. Cases occasionally occur in unvaccinated individuals or as outbreaks in young adults with waning immunity.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Transmission**: Spread via respiratory droplets, direct contact with infected saliva, or fomites.
- **Incubation Period**: 14 to 21 days.
- **Infectivity**: From a few days before the onset of parotitis until 5 days after the swelling begins.
- **Pathogenesis**: The virus enters the respiratory tract, replicates locally, and causes a viraemia. It has a strong tropism for glandular tissue (salivary glands, testes, pancreas, ovaries) and the central nervous system (meninges).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- **Prodrome**: Non-specific symptoms for 1-2 days: low-grade fever, headache, malaise, myalgia, and anorexia.
- **Parotitis**: The hallmark feature. Sudden onset of pain and swelling in one or both parotid glands.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Parotid Swelling**:
  - Usually bilateral (though one side may swell before the other).
  - The swelling obscures the angle of the mandible and pushes the earlobe upward and outward.
  - The gland is tender, and the overlying skin is tense but usually *not* erythematous or warm (unlike bacterial parotitis).
- **Earache / Jaw Pain**: Pain is exacerbated by chewing or eating sour/acidic foods (which stimulate salivary flow).
- **Examination of the Mouth**: The opening of Stensen's duct (on the buccal mucosa opposite the upper second molar) may be red and swollen.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Cervical Lymphadenitis**: Swollen lymph nodes are usually located *below* the angle of the mandible and do not obscure it. Parotid swelling crosses the angle of the mandible and pushes the earlobe out.
- **Bacterial Parotitis (e.g., Staph aureus)**: Usually unilateral, extremely painful, the overlying skin is red and hot, and pus may be expressed from Stensen's duct. The patient is toxic. Mumps is usually bilateral, non-erythematous, and viral.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Cervical lymphadenitis (bacterial or viral, e.g., EBV).
- Bacterial parotitis.
- Salivary gland calculus (sialolithiasis) - usually unilateral, pain worsens specifically during meals.
- Other viral parotitis (e.g., Parainfluenza, Coxsackievirus, HIV).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Diagnosis is primarily clinical**.
- **Virology**: Mumps virus PCR from a buccal/saliva swab or urine.
- **Serology**: Mumps-specific IgM antibodies (may be negative in the first few days).
- **Serum Amylase**: Often elevated (due to salivary gland involvement, not necessarily pancreatitis).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Supportive Care**: The mainstay of treatment, as there is no specific antiviral therapy.
  - Analgesia and antipyretics (Paracetamol, Ibuprofen).
  - Hydration.
  - Soft diet, avoiding sour or acidic foods that stimulate saliva production and cause pain.
- **Infection Control**: Droplet precautions. The child must be excluded from school/nursery for **5 days** after the onset of parotid swelling.
- **Public Health**: Mumps is a statutory notifiable disease in Hong Kong.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Prevention**: The MMR vaccine is highly effective. Given at 1 year and 1.5 years in the HKCIP.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Epididymo-orchitis**: The most common complication in post-pubertal males (up to 30%). Presents with sudden onset of high fever, severe testicular pain, and swelling (usually unilateral). Can lead to testicular atrophy, but sterility is rare (as it is usually unilateral).
- **Aseptic Meningitis**: Very common (up to 10% clinically, but CSF pleocytosis is present in 50%). Presents with headache, neck stiffness, and photophobia. Usually benign and resolves without sequelae.
- **Encephalitis**: Rare but severe.
- **Sensorineural Hearing Loss**: Rare, but mumps was historically a leading cause of unilateral acquired deafness in childhood.
- **Pancreatitis**: Presents with severe epigastric pain and vomiting.
- **Oophoritis**: Inflammation of the ovaries in post-pubertal females.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "How can you clinically distinguish between a swollen parotid gland and a swollen upper cervical lymph node?" (A swollen parotid gland obscures the angle of the mandible and pushes the earlobe upward and outward. A cervical lymph node is typically located below the angle of the mandible).
- *Viva*: "What is the most common complication of mumps in a post-pubertal male?" (Epididymo-orchitis).
- *Pitfall*: Prescribing antibiotics for bilateral, non-erythematous parotid swelling in a well child, mistaking a viral mumps infection for bacterial parotitis.
