# Mumps (流行性腮腺炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition of Mumps
**Mumps (流行性腮腺炎)**: Acute, self-limiting viral infection caused by the **Mumps virus** (a paramyxovirus). Characterized by painful swelling of salivary glands, most classically the **parotid glands (腮腺)**.

### Hong Kong Epidemiology & Public Health
* **Incidence**: ↓↓ locally due to high coverage of universal **MMR/MMRV** vaccination. Occasional outbreaks in unvaccinated cohorts or young adults due to waning immunity.
* **Public Health Law**: A statutory **notifiable disease (法定呈報傳染病)** in Hong Kong → must notify the **Centre for Health Protection (CHP) (衛生防護中心)**.
* **HKCIP Schedule**: Dose 1 at 12 months, Dose 2 at 18 months via Maternal and Child Health Centres (MCHC).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Viral Pathogenesis
* **Agent**: RNA virus belonging to the *Paramyxoviridae* family.
* **Transmission Route**: Respiratory droplets, direct contact with infected saliva, or fomites.
* **Disease Mechanism**: Virus enters respiratory tract → local replication in epithelial cells → primary viraemia → strong tissue tropism for glandular tissues (salivary glands, testes, pancreas, ovaries) & CNS (meninges).

### Infection Timeline
* **Incubation Period**: 15–24 days (median 19 days).
* **Infectivity Window**: From several days before parotitis onset until **5 days** after parotid swelling begins.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
### Viral Prodrome
* **Pre-swelling Phase (1–2 days)**: Non-specific systemic symptoms.
* Low-grade fever.
* Malaise & anorexia.
* Headache & myalgia.
* **Subclinical Rate**: Up to 30% of pediatric cases are completely asymptomatic but remain infectious.

### Acute Glandular Phase
* **Sudden Onset**: Acute pain and swelling of one parotid gland, often progressing to bilateral involvement over the next 2-3 days.
* **Systemic Resolution**: Fever typically disappears within 3–4 days of glandular swelling.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Parotid Swelling Characteristics
* **Location & Displacement**: Swelling crosses & **obscures the angle of the mandible**. Pushes the earlobe upward and outward.
* **Palpation**: Tense and tender, but overlying skin is usually **normal in colour and temperature** (non-erythematous, not hot).
* **Laterality**: Usually bilateral, though often starts unilaterally.

### Oral & Mucosal Signs
* **Stensen's Duct (腮腺管)**: Opening on the buccal mucosa opposite the upper 2nd molar may appear erythematous and oedematous. 
* **Pain Triggers**: Exacerbated by chewing or eating sour/acidic foods (e.g., citrus) → ↑ salivary flow against an inflamed gland.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Mumps vs. Cervical Lymphadenitis
* **Anatomical Landmark**: Cervical lymph nodes are located strictly **below** the angle of the mandible and do not obscure it. Mumps parotitis crosses the angle and displaces the earlobe laterally and superiorly.

### Mumps vs. Bacterial Parotitis
* **Clinical Appearance**: Bacterial parotitis (e.g., *S. aureus*) features unilateral, exquisitely tender swelling with hot, erythematous overlying skin. Purulent discharge may be expressed from Stensen's duct, and the child looks toxic. Mumps is often bilateral, skin remains normal, and the discharge is serous.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
### Table of Differentials for Parotid/Neck Swelling
| Condition | Key Distinguishing Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Cervical Lymphadenitis (頸淋巴結炎)** | Swelling isolated below mandible angle; no earlobe displacement; ± overlying erythema. |
| **Bacterial Parotitis (細菌性腮腺炎)** | Highly toxic; unilateral; hot/red skin; purulent discharge from Stensen's duct. |
| **Sialolithiasis (唾液腺結石)** | Usually unilateral; colicky pain specifically ↑ during meals; palpable stone in duct. |
| **Other Viral Parotitis** | EBV, Parainfluenza, Coxsackievirus, HIV. Clinically similar; distinguished via PCR/Serology. |

## 7. Tiered Investigations
### Bedside & Initial Investigations
* **Clinical Diagnosis**: Gold standard in an outbreak setting or typical presentation.
* **Serum Amylase**: Often ↑ (originates from inflamed salivary glands, does not automatically imply pancreatitis).

### Microbiological & Virological Tests
* **Viral Swabs/Fluid**: Mumps virus PCR from buccal swab, saliva, throat swab, or urine.
* **Serology**: Mumps-specific IgM (+ve) or 4-fold ↑ in IgG titre (IgM may be -ve in the first few days).

### Special Investigations (If Complicated)
* **CSF Analysis**: Indicated if meningeal signs present. Shows aseptic picture: ↑ lymphocytes, normal/↓ glucose, viral PCR +ve.
* **Scrotal Ultrasound**: If epididymo-orchitis is suspected to rule out testicular torsion.

## 8. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Acute Supportive Care
* **Analgesia & Antipyretics**: Paracetamol or Ibuprofen for pain and fever.
* **Hydration**: Encourage oral fluids.
* **Dietary Modification**: Soft diet; strictly **avoid** sour, spicy, or acidic foods that stimulate saliva production and exacerbate pain.

### Hospital Authority (HA) & Public Health Protocols
* **Infection Control**: Strict droplet precautions in pediatric wards.
* **School Exclusion**: Child **MUST** be excluded from school/nursery for **5 days** from the onset of parotid swelling.
* **Notification**: Formally notify the Centre for Health Protection (CHP).

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Preventive & Community Care
* **MCHC Vaccination**: Ensure up-to-date MMRV vaccination for siblings/contacts.
* **Parental Education**: Reassure regarding the benign, self-limiting nature of the illness. Educate parents on red flags for complications (e.g., severe headache, vomiting, testicular pain, severe abdominal pain).

## 10. Complications & Prognosis
### Genitourinary Complications
* **Epididymo-orchitis (附睪睪丸炎)**: Most common complication in post-pubertal males (up to 30%). Sudden onset high fever, unilateral testicular pain, and swelling. May cause testicular atrophy, but **infertility is extremely rare** (usually unilateral).
* **Oophoritis (卵巢炎)**: Occurs rarely in post-pubertal females; presents with lower abdominal pain.

### Neurological Complications
* **Aseptic Meningitis (無菌性腦膜炎)**: Very common (clinically symptomatic in 10%, but asymptomatic CSF pleocytosis in 50%). Presents with headache, photophobia, neck stiffness. Usually benign with full recovery.
* **Encephalitis (腦炎)**: Rare (1 in 5000) but life-threatening.
* **Sensorineural Hearing Loss (感覺神經性聽力喪失)**: Rare, but historically a leading cause of acquired unilateral deafness in children. Can be transient or permanent.

### Gastrointestinal Complications
* **Pancreatitis (胰腺炎)**: Presents with severe epigastric pain, vomiting, and ↑ amylase/lipase.

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Viva Question: How can you clinically distinguish between a swollen parotid gland and a swollen upper cervical lymph node? Answer: A swollen parotid gland obscures the angle of the mandible and pushes the earlobe upward and outward, whereas a cervical lymph node is typically located below the angle of the mandible.
* Viva Question: What is the most common complication of mumps in a post-pubertal male? Answer: Epididymo-orchitis.
* Trap: Assuming bilateral parotid swelling with high fever automatically requires antibiotics. Pearl: Unless the overlying skin is hot/erythematous and there is purulent discharge from Stensen's duct, parotitis is overwhelmingly viral (Mumps) and requires only supportive care.
* Pitfall: Falsely diagnosing pancreatitis solely based on a raised serum amylase in a patient with mumps. Raised amylase is expected in mumps due to salivary gland inflammation; true pancreatitis requires clinical correlation (severe epigastric pain/vomiting) and a raised serum lipase.
* Pitfall: Advising patients that mumps orchitis commonly leads to sterility. This causes unnecessary severe anxiety; explain that mumps orchitis is usually unilateral and infertility is actually extremely rare.
