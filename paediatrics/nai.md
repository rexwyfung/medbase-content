# Non-Accidental Injury (NAI) / Child Abuse (非意外傷害 / 虐待兒童)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Definition of Child Maltreatment
* **Child Abuse (虐待兒童)**: Physical, emotional, or sexual abuse, or neglect of a child by a parent, caregiver, or other responsible person.
* **Non-Accidental Injury (NAI) (非意外傷害)**: Specifically refers to physical injuries inflicted deliberately.
* **Fabricated or Induced Illness (FII)**: Parent/carer invents or induces symptoms in child, leading to unnecessary medical interventions (formerly Munchausen Syndrome by Proxy).

### Local Epidemiology & Risk Factors
* **Incidence**: Significant, often under-reported in Hong Kong; hundreds of cases annually on the SWD Child Protection Registry.
* **Most Common Types**: Physical abuse & neglect.
* **Child Risk Factors**: Disability, "difficult" temperament, premature birth, unwanted pregnancy, wrong gender.
* **Parental/Family Risk Factors**: High stress, poverty, domestic violence, parental substance/alcohol abuse, mental illness, young parental age, step-parents, social isolation.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics

### Mechanism of Physical Injury
* **Blunt Force Trauma**: Punching, kicking, hitting with objects → fractures, bruises, solid organ injury (e.g., liver/spleen lacerations).
* **Thermal Injury**: Deliberate burning (cigarettes) or forced immersion in hot water.
* **Poisoning**: Deliberate ingestion/administration of drugs, toxins, or excessive salt.

### Pathophysiology of Abusive Head Trauma (AHT)
* **Mechanism**: Violent shaking (Shaken Baby Syndrome) → relatively large/heavy head rapidly accelerates & decelerates.
* **Subdural Haemorrhage (SDH) (硬膜下出血)**: Shearing/tearing of bridging veins.
* **Diffuse Axonal Injury (DAI) / HIE**: Severe shearing forces disrupt axons → profound hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy, often without external head trauma signs.
* **Retinal Haemorrhages (RH) (視網膜出血)**: Vitreoretinal traction forces during shaking cause multi-layered bleeding extending to the periphery.

## 3. Clinical Presentation

### The History: Red Flags
* **Incompatible History**: Explanation mechanism does not match injury severity (e.g., "rolled off sofa" → complex femur fracture).
* **Developmental Mismatch**: Mechanism impossible for child's developmental age (e.g., 3-month-old "pulling boiling water").
* **Changing Story**: History changes over time or differs between caregivers.
* **Delayed Presentation**: Unreasonable delay in seeking medical attention.
* **Attribution to Others**: Injury blamed on young sibling or family pet.
* **Parental Affect**: Vague, evasive, unconcerned, excessively aggressive, or inappropriate reaction.

### Presentation Scenarios
* **Direct Presentation**: Brought in for specific acute injury (e.g., fracture, burn, seizure).
* **Incidental Finding**: Injury noticed during examination for unrelated illness (e.g., URTI).
* **Late Presentation**: Faltering growth, developmental delay, behavioural changes (withdrawal, aggression, sexualized behaviour, self-harm).

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs (Red Flag Injuries)

### Suspicious Bruising Patterns
* **Pre-mobile Infants**: "Those who don't cruise, rarely bruise" → any bruise in non-mobile infant is highly suspicious.
* **TEN-4 FACESp Rule**: Bruising on **T**orso, **E**ars, **N**eck, **F**renulum, **A**ngle of jaw, **C**heeks, **E**yelids, **S**ubconjunctivae, **p**atterned.
* **Patterned Bruises**: Shape of handprint, slap mark, belt, loop marks (cords/wires), bite marks (adult dental arch >3cm).
* **Protected Areas**: Inner thighs, buttocks, genitalia.

### Suspicious Burn Patterns
* **Cigarette Burns**: Circular, punched-out, uniform depth (often limbs).
* **Immersion Scalds (浸湯燙傷)**: Glove-and-stocking distribution, clear sharp demarcation lines, sparing of flexion creases (indicates child forcefully held in hot water).

### Suspicious Bone Fractures
* **Rib Fractures**: Especially posterior ribs (squeezing chest violently).
* **Metaphyseal Corner/Bucket-Handle Fractures**: Yanking/pulling/twisting limbs.
* **Skull Fractures**: Complex, crossed sutures, wide, or depressed fractures in young infants.
* **Dating**: Multiple fractures of varying ages (healing callus visible).
* **Non-mobile infant**: Any fracture in an infant not yet walking.

### Signs of Abusive Head Trauma (AHT)
* **Encephalopathy**: Lethargy, irritability, poor feeding, apnea, seizures, coma, bulging fontanelle (↑ICP).
* **Imaging**: Subdural haemorrhage (often different ages/densities).
* **Fundoscopy**: Multi-layered retinal haemorrhages extending to periphery.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)

### NAI vs Osteogenesis Imperfecta (OI) (成骨不全症)
| Feature | NAI | Osteogenesis Imperfecta (OI) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **History** | Inconsistent/Suspicious | Often family hx of OI |
| **Sclerae** | White/Normal | **Blue sclerae** |
| **Bones on X-ray** | Normal density, specific NAI fractures | **Osteopenia**, thin cortices, Wormian bones |
| **Associated features** | Bruising, burns, neglect | Dentinogenesis imperfecta, deafness |

### Bruising vs Mongolian Blue Spots (蒙古斑)
| Feature | Bruises (NAI) | Mongolian Blue Spots (Congenital Dermal Melanocytosis) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Onset** | Acquired | **Present from birth** |
| **Evolution** | Changes colour (red → blue → green → yellow) | **Does not change colour** over days |
| **Location** | TEN-4 areas, unusual sites | Typically **sacrum, buttocks**, lower back |
| **Texture/Shape** | May have swelling/pattern | Flat, blue-grey macules, diffuse |

### Bruising vs Bleeding Disorders (e.g., ITP, Haemophilia)
* **Bleeding Disorders**: Extensive bruising/petechiae often in normal play areas, accompanied by abnormal coagulation profile (↑PT/APTT) or FBC (↓Platelets). No associated specific NAI fractures or burns.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis

### Broad Differential Diagnosis for Suspected NAI
* **Accidental Injury**: True accidents with compatible mechanism.
* **Bleeding Disorders**: ITP, Haemophilia, von Willebrand disease, Leukaemia.
* **Bone Fragility Disorders**: Osteogenesis Imperfecta, Rickets (Vitamin D deficiency), Prematurity osteopenia.
* **Skin Conditions/Practices**: Mongolian spots, Phytophotodermatitis, Staphylococcal Scalded Skin Syndrome (SSSS).
* **Traditional Medicine**: Cupping (拔罐), Coining (刮痧) → symmetrical, uniform, culturally explained.

## 7. Tiered Investigations

### Bedside & Clinical Documentation
* **Clinical Photography**: Mandatory to document all visible injuries. Must follow HA consent protocols.
* **Body Map**: Draw injuries meticulously.
* **Verbatim Notes**: Record exact quotes from caregivers.

### Blood Investigations
* **FBC & Blood Film**: R/o leukaemia, thrombocytopenia.
* **Coagulation Screen**: PT, APTT, Factor VIII/IX levels, von Willebrand screen → definitively r/o bleeding disorders.
* **Bone Profile**: Ca2+, PO4, ALP, Vitamin D, PTH → r/o rickets.
* **Liver & Pancreatic Enzymes**: AST, ALT, Amylase, Lipase → screen for occult abdominal trauma.
* **Toxicology Screen**: Urine/blood if poisoning/FII suspected.

### Imaging Investigations
* **Skeletal Survey (骨骼X光檢查)**: Mandatory for all suspected NAI < 2 years old. High-detail X-rays of every bone to find occult/healing fractures (includes oblique ribs). Repeat in 11-14 days to see callus formation if initial is negative but suspicion is high.
* **Neuroimaging**: 
  * **Urgent CT Brain**: Acute suspected AHT (detects acute blood/SDH).
  * **MRI Brain/Spine**: Subacute/chronic evaluation, detects DAI and spinal subdural bleeds.

### Special Assessments
* **Ophthalmology Assessment**: Dilated fundoscopy by senior ophthalmologist for retinal haemorrhages.

## 8. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)

### Acute Medical Management
* **Resuscitation**: ABCDE approach. Treat shock, ↑ICP, status epilepticus.
* **Surgical/Orthopaedic**: Neurosurgical evacuation of SDH if indicated, orthopaedic casting/fixation of fractures.
* **Treat Burns**: Fluid resuscitation, analgesia, dressings.

### Ward Admission & Safety (HA Protocols)
* **ADMIT TO PLACE OF SAFETY**: Admit child to paediatric ward immediately. **NEVER** discharge if NAI is suspected until full MDT assessment.
* **Protect Siblings**: Consider immediate risk to other children at home; inform authorities to check siblings.
* **Infection Control/Medical Care**: Treat incidental findings (e.g., malnutrition, infections).

### Documentation & Legal Protocols
* **Meticulous Records**: Objective, non-judgmental. Do not write "child abuse confirmed" before MDT conclusion; write "injuries highly suspicious for NAI".

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care

### Mandatory MDT Approach in Hong Kong
* **Medical Social Worker (MSW) (醫務社工)**: Must be consulted immediately. They liaise with Social Welfare Department (SWD).
* **Child Abuse Investigation Unit (CAIU) / Police (虐兒案件調查組 / 警察)**: Must be informed if a crime is suspected.
* **Maternal and Child Health Centre (MCHC)**: Review past growth, vaccination, and developmental records.

### Multidisciplinary Case Conference (MDCC) (多專業個案會議)
* **Participants**: SWD, MSW, Paediatrician, Police, Teachers, Parents.
* **Goal**: Formulate a comprehensive welfare and protection plan.
* **Legal Protection**: Can apply for a **Care or Protection Order (照顧或保護令)** via Juvenile Court to mandate safe placement (foster care, relative, or monitored home return).

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags

### Core Safeguarding Principles
* **Child Safety is Paramount**: Overrides parental rights or maintaining a "good relationship" with the family.
* **Duty to Act**: Professional and ethical obligation to report suspicion. Silence = complicity.
* **Early Intervention**: Early recognition of vulnerabilities (e.g., maternal depression, poverty) and providing support prevents progression to severe abuse.

### Clinical Red Flags Reminders
* Pre-mobile bruising.
* Unexplained oral/frenulum injuries in infants (force-feeding).
* Delay in seeking care.
* History incompatible with injury.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis

### Physical Complications
* **Neurological Disability**: Cerebral palsy, cortical blindness, severe learning difficulties, epilepsy (post-AHT).
* **Orthopaedic Deformity**: Malunion of untreated fractures.
* **Death**: AHT and severe abdominal trauma are leading causes of fatal NAI.

### Psychosocial & Psychiatric Complications
* **Mental Health**: PTSD, depression, anxiety, eating disorders.
* **Behavioural**: Substance abuse, risky sexual behaviour, criminality in adolescence/adulthood.
* **Cycle of Abuse**: Increased risk of perpetuating abuse in next generation.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: What is the classic triad of Abusive Head Trauma (Shaken Baby Syndrome)? Answer: Subdural haemorrhage, Retinal haemorrhages, and Encephalopathy.
* Viva Question: You see a 4-month-old infant in the AED with a spiral fracture of the femur. The parents say the baby rolled off the bed. What is your immediate action? Answer: Admit the child to the paediatric ward for safety. The mechanism is incompatible with developmental age. Consult MSW and arrange skeletal survey/bloods to rule out bone/bleeding disorders.
* Viva Question: What specific X-ray is required to identify characteristic rib fractures in suspected NAI? Answer: Oblique views of the ribs (part of the mandatory skeletal survey) to detect posterior rib fractures.
* Trap: Assuming bruising on a 6-month-old's shins is normal from "bumping into things". Pearl: Infants who are not yet cruising or walking ("pre-mobile") rarely bruise accidentally. Any bruise in this age group warrants an NAI workup.
* Trap: Believing a skeletal survey is sufficient if done once on day 1 of admission. Pearl: Occult fractures may not be visible immediately. A repeat skeletal survey is often required 11-14 days later to look for healing callus formation.
* Pitfall: Discharging a child with a suspicious injury because the parents seem "nice", "middle-class", or "well-educated". Abuse occurs across all socioeconomic backgrounds; the mechanism of injury must remain the primary focus.
* Pitfall: Forgetting to examine the child's siblings. If one child is abused, siblings are at high risk and must be evaluated by the MDT and potentially brought in for medical assessment.
