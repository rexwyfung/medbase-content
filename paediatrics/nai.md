# Non-Accidental Injury (NAI) / Child Abuse (非意外傷害 / 虐待兒童)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Physical, emotional, or sexual abuse, or neglect of a child by a parent, caregiver, or other person with responsibility for the child. NAI specifically refers to physical injuries inflicted deliberately.
- **Local Epidemiology**: A significant and under-reported issue in Hong Kong. The Social Welfare Department (SWD) Child Protection Registry records hundreds of cases annually, with physical abuse and neglect being the most common. High-stress environments, poverty, domestic violence, and parental substance abuse are major risk factors.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Mechanism of Injury**: Injuries are often the result of blunt force trauma, shaking (Abusive Head Trauma), burning, or poisoning.
- **Abusive Head Trauma (AHT) / Shaken Baby Syndrome**: Violent shaking causes the infant's relatively large, heavy head to rapidly accelerate and decelerate. This causes tearing of bridging veins (subdural haemorrhage) and severe diffuse axonal injury (hypoxic-ischaemic encephalopathy), often without external signs of head trauma.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Presentation is highly variable. The child may be brought in for a specific injury, or the injury may be an incidental finding during an examination for an unrelated illness.
- **The History**: The most critical component. Red flags include:
  - History is incompatible with the injury (e.g., "he rolled off the sofa" causing a complex femur fracture in a 2-month-old).
  - History changes over time or differs between caregivers.
  - Delay in seeking medical attention.
  - Injury is attributed to a sibling or a pet.
  - The developmental age of the child makes the mechanism impossible (e.g., a 3-month-old "pulling boiling water onto themselves").

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs (Red Flag Injuries)
- **Bruising**: 
  - "Those who don't cruise, rarely bruise" (bruising in a pre-mobile infant is highly suspicious).
  - Bruising in unusual locations: **TEN-4 FACESp** rule (Torso, Ears, Neck, Frenulum, Angle of jaw, Cheeks, Eyelids, Subconjunctivae, pattern bruises).
  - Patterned bruises (e.g., handprint, belt mark, loop marks).
- **Burns**: 
  - Cigarette burns (circular, punched-out).
  - Immersion scalds (glove-and-stocking distribution with clear demarcation lines and sparing of flexion creases, indicating the child was held forcefully in hot water).
- **Fractures**: 
  - Rib fractures (especially posterior, caused by squeezing the chest).
  - Metaphyseal corner/bucket-handle fractures (caused by violent pulling/twisting of a limb).
  - Multiple fractures of different ages.
  - Any fracture in a non-mobile infant.
- **Abusive Head Trauma**: Encephalopathy (lethargy, seizures, coma), Subdural Haemorrhage (SDH), and **Retinal Haemorrhages** (multilayered, extending to the periphery).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Osteogenesis Imperfecta (OI)**: Causes multiple fractures, but usually associated with blue sclerae, family history, and osteopenia on X-ray.
- **Mongolian Blue Spots (Congenital Dermal Melanocytosis)**: Common in Asian infants. Flat, blue-grey macules usually on the sacrum/buttocks. They do not change colour over days (unlike bruises) and are present from birth.
- **Bleeding Disorders (e.g., Haemophilia, ITP, Leukaemia)**: Can cause extensive bruising, but usually accompanied by abnormal coagulation profiles or full blood counts.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Accidental injury.
- Bleeding disorders (ITP, Haemophilia, von Willebrand disease, Leukaemia).
- Bone fragility disorders (Osteogenesis Imperfecta, Rickets).
- Skin conditions (Mongolian spots, Phytophotodermatitis, Cupping/Coining - traditional medicine practices).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Skeletal Survey**: Mandatory for all suspected NAI cases in children < 2 years old. A series of highly detailed X-rays of every bone in the body to look for occult or healing fractures.
- **Neuroimaging**: CT Brain (acute) or MRI Brain (subacute/chronic) to look for subdural haemorrhages or hypoxic-ischaemic injury in suspected AHT.
- **Ophthalmology Assessment**: Dilated fundoscopy by a senior ophthalmologist to document retinal haemorrhages.
- **Bloods**: FBC, Coagulation screen (PT/APTT), Factor VIII/IX levels, von Willebrand screen (to definitively rule out bleeding disorders before accusing caregivers). Bone profile (Calcium, Phosphate, ALP) and Vitamin D to rule out rickets.
- **Clinical Photography**: Mandatory to document all visible injuries. Must be done with consent and following HA protocols.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Medical Management**: Treat the acute injuries (e.g., neurosurgical evacuation of SDH, orthopaedic casting of fractures, treating shock).
- **Admission**: **ADMIT THE CHILD TO A PLACE OF SAFETY (THE WARD)**. Do not discharge the child if there is any suspicion of NAI until a full multidisciplinary assessment is complete.
- **Documentation**: Must be meticulous, objective, and verbatim (record exactly what the parents say in quotes). Draw injuries on a body map.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **The MDT Approach is Mandatory in Hong Kong**:
  - **Medical Social Worker (MSW)**: Must be consulted immediately. They will liaise with the Social Welfare Department (SWD).
  - **Police (Child Abuse Investigation Unit - CAIU)**: Must be informed if a crime is suspected.
  - **Multidisciplinary Case Conference (MDCC)**: A formal meeting organized by the SWD involving doctors, MSW, police, teachers, and parents to formulate a welfare and protection plan for the child (e.g., Care or Protection Order).

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- *This entire topic is the core of safeguarding.*
- **The overriding principle**: The safety and welfare of the child are paramount. If you suspect abuse, you have a professional and ethical duty to act, even if it damages the relationship with the parents.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Complications**: Severe permanent neurological disability (cerebral palsy, blindness, severe learning difficulties) from Abusive Head Trauma. Death.
- **Psychological**: Long-term emotional trauma, PTSD, increased risk of substance abuse and perpetuating the cycle of abuse in adulthood.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "What is the classic triad of Abusive Head Trauma (Shaken Baby Syndrome)?" (Subdural haemorrhage, Retinal haemorrhages, and Encephalopathy).
- *Viva*: "You see a 4-month-old infant in the AED with a spiral fracture of the femur. The parents say the baby rolled off the bed. What is your immediate action?" (Admit the child to the paediatric ward for safety. The mechanism is incompatible with the developmental age, as 4-month-olds cannot roll over forcefully enough to cause a spiral femur fracture. Consult the Medical Social Worker and arrange a skeletal survey and bloods to rule out bleeding/bone disorders).
- *Pitfall*: Discharging a child with a suspicious injury because the parents seem "nice" or "middle-class." Abuse occurs across all socioeconomic backgrounds.
