# Bell's Palsy (貝爾氏麻痺症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition
* Acute, unilateral, idiopathic lower motor neuron (LMN) facial nerve (CN VII) palsy
* Isolated LMN paresis of CN VII → facial weakness (面部肌肉無力)
### Local Epidemiology
* Most common cause of acute facial paralysis in children, relatively uncommon overall

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Pathophysiology
* Post-viral inflammatory & immune-mediated swelling of CN VII at the strictly confined geniculate ganglion or within the tight bony facial canal → nerve compression
* Suspected triggers: Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV-1), Varicella Zoster reactivation, Lyme disease
* Anatomy: LMN lesion distal to the facial motor nucleus in the pons → affects ALL branches of CN VII

## 3. Clinical Presentation
### Classical Presentation
* A child wakes up well but parents notice an asymmetrical face or "crooked" smile
* Drooling of fluids from the affected side of the mouth during feeds
* Difficulty or inability to close the eye on the affected side

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Key Symptoms
* Sudden onset unilateral facial droop
* Inability to close the eye tightly (→ risk of corneal exposure)
* Drooling from the affected corner of the mouth
* Hyperacusis (聽覺過敏): Loud sounds are painful (CN VII innervates stapedius muscle)
* Loss of taste on anterior 2/3 of tongue (Chorda Tympani branch involvement)
* Painful vesicles on tonsillar fauces & external ear (if Herpes Zoster Oticus)
### Key Signs
* Forehead involvement: Flattened wrinkles, inability to raise eyebrow on the affected side
* Loss of nasolabial fold (鼻唇溝消失)
* Asymmetrical smile with drooping corner of the mouth
* Bell's phenomenon: Eye rolls upward and outward on attempted eye closure

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Distinguishing from UMN Lesion
* LMN Lesion (Bell's Palsy): Forehead WEAK. Affects both upper & lower face.
* UMN Lesion (Stroke/Tumor): Forehead PRESERVED. Upper face receives redundant bilateral cortical innervation → only lower face drops.
### Distinguishing from Brainstem Lesion
* Brainstem lesion +ve if: concurrent CN VI (abducens) paresis, ipsilateral cerebellar signs, or contralateral UMN signs.
### Distinguishing from Cerebellopontine Angle Lesion
* Compressive lesion +ve if: concurrent CN VIII (auditory) paresis.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
### Common Differentials
| Condition | Differentiating Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Stroke / Tumor (UMN)** | Forehead spared, contralateral hemiparesis, +ve UMN signs |
| **Ramsay Hunt Syndrome** | Herpes Zoster Oticus, painful vesicles in ear/throat, intense ear pain |
| **Lyme Disease** | Rare in HK, tick exposure, bilateral facial palsy possible, erythema migrans |
| **Otitis Media / Mastoiditis** | Fever, bulging red tympanic membrane, otorrhea |
| **Brainstem Glioma / Tumor** | Associated CN VI palsy, cerebellar/UMN signs |
| **Hypertension / Coarctation** | Associated with BP ↑, renal failure, heart murmur |
| **Guillain-Barré Syndrome** | Ascending weakness, bilateral facial weakness (easily missed in young children) |

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
### Bedside Investigations
* **Blood Pressure (CRITICAL)**: Mandatory to exclude underlying severe hypertension (associated with coarctation of aorta & renal failure)
* **Otoscopy**: Mandatory to rule out acute otitis media & Ramsay Hunt vesicles
* **Neurological Exam**: Full CN exam to exclude brainstem/cerebellopontine angle lesions
### Bloods & Special Tests
* Primarily a clinical diagnosis
* Routine bloods not indicated unless atypical presentation
### Imaging
* **MRI Brain/IAC**: ONLY indicated if UMN signs present, multiple CN deficits, or no improvement after 1-2 months

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Acute Management (1st Line)
* **Corticosteroids**: Oral **Prednisolone** (10-day tapering course)
* Start within 72 hrs of symptom onset → ↓ nerve inflammation & swelling in facial canal → speeds full recovery
### Eye Care (CRITICAL)
* Goal: Prevent conjunctival infection & corneal ulceration (角膜潰瘍) due to incomplete blinking
* Day: Frequent lubricating eye drops (人工淚液)
* Night: Ocular lubricant ointment + strictly taping the eye shut vertically
* Severe cases: Tarsorrhaphy (瞼緣縫合術)
### Antiviral Therapy
* **Aciclovir** (Acyclovir): Add if HSV or Varicella Zoster (Ramsay Hunt) suspected. Not routinely recommended for simple Bell's Palsy in children.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
### MDT Support
* **Ophthalmology**: Urgent referral if corneal abrasions or conjunctival infections develop
* **Physiotherapy**: Facial nerve exercises/stimulation if delayed recovery

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Safeguarding Red Flags
* NAI (Non-Accidental Injury) is a rare cause but evaluate for temporal bone fractures if there are signs of head trauma, bruising, or basal skull fracture.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
### Complications
* Conjunctival infection & Corneal ulceration (→ permanent blindness if neglected)
* Synkinesis (聯帶運動): Miswiring during nerve regeneration → e.g., eye twitching when smiling, "crocodile tears" (crying while eating)
### Prognosis
* Excellent in children. >90% fully recover facial nerve function within 1 to 6 months.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Viva Question: If a child presents with a facial droop but can wrinkle his forehead symmetrically, what level of the nervous system is the lesion in, and why is the forehead spared?
* Answer: It is an Upper Motor Neuron (UMN) lesion above the facial nucleus. The forehead is spared because the upper face portion of the facial nucleus receives redundant, bilateral cortical input from both cerebral hemispheres.
* Trap: Sending a child home without thoroughly checking their ability to close their eye, and neglecting to prescribe eye drops.
* Pearl: You must actively demonstrate eye taping to the parents and prescribe daytime drops and nighttime ointment to prevent permanent corneal damage.
* Pitfall: Assuming all facial palsies are idiopathic Bell's palsy. 
* Pearl: Always check blood pressure to exclude severe hypertension (linked to coarctation/renal failure) and perform a full cranial nerve exam to exclude brainstem lesions (esp. CN VI involvement).
