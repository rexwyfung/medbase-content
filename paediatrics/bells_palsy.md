# Bell's Palsy (貝爾氏麻痺症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: An acute, unilateral, idiopathic lower motor neuron (LMN) facial nerve (Cranial Nerve VII) palsy. 
- **Local Epidemiology**: The most common cause of acute facial paralysis in children, though still relatively uncommon. 

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Thought to be a post-viral inflammatory and immune-mediated swelling of the facial nerve at the strictly confined geniculate ganglion or as it exits the narrow stylomastoid foramen. 
- Suspected triggers often include Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV-1) or reactivated Varicella Zoster.
- **Anatomy**: Because it occurs *after* the facial motor nucleus in the pons (a Lower Motor Neuron lesion), it affects all branches of the facial nerve.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- A 7-year-old boy wakes up perfectly well but his mother notices his face is "crooked." When he drinks milk, it dribbles out the right side of his mouth. 

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- Unilateral facial droop.
- **Includes the Forehead**: Flattened winkles, inability to raise the eyebrow on the affected side.
- Inability to close the eye tightly (can lead to corneal exposure/ulceration).
- Drooping of the corner of the mouth, loss of the nasolabial fold.
- May have hyperacusis (loud sounds are painful because CN VII innervates the stapedius muscle which normally dampens the eardrum).
- Loss of taste on the anterior 2/3 of the tongue (Chorda Tympani nerve involvement).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from Stroke (Upper Motor Neuron): In a stroke, the forehead is typically **SPARED** because the forehead receives bilateral innervation from the cortex. If the child CAN raise both eyebrows normally but the lower mouth drops, it is an UMN lesion (e.g., stroke, brain tumor).

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Stroke (UMN lesion)
- Lyme Disease (rare in HK but classical in US/Europe - can cause bilateral facial palsy)
- Ramsay Hunt Syndrome (Herpes Zoster Oticus - facial palsy accompanied by intense ear pain and a vesicular rash in the ear canal)
- Otitis Media (can occasionally involve the facial nerve directly as it passes through the middle ear)
- Brainstem tumor (e.g., Diffuse Intrinsic Pontine Glioma)

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- Mostly a clinical diagnosis.
- **Otoscopy**: Mandatory to rule out acute otitis media or Ramsay Hunt vesicles.
- **Imaging**: MRI brain/IAC is ONLY indicated if there are other cranial nerve deficits, no improvement after 1-2 months, or if UMN signs are present.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Corticosteroids**: Oral Prednisolone (short 10-day tapering course). Highly effective if started within 72 hours of symptom onset to desperately reduce nerve swelling in the tight bony canal.
- **Eye Care (CRITICAL)**: Because they cannot blink, the cornea will dry out, ulcerate, and cause permanent blindness. 
  - Prescribe frequent **lubricating eye drops** during the day.
  - Ocular lubricant ointment and explicitly taping the eye shut vertically at night.
- **Antivirals**: Routine use of Acyclovir is generally not recommended in children unless Ramsay Hunt is suspected.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Excellent prognosis in children. >90% fully recover facial nerve function within 1 to 6 months.
- Synkinesis (miswiring during nerve regeneration, causing e.g., the eye to twitch when the child smiles).

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Sending a child home without thoroughly checking their ability to close their eye, and neglecting to prescribe eye drops. You must actively demonstrate eye taping to the parents.
- **Viva Q**: "If a child presents with a facial droop but can wrinkle his forehead symmetrically, what level of the nervous system is the lesion in, and why is the forehead spared?" Answer: It is an Upper Motor Neuron (UMN) lesion above the facial nucleus. The forehead is spared because the upper face portion of the facial nucleus receives redundant, bilateral cortical input from both cerebral hemispheres.
