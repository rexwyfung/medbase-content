# Pierre Robin Sequence (皮埃爾羅賓序列症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition
* **Pierre Robin Sequence (皮埃爾羅賓序列症)**: Congenital anomaly sequence (not a primary syndrome) characterized by a pathognomonic triad: **Micrognathia** (小頜畸形), **Glossoptosis** (舌下垂), and a **U-shaped cleft palate** (U型顎裂).
* Primary defect is mandibular hypoplasia → initiates a secondary cascade of airway and palatal defects.

### Local Epidemiology & HA Context
* Incidence: ~1 in 8,500 to 14,000 live births globally; similar rates in Hong Kong.
* Syndromic association: ~20-40% of cases are isolated; ~60-80% are syndromic (most commonly **Stickler syndrome** in local genetic cohorts).
* Encountered frequently by regional Hospital Authority (HA) Cleft Lip and Palate Multidisciplinary Clinics (e.g., at QEH, UCH, PWH).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics

### Embryological Cascade (The Sequence)
* **Wk 7-11 Gestation**: Primary **mandibular hypoplasia** (小頜畸形) occurs → mechanically prevents normal descent of the tongue.
* **Glossoptosis** (舌下垂): Tongue remains abnormally superior & posterior within the oral cavity.
* **Mechanical Barrier**: Elevated tongue physically blocks the fusion of palatal shelves → results in a characteristic **U-shaped cleft palate** (U型顎裂).

### Genetic Associations
* Highly associated with `SOX9`, `KCNJ9`, `COL2A1`, `COL11A1`, `COL11A2` mutations.
* Common associated syndromes:
  * **Stickler syndrome** (most common, COL2A1).
  * **Treacher Collins syndrome**.
  * **22q11.2 Deletion (DiGeorge syndrome)**.

## 3. Clinical Presentation

### Immediate Neonatal Presentation
* **Severe Upper Airway Obstruction** (上呼吸道阻塞): Immediately post-delivery, the hypotonic tongue falls back → pharyngeal obstruction.
* **Respiratory Distress**: Stridor, sternal retractions, cyanotic episodes (發紺). ↑ risk of **cor pulmonale** if untreated.

### Feeding Presentation
* **Severe Feeding Difficulties**: Mechanical inability to create negative intraoral pressure due to the cleft + airway compromise during the suck-swallow-breathe cycle.
* ↑ risk of **recurrent choking**, **aspiration pneumonia** (吸入性肺炎), and **faltering growth** (生長遲緩).

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Pathognomonic Triad Signs
* **Micrognathia / Retrognathia** (小頜畸形): Visibly small & recessed mandible.
* **Glossoptosis** (舌下垂): Downward displacement/retraction of the tongue base.
* **Cleft Palate** (顎裂): Distinctive wide, U-shaped defect of the secondary palate.

### Airway & Feeding Signs
* **Inspiratory Stridor** (喘鳴).
* **Cyanosis** (發紺): Classically relieved by prone positioning.
* **Recession**: Subcostal, intercostal, sternal retractions.
* Failure to thrive (↓ weight centiles on HK DH growth charts).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)

### U-Shaped vs. V-Shaped Cleft Palate
* **U-shaped cleft** (Pierre Robin): Indicates a mechanical obstruction (tongue) physically preventing shelf fusion. Highly discriminatory.
* **V-shaped cleft** (Standard): Indicates primary failure of shelf fusion or medial edge epithelial breakdown without a mechanical barrier.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis

### Syndromic Differentials
| Differential Diagnosis | Key Discriminatory Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Stickler Syndrome** | Myopia (↑ risk of retinal detachment), sensorineural/conductive hearing loss, arthropathy. Commonest syndromic cause. |
| **Treacher Collins Syndrome** | Autosomal dominant. Malar hypoplasia, downward slanting palpebral fissures, microtia, hearing loss. |
| **22q11.2 Deletion (DiGeorge)** | Congenital heart defects (e.g., Tetralogy of Fallot), hypocalcaemia, thymic hypoplasia. |
| **Isolated Cleft Palate** | Usually V-shaped. No micrognathia or glossoptosis. Airway is usually secure. |

## 7. Tiered Investigations

### Bedside & Physiological Investigations
* **Continuous Cardiorespiratory Monitoring**: SpO2 & ECG monitoring to detect hypoxic/apnoeic events.
* **Airway Assessment**: Direct laryngoscopy/bronchoscopy by Paediatric ENT/Anaesthesia to evaluate the degree of obstruction and rule out concurrent lower airway anomalies (e.g., tracheomalacia).

### Imaging & Special Investigations
* **Polysomnography (Sleep Study)**: Gold standard to assess the severity of Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA). Guides necessity of surgical vs. conservative airway management.
* **Echocardiogram**: Rule out congenital heart disease (esp. if 22q11.2 deletion suspected).
* **Ophthalmology Assessment**: Crucial screening to rule out severe myopia/retinal detachment (Stickler syndrome).

### Laboratory Investigations
* **Genetics Workup**: Chromosomal microarray (CMA) or Whole Exome Sequencing (WES) via HK Clinical Genetic Service (CGS) to rule out syndromic causes.
* **Pre-operative Bloods**: CBC, LRFT, Clotting profile prior to any airway or palatal surgery.

## 8. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)

### Acute Airway Management (HA Tiered Protocol)
* **Mild Obstruction (1st Line)**: **Prone positioning** (俯臥姿勢). Gravity pulls the tongue forward, clearing the posterior pharynx.
* **Moderate Obstruction (2nd Line)**: **Nasopharyngeal airway (NPA)** insertion. Bypasses the tongue base. Often requires prolonged home NPA care by parents.
* **Severe Obstruction (3rd Line/Surgical)**:
  * **Tongue-lip adhesion (Glossopexy)**.
  * **Mandibular distraction osteogenesis (MDO)** (下頜骨牽引成骨術): Gradually lengthens the mandible to pull the tongue forward.
  * **Tracheostomy** (氣管造口術): Last resort for refractory severe obstruction.

### Feeding & Nutritional Management
* **Enteral Feeding**: **Nasogastric (NG) tube** feeding is universally required initially to prevent aspiration & ensure caloric intake.
* **Specialized Bottles**: Haberman feeder or Mead Johnson cleft palate squeezy bottles once airway is secured and suck-swallow coordination improves.
* **Dietitian Input**: High-calorie formula to promote "catch-up" growth (生長追趕) prior to cleft surgeries.

### Chronic & Surgical Management
* **Cleft Palate Repair**: Usually performed at **9-12 months** by HA Cleft Lip & Palate Team (Plastic Surgery/ENT).
* **Speech Therapy**: Long-term follow-up to manage velopharyngeal insufficiency (VPI) and language delay.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care

### Multidisciplinary Team (MDT) Involvement
* **Core Team**: General Paediatrician, Paediatric ENT, Plastic Surgery, Clinical Geneticist, Respiratory (for sleep studies).
* **Allied Health**: Speech Therapist (ST) for feeding/swallowing, Dietitian, Occupational Therapist, Audiologist.

### Psychosocial Support & Parental Training
* **Caregiver Training**: Extensive pre-discharge training for NPA care, NG tube feeding, CPR, and recognizing respiratory distress.
* **Medical Social Worker (MSW)**: Financial assistance for special feeding equipment, suction machines, and home monitoring.
* **Maternal and Child Health Centre (MCHC)**: Routine developmental and growth surveillance post-discharge.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags

### Caregiver Burnout & Neglect Risks
* **High Medical Complexity**: Extreme parental fatigue due to 24/7 NPA/NG tube care, suctioning, and apnoea alarms.
* **Risk of Accidental Neglect**: Missed HA follow-ups, poor tube hygiene, or medication/feed non-compliance due to overwhelming stress.
* **Action**: Early Social Welfare Department (SWD) or MSW involvement for respite care and psychological support is critical to prevent severe caregiver exhaustion and non-accidental injury (NAI).

## 11. Complications & Prognosis

### Medical & Surgical Complications
* **Airway/Cardiac**: Chronic upper airway obstruction → Hypoxia → **Cor Pulmonale** (肺源性心臟病).
* **Nutritional**: Severe failure to thrive (FTT).
* **ENT**: Recurrent otitis media with effusion (glue ear) due to cleft palate (Eustachian tube dysfunction) → conductive hearing loss.

### Long-Term Prognosis
* **Airway Resolution**: The hypoplastic mandible usually undergoes **"catch-up" growth** (生長追趕) in the first 2-3 years, eventually allowing airway independence and weaning of NPA/tracheostomy.
* **Development**: Normal cognitive development is expected unless associated with a specific neurodevelopmental syndrome.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: Why is it called a sequence and not a syndrome?
  Answer: Because a single primary embryological defect (mandibular hypoplasia) leads to a cascade of secondary mechanical defects (glossoptosis → cleft palate).
* Trap: Assuming intubation is straightforward in standard airway distress.
  Pearl: Intubating these infants is notoriously difficult (anesthetic emergency) due to the micrognathia and anterior larynx. Always call senior ENT/Anaesthesia early for fiberoptic intubation.
* Pitfall: Discharging an infant solely based on adequate weight gain via NG tube.
  Avoidance: Never discharge until polysomnography (sleep study) confirms the airway is safe during sleep, as glossoptosis worsens during REM sleep hypotonia.
* Viva Question: What is the most important routine screening for an infant with Pierre Robin Sequence?
  Answer: Ophthalmology referral to screen for Stickler syndrome (retinal detachment risk) and audiology for associated syndromic hearing loss.
