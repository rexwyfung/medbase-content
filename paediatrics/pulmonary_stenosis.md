
# Pulmonary Stenosis (肺動脈瓣狹窄)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Narrowing of the pulmonary valve (valvar), below the valve (subvalvar/infundibular), or above the valve (supravalvar), obstructing blood flow from the right ventricle to the pulmonary artery.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Accounts for ~10% of congenital heart disease in HK. Often isolated but frequently associated with genetic syndromes (e.g., Noonan syndrome, Congenital Rubella Syndrome).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Obstruction leads to Right Ventricular Hypertrophy (RVH) to maintain cardiac output.
- Over time, severe obstruction can lead to right heart failure.
- **Critical Pulmonary Stenosis**: In neonates, severe valvar stenosis can be duct-dependent. The RV cannot pump enough blood to the lungs, so survival depends on right-to-left shunting across the foramen ovale (causing cyanosis) and left-to-right shunting across the PDA to supply the lungs.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- See below.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Mild**: Asymptomatic. Detected as an incidental murmur during routine checks.
- **Moderate/Severe**: Exertional dyspnoea, fatigue, signs of right heart failure (hepatomegaly, peripheral oedema).
- **Auscultation**:
  - **Ejection systolic murmur** at the Left Upper Sternal Border (LUSB), radiating to the back/left axilla.
  - **Ejection click** (if valvar): The *only* right-sided event that *decreases* with inspiration (due to increased venous return opening the valve slightly before systole).
  - Widely split S2 (delayed P2 due to prolonged RV ejection time).
  - Prominent 'a' wave in JVP (forceful RA contraction against a stiff RV).
  - Parasternal heave (RVH).
## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **ASD**: Also has an ejection systolic murmur at LUSB and widely split S2, but the split is *fixed* (does not vary with respiration), and there is no ejection click.
- **Aortic Stenosis**: Murmur is at the Right Upper Sternal Border (RUSB), radiates to the carotids.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Most Likely**:
  - Isolated valvar pulmonary stenosis.
- **Must-Not-Miss**:
  - Tetralogy of Fallot (PS is a key component, but accompanied by VSD, overriding aorta, and RVH).
  - Critical PS in a neonate (duct-dependent, presents with severe cyanosis).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**:
  - **ECG**: Right axis deviation, Right Ventricular Hypertrophy (tall R waves in V1, deep S waves in V6). Right atrial enlargement (peaked P waves) if severe.
  - **CXR**: Normal heart size (unless heart failure). Prominent main pulmonary artery (post-stenotic dilatation). Normal or decreased pulmonary vascular markings.
- **Gold Standard**:
  - **Echocardiogram**: Diagnostic. Determines the site of stenosis (valvar, subvalvar, supravalvar), measures the pressure gradient (mild <30 mmHg, moderate 30-50 mmHg, severe >50 mmHg), and assesses RV function.
- **Monitoring**:
  - Not applicable.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**:
  - **Neonatal Critical PS**: Prostaglandin E1 (Alprostadil) infusion to keep the PDA open.
- **Chronic/Definitive**:
  - **Mild**: Observation. Regular cardiology follow-up.
  - **Moderate/Severe (Gradient > 50 mmHg or symptomatic)**:
    - **Transcatheter Balloon Valvuloplasty**: First-line treatment of choice for typical valvar PS. Highly effective and safe (performed at HKCH).
    - **Surgical Valvotomy/Repair**: Indicated if balloon valvuloplasty fails, or for subvalvar/supravalvar stenosis, or dysplastic valves (e.g., in Noonan syndrome).
## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Genetic assessment if syndromic features are present (e.g., Noonan syndrome).
## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Reassure parents that balloon valvuloplasty is minimally invasive with excellent outcomes.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Excellent prognosis post-intervention.
- Complications (uncommon): Right heart failure, arrhythmias, infective endocarditis (prophylaxis no longer routinely recommended for isolated PS unless there's a history of IE).

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Forgetting the radiation of the murmur. *Pearl*: Aortic stenosis radiates to the carotids; Pulmonary stenosis radiates to the back/left axilla.
- **OSCE Pearl**: If you hear an ejection click, listen carefully during respiration. If it gets softer on inspiration, it's pulmonary. If it doesn't change, it's aortic.
