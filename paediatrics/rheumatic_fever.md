# Acute Rheumatic Fever (ARF) (急性風濕熱)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A delayed, non-suppurative, autoimmune, multisystem inflammatory disease that follows a pharyngeal infection with Group A Beta-Haemolytic Streptococcus (GAS).
- **Local Epidemiology**: Now rare in Hong Kong due to improved living standards and prompt antibiotic treatment of streptococcal pharyngitis. However, it remains a critical "must-not-miss" diagnosis because of its potential to cause permanent, severe heart damage.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Molecular Mimicry**: The fundamental mechanism.
  - A susceptible child develops a GAS throat infection (Strep throat).
  - The immune system mounts a defense, producing antibodies against the M protein on the surface of the Streptococcus bacteria.
  - Unfortunately, these antibodies cross-react with structurally similar proteins in the host's own tissues—specifically the heart (myosin, valves), joints (synovium), skin, and brain (basal ganglia).
  - This cross-reactivity triggers severe, sterile inflammation in these target organs, typically 2 to 4 weeks after the initial sore throat.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- A classic presentation is a school-aged child (5-15 years) who had a sore throat a few weeks ago and now presents with a high fever and severe, migrating joint pain.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs (The Revised Jones Criteria)
Diagnosis requires evidence of a preceding GAS infection PLUS either 2 Major criteria OR 1 Major + 2 Minor criteria.
- **Major Criteria (J♥NES)**:
  1. **J**oints (Migratory Polyarthritis): The most common presentation. Exquisitely painful, swollen, red, hot large joints (knees, ankles, wrists, elbows). The inflammation characteristically "migrates" from one joint to another over a few days. It is highly responsive to NSAIDs.
  2. **♥** (Carditis): The most serious manifestation. It is a pancarditis (involving endocardium, myocardium, and pericardium). Presents with a new heart murmur (classically mitral regurgitation), tachycardia out of proportion to the fever, or signs of congestive heart failure.
  3. **N**odules (Subcutaneous): Firm, painless, pea-sized nodules over the extensor surfaces of joints or the spine. Rare.
  4. **E**rythema Marginatum: A classic, non-pruritic, pink maculopapular rash with pale centres and serpiginous (snake-like) advancing margins. Usually on the trunk and proximal limbs. Rare.
  5. **S**ydenham Chorea (St. Vitus Dance): A late neurological manifestation (can appear months later). Characterized by involuntary, purposeless, rapid, jerking movements of the face and limbs, often accompanied by emotional lability and muscle weakness.
- **Minor Criteria**:
  - Fever.
  - Arthralgia (joint pain without swelling - only counts if polyarthritis is not used as a major criterion).
  - Elevated acute phase reactants (ESR or CRP).
  - Prolonged PR interval on ECG.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Septic Arthritis**: Usually involves a single joint (monoarticular), the child is highly toxic, and joint aspiration yields pus. ARF is polyarticular, migratory, and the joint fluid is sterile.
- **Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA)**: Arthritis must last for at least 6 weeks. It is not typically migratory and lacks the preceding strep infection, carditis, or chorea.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Septic arthritis.
- Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA).
- Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE).
- Kawasaki Disease.
- Infective Endocarditis.
- Post-Streptococcal Reactive Arthritis (PSRA).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Evidence of Preceding GAS Infection (Mandatory for diagnosis)**:
  - **Anti-Streptolysin O Titre (ASOT)** or Anti-DNase B: Elevated or rising titres.
  - Positive throat swab culture or rapid antigen test for GAS (often negative by the time ARF presents).
- **Bloods**: Markedly elevated ESR and CRP. FBC (leukocytosis).
- **Echocardiogram**: Mandatory for all suspected cases. It is the gold standard for detecting subclinical carditis (valvulitis) even if no murmur is heard.
- **ECG**: To check for a prolonged PR interval (a minor criterion) or signs of pericarditis.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **1. Eradicate the Streptococcus**: A single dose of IM Benzathine Penicillin G or a 10-day course of oral Penicillin V, to eliminate any residual GAS in the pharynx.
- **2. Anti-inflammatory Therapy**:
  - *Arthritis only*: High-dose **NSAIDs** (e.g., Naproxen) or Aspirin. The response is usually dramatic within 48 hours.
  - *Severe Carditis/Heart Failure*: High-dose **Corticosteroids** (Prednisolone) are used to suppress the severe cardiac inflammation.
- **3. Secondary Prophylaxis (The most critical long-term intervention)**:
  - Patients who have had ARF are highly susceptible to recurrent attacks with subsequent strep infections, which cause cumulative, irreversible damage to the heart valves.
  - **IM Benzathine Penicillin G every 3-4 weeks** (or daily oral Penicillin V).
  - *Duration*: Depends on cardiac involvement.
    - No carditis: Until age 21 or for 5 years after the last attack (whichever is longer).
    - Mild carditis (healed): Until age 21 or for 10 years.
    - Severe carditis/permanent valve damage: Until age 40 or lifelong.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Paediatric Cardiology**: Essential for the long-term monitoring and management of Rheumatic Heart Disease.
- **Nursing Support**: Ensuring compliance with painful, monthly IM penicillin injections for years is a major challenge.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Rheumatic Heart Disease (RHD)**: The permanent, long-term consequence of ARF carditis. Chronic scarring, fibrosis, and calcification of the valves lead to **Mitral Stenosis** (the classic late lesion), mitral regurgitation, or aortic valve disease. This can lead to heart failure, atrial fibrillation, and the need for valve replacement surgery in adulthood.
- **Prognosis**: Depends entirely on the severity of the initial carditis and strict adherence to secondary penicillin prophylaxis to prevent further damage.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "What is the underlying pathophysiological mechanism of Acute Rheumatic Fever?" (Molecular mimicry. Antibodies produced against the Group A Streptococcus cross-react with the host's own tissues, particularly the heart and joints).
- *Viva*: "A 12-year-old is diagnosed with ARF and has evidence of mitral regurgitation on echo. How long must they receive secondary penicillin prophylaxis?" (Until they are at least 21 years old, or for 10 years after the attack, whichever is longer. If the valve damage is severe and permanent, it may be lifelong).
- *Pitfall*: Failing to order an Echocardiogram in a child with suspected ARF who has no audible murmur. Subclinical carditis is common and must be identified to determine the duration of secondary prophylaxis.
