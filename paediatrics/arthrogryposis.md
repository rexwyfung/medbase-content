# Arthrogryposis Multiplex Congenita (先天性多發性關節攣縮症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A descriptive term (not a single disease) for the presence of multiple, non-progressive joint contractures visible at birth, affecting two or more areas of the body.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Occurs in 1 in 3000 births.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- The ultimate common pathway is **fetal akinesia** (lack of fetal movement). Movement is essential for normal joint development.
- **Causes of Akinesia**: 
  - Neurological (Spinal muscular atrophy, CNS malformations).
  - Muscle issues (Congenital muscular dystrophies).
  - Connective tissue restriction (Diastrophic dysplasia).
  - Mechanical restriction (Oligohydramnios, bicornuate uterus, multiple pregnancy).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- The newborn appears "stiff" or "frozen" in distinct postures. The limbs are rigid with extremely poor range of motion.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- Classic Amyoplasia pattern:
  - Internally rotated shoulders, extended elbows, flexed wrists ("policeman tip" stance).
  - Hip dislocations, severe knee contractures (often hyperextended), and severe bilateral clubfoot (talipes equinovarus).
  - Limbs look "tubular" and featureless (lacking normal skin creases due to absent underlying muscle bulk).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Must determine if it is primary Amyoplasia (a sporadic event with normal brain function) versus part of a lethal generic neuromuscular cascade (e.g. SMA type 0).

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Spinal Muscular Atrophy 
- Trisomy 18
- Fetal Akinesia Deformation Sequence

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bloods**: CK (Creatine Kinase) to screen for muscular dystrophies.
- **Imaging**: Ultrasound of hips (developmental dysplasia is almost universal).
- **Special**: EMG/NCS and potentially a muscle biopsy to differentiate neurogenic vs. myopathic causes. Microarray/Exome sequencing.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**: Ensure the airway is clear (if temporomandibular joints are contracted, intubation is extremely difficult).
- **Physical Therapy**: Aggressive, highly intensive daily passive stretching starting within days of birth. Sequential casting/splinting (Ponseti method for clubfoot).
- **Surgical**: Significant orthopaedic operations (osteotomies, tendon transfers) arranged to allow the child to perform ADLs (bending arms to feed themselves) or to stand.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- MDT is expansive: Orthopaedic surgeon, Neurologist, Physical/Occupational Therapists. Wheelchair access planning.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- The rigid joints are extremely susceptible to iatrogenic fractures during delivery or during overly aggressive physiotherapy by frightened parents. Care must be meticulous.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Most have typical intelligence and lifespan if the underlying cause is simply mechanical/amyoplasia.
- Extreme difficulty with ADLs and mobility. Secondary scoliosis is common.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Viva Q**: "Why are there dimples over the joints?" Answer: Connective tissue tethers the skin directly to the bone in areas that didn't move in utero, forming classic fibrotic dimples over elbows and knees.
