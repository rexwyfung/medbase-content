# Food Allergy (IgE & Non-IgE) (食物過敏 - IgE 及 非IgE介導)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: An adverse, reproducible immune response triggered by a specific food protein. Can be broadly divided into IgE-mediated (rapid onset) and Non-IgE mediated (delayed onset, mostly gastrointestinal/skin).
- **Local Epidemiology**: Cow's Milk Protein Allergy (CMPA) is the most common food allergy in Hong Kong infants, followed by egg and subsequently peanut and shellfish as children grow older.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **IgE-Mediated**: Prior sensitization leads to food-specific IgE antibodies bound to mast cells. Re-exposure causes cross-linking and massive degranulation of histamine, leukotrienes, and prostaglandins resulting in immediate vasodilation, smooth muscle spasm, and mucosal edema.
- **Non-IgE Mediated (e.g., FPIES, Allergic Proctocolitis)**: T-cell mediated. The inflammatory process primarily targets the gut mucosa, taking hours to days to develop after ingestion.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- **IgE-Mediated**: 15-year-old eats a peanut trace and within 10 minutes develops hives, lip swelling, wheezing, and feels faint.
- **Non-IgE Mediated (CMPA)**: A 6-week-old formula-fed infant presenting with blood-streaked stools ("red threads"), severe colic, and worsening eczema.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **IgE**: Urticaria (hives), angioedema (swollen lips/eyes), vomiting, bronchospasm, hypotension (anaphylaxis).
- **Non-IgE**: Chronic diarrhea, mucous/blood in stool, failure to thrive, severe atopic dermatitis, delayed vomiting.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from Lactose Intolerance: Lactose intolerance is purely enzymatic (lactase deficiency) causing bloating/watery diarrhea. It NEVER causes hives, blood in stool, or anaphylaxis, whereas CMPA does.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Lactose Intolerance
- Acute Gastroenteritis (viral/bacterial)
- Celiac Disease (rare in HK, but possible)
- Intussusception (if presenting with bloody stool)

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **IgE-Mediated**:
  - Skin Prick Test (SPT): Quick and highly sensitive.
  - Blood: Specific IgE (sIgE - historically known as RAST).
- **Non-IgE Mediated (CMPA)**:
  - SPT and sIgE are NEGATIVE.
  - Diagnosis is purely clinical: Trial of elimination (2-4 weeks) followed by a re-introduction challenge.
- **Gold Standard**: Oral Food Challenge (done under strict medical supervision).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Anaphylaxis Strategy**: 
  - IM Adrenaline (Epinephrine) 1:1,000 into the anterolateral thigh (Dose: 0.01 mg/kg, max 0.5 mg).
  - ABCDE supportive care.
  - Antihistamines and steroids have NO role in life-saving acute management, they only prevent biphasic reactions or treat purely cutaneous symptoms.
- **Chronic/Avoidance**:
  - Strict allergen avoidance. Dietician involvement is mandatory.
  - For CMPA in infants: Switch to Extensively Hydrolyzed Formula (EHF) or Amino Acid Formula (AAF). (Soy formula is generally avoided <6 months due to cross-reactivity risk).
  - Provide EpiPen and a written Anaphylaxis Action Plan for schools.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Massive anxiety for parents and significant school preparation required (nut-free zones, staff training for EpiPen).

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Intentional allergen exposure or medical neglect by ignoring strict dietary restrictions.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Fatal anaphylaxis.
- CMPA and egg allergies are often outgrown by school age. Peanut, tree nut, and shellfish allergies tend to be lifelong.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Relying on "Total IgE" for food allergy diagnosis. Total IgE is useless; it can be high in eczema/asthma without food allergy. You must request *Specific IgE* or do a Skin Prick Test.
- **Viva Q**: "A mother breastfeeding her baby who has CMPA asks if she needs to change her diet." Answer: Yes. Intact cow's milk proteins cross into breastmilk. The mother must strictly eliminate dairy (and take calcium supplements) to continue breastfeeding.
