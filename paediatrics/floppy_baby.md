
# The Floppy Infant (Hypotonia) (嬰兒肌張力低下)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Reduced resistance to passive movement in an infant. Often presents with delayed motor milestones and abnormal postures (e.g., "frog-leg" posture).
- **Local Epidemiology**: A common presenting complaint to general paediatric and child neurology clinics in HK.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Hypotonia can originate from anywhere along the neuraxis.
- **Central Causes (Brain/Spinal Cord)**: Accounts for ~60-80%. Includes HIE, genetic syndromes (Down, Prader-Willi), structural brain malformations, metabolic disorders.
- **Peripheral Causes (Motor Unit)**:
  - *Anterior Horn Cell*: Spinal Muscular Atrophy (SMA).
  - *Peripheral Nerve*: Charcot-Marie-Tooth (rare in infancy).
  - *Neuromuscular Junction*: Neonatal Myasthenia Gravis, Botulism.
  - *Muscle*: Congenital Myopathies, Muscular Dystrophies.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- See below.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **General**: Slips through hands when held under axillae, marked head lag on pull-to-sit, "frog-leg" posture when supine, inverted "U" posture on ventral suspension.
- **Differentiating Central vs. Peripheral**:
  - **Central**: Profound hypotonia but *preserved* or brisk reflexes. Often associated with poor feeding, seizures, microcephaly, dysmorphic features, and global developmental delay (cognitive impairment).
  - **Peripheral**: Profound weakness, *absent* or depressed reflexes, fasciculations (e.g., tongue in SMA). Cognition and social interaction are often completely normal (bright, alert eyes).
## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Ligamentous laxity**: Joints are hypermobile, but muscle tone and power are normal.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Most Likely**:
  - **Central**: Hypoxic Ischaemic Encephalopathy (HIE), Down Syndrome.
  - **Peripheral**: Spinal Muscular Atrophy (SMA).
- **Must-Not-Miss**:
  - SMA Type 1 (fatal without early treatment).
  - Infant Botulism (constipation, descending paralysis, bulbar weakness).
  - Treatable metabolic disorders (e.g., Pompe disease).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**:
  - **Thorough Clinical Exam**: Is it central or peripheral? Are there dysmorphic features?
  - **Bloods**: Creatine Kinase (CK) - markedly elevated in muscular dystrophies, mildly elevated/normal in SMA. Thyroid function tests, basic metabolic panel.
- **Gold Standard**:
  - **Genetic Testing**: First-line for suspected SMA (*SMN1* gene deletion) or Prader-Willi (methylation studies). Chromosomal Microarray for syndromic central hypotonia.
  - **MRI Brain**: If central cause suspected.
  - **EMG / Nerve Conduction Studies**: To differentiate nerve vs. muscle disease (less commonly used now due to advances in genetics).
  - **Muscle Biopsy**: For congenital myopathies.
- **Monitoring**:
  - Not applicable for diagnosis.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**:
  - **A/B**: Assess airway and breathing. Severe peripheral hypotonia (e.g., SMA 1) may present with respiratory failure requiring non-invasive ventilation (BiPAP) or intubation.
  - **C**: Assess feeding/swallowing safety. May require NG tube feeding to prevent aspiration.
- **Chronic/Definitive**:
  - Depends entirely on the cause.
  - **SMA**: Disease-modifying therapies (Nusinersen, Risdiplam, Zolgensma) are now available in HK.
  - **Supportive**: Physiotherapy (prevent contractures), Occupational therapy, Speech therapy (swallowing).
## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Multidisciplinary care is essential. Involve respiratory physicians, gastroenterologists, and orthopaedic surgeons.
## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Severe hypotonia with poor prognosis requires sensitive palliative care discussions and MSW support.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Varies widely. Central causes often lead to cerebral palsy and intellectual disability. Severe peripheral causes (untreated SMA 1) lead to respiratory failure and death in early childhood.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Ordering an EMG/Muscle biopsy for a floppy infant with brisk reflexes. *Pearl*: Brisk reflexes = Central cause. Do an MRI Brain or genetics, not a muscle biopsy.
- **Viva Question**: "How do you differentiate central from peripheral hypotonia clinically?" *Answer*: Reflexes (brisk in central, absent in peripheral) and cognition (often impaired in central, usually preserved and alert in peripheral).
