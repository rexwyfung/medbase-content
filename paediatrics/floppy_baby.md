# The Floppy Infant (Hypotonia) (嬰兒肌張力低下)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Hypotonia
* ↓ resistance to passive movement during handling
* ↑ joint mobility & laxity
* Presents with delayed motor milestones
* Abnormal resting postures (e.g., "frog-leg" posture)

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
* Common presentation to HA general paediatric & child neurology clinics
* SMA carrier rate in Chinese population: ~1/50
* ↑ pick-up of SMA pre-symptomatically due to local private newborn screening, though standard HA newborn screening focuses on metabolic conditions

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics

### Pathophysiology of Central Hypotonia
* Originates from Upper Motor Neuron (UMN) lesions (Brain/Spinal Cord)
* Accounts for ~60-80% of all floppy infant cases
* **Mechanism**: Disruption of descending inhibitory pathways from cortex → basal ganglia/brainstem → spinal cord
* **Causes**: 
  * Hypoxic Ischaemic Encephalopathy (HIE) (缺氧缺血性腦病)
  * Brain malformations (e.g., lissencephaly)
  * Genetic/chromosomal syndromes

### Pathophysiology of Peripheral Hypotonia
* Originates from Lower Motor Neuron (LMN) lesions (Motor Unit)
* **Motor Unit Components affected**:
  * Anterior Horn Cell
  * Peripheral Nerve
  * Neuromuscular Junction (NMJ)
  * Muscle fiber

### Genetics of Spinal Muscular Atrophy (SMA)
* Autosomal Recessive inheritance
* **Mutation**: *SMN1* gene deletion (survival motor neuron 1) on chromosome 5q
* **Pathology**: Progressive degeneration of anterior horn cells → muscle denervation → atrophy
* Disease severity inversely proportional to *SMN2* backup gene copy number

### Genetics of Syndromic Hypotonia
* **Prader-Willi Syndrome** (普瑞德威利症候群): Loss of paternally expressed genes on 15q11-13 (usually deletion or maternal uniparental disomy)
* **Down Syndrome** (唐氏綜合症): Trisomy 21

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### General Signs of The Floppy Infant
* **Pull-to-sit**: Marked head lag 
* **Ventral suspension**: Inverted "U" posture (failure to hold head/back straight)
* **Vertical suspension**: "Slips through hands" when held under axillae
* **Supine posture**: "Frog-leg" posture (hips abducted, knees flexed)

### Clinical Signs of Central Hypotonia
* Profound hypotonia BUT preserved/brisk deep tendon reflexes (DTRs)
* Clenched fists / cortical thumb (fisted hands)
* Poor feeding & lethargy
* Microcephaly (小頭症) or macrocephaly
* Dysmorphic features
* Global developmental delay / poor visual tracking (cognition affected)
* Associated seizures

### Clinical Signs of Peripheral Hypotonia
* Profound muscle weakness (paresis/paralysis)
* Absent or markedly ↓ DTRs
* Fasciculations (e.g., tongue fasciculations in SMA)
* Normal head circumference
* Bright, alert eyes & normal social interaction (cognition preserved)

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)

### Central vs. Peripheral Hypotonia Differentiation
| Feature | Central Hypotonia (中樞性肌張力低下) | Peripheral Hypotonia (周邊性肌張力低下) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Weakness** | Mild-Moderate | Severe (Profound) |
| **Reflexes (DTRs)** | Brisk / ↑ | Absent / ↓ |
| **Alertness/Cognition** | Dull / Impaired | Bright / Normal |
| **Fasciculations** | Absent | Present (e.g., tongue in SMA) |
| **Dysmorphism/Seizures**| Common | Rare |
| **Muscle Bulk** | Normal (initially) | Wasted / Atrophic |

### Differentiating Hypotonia from Benign Joint Hypermobility
* **Ligamentous Laxity/Hypermobility**: Joints hyperextend, but core muscle tone & anti-gravity power are NORMAL. DTRs are normal.

## 5. Differential Diagnosis

### Anatomic Differential Diagnosis of Peripheral Hypotonia
| Anatomic Level | Condition | Key Clinical Clues |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Anterior Horn Cell** | Spinal Muscular Atrophy (SMA) Type 1 (脊髓性肌萎縮症) | Bell-shaped chest, paradoxical breathing, tongue fasciculations |
| **Peripheral Nerve** | Charcot-Marie-Tooth (CMT) | Very rare in infancy; distal weakness, absent reflexes |
| **NMJ** | Infant Botulism (嬰兒肉毒桿菌中毒) | Constipation, bulbar weakness, ptosis, descending paralysis |
| **NMJ** | Transient Neonatal Myasthenia Gravis | Maternal history of MG, hypotonia, poor suck, recovers in weeks |
| **Muscle** | Congenital Muscular Dystrophies / Myopathies | High CK, early contractures, respiratory failure |
| **Muscle** | Pompe Disease (Type II Glycogen Storage) | Cardiomegaly, hepatomegaly, macroglossia |

### Differential Diagnosis of Central Hypotonia
* **Hypoxic Ischaemic Encephalopathy (HIE)**: Birth asphyxia history
* **Chromosomal**: Down Syndrome, Prader-Willi Syndrome (neonatal hypotonia + poor suck → childhood hyperphagia/obesity)
* **Metabolic**: Urea cycle defects, aminoacidopathies (presents with encephalopathy, acidosis, hyperammonaemia)
* **Congenital Infections (TORCH)**: CMV, Rubella, Toxoplasmosis

## 6. Tiered Investigations

### Bedside Investigations for Hypotonia
* **Neurological Examination**: Differentiate central vs. peripheral (Reflexes, Power, Tone, Cranial nerves)
* **Dysmorphology Exam**: Look for syndromic facies

### Blood Investigations for Hypotonia
* **Creatine Kinase (CK) (肌酸激酶)**: 
  * Markedly ↑ (>1000): Muscular dystrophies (e.g., Duchenne, Congenital)
  * Normal / Mildly ↑: SMA, Central causes, Myopathies
* **Thyroid Function Tests (TFTs)**: R/O Congenital Hypothyroidism (呆小症)
* **Metabolic Screen**: ABG, glucose, ammonia, lactate, plasma amino acids (r/o inborn errors of metabolism)

### Genetic Investigations (Gold Standard)
* **Targeted Gene Testing**: *SMN1* deletion (1st line for suspected SMA)
* **Methylation Studies**: 1st line for suspected Prader-Willi Syndrome
* **Chromosomal Microarray (CMA)**: 1st line for syndromic central hypotonia
* **Whole Exome Sequencing (WES)**: Increasingly used in HA for undiagnosed neuromuscular/genetic conditions

### Neuroimaging & Special Investigations
* **MRI Brain**: 1st line for central hypotonia (assess for HIE changes, malformations)
* **EMG / Nerve Conduction Studies**: Differentiates nerve vs. muscle. Painful; now 2nd line behind genetics.
* **Muscle Biopsy**: For specific congenital myopathies if genetics non-diagnostic.

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)

### Acute Resuscitation & Airway Management
* **A/B**: Assess airway patency & work of breathing
* Monitor O2 sat and capnography (CO2)
* Interventions: Non-invasive ventilation (BiPAP/CPAP) or intubation for respiratory failure (e.g., SMA Type 1, Pompe)
* **Seizure Control**: IV Phenobarbitone / Levetiracetam if central hypotonia with status epilepticus

### Bulbar & Nutritional Management
* **C**: Assess swallow safety (high risk of silent aspiration)
* Interventions: Nil by mouth (NBM) if unsafe
* Insert Nasogastric (NG) tube for feeds
* Long-term: Percutaneous Endoscopic Gastrostomy (PEG) feeding

### Disease-Modifying Therapies (HA Protocols)
* **SMA Specific Therapies** (Now available under HA special drug formulary / Samaritan Fund for eligible patients):
  * **Nusinersen (Spinraza)** (諾西那生): Antisense oligonucleotide, given via repeated intrathecal lumbar punctures. ↑ *SMN2* exon 7 inclusion.
  * **Risdiplam (Evrysdi)**: Oral daily SMN2 splicing modifier.
  * **Onasemnogene abeparvovec (Zolgensma)**: One-off IV AAV9 gene replacement therapy. Restores *SMN1*. Strict age/weight eligibility criteria in HK.
* **Pompe Disease**: Enzyme Replacement Therapy (ERT) (Alglucosidase alfa).

### Supportive Management
* **Physiotherapy (PT)**: Chest physio (cough assist), passive ROM exercises to prevent contractures
* **Occupational Therapy (OT)**: Adaptive seating, modified toys

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care

### Multidisciplinary Team Structure
* **Paediatric Neurologist**: Lead diagnostic & DMT coordination
* **Respiratory Physician**: Manage nocturnal hypoventilation, sleep studies
* **Gastroenterologist / Dietitian**: Optimise nutrition, manage reflux/PEG
* **Orthopaedic Surgeon**: Manage scoliosis (common in SMA) & hip subluxation

### Psychosocial Care
* **Medical Social Worker (MSW)**: Financial assistance for extremely expensive DMTs (Samaritan Fund/CCF), disability allowance in HK
* **Patient Support Groups**: Refer to Hong Kong SMA Charitable Foundation / HK Mucopolysaccharidoses & Rare Mutant Diseases Mutual Aid Group

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags

### Non-Accidental Injury (NAI) Red Flags in Hypotonia
* Acute onset central hypotonia + encephalopathy without obvious birth history/metabolic cause → R/O Abusive Head Trauma (Shaken Baby Syndrome)
* Mandatory ophthalmic exam (retinal haemorrhages) & skeletal survey if suspected.

## 10. Complications & Prognosis

### Complications of Severe Hypotonia
* **Respiratory**: Recurrent aspiration pneumonia, type II respiratory failure, sleep-disordered breathing
* **Musculoskeletal**: Severe progressive scoliosis, joint contractures
* **Nutritional**: Failure to thrive (FTT)

### Prognosis by Aetiology
* **Central Causes**: Permanent motor deficits (Cerebral Palsy) & varying degrees of intellectual disability.
* **SMA Type 1 (Untreated)**: Rapid bulbar/respiratory decline. Fatal before age 2.
* **SMA Type 1 (Treated)**: Dramatically altered natural history; infants can sit, stand, and breathe independently depending on timing of DMT initiation.

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Pitfall: Rushing to perform an EMG or muscle biopsy for a floppy infant without checking reflexes. Reflexes must guide your algorithm; brisk reflexes point to the brain, not the muscle.
* Trap: Assuming a floppy baby with poor feeding and lethargy has a primary neuromuscular disease. Pearl: Lethargy and poor interaction point strongly toward a central cause (e.g., metabolic encephalopathy, sepsis, or HIE), whereas peripheral causes present with alert infants.
* Viva Question: How do you clinically differentiate central from peripheral hypotonia? Answer: Central hypotonia presents with brisk reflexes, cognitive impairment, and possible dysmorphism or seizures; peripheral hypotonia presents with absent reflexes, profound weakness, and normal cognition/alertness.
* Viva Question: What is the pathognomonic physical sign of SMA Type 1 on chest inspection? Answer: A "bell-shaped" chest with paradoxical breathing (due to profound intercostal muscle weakness but preserved diaphragmatic function).
* Trap: Missing a treatable metabolic cause of hypotonia. Pearl: Always check blood glucose, ammonia, and acid-base status acutely to rule out reversible inborn errors of metabolism.
