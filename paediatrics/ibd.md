# Inflammatory Bowel Disease (Crohn’s Disease & Ulcerative Colitis) (炎症性腸病)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Chronic, relapsing idiopathic inflammation of the gastrointestinal tract. Comprises Crohn's Disease (CD) and Ulcerative Colitis (UC).
- **Local Epidemiology**: Incidence in Hong Kong and Asia is rapidly rising due to westernization of diets and environments.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- An inappropriate, aggressive mucosal immune response to normal intestinal flora in a genetically susceptible host (e.g., NOD2 gene mutations).
- **Crohn's Disease**: Transmural (full-thickness) inflammation. Can affect *any* part of the GI tract from mouth to anus. "Skip lesions" (patchy).
- **Ulcerative Colitis**: Mucosal inflammation only. Affects *only* the colon. Always starts at the rectum and spreads continuously proximally.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- A teenager presenting with months of insidious weight loss, abdominal pain, and bloody diarrhea.
- Unexplained profound growth failure or delayed puberty may be the *only* initial sign of Crohn's.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Common to both**: Abdominal pain, bloody diarrhea, lethargy, weight loss, fever.
- **Crohn's specifics**: Mouth aphthous ulcers, right lower quadrant pain (terminal ileitis mimicking appendicitis), perianal disease (deep fissures, complex fistulas, massive skin tags).
- **Extraintestinal manifestations**: Clubbing, erythema nodosum, pyoderma gangrenosum, anterior uveitis, arthritis, primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC - tightly linked with UC).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Differentiating from infectious colitis: infectious is usually acute (days to weeks), while IBD is chronic (months).

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Infectious Colitis (Salmonella, Shigella, Campylobacter, C. diff)
- Coeliac Disease
- Appendicitis (can mimic terminal ileal Crohn's)
- Tuberculosis of the gut

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bloods**: High CRP/ESR, microcytic anemia (bleeding), low albumin.
- **Stool**: Fecal Calprotectin (highly sensitive and specific marker for active gut inflammation, used to track disease activity).
- **Definitive**: Upper and Lower Endoscopy with multiple biopsies.
  - CD: Cobblestone mucosa, deep linear ulcers, non-caseating granulomas on biopsy.
  - UC: Continuous mucosal erythema, friability, loss of vascular pattern, crypt abscesses on biopsy.
- **Imaging**: MRI Enterography (MRE) to evaluate small bowel involvement in Crohn's (strictures, fistulas).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Crohn's Induction**: Exclusive Enteral Nutrition (EEN) for 6-8 weeks is first-line in paediatrics to induce remission and promote mucosal healing without steroids. Systemic steroids (Prednisolone) if EEN fails/is intolerable.
- **UC Induction**: 5-ASA (Mesalazine) oral and topical (enemas). Systemic steroids for severe flares. Severe acute colitis requires IV Hydrocortisone and monitoring for toxic megacolon.
- **Maintenance (Both)**: Immunomodulators (Azathioprine, Methotrexate). Biologics (Infliximab, Adalimumab - anti-TNF antibodies) are increasingly used early ("top-down" therapy) in severe disease.
- **Surgical**: Curative for UC (panproctocolectomy with ileoanal pouch). Not curative for CD (only for strictureplasty or abscess drainage, bowel conservation is key).

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Massive psychosocial impact due to chronic illness, steroid side effects (moon face), and delayed puberty. IBD specialist nurses are pivotal.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Medical neglect if parents refuse immunosuppressants due to "natural therapy" beliefs, leading to permanent bowel destruction.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Toxic megacolon (especially UC).
- Strictures leading to bowel obstruction (CD).
- Massive risk of colorectal cancer 10-15 years post-diagnosis (requires regular surveillance colonoscopies).

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Forgetting to look at the anus in the exam. In Crohn's, the perianal inspection can clinch the diagnosis (fistulas/tags).
- **Viva Q**: "Why is surgery curative for Ulcerative Colitis but not Crohn's?" Answer: Because UC is strictly limited to the colonic mucosa. If you remove the entire colon, the disease is gone. Crohn's can affect the entire GI tract, so removing a segment of bowel does not prevent it from recurring elsewhere in the remaining bowel.
