# Pulmonary Hypertension (PH) (肺動脈高壓)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A mean pulmonary arterial pressure (mPAP) $\ge$ 20 mmHg at rest, determined by right heart catheterization. 
- **Local Epidemiology**: Mostly secondary to congenital heart disease (left-to-right shunts) or chronic lung disease (like BPD in ex-premature infants). Idiopathic PH is rare but fatal.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Vascular remodeling (hypertrophy of smooth muscle and endothelial proliferation) of the small pulmonary arteries.
- Leads to increased pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR).
- The right ventricle (RV) is forced to pump against extremely high pressures, leading to RV hypertrophy, eventual RV dilation, and right heart failure (cor pulmonale).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Classic presentation: An older child with progressive, unexplained shortness of breath on exertion, fatigue, and episodes of syncope (especially syncope *during* exercise, which is a massive red flag).

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- Loud, palpable **P2** (the pulmonary component of the second heart sound).
- **Right ventricular heave** (lift) felt at the left sternal edge.
- Signs of Right Heart Failure: Raised JVP, tender hepatomegaly, peripheral edema.
- Cyanosis (if the pressure becomes so high it reverses a left-to-right shunt into a right-to-left shunt, i.e., Eisenmenger's syndrome).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Vasovagal syncope occurs usually when standing still, hot, or emotional. Syncope *during* peak exercise strongly points to fixed cardiac output (PH or severe aortic stenosis).

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Severe Asthma (causes SOB, but not loud P2 or RV heave)
- Severe Congenital Aortic Stenosis/Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (both cause exertional syncope but have left heart signs)
- Eisenmenger Syndrome

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **ECG**: Right Axis Deviation, Right Ventricular Hypertrophy (tall R waves in V1, deep S in V6).
- **Imaging**: Echocardiogram is the best non-invasive screening tool (estimates pulmonary pressures via tricuspid regurgitation jet velocity).
- **Gold Standard**: **Right Heart Catheterization**. Mandatory for formal diagnosis and to test "vasoreactivity" (giving inhaled nitric oxide to see if the vessels can still dilate, which dictates treatment).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Avoid triggers**: STRICT avoidance of dehydration, hypercarbia, hypoxia, and acidosis (all of which powerfully constrict pulmonary vessels and trigger fatal crises).
- **Targeted Pulmonary Vasodilators**:
  - *Phosphodiesterase-5 inhibitors*: Sildenafil (oral/IV).
  - *Endothelin receptor antagonists*: Bosentan (oral).
  - *Prostacyclin analogues*: Epoprostenol (continuous IV infusion, very complex to manage).
- **Surgical**: Lung or Heart-Lung transplant for refractory end-stage disease.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Continuous IV Epoprostenol requires a permanent central line. Parents must be trained to mix the drug daily; if the pump stops for even 5 minutes, the child can die of immediate rebound pulmonary hypertension.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- N/A.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Pulmonary hypertensive crises (often triggered by general anesthesia, crying, or pain), leading to acute right heart failure and death.
- Idiopathic PH has historically poor survival, though modern triple therapy is improving outcomes.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Forgetting to listen for the loud P2 in a breathless child.
- **Viva Q**: "Why is giving supplemental oxygen important in a child with a pulmonary hypertensive crisis?" Answer: Because alveolar hypoxia is the most potent natural stimulus for pulmonary vasoconstriction. Oxygen directly dilates the pulmonary vascular bed, lowering the resistance the RV has to pump against.
