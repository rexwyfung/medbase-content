# Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD) (手足口病)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition of HFMD
* Highly contagious viral illness of childhood
* Characterized by fever + vesicular eruption on hands, feet, & oral mucosa

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
* Endemic in HK
* Peak seasons: Early summer (May–July) & autumn (Oct–Dec)
* Outbreaks extremely common in kindergartens/child care centres (KG/CCC)
* Closely monitored by Centre for Health Protection (CHP) (衞生防護中心)

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Causative Pathogens
* Enteroviruses (EV) family
* Most common: Coxsackievirus A16 (CV-A16) (柯薩奇病毒A16型)
* Most severe: Enterovirus 71 (EV71) (腸病毒71型) → high risk of neurological/cardiopulmonary complications

### Modes of Transmission
* Primary: Faecal-oral route
* Secondary: Contact with respiratory droplets, saliva, or vesicular fluid
* Highly contagious during 1st week of illness
* Viral shedding in stool can persist for weeks (up to 6-8 weeks)

### Pathogenesis Mechanism
* Viral replication in lower intestine & pharyngeal lymphoid tissue → viraemia → dissemination to skin & mucous membranes
* Incubation period: 3-7 days

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Prodromal Phase Symptoms
* Low-grade fever, malaise, sore throat, ↓ appetite (1-2 days)

### Enanthem (Oral Lesions) (口腔黏膜疹)
* Painful vesicles on buccal mucosa, tongue, soft palate, gums
* Rapidly ulcerate
* Causes severe odynophagia (吞嚥痛) → drooling, refusal to eat/drink

### Exanthem (Skin Lesions) (皮疹)
* Maculopapular rash → progresses to small, greyish, oval vesicles with erythematous halo
* Distribution: Palms, soles, buttocks/groin (esp. in infants wearing nappies)
* Lesions typically non-pruritic & painless (unlike chickenpox)

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Distinguishing Herpangina (疱疹性咽峽炎)
* Same causative viruses (Enteroviruses)
* Painful vesicles/ulcers confined EXCLUSIVELY to posterior pharynx (soft palate, uvula, tonsils)
* NO lesions on hands, feet, or body

### Distinguishing Chickenpox (Varicella) (水痘)
* Intensely pruritic rash
* Centripetal distribution (mostly trunk)
* Lesions present in multiple stages of development simultaneously (crops)
* Rarely affects palms/soles

### Distinguishing Herpes Simplex Gingivostomatitis (疱疹性齒齦口腔炎)
* Severe, painful ulcers primarily on anterior mouth (gums, lips, anterior tongue)
* Often associated with friable, bleeding gums & high fever
* NO lesions on hands/feet

## 5. Differential Diagnosis
### Summary of Common Vesicular Eruptions in Childhood
| Condition | Primary Pathogen | Distribution of Lesions | Key Differentiating Features |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **HFMD (手足口病)** | CV-A16, EV71 | Oral cavity + Palms/Soles/Buttocks | Painless/non-itchy vesicles on extremities |
| **Herpangina (疱疹性咽峽炎)** | Enteroviruses | Posterior pharynx ONLY | Absence of skin lesions |
| **Chickenpox (水痘)** | VZV | Centripetal (Trunk > Extremities) | Intensely pruritic, lesions in multiple stages |
| **HSV Gingivostomatitis** | HSV-1 | Anterior mouth, gums, lips | Bleeding gums, high fever, no palm/sole lesions |
| **Aphthous Ulcers** | Idiopathic | Oral mucosa | Recurrent, isolated ulcers, no systemic symptoms |

## 6. Tiered Investigations
### Bedside Clinical Assessment
* Diagnosis is entirely clinical
* Hydration status assessment: Capillary refill time (CRT), skin turgor, mucous membranes, sunken eyes/fontanelle

### Blood Investigations
* Seldom indicated for uncomplicated cases
* Considered if severe dehydration (U&Es for hyper/hyponatraemia) or suspecting EV71 complications (FBC, inflammatory markers)

### Microbiological & Virological Testing
* Throat swab, vesicle swab, or stool sample for Enterovirus PCR
* Usually ONLY performed for epidemiological surveillance by CHP during institutional outbreaks, or in severe/hospitalized cases

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Acute Supportive Care
* Mainstay of treatment; illness self-limiting in 7-10 days
* **Hydration**: Critical priority. Encourage cool, soft foods & fluids (ice lollies, cold milk)
* **IV Fluids**: Indicated if clinically dehydrated & refusing oral intake

### Symptomatic Pharmacological Therapy
* Analgesia: Regular PO Paracetamol/Ibuprofen for fever & pain
* Topical oral analgesic gels (e.g., lidocaine-containing) applied pre-meals to facilitate feeding
* Antibiotics are strictly CONTRAINDICATED (ineffective & unnecessary)

### Hong Kong HA & CHP Infection Control Protocols
* **Standard Exclusion**: Exclude from school/nursery until fever settled + ALL vesicles dried & crusted
* **EV71 Exclusion**: Exclude for an ADDITIONAL 2 weeks post-recovery due to prolonged viral shedding & severity risk
* **Hand Hygiene**: Alcohol rub is INEFFECTIVE against non-enveloped enteroviruses; must use liquid soap & water

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Public Health (CHP) Reporting
* Institutional outbreaks (e.g., in KG/CCC) MUST be reported to CHP
* CHP may order temporary school closure (typically 14 days) if outbreak is severe or involves EV71

### Parental Education & Environmental Hygiene
* Advise regular cleaning of frequently touched surfaces & toys using 1:99 diluted household bleach
* Maintain good indoor ventilation
* Close observation for red flags of deterioration

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Safeguarding Considerations in HFMD
* Generally N/A for the disease process itself
* Medical neglect should be considered if parents persistently ignore severe dehydration (anuria, lethargy) or refuse hospital admission despite medical advice

## 10. Complications & Prognosis
### Hydration Complications
* **Dehydration (脫水)**: Most common complication due to severe odynophagia, requiring hospital admission for IV rehydration

### Enterovirus 71 (EV71) Severe Complications
* EV71 (腸病毒71型) is notorious for life-threatening complications, especially in children <5 years
* **Neurological**: Brainstem encephalitis (腦幹腦炎), aseptic meningitis, acute flaccid paralysis
* **Cardiopulmonary**: Rapid-onset neurogenic pulmonary oedema (神經性肺水腫), cardiopulmonary collapse

### Red Flags for EV71 Deterioration
* Persistent high fever (>39°C)
* Repeated vomiting
* Persistent lethargy / drowsiness
* Myoclonic jerks (肌躍型抽搐)
* Tachycardia out of proportion to fever
* Sudden limb weakness

### Late Dermatological Complications
* **Onychomadesis (脫甲症)**: Painless shedding of fingernails & toenails 1-2 months post-infection (spontaneous regrowth occurs)

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Viva Question: A 2-year-old with HFMD is refusing to drink and has dry mucous membranes. What is the most common complication of HFMD, and what is the management? Answer: Dehydration due to severe pain from oral ulcers. Management is adequate analgesia and oral rehydration; if unsuccessful, admission for IV fluids.
* Viva Question: Which specific virus causing HFMD is associated with severe neurological complications like brainstem encephalitis and pulmonary oedema? Answer: Enterovirus 71 (EV71).
* Trap: Advising parents to rely solely on alcohol-based hand sanitizers to prevent HFMD transmission. Pearl: Alcohol hand rubs are ineffective against non-enveloped enteroviruses like EV71; strict handwashing with liquid soap and water is required.
* Pitfall: Prescribing antibiotics for HFMD. Antibiotics are ineffective for this viral illness and unnecessary; management is strictly supportive.
* Viva Question: What late dermatological complication can occur 1-2 months after a child recovers from HFMD? Answer: Onychomadesis (painless shedding of the nails).
* Trap: Assuming a child with HFMD can return to kindergarten immediately after the fever breaks. Pearl: Under CHP guidelines, the child MUST be excluded until all vesicles have dried and crusted. If EV71 is confirmed, exclusion is extended for an additional 2 weeks post-recovery.
