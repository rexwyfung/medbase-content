# Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD) (手足口病)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A common, highly contagious viral illness of childhood characterized by fever and a vesicular eruption on the hands, feet, and oral mucosa.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Endemic in Hong Kong with peak seasons in early summer (May-July) and autumn (Oct-Dec). Outbreaks in kindergartens and childcare centres are extremely common and are closely monitored by the Centre for Health Protection (CHP).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Causative Organisms**: Enteroviruses, most commonly **Coxsackievirus A16** and **Enterovirus 71 (EV71)**.
- **Transmission**: Faecal-oral route (the primary route) and contact with respiratory droplets or vesicular fluid. The virus can be shed in the stool for weeks after the symptoms resolve.
- **Pathogenesis**: The virus replicates in the lymphoid tissue of the lower intestine and pharynx, leading to viraemia and subsequent dissemination to the skin and mucous membranes.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- **Incubation Period**: 3-5 days.
- **Prodrome**: Low-grade fever, malaise, sore throat, and decreased appetite for 1-2 days.
- **The Rash**: Follows the prodrome. The child develops painful mouth ulcers and a characteristic rash on the extremities.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Enanthem (Oral Lesions)**: Painful vesicles on the buccal mucosa, tongue, soft palate, and gums that rapidly ulcerate. This causes severe odynophagia (pain on swallowing), leading to drooling and refusal to eat or drink.
- **Exanthem (Skin Lesions)**:
  - Maculopapular rash that progresses to small, greyish, oval vesicles with an erythematous halo.
  - **Distribution**: Classically on the **palms of the hands, soles of the feet**, and the **buttocks/groin** (especially in infants wearing nappies).
  - The skin lesions are usually *not* itchy or painful (unlike chickenpox).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Herpangina**: Caused by the same viruses, but the painful vesicles/ulcers are confined *exclusively* to the posterior pharynx (soft palate, uvula, tonsils). There are NO lesions on the hands, feet, or body.
- **Chickenpox (Varicella)**: The rash is intensely itchy, centripetal (mostly on the trunk), and lesions are in multiple stages of development (crops). Chickenpox rarely affects the palms and soles.
- **Herpes Simplex Gingivostomatitis**: Severe, painful ulcers primarily on the anterior mouth (gums, lips, anterior tongue), often with bleeding gums and high fever. No lesions on hands/feet.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Herpangina.
- Chickenpox.
- Herpes Simplex Gingivostomatitis.
- Aphthous ulcers.
- Erythema Multiforme.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Diagnosis is entirely clinical**.
- **Virology**: Throat swab, vesicle swab, or stool sample for Enterovirus PCR. This is usually only performed for epidemiological surveillance by the CHP during outbreaks, or in severe/atypical cases admitted to the hospital.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Supportive Care**: The mainstay of treatment. The illness is usually self-limiting and resolves in 7-10 days.
- **Hydration**: The most critical aspect. The severe mouth pain often leads to decreased fluid intake and dehydration. Encourage cool, soft foods and fluids (ice lollies, cold milk). IV fluids are required if the child is clinically dehydrated and refusing oral intake.
- **Analgesia**: Regular Paracetamol or Ibuprofen for fever and mouth pain. Topical oral analgesic gels (e.g., containing lidocaine) can be used before meals.
- **Infection Control**: Strict hand hygiene. The child must be excluded from school/nursery until the fever has settled and all vesicles have dried up.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Public Health (CHP)**: Institutional outbreaks (e.g., in a kindergarten) must be reported to the CHP. If an outbreak is severe or involves EV71, the CHP may order the temporary closure of the school.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Dehydration**: The most common complication, requiring hospital admission for IV fluids.
- **Severe Neurological/Cardiopulmonary Complications (The EV71 Threat)**:
  - While Coxsackievirus A16 usually causes mild disease, **Enterovirus 71 (EV71)** is notorious for causing severe, life-threatening complications, particularly in children under 5.
  - *Complications*: Brainstem encephalitis, aseptic meningitis, acute flaccid paralysis, and rapid-onset neurogenic pulmonary oedema/cardiopulmonary collapse.
  - *Red Flags for EV71*: Persistent high fever, repeated vomiting, lethargy, myoclonic jerks, tachycardia out of proportion to fever.
- **Onychomadesis**: Painless shedding of the fingernails and toenails 1-2 months after the infection.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "A 2-year-old with HFMD is refusing to drink and has dry mucous membranes. What is the most common complication of HFMD, and what is the management?" (Dehydration due to severe pain from oral ulcers. Management is adequate analgesia and oral rehydration; if unsuccessful, admission for IV fluids).
- *Viva*: "Which specific virus causing HFMD is associated with severe neurological complications like brainstem encephalitis and pulmonary oedema?" (Enterovirus 71 / EV71).
- *Pitfall*: Prescribing antibiotics for HFMD. It is a viral illness; antibiotics are ineffective and unnecessary.
