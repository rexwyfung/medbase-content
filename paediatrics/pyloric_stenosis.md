# Pyloric Stenosis (幽門狹窄)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition
- Hypertrophy of circular muscle of pylorus → gastric outlet obstruction.

### Local Epidemiology
- Incidence ~1-2/1000 live births.
- Males > Females (4:1), ↑ risk in first-born males.
- Presents at 2-8 wks of age, irrespective of gestational age.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Pathophysiology
- Unknown exact cause → progressive hypertrophy & hyperplasia of pyloric sphincter.
- Obstruction → vomiting of gastric contents (HCl, K+, Na+).

### Genetic & Environmental Factors
- Associated with macrolide (e.g., Erythromycin) use in 1st 2 wks of life (direct or via breast milk).
- Family history +ve (esp. maternal side).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
### Timeline of Symptoms
- Healthy at birth, normal feeding initially.
- Vomiting begins around 2-8 weeks → progressively worsens over time.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Cardinal Symptoms
- **Vomiting (嘔吐)**: Non-bilious, projectile (噴射式), progressively worsening, shortly after feeds.
- **Hunger (飢餓)**: Infant eager to feed immediately post-vomiting ("hungry vomiter").

### Cardinal Signs
- **Weight**: Failure to thrive (生長遲緩), weight loss, dehydration signs (sunken fontanelle, ↓ skin turgor).
- **Visible Gastric Peristalsis**: Left-to-right waves in upper abdomen during/after feed.
- **Palpable Pyloric Tumour ("Olive" Mass)**: Firm, olive-sized mass in RUQ/epigastrium (palpable when relaxed/feeding/post-vomit).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Differentiating GORD
- **GORD (胃食管反流)**: Effortless possetting, thriving (unless severe), no mass.

### Differentiating Malrotation
- **Intestinal Obstruction (Malrotation/Volvulus) (腸扭轉)**: Bilious (green) vomit → Surgical emergency.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
### Summary of Differentials
| Condition | Distinguishing Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Pyloric Stenosis (幽門狹窄)** | 2-8 wks, non-bilious projectile vomiting, "olive" mass, hungry vomiter. |
| **Malrotation/Volvulus (腸扭轉)** | Bilious vomiting, acutely unwell, surgical emergency. |
| **Gastro-oesophageal Reflux (GORD)** | Effortless vomiting/possetting, normal growth, no mass. |
| **Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia (CAH)** | Vomiting, shock, hyponatraemia, hyperkalaemia, metabolic acidosis. |
| **Infection/Sepsis (感染)** | Fever, lethargy, UTI/meningitis/gastroenteritis signs. |

## 7. Tiered Investigations
### Bedside Investigations
- **Test Feed**: Observe feeding for visible peristalsis, palpate for "olive". May require NGT aspiration to deflate stomach for proper palpation.

### Blood Investigations
- **VBG & U&E**: CRITICAL. Classic derangement: **Hypochloraemic (低氯), hypokalaemic (低鉀) metabolic alkalosis (代謝性鹼中毒)**.
- **Mechanism**: Vomiting loses HCl. Kidneys conserve volume by reabsorbing Na+ in exchange for K+ and H+ → worsening hypokalaemia/alkalosis ("paradoxical aciduria"). Hyponatraemia (低血鈉) may also be present.

### Gold Standard Imaging
- **Ultrasound Abdomen (腹部超聲波)**: Investigation of choice. Thickened pyloric muscle (>3-4 mm), elongated pyloric canal (>15-16 mm).

## 8. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Acute Medical Resuscitation
- **Priority**: MEDICAL emergency, NOT immediate surgical emergency. Correct dehydration & metabolic alkalosis (often takes >24hrs).
- **Fluid Protocol**: 0.9% NaCl + KCl (once urine output established). Strict fluid balance & U&E monitoring.

### Pre-operative Targets
- **Target**: Cl- >100 mmol/L, HCO3- <30 mmol/L. Operating on alkalotic infant ↑ risk of post-op apnoea.

### Definitive Surgical Management
- **Ramstedt Pyloromyotomy (幽門環肌切開術)**: Longitudinal split of hypertrophied muscle down to submucosa. Open (periumbilical) or Laparoscopic (standard in HK HA). Post-op feeding within hours.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Parent Support
- Reassure parents (curative condition, excellent prognosis).
- Lactation consultant / Midwife support to rapidly resume breastfeeding post-op.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Differentiating Neglect
- Distinguish dehydration/failure to thrive due to pyloric stenosis from intentional neglect.

### Post-operative Education
- Ensure parents understand some post-op vomiting is common as stomach adapts; resolves quickly.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
### Disease Prognosis
- Excellent prognosis post-surgery. Curative.

### Surgical Complications
- Mucosal perforation (rare).
- Incomplete myotomy (leads to recurrent vomiting).
- Wound infection.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Trap: Rushing the baby to the operating theatre. Pearl: "Resuscitate before you operate." The examiner wants to hear that you will correct the hypochloraemic hypokalaemic metabolic alkalosis with IV fluids first to prevent anaesthetic complications.
* Viva Question: "Why is the baby hypokalaemic if they are only vomiting gastric juice (which is mostly HCl)?" Answer: The hypokalaemia is primarily renal. The contracted intravascular volume activates the RAAS system. Aldosterone causes the kidneys to reabsorb sodium at the expense of excreting potassium and hydrogen ions in the distal tubule (paradoxical aciduria).
* Pitfall: Assuming bilious vomiting can be pyloric stenosis. Pyloric stenosis is strictly PRE-ampullary; vomiting MUST be non-bilious. If bilious, immediately suspect malrotation/volvulus.
