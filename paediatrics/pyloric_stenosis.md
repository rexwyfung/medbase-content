
# Pyloric Stenosis (幽門狹窄)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Hypertrophy of the circular muscle of the pylorus, leading to gastric outlet obstruction.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Incidence ~1-2 per 1000 live births. More common in males (4:1), especially first-born males. Often presents between 2 to 8 weeks of age.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- The exact cause is unknown, but involves progressive hypertrophy and hyperplasia of the pyloric sphincter.
- Associated with macrolide antibiotic use (e.g., Erythromycin) in the first two weeks of life (either directly to the infant or via breast milk).
- The obstruction causes vomiting of gastric contents (HCl, K+, Na+), leading to the classic metabolic derangement.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- See below.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Vomiting**: Progressively worsening, **non-bilious**, **projectile** vomiting occurring shortly after feeds.
- **Hunger**: The infant is typically very hungry and eager to feed immediately after vomiting (the "hungry vomiter").
- **Signs**:
  - Failure to thrive, weight loss, signs of dehydration.
  - **Visible Gastric Peristalsis**: Left-to-right peristaltic waves visible on the upper abdomen during or after a feed.
  - **Palpable Pyloric Tumour ("Olive")**: A firm, olive-sized mass palpable in the right upper quadrant or epigastrium. Best felt when the infant is relaxed, feeding, or immediately after vomiting.
## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Gastro-oesophageal Reflux (GORD)**: Vomiting is usually effortless (possetting), not projectile. The infant is usually thriving (unless severe).
- **Intestinal Obstruction (e.g., Malrotation/Volvulus)**: Vomiting is **bilious** (green). This is a surgical emergency.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Most Likely**:
  - Infantile Hypertrophic Pyloric Stenosis.
- **Must-Not-Miss**:
  - Malrotation with midgut volvulus (if vomiting is bilious).
  - Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia (salt-wasting crisis can present with vomiting and dehydration, but has hyponatraemia and hyperkalaemia).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**:
  - **Test Feed**: Observe the infant feeding to look for visible peristalsis and palpate for the "olive".
  - **Bloods (VBG and U&E)**: **CRITICAL**. Look for the classic metabolic derangement: **Hypochloraemic, Hypokalaemic Metabolic Alkalosis**.
  - *Mechanism*: Vomiting loses HCl (alkalosis, hypochloraemia). The kidney tries to conserve volume by reabsorbing Na+ in exchange for K+ and H+ (worsening the hypokalaemia and alkalosis - "paradoxical aciduria").
- **Gold Standard**:
  - **Ultrasound Abdomen**: The investigation of choice. Shows a thickened pyloric muscle (thickness > 3-4 mm) and an elongated pyloric canal (length > 15-16 mm).
- **Monitoring**:
  - Strict fluid balance and regular U&E monitoring during resuscitation.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**:
  - **Medical Resuscitation is the Priority**: Pyloric stenosis is a *medical emergency*, not an immediate surgical emergency.
  - **Fluid Resuscitation**: Correct dehydration and the metabolic alkalosis. Use 0.9% Normal Saline with added Potassium Chloride (once urine output is established).
  - **Target**: Surgery MUST NOT proceed until the chloride is >100 mmol/L and bicarbonate is <30 mmol/L. Operating on an alkalotic infant increases the risk of post-operative apnoea.
- **Chronic/Definitive**:
  - **Ramstedt Pyloromyotomy**: The definitive surgical treatment. The hypertrophied muscle is split longitudinally down to the submucosa, relieving the obstruction. Usually done laparoscopically in HK HA hospitals.
## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Reassure parents. Post-operative feeding can usually commence within hours.
## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Explain that some post-operative vomiting is common as the stomach adapts, but it should resolve.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Excellent prognosis post-surgery. Curative.
- Complications of surgery: Mucosal perforation (rare), incomplete myotomy.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Rushing the baby to the operating theatre. *Pearl*: "Resuscitate before you operate." The examiner wants to hear that you will correct the hypochloraemic hypokalaemic metabolic alkalosis with IV fluids first to prevent anaesthetic complications.
- **Viva Question**: "Why is the baby hypokalaemic if they are only vomiting gastric juice (which is mostly HCl)?" *Answer*: The hypokalaemia is primarily renal. The contracted intravascular volume activates the RAAS system. Aldosterone causes the kidneys to reabsorb sodium at the expense of excreting potassium and hydrogen ions in the distal tubule.
