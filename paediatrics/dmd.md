# Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy (DMD) (杜興氏肌肉營養不良症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: The most common and most severe form of childhood muscular dystrophy. It is an X-linked recessive genetic disorder characterized by progressive, irreversible muscle weakness and wasting.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Affects approximately 1 in 3,500 to 5,000 live male births. Females are usually asymptomatic carriers.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Genetics**: Caused by mutations (mostly deletions) in the **DMD gene** on the X chromosome. This is the largest known human gene, making it highly susceptible to spontaneous mutations (1/3 of cases are new mutations with no family history).
- **Pathophysiology**: The DMD gene encodes **Dystrophin**, a crucial structural protein that anchors the muscle cell cytoskeleton to the extracellular matrix.
- Without dystrophin, the muscle cell membrane becomes fragile and tears during normal muscle contraction. This allows calcium to flood into the cell, causing necrosis of the muscle fibers.
- The dead muscle fibers are eventually replaced by fat and fibrotic connective tissue (pseudohypertrophy).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- The boy usually appears normal at birth and in infancy.
- Presents between 2 and 5 years of age with delayed motor milestones (e.g., late walking), frequent falls, clumsiness, or difficulty running and climbing stairs.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Proximal Muscle Weakness**: The weakness starts in the proximal muscles of the pelvis and legs, then progresses to the shoulder girdle and arms.
- **Gowers' Sign**: A classic, pathognomonic sign. When asked to rise from sitting on the floor, the child has to roll over, stick their buttocks in the air, and "climb up their own legs" using their hands to push on their thighs, due to severe weakness of the gluteal and quadriceps muscles.
- **Waddling Gait**: Due to weakness of the gluteus medius (Trendelenburg gait).
- **Calf Pseudohypertrophy**: The calf muscles appear unusually large and firm, but this is not muscle; it is the replacement of dead muscle with fat and scar tissue.
- **Toe Walking**: Due to early contractures of the Achilles tendon.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Becker Muscular Dystrophy (BMD)**: Caused by a mutation in the same gene, but the dystrophin protein is partially functional (reduced amount or truncated). The clinical course is much milder, with onset in late childhood/adolescence and ambulation maintained into adulthood.
- **Spinal Muscular Atrophy (SMA)**: A disease of the anterior horn cells (motor neurons), not the muscle itself. Presents with profound hypotonia, absent reflexes, and fasciculations.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Becker Muscular Dystrophy.
- Spinal Muscular Atrophy (SMA).
- Inflammatory myopathies (e.g., Dermatomyositis).
- Cerebral Palsy (spasticity, not progressive weakness).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Creatine Kinase (CK)**: The best initial screening test. Levels are **massively elevated** (often >10,000 U/L, normal is <200) even before clinical symptoms appear, due to the ongoing massive destruction of muscle cells.
- **Genetic Testing (The Definitive Diagnostic Test)**: Multiplex Ligation-dependent Probe Amplification (MLPA) or sequencing of the DMD gene from a blood sample to identify the specific deletion/mutation.
- **Muscle Biopsy**: Historically the gold standard, showing absent dystrophin staining. Now rarely needed if genetic testing is confirmatory.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **There is currently no cure.** Management aims to prolong ambulation, preserve respiratory/cardiac function, and maximize quality of life.
- **Corticosteroids (Prednisolone or Deflazacort)**: The only medication proven to slow the decline in muscle strength, prolong ambulation (by 2-3 years), and delay the onset of severe scoliosis and respiratory failure. Started around age 4-5.
- **Physiotherapy & Orthotics**: Daily stretching to prevent severe contractures (especially Achilles). Ankle-foot orthoses (AFOs) for night wear.
- **Respiratory Care**: As the diaphragm and intercostal muscles weaken, patients require Non-Invasive Ventilation (NIV / BiPAP) at night, and eventually a tracheostomy and full-time ventilation. Cough assist devices are crucial to clear secretions and prevent fatal pneumonias.
- **Cardiac Care**: All patients develop a dilated cardiomyopathy. ACE inhibitors are started early (often prophylactically) to delay heart failure.
- **Emerging Therapies**: Exon-skipping drugs (e.g., Eteplirsen) and gene therapy are in development/early clinical use for specific mutations, but are not yet curative.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **The MDT is extensive**: Paediatric Neurologist, Respiratory Physician, Cardiologist, Orthopaedic Surgeon, Physiotherapist, Occupational Therapist, Dietitian, and Medical Social Worker.
- **Psychosocial Support**: The diagnosis is devastating. The family must cope with the progressive loss of their child's abilities and a shortened lifespan. Support groups (e.g., HK Neuromuscular Disease Association) are vital.
- **Genetic Counseling**: Essential for the mother (to determine if she is a carrier) and for future pregnancies.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Complications**: Severe scoliosis (often requiring spinal fusion surgery once the child is wheelchair-bound), joint contractures, respiratory failure, dilated cardiomyopathy.
- **Prognosis**: Relentlessly progressive.
  - Loss of independent ambulation (wheelchair bound) by age 10-12.
  - Death usually occurs in the late 20s or 30s due to respiratory failure (pneumonia) or intractable heart failure.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "Describe Gowers' sign and explain its underlying pathophysiology." (The child uses their hands to 'climb up their own legs' to stand from a sitting position. It is caused by severe proximal muscle weakness, specifically the gluteus maximus and quadriceps).
- *Viva*: "A 4-year-old boy presents with frequent falls and large calves. What is the best initial blood test to order, and what result do you expect?" (Creatine Kinase (CK). It will be massively elevated, often in the tens of thousands, due to ongoing muscle necrosis).
- *Pitfall*: Assuming large calf muscles in a clumsy toddler are a sign of strength. In DMD, this is pseudohypertrophy (fat and scar tissue replacing muscle).
