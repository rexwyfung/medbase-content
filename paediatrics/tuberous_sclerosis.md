
# Tuberous Sclerosis Complex (結節性硬化症)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A neurocutaneous syndrome characterised by the development of benign tumours (hamartomas) in multiple organs, including the brain, skin, heart, kidneys, and eyes.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Incidence ~1 in 6,000. Managed in tertiary paediatric neurology clinics (e.g., QMH, HKCH, PWH).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Genetics**: Autosomal Dominant. Mutations in *TSC1* (Chromosome 9, encodes hamartin) or *TSC2* (Chromosome 16, encodes tuberin).
- **Mechanism**: Hamartin and tuberin form a complex that inhibits mTOR (mammalian target of rapamycin). Loss of function leads to mTOR overactivation and uncontrolled cell growth (hamartomas).
- ~70% are *de novo* mutations.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- See below.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Classic Triad (Vogt's Triad)**: Epilepsy, Intellectual Disability, Adenoma Sebaceum (facial angiofibromas). *Note: Only present in ~30% of patients.*
- **Skin Signs**:
  - **Ash-leaf spots**: Hypomelanotic macules (often the earliest sign, use Wood's lamp).
  - **Adenoma sebaceum** (Facial angiofibromas): Red papules over the malar region (appear in childhood).
  - **Shagreen patch**: Leathery, thickened skin patch, usually on the lower back.
  - **Ungual fibromas**: Tumours around/under the nails (appear post-puberty).
- **Neurological**: Infantile spasms (West syndrome), focal seizures, intellectual disability, autism.
## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **NF1**: Has café-au-lait spots (hyperpigmented) rather than ash-leaf spots (hypopigmented).

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Most Likely**:
  - TSC (if multiple characteristic skin lesions and seizures are present).
- **Must-Not-Miss**:
  - **Subependymal Giant Cell Astrocytoma (SEGA)**: Brain tumour near the foramen of Monro that can cause obstructive hydrocephalus and raised ICP.
  - **Cardiac Rhabdomyomas**: Can cause arrhythmias or outflow tract obstruction in neonates (often regress spontaneously).
  - **Renal Angiomyolipomas (AML)**: Risk of spontaneous, life-threatening haemorrhage.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**:
  - **Wood's Lamp Examination**: To identify ash-leaf spots in pale-skinned infants.
  - **EEG**: If seizures are present (hypsarrhythmia in infantile spasms).
- **Gold Standard**:
  - **MRI Brain**: To identify cortical tubers, subependymal nodules (SENs), and SEGAs.
  - **Echocardiogram**: For cardiac rhabdomyomas (often detected on antenatal US).
  - **Renal Ultrasound**: For angiomyolipomas and renal cysts.
  - **Genetic Testing**: *TSC1* and *TSC2* sequencing.
- **Monitoring**:
  - Annual renal US, regular MRI brain (to monitor for SEGA growth), regular ECG.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**:
  - Management of status epilepticus if presenting with prolonged seizures.
- **Chronic/Definitive**:
  - **Epilepsy**:
    - **Vigabatrin**: First-line for infantile spasms in TSC. *Crucial side effect*: Irreversible visual field constriction (requires regular ophthalmology monitoring).
    - Other anti-seizure medications (e.g., Everolimus, Cannabidiol) or epilepsy surgery for refractory cases.
    - **mTOR Inhibitors (Everolimus/Sirolimus)**: Disease-modifying therapy! Used to shrink SEGAs (avoiding brain surgery) and renal AMLs. Available in HK HA system for specific indications.
    - **Topical mTOR inhibitors**: For facial angiofibromas.
## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- TAND (TSC-Associated Neuropsychiatric Disorders): High rates of autism, ADHD, and intellectual disability require intensive educational and psychological support.
## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Genetic counselling.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Highly variable. Some have normal intellect and mild skin signs; others have severe intractable epilepsy and profound intellectual disability.
- Renal failure or haemorrhage from AMLs is a significant cause of morbidity in adulthood.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Forgetting the specific treatment for infantile spasms in TSC. *Pearl*: Always say **Vigabatrin** for TSC-associated infantile spasms, and immediately mention the risk of visual field defects.
- **OSCE Pearl**: If you see facial angiofibromas, explicitly state you want to examine the lower back for a Shagreen patch and the nails for ungual fibromas.
