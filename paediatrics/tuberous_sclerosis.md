# Tuberous Sclerosis Complex (結節性硬化症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition
- A neurocutaneous syndrome characterised by the development of benign tumours (hamartomas) in multiple organs, including the brain, skin, heart, kidneys, and eyes.
### Local Epidemiology
- Incidence ~1 in 6,000. 
- Managed in tertiary paediatric neurology clinics (e.g., QMH, HKCH, PWH) in Hong Kong.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Autosomal Dominant Inheritance
- **Genetics**: Autosomal Dominant inheritance. 
- **De Novo Mutations**: ~70% are *de novo* mutations.
### Molecular Pathophysiology
- **Mutations**: Occur in **TSC1** (Chromosome 9, encodes hamartin) or **TSC2** (Chromosome 16, encodes tuberin).
- **mTOR Pathway**: Hamartin and tuberin form a complex that inhibits **mTOR** (mammalian target of rapamycin). 
- **Hamartoma Formation**: Loss of function leads to mTOR overactivation and uncontrolled cell growth, forming hamartomas.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
### Systemic Involvement
- Multi-system involvement presenting at different ages. 
- Cardiac rhabdomyomas may present antenatally, while infantile spasms present in the first year, and skin/renal findings later in childhood/adolescence.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Classic Triad (Vogt's Triad)
- **Vogt's Triad**: Epilepsy, Intellectual Disability, Adenoma Sebaceum (facial angiofibromas). 
- Note: Only present in ~30% of patients.
### Dermatological Signs
- **Ash-leaf spots**: Hypomelanotic macules (often the earliest sign, visible under Wood's lamp).
- **Adenoma sebaceum** (Facial angiofibromas): Red papules over the malar region (appear in childhood).
- **Shagreen patch**: Leathery, thickened skin patch, usually on the lower back.
- **Ungual fibromas**: Tumours around/under the nails (appear post-puberty).
### Neurological Signs
- **Seizures**: Infantile spasms (West syndrome), focal seizures.
- **Cognitive**: Intellectual disability, autism spectrum disorder, ADHD.
### Ophthalmic Signs
- **Retinal Hamartomas**: Often asymptomatic but visible on fundoscopy.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Differentiating from Neurofibromatosis Type 1 (NF1)
- **Skin Lesions**: NF1 has **café-au-lait spots** (hyperpigmented) rather than **ash-leaf spots** (hypopigmented).
- **Tumour Type**: NF1 has neurofibromas; TSC has angiofibromas and hamartomas.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
### Most Likely
- **TSC**: If multiple characteristic skin lesions and seizures are present.
### Must-Not-Miss Tumours in TSC
- **Subependymal Giant Cell Astrocytoma (SEGA)**: Brain tumour near the foramen of Monro that can cause obstructive hydrocephalus and raised ICP (↑ ICP).
- **Cardiac Rhabdomyomas**: Can cause arrhythmias or outflow tract obstruction in neonates (often regress spontaneously).
- **Renal Angiomyolipomas (AML)**: Risk of spontaneous, life-threatening haemorrhage.

## 7. Tiered Investigations
### Bedside Investigations
- **Wood's Lamp Examination**: To identify ash-leaf spots in pale-skinned infants.
- **EEG**: If seizures are present (hypsarrhythmia indicates infantile spasms).
- **ECG**: To screen for arrhythmias if cardiac rhabdomyomas are present.
### Imaging Investigations
- **Gold Standard**: **MRI Brain** to identify cortical tubers, subependymal nodules (SENs), and SEGAs.
- **Echocardiogram**: For cardiac rhabdomyomas (often detected on antenatal US).
- **Renal Ultrasound**: For angiomyolipomas and renal cysts.
### Special Investigations
- **Genetic Testing**: **TSC1** and **TSC2** sequencing.
### Long-term Monitoring
- Annual renal US, regular MRI brain (to monitor for SEGA growth), regular ECG.

## 8. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Acute Management
- **Status Epilepticus**: Acute management following HA APLS protocols if presenting with prolonged seizures.
### Chronic Neurological Management
- **Infantile Spasms**: **Vigabatrin** is 1st line for infantile spasms in TSC. 
- **Ophthalmology review**: Crucial side effect of Vigabatrin is irreversible visual field constriction (requires regular ophthalmology monitoring).
- **Refractory Epilepsy**: Other anti-seizure medications (e.g., **Everolimus**, Cannabidiol) or epilepsy surgery for refractory cases.
### Disease-Modifying Therapy (mTOR Inhibitors)
- **mTOR Inhibitors** (**Everolimus**/**Sirolimus**): Disease-modifying therapy! Used to shrink SEGAs (avoiding brain surgery) and renal AMLs. Available in HK HA system for specific indications.
- **Topical mTOR inhibitors**: For facial angiofibromas.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### TAND Management
- **TAND** (TSC-Associated Neuropsychiatric Disorders): High rates of autism, ADHD, and intellectual disability.
- **MDT Support**: Requires intensive educational and psychological support via Medical Social Worker (MSW), Clinical Psychologist, and Special Educational Needs (SEN) coordinators.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Genetic Counselling
- Proper genetic counselling for parents regarding recurrence risk (autosomal dominant, 50% risk if parent affected, though ~70% are *de novo*).
- Screening of parents and siblings if index case is diagnosed.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
### Neurological Prognosis
- Highly variable. Some have normal intellect and mild skin signs; others have severe intractable epilepsy and profound intellectual disability.
### Systemic Complications
- **Renal Complications**: Renal failure or haemorrhage from AMLs is a significant cause of morbidity and mortality in adulthood.
- **Neurological Complications**: Hydrocephalus secondary to SEGA.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Trap: Forgetting the specific treatment for infantile spasms in TSC.
* Pearl: Always say Vigabatrin for TSC-associated infantile spasms, and immediately mention the risk of visual field defects.
* Pitfall: Missing subtle skin signs in fair-skinned infants. Always use a Wood's lamp to look for hypopigmented ash-leaf macules.
* Viva Question: What is the classic triad of Tuberous Sclerosis?
* Answer: Vogt's triad comprises Epilepsy, Intellectual Disability, and Adenoma Sebaceum (facial angiofibromas).
* Viva Question: What is the mechanism of action of Everolimus in TSC?
* Answer: It is an mTOR inhibitor that suppresses the overactive mTOR pathway caused by TSC1/TSC2 mutations, shrinking hamartomas like SEGAs and renal AMLs.
* Pearl: If you see facial angiofibromas, explicitly state you want to examine the lower back for a Shagreen patch and the nails for ungual fibromas.
