# Dyslexia & Developmental Language Disorder (DLD) (讀寫障礙及發展性語言障礙)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Dyslexia (Specific Learning Difficulty in Reading/Spelling)**: An unexpected, persistent difficulty in acquiring fluent reading, writing, and spelling skills despite normal intelligence, adequate schooling, and normal vision/hearing. 
- **Developmental Language Disorder (DLD)**: A condition where a child has significant, persistent difficulties understanding or using spoken language, not caused by known neurological disorders, hearing impairment, or global intellectual disability.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Extremely common (Dyslexia affects ~10% of children in Hong Kong). Note that learning to read traditional Chinese characters involves dual visual-orthographic and phonological processing, presenting unique challenges compared to alphabetical languages.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Dyslexia**: Rooted primarily in a **phonological processing deficit**. The brain has difficulty matching letters (graphemes) to sounds (phonemes) and rapidly decoding them. Strong genetic component (often runs in families). Functional MRI shows under-activation in left parieto-temporal reading pathways.
- **DLD**: Neurodevelopmental delay in the brain's language centers (Broca/Wernicke networks), affecting syntax (grammar), semantics (vocabulary), and morphology.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- **Dyslexia**: An 8-year-old boy in Primary 2 has an excellent verbal vocabulary and is a math genius, but he reads haltingly, reverses letters ('b' for 'd'), skips words, and his spelling is completely phonetic but incorrect.
- **DLD**: A 4-year-old girl who can hear perfectly and socializes normally, but struggles to follow multi-step instructions, uses very short sentences, and often drops verbs or connecting words.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Dyslexia**:
  - Poor phonemic awareness (difficulty rhyming or manipulating sounds).
  - Slow, inaccurate reading fluency (decoding).
  - Poor spelling (encoding).
  - Preserved verbal intelligence and general cognitive ability.
- **DLD**:
  - Expressive deficit: Limited vocabulary, grammatical errors beyond their age, struggle to narrate a story.
  - Receptive deficit: Misunderstanding questions, struggling with complex grammar (e.g., "The boy was chased by the dog" -> child thinks the boy did the chasing).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from Global Developmental Delay (Intellectual Disability): In DLD and Dyslexia, the child's Non-Verbal IQ (e.g., solving physical puzzles, recognizing patterns) is completely normal or high. The deficit is specific to the language/reading domain.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Untreated hearing loss (glue ear / OME strongly mimicking DLD).
- Visual impairment (refractive errors mimicking Dyslexia).
- Autism Spectrum Disorder (language deficits in ASD involve poor *social use* / pragmatics, whereas DLD involves grammar/vocabulary but social intent is preserved).
- ADHD (frequently co-morbid with Dyslexia).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Mandatory First Step**: Formal specific audiometry and detailed vision screening.
- **Educational Psychologist Assessment**: Uses standardized IQ tests (like WISC) to confirm normal cognitive ability, combined with specific literacy/phonological tests.
- **Speech & Language Therapy (ST) Assessment**: Standardized tests (e.g., CELF) to measure receptive and expressive language quotients against age norms for DLD.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Dyslexia**: No "cure." Requires **Specific Structured Phonics Interventions** (Multisensory reading programs). School accommodations are legally required (e.g., 25% extra time in exams, larger font, use of text-to-speech software, waiver of spelling penalties).
- **DLD**: Intensive, play-based early Speech and Language Therapy. Classroom seating strategies (sitting near the teacher).
- Medical treatments (like Omega-3 supplements or colorful glasses) lack robust clinical evidence for core reading decoding.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- immense psychosocial impact. Children with undiagnosed Dyslexia are repeatedly labeled as "lazy" or "stupid," causing severe school anxiety, depression, and acting out in class. Self-esteem counseling is paramount.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Dyslexia persists into adulthood, though individuals compensate well with technology.
- Severe untreated DLD often leads to significant behavioral issues because the child cannot express their frustrations verbally.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Telling a parent "he is a late bloomer, boys just talk late" for an isolated language delay at age 3. While some boys do talk later, DLD is a distinct pathology. If a child has no single words by 18 months, or no 2-word phrases by 24 months, urgent audiology and ST referral is mandatory.
- **Viva Q**: "A child reads poorly but has normal intelligence. A teacher suggests using colored overlays over text to cure his visual dyslexia. What is the evidence-based consensus?" Answer: Dyslexia is primarily a phonological (auditory-linguistic) processing defect, not a visual one. Multi-sensory structured language teaching (phonics) is the gold standard, while colored overlays have not been proven to treat the core decoding deficit of classical dyslexia.
