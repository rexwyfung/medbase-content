
# Necrotising Enterocolitis (NEC) (壞死性小腸結腸炎)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: An acquired, devastating inflammatory disease of the bowel (primarily the terminal ileum and ascending colon) leading to ischaemia, necrosis, and potential perforation.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Primarily affects premature infants in the Neonatal Intensive Care Unit (NICU). Incidence is inversely proportional to gestational age and birth weight. Managed in tertiary NICUs (e.g., QMH, PWH, KWH, UCH).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Multifactorial pathogenesis:
  1. **Prematurity**: Immature gut barrier, immature immune system, poor motility.
  2. **Enteral Feeding**: Formula feeding increases risk significantly compared to breast milk. Substrate in the gut allows bacterial proliferation.
  3. **Ischaemia/Hypoxia**: Perinatal asphyxia, severe RDS, PDA, or hypotension diverts blood away from the gut.
  4. **Bacterial Colonisation**: Abnormal microbiome leads to an exaggerated inflammatory response, mucosal damage, and gas production within the bowel wall.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- See below.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Onset**: Typically in the 2nd or 3rd week of life in very preterm infants (after enteral feeds have been established).
- **Systemic Signs**: Temperature instability, apnoea, bradycardia, lethargy, poor perfusion, shock.
- **Gastrointestinal Signs**:
  - Feed intolerance, large gastric aspirates (often bilious).
  - **Abdominal distension** (often the earliest sign).
  - Abdominal tenderness, erythema or bluish discoloration of the abdominal wall (indicates underlying peritonitis/necrosis).
  - Bloody stools.
## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Sepsis**: Can present identically with systemic signs and feed intolerance. NEC is distinguished by the specific abdominal signs and radiological findings.
- **Spontaneous Intestinal Perforation (SIP)**: Usually occurs earlier (first week), often associated with Indomethacin/steroid use, lacks the widespread inflammation and pneumatosis seen in NEC.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Most Likely**:
  - NEC (in a preterm infant with distension and bloody stools).
- **Must-Not-Miss**:
  - Bowel perforation (requires urgent surgical intervention).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**:
  - **Abdominal X-ray (AP and Lateral Decubitus)**: The most critical investigation.
  - *Early*: Dilated bowel loops, thickened bowel walls.
  - *Pathognomonic*: **Pneumatosis Intestinalis** (gas within the bowel wall, looks like bubbles or train tracks).
  - *Advanced*: **Portal venous gas** (branching lucencies over the liver).
  - *Perforation*: **Pneumoperitoneum** (free air under the diaphragm on erect, or anteriorly on lateral decubitus view).
- **Gold Standard**:
  - **Bloods**:
    - CBC (thrombocytopenia is a poor prognostic sign, neutropenia).
    - CRP (elevated).
    - VBG (metabolic acidosis, elevated lactate indicating ischaemia).
    - Blood cultures (frequently positive).
- **Monitoring**:
  - Serial abdominal X-rays (every 6-12 hours initially) to monitor for progression or perforation.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**:
  - **Medical Management (Bell's Stage I & II)**:
    - **NBM (Nil By Mouth)**: Stop all enteral feeds immediately.
    - **Gastric Decompression**: Insert a large-bore Replogle tube on free drainage/low suction.
    - **IV Fluids & TPN**: Provide total parenteral nutrition.
    - **Broad-Spectrum IV Antibiotics**: Cover Gram-positives, Gram-negatives, and anaerobes (e.g., Ampicillin, Gentamicin, Metronidazole).
    - Inotropic support and mechanical ventilation if in shock.
- **Chronic/Definitive**:
  - **Surgical Management (Bell's Stage III - Perforation or failure of medical therapy)**:
    - **Laparotomy**: Resection of necrotic bowel and formation of a stoma (ileostomy/colostomy). Primary anastomosis is rarely done in the acute setting.
    - **Peritoneal Drain**: Sometimes used as a temporising measure in extremely unstable, extremely low birth weight (ELBW) infants who cannot tolerate a laparotomy.
## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Prevention**: The most important strategy. **Breast milk** is highly protective. Standardised, slow feeding protocols in HA NICUs.
## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- High mortality rate (15-30%). Requires frequent, sensitive communication with parents regarding the critical condition and potential need for surgery.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
**Acute**: Sepsis, DIC, death.
- **Long-term**:
  - **Strictures**: Common complication (10-20%) occurring weeks to months after recovery, presenting with bowel obstruction.
  - **Short Bowel Syndrome**: If massive resection is required, leading to malabsorption and dependence on long-term TPN (with risk of TPN-associated liver disease).
  - Neurodevelopmental impairment.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Waiting for pneumoperitoneum to diagnose NEC. *Pearl*: Pneumatosis intestinalis is the hallmark. If you see it, treat aggressively. Pneumoperitoneum means it has already perforated.
- **Viva Question**: "What is the single most effective intervention to prevent NEC in premature infants?" *Answer*: The use of exclusive human milk (mother's own milk or donor breast milk).
