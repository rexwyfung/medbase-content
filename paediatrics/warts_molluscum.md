# Viral Warts & Molluscum Contagiosum (病毒疣及傳染性軟疣)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Viral Warts (Verrucae)**: Benign epidermal proliferations caused by Human Papillomavirus (HPV). Extremely common in school-aged children.
- **Molluscum Contagiosum**: A superficial skin infection caused by a Poxvirus. Highly contagious among toddlers and young children, often spread via shared baths or swimming pools.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Both are superficial skin viruses transmitted by direct skin-to-skin contact or fomites (e.g., damp towels, swimming pool floors).
- They specifically infect the epidermis, causing localized hyperproliferation.
- They completely evade the systemic immune system by staying superficial, which is why it takes the body's immune system locally 12-18 months to finally "notice" them and clear them.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- **Warts**: A 7-year-old with a cluster of rough, hard bumps on the sole of his foot that hurt when he walks.
- **Molluscum**: A 4-year-old with two dozen pearly, dome-shaped, painless little spots scattered across his chest and under his armpits.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **VIRAL WARTS**:
  - Hyperkeratotic (thick, scaly), rough, exophytic papules.
  - Can be common (hands/knees), plantar (verrucae on soles), or plane (flat, on the face).
  - **Thrombosed Capillaries**: If you gently pare (shave) the top of a wart with a scalpel, you will see tiny black dots. These are coagulated blood vessels feeding the wart.
  - Often disrupt normal skin fingerprints/dermatoglyphics.
- **MOLLUSCUM CONTAGIOSUM**:
  - Pearly, firm, dome-shaped papules (2-5mm).
  - Pathognomonic **Central Umbilication**: A distinct tiny "dimple" or crater in the center of the dome.
  - Painless, but can become intensely itchy and red right before they heal (BOTE sign - Beginning of the End, showing immune recognition).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from Calluses/Corns: A callus on the foot will have normal skin lines running *through* it, and paring it down reveals smooth hard skin. A plantar wart pushes skin lines *around* it, and paring it down reveals bleeding black dots.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Chickenpox (Varicella) - highly febrile, vesicles crust over rapidly.
- Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease.
- Pyogenic Granuloma.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- Purely clinical diagnoses based on visual inspection. No investigations required.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Active Non-Treatment (Wait and See)**: The cornerstone of pediatric management. Because both are benign and self-correcting (usually within 1-2 years), and active treatments are highly painful and cause scarring, observation is best unless the lesions are directly painful (like a plantar foot wart) or causing severe psychological distress.
- **Wart Treatments**: 
  - Salicylic acid paints (must apply daily, file down dead skin; takes 12 weeks).
  - Cryotherapy (Liquid nitrogen. Very painful, often requires multiple sessions).
- **Molluscum Treatments**: 
  - Do nothing. 
  - If pressed: Cantharidin ("beetle juice" blistering agent), curettage (scraping), or cryotherapy.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- School exclusions: Neither condition requires exclusion from school. For swimming, warts should be covered with a waterproof plaster.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Extensive warts or molluscum in the *anogenital* region of a child MUST raise the suspicion of sexual abuse, though auto-inoculation from scratching other areas is also possible. CPPU referral for formal assessment is mandatory if uncertain.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Secondary bacterial cellulitis from aggressive scratching.
- Excellent prognosis; self-resolution leaves perfect skin, while iatrogenic surgical destruction leaves permanent scars.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Eagerly freezing off a dozen painless molluscum spots on a screaming 3-year-old child at the mother's request. You have caused immense trauma and likely left permanent hypopigmented scars for a condition that would have vanished beautifully on its own. Reassure the mother strongly.
- **Viva Q**: "You shave the callous over a tough bump on a child's heel. It reveals multiple tiny black dots. What are these dots, and what is your diagnosis?" Answer: The black dots are thrombosed capillaries. The diagnosis is a Viral Plantar Wart.
