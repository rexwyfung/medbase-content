# Viral Warts & Molluscum Contagiosum (病毒疣及傳染性軟疣)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Definition of Viral Warts (Verrucae)
* Benign epidermal proliferations caused by **Human Papillomavirus (HPV) (人類乳頭瘤病毒)**.
* Extremely common in school-aged children globally and locally.

### Definition of Molluscum Contagiosum
* Superficial localized skin infection caused by a **Poxvirus (痘病毒)**.
* Highly contagious among toddlers/young children.
* Often spreads via shared baths, swimming pools, or fomites (e.g., damp towels).

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
* Peak incidence in early school years (K3 to P3).
* High transmission rates in Hong Kong's humid climate and dense living environments.
* Major cause of parental anxiety presenting to **Maternal and Child Health Centres (MCHC) (母嬰健康院)** and general paediatric clinics.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics

### Pathophysiology of Viral Warts
* **Transmission**: Direct skin-to-skin contact or fomites → viral entry via epithelial micro-abrasions.
* **Cellular Mechanism**: HPV infects basal keratinocytes → induces epidermal hyperproliferation (acanthosis) and hyperkeratosis → verrucous exophytic papule.
* **Immune Evasion**: Virus remains confined to epidermis (avascular) → completely evades systemic immune surveillance → delayed local cellular immunity (takes 12-24 months for clearance).

### Pathophysiology of Molluscum Contagiosum
* **Transmission**: Direct contact, autoinoculation (scratching), or fomites.
* **Cellular Mechanism**: Poxvirus replicates in host cell cytoplasm → forms intracytoplasmic inclusion bodies (Henderson-Patterson bodies) → cellular hypertrophy → characteristic dome-shaped papule.
* **Inflammatory Response**: Sudden robust local immune response eventually triggered → manifests as acute inflammation before spontaneous resolution (**BOTE sign**).

## 3. Clinical Presentation

### Typical Presentation of Viral Warts
* 7-year-old boy.
* Presents with a cluster of rough, hard, hyperkeratotic bumps on hands or soles of feet (**Plantar Warts/Verrucae**).
* Painful on direct pressure/walking (if plantar).

### Typical Presentation of Molluscum Contagiosum
* 4-year-old girl with a history of atopic dermatitis.
* Presents with 10-30 pearly, dome-shaped, painless papules.
* Scattered across trunk, axillae, or skin folds.
* Mother reports sudden reddening/swelling of a few lesions.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Clinical Signs of Viral Warts
* Hyperkeratotic (thick, scaly), rough, exophytic papules.
* Disrupts normal skin lines/dermatoglyphics.
* **Pathognomonic Sign**: Paring (shaving) the hyperkeratotic surface reveals **thrombosed capillaries (血栓微血管)** appearing as tiny black dots.
* **Subtypes**:
  * Common warts (Verruca vulgaris): Hands, knees.
  * Plantar warts (Verruca plantaris): Soles of feet, flat due to pressure.
  * Plane warts (Verruca plana): Flat-topped, commonly on face.

### Clinical Signs of Molluscum Contagiosum
* 2-5mm pearly, firm, dome-shaped papules.
* **Pathognomonic Sign**: **Central umbilication (中央凹陷)** (a distinct tiny crater in the center).
* Expressible white, cheesy core (molluscum body).
* **BOTE Sign (Beginning of the End)**: Sudden intense erythema, swelling, and pruritus signalling immune recognition and impending resolution.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)

### Differentiating Plantar Warts from Calluses
| Feature | Plantar Wart (Verruca) | Callus/Corn |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Skin Lines (Dermatoglyphics)** | Disrupted/bypasses lesion | Preserved/runs through lesion |
| **Pain** | Painful on lateral squeezing | Painful on direct downward pressure |
| **Paring (Scalpel Shaving)** | Reveals black dots (thrombosed capillaries) | Reveals smooth, translucent hard skin core |

## 6. Differential Diagnosis

### Differential Diagnosis for Viral Warts
* **Squamous Cell Carcinoma**: Very rare in children, but suspect if rapid growth, ulceration, or immunosuppressed.
* **Pyogenic Granuloma (化膿性肉芽腫)**: Rapidly growing, friable, bleeds profusely with minor trauma.
* **Callus/Corn**: Thickened skin secondary to friction.

### Differential Diagnosis for Molluscum Contagiosum
* **Varicella (Chickenpox) (水痘)**: Highly febrile, vesicular, rapidly crusts over, centripetal distribution.
* **Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (手足口病)**: Febrile, painful vesicles on palms, soles, and oral mucosa.
* **Cryptococcosis**: Can mimic molluscum in severe immunocompromised states (e.g., advanced HIV).

## 7. Tiered Investigations

### First-Line Clinical Evaluation
* Purely clinical diagnoses based on visual inspection and classic signs.
* **Bedside**: Gentle paring of hyperkeratotic lesions with a #15 scalpel blade to identify thrombosed capillaries in warts.

### Indications for Special Investigations (Rare)
* **Skin Biopsy**: Strictly reserved for atypical, rapidly growing, ulcerating lesions, or severe treatment resistance to rule out malignancy or deep fungal infections.
* **HIV Testing**: Consider if giant, rapidly disseminating, or hundreds of recalcitrant molluscum lesions (suspicion of severe cellular immunodeficiency).

## 8. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)

### 1st Line Management (HA Protocol)
* **Active Non-Treatment (Wait and See)**: Gold standard for pediatric cases in HA clinics.
* Strongly reassure parents regarding the benign, self-limiting nature (resolves in 12-24 months).
* Active physical destruction causes immense pain, trauma, and iatrogenic scarring, which is worse than the disease itself.

### Medical Therapy for Symptomatic Warts
* **Indications**: Painful plantar warts impeding walking, severe psychological distress.
* **Topical Salicylic Acid (水楊酸)** (15-40%): 1st line medical therapy. Apply daily for up to 12 weeks. Must soak in warm water and file down dead skin before application.
* **Cryotherapy (冷凍治療)**: Liquid nitrogen. Highly painful. Requires multiple sessions (2-3 week intervals). High drop-out rate in children.

### Medical Therapy for Molluscum Contagiosum
* **Strictly Observation**.
* Emollients for surrounding dry skin or associated **Eczema Molluscum**.
* Mild topical corticosteroids (e.g., 1% Hydrocortisone) if the BOTE inflammatory phase is severely pruritic.
* Avoid destructive therapies (curettage, cryotherapy, Cantharidin) in young children.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care

### School and Activity Guidelines
* **School Exclusion**: NOT required for either condition.
* **Physical Education/Swimming**: Cover warts with a waterproof plaster/tape. Do not share towels, kickboards, or clothing.

### Primary Care & MSW Support
* Reassure anxious parents who demand "quick fixes". Explain the viral life cycle and immune clearance.
* Address bullying or stigma in school if lesions are highly visible (e.g., facial plane warts).

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags

### Evaluation of Anogenital Lesions
* **Red Flag**: Extensive warts (Condyloma acuminata) or molluscum in the anogenital region of a child.
* **Safeguarding Protocol**: MUST raise high suspicion of **Child Sexual Abuse (C.S.A.)**.
* Differential: Auto-inoculation from scratching other body parts or vertical transmission (if infant <2 years).
* **Action**: Mandatory admission for formal evaluation. Referral to **Child Protection Police Unit (CPPU)**, **Medical Social Worker (MSW)**, and specialist pediatric team for forensic assessment.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis

### Complications
* **Secondary Bacterial Infection (Cellulitis/Impetigo)**: ↑ risk due to aggressive scratching or unhygienic home paring. Requires topical/oral antibiotics (e.g., Flucloxacillin).
* **Eczema Molluscum**: Autoinoculation and rapid spread of molluscum along areas of atopic dermatitis.
* **Iatrogenic**: Permanent hypopigmented or hypertrophic scarring from unnecessary surgical/cryo-destructive procedures.

### Prognosis
* **Excellent**. Complete spontaneous resolution leaves perfect, unscarred skin once the local immune system clears the virus.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Trap: Eagerly freezing off a dozen painless molluscum spots on a screaming 3-year-old child at the mother's request. Pearl: You have caused immense trauma and likely left permanent hypopigmented scars for a condition that would have vanished beautifully on its own. Reassure the mother strongly and practice active non-treatment.
* Viva Question: You shave the callous over a tough bump on a child's heel. It reveals multiple tiny black dots. What are these dots, and what is your diagnosis? Answer: The black dots are thrombosed capillaries. The diagnosis is a Viral Plantar Wart.
* Pitfall: Diagnosing an anogenital wart in a 5-year-old and discharging them with salicylic acid paint. Missing a glaring red flag for child sexual abuse. You must involve the child protection team (CPPU/MSW) for a multidisciplinary safeguarding assessment.
* Viva Question: A mother panics because her child's molluscum lesions have suddenly become intensely red, swollen, and crusty. She fears a severe bacterial infection. What is the most likely physiological cause? Answer: This is the BOTE (Beginning of the End) sign, representing the host's delayed immune response recognizing and destroying the virus. It heralds imminent spontaneous resolution.
