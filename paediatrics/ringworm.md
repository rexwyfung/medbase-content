# Ringworm (Tinea) & Kerion (金錢癬及膿癬)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A superficial infection of the skin, hair, or nails caused by Dermatophyte fungi (mostly *Trichophyton* or *Microsporum* species). 
- **Local Epidemiology**: Extremely common, especially in hot, humid climates like Hong Kong. Frequently acquired from infected pets (especially kittens and guinea pigs) or from soil.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Dermatophytes are fungi that uniquely possess the enzyme keratinase. They actively invade and eat keratin, which is the structural protein comprising the stratum corneum of the skin, hair shafts, and nails.
- They cause intense localized inflammation as they spread outward in a circle, dying off in the center where they have exhausted the keratin supply.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- A 6-year-old boy recently adopted a stray kitten. He now has three highly itchy, red ring-shaped rashes on his forearms, and a large bald patch on his scalp that oozes pus.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Tinea Corporis (Body)**: The classic "Ringworm." An annular (ring-shaped), erythematous, scaly plaque. It has a raised, active, red leading edge and central clearing. Intensely pruritic.
- **Tinea Capitis (Scalp)**: 
  - Patchy hair loss (alopecia) with visible scaling on the scalp.
  - "Black dot" appearance: The hair shafts become so brittle they break off flush with the scalp surface.
  - **KERION**: The most severe form of Tinea Capitis. A massive hypersensitivity reaction to the fungus creates a large, boggy, exquisitely tender, heavily inflamed, pus-filled mass that mimics a bacterial abscess.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from Pityriasis Rosea: PR has a single Herald Patch followed by a massive diffuse truncal breakout following skin tension lines, and does not respond to antifungals. Tinea is usually localized, asymmetric, and spreads out as distinct rings.
- Distinct from Bacterial Scalp Abscess: A Kerion usually features profound localized hair loss (alopecia) and broken hairs, which a standard Staph abscess does not.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Nummular (Discoid) Eczema (Very itchy coin-shaped lesions, but usually filled-in solid red, completely lacking central clearing).
- Granuloma Annulare (Painless ring without surface scale).
- Alopecia Areata (Causes bald spots, but the scalp is beautifully smooth and flesh-colored, absolutely ZERO scaling or inflammation).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Clinical**: Use a Wood's Lamp (UV light) in a dark room. Some species (like *Microsporum canis*) will fluoresce a bright apple-green.
- **Gold Standard**: Skin scrapings or plucked hairs (from the active edge of the lesion) sent for **KOH preparation microscopy** (shows branching fungal hyphae) and fungal culture.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Tinea Corporis (Skin)**: Respond perfectly to **Topical Antifungals** (e.g., Clotrimazole, Terbinafine, Ketoconazole creams) applied twice daily until 1 week after the rash disappears.
- **Tinea Capitis & Kerion (Scalp)**: 
  - **TOPICAL CREAMS DO NOT WORK.** The fungus has invaded deep into the hair follicle where creams cannot reach.
  - MUST use **Systemic Oral Antifungals** (e.g., Oral Griseofulvin for 6-8 weeks, or Oral Terbinafine for 4 weeks).
  - An adjunctive antifungal shampoo (Ketoconazole 2%) reduces shedding of spores to protect classmates.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Must advise parents to have all household pets universally checked by a veterinarian, as an asymptomatic cat is often the recurring vector.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- N/A.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Unrecognized/Untreated Kerion will permanently destroy the hair follicles, leading to irreversible cicatricial (scarring) alopecia in a young child.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Giving a child with a boggy scalp mass (Kerion) a course of oral Flucloxacillin thinking it is a bacterial abscess, or giving them just topical Clotrimazole cream. You must recognize it as deep fungal and prescribe oral antifungals to save their hair.
- **Trap**: Prescribing combination "Steroid + Antifungal" creams (like Daktacort/Travocort). The steroid dampens the local immune response, allowing the fungus to grow wildly while suppressing the redness (Tinea Incognito).
- **Viva Q**: "A child presents with a highly inflamed, boggy, pus-oozing mass on the scalp with severe hair loss over the area. What is the diagnosis and what is the MUST-GIVE route of medication?" Answer: Diagnosis is a Kerion (severe Tinea Capitis). It MUST be treated with ORAL systemic antifungals to penetrate the hair follicles.
