# Selective IgA Deficiency (選擇性 IgA 缺乏症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: An undetectable serum IgA level (<7 mg/dL) with absolutely normal IgG and IgM levels.
- **Local Epidemiology**: The most common primary immunodeficiency in the world (up to 1 in 500 Caucasians, though slightly less common in Asian populations).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Exact genetic defect usually unknown. 
- There is a failure of B-cells to terminally differentiate into IgA-producing plasma cells.
- Because IgA is the primary protective antibody on mucosal surfaces (gut, lungs, sinuses, tears), these frontiers are left slightly more vulnerable.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- **VAST MAJORITY (>80%)**: Completely asymptomatic and never know they have it, often discovered incidentally during completely unrelated blood work (e.g., screening for Coeliac disease).
- Symptomatic minority: A 6-year-old with recurrent chest infections and chronic foul-smelling diarrhea.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- Recurrent viral/bacterial infections strictly confined to mucosal boundaries (Otitis media, sinusitis, mild pneumonia).
- GI infections: Uniquely susceptible to **Giardia lamblia**, a gut parasite usually cleared by secretory IgA.
- Increased incidence of autoimmune diseases (Coeliac disease, Type 1 Diabetes) and atopy (allergies, asthma).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from CVID/XLA: Patients with purely Selective IgA deficiency have NORMAL IgG levels and make perfectly normal memory antibodies after vaccines.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Common Variable Immunodeficiency (CVID)
- Ataxia-telangiectasia (frequently features IgA deficiency)
- Secondary IgA deficiency (e.g., due to medications like Phenytoin or Sulfasalazine)

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Quantitative Immunoglobulins**: IgA < 7 mg/dL. IgG and IgM must be normal.
- **Vaccine Titers**: Must be normal (tests differentiate it from early CVID).
- **Rule out Coeliac**: If ordering a tissue transglutaminase (tTG) IgA to screen for coeliac disease in these patients, it will be falsely negative (because they have no IgA). You must order tTG **IgG** instead.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Supportive**: No specific treatment required for asymptomatic patients. Standard antibiotics for infections.
- **IVIG is NOT INDICATED**: IVIG products consist almost entirely of IgG. You cannot replace IgA this way.
- **Blood Transfusion Protocols**: This is the most critical management point. 

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Must carry a medical alert bracelet regarding blood transfusions.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- N/A.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Anaphylaxis during blood transfusions**: This is the classic complication. Because the patient's body has never seen the IgA protein, some patients develop severe IgG or IgE **anti-IgA antibodies**. If they are given whole blood or plasma from a normal donor (which is packed with the donor's normal IgA), the patient's immune system attacks it, causing fatal anaphylaxis instantly.
- To prevent this, they must receive **Washed Red Blood Cells** (where all plasma/IgA is rinsed away) or blood from another IgA-deficient donor.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Ordering a standard tTG-IgA test to check for Coeliac disease in a child known to have IgA deficiency. It will come back negative even if their intestines are destroyed.
- **Viva Q**: "An asthmatic 12-year-old child goes into hemorrhagic shock after trauma. 10 minutes into a packed RBC transfusion, he develops acute stridor, hives, and his blood pressure plummets. His parents mention he has a mild immune deficiency. What deficiency, and what went wrong?" Answer: Selective IgA deficiency. He developed anaphylaxis because he has anti-IgA antibodies, and the donor blood contained small amounts of donor IgA. He needed washed cells.
