# Selective IgA Deficiency (選擇性 IgA 缺乏症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition of Selective IgA Deficiency
* **Undetectable serum IgA** (<7 mg/dL or <0.07 g/L) in a patient >4 yrs old
* **NORMAL** serum **IgG** & **IgM** levels
* **NORMAL** T-cell function & cellular immunity
* **NORMAL** IgG antibody response to vaccinations

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
* Most common **Primary Immunodeficiency (原發性免疫缺陷)** globally
* Prevalence: ~1:500 in Caucasians
* ↓ common in Asian/HK population (HA data suggests ~1:4000 to 1:5000)
* Male to female ratio = 1:1
* Often undiagnosed due to asymptomatic nature

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Genetic Basis of Selective IgA Deficiency
* Exact genetic defect unknown in most cases
* Polygenic inheritance; familial clustering observed (autosomal dominant or recessive patterns)
* Associated with specific **HLA genotypes** (HLA-A1, B8, DR3, DQ2)

### Immunological Pathophysiology
* **B-cell maturation arrest**: B-cells express surface IgA but fail to terminally differentiate into IgA-secreting plasma cells
* → **Absent secretory IgA (sIgA)** at mucosal surfaces (GI tract, respiratory tract, urogenital tract, tears, saliva)
* sIgA normally neutralizes pathogens/toxins & prevents mucosal adherence
* → ↑ susceptibility to mucosal infections & ↑ absorption of intact macromolecules (triggering autoimmune/allergic responses)
* Compensatory ↑ in secretory IgM often occurs (mitigates symptom severity)

## 3. Clinical Presentation
### Asymptomatic Presentation
* **VAST MAJORITY (>80%)**: Completely asymptomatic
* Discovered incidentally on routine bloods or targeted screening (e.g., for **Coeliac disease (乳糜瀉)**)

### Symptomatic Presentation
* Onset typically >4 years old (IgA normally reaches adult levels by age 4)
* Recurrent mild-to-moderate mucosal infections (respiratory/GI)
* Presentations with associated autoimmune conditions or atopy

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Respiratory & ENT Symptoms
* Recurrent viral/bacterial infections confined to mucosal boundaries
* **Otitis media (中耳炎)**
* Sinusitis
* Bronchitis
* Mild pneumonia

### Gastrointestinal Symptoms
* Chronic or recurrent diarrhoea
* Uniquely susceptible to **Giardia lamblia (賈第蟲)** (gut parasite usually cleared by sIgA)
* Nodular lymphoid hyperplasia in gut (endoscopic finding)

### Autoimmune & Atopic Associations
* ↑ incidence of **Coeliac disease (乳糜瀉)**
* ↑ incidence of **Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus (一型糖尿病)**
* ↑ incidence of Thyroiditis, Rheumatoid Arthritis, SLE
* Severe atopy: **Asthma (哮喘)**, allergic rhinitis, food allergies

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Distinguishing Selective IgA Deficiency from CVID & XLA
| Feature | Selective IgA Deficiency | CVID | XLA (Bruton's) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **IgA Level** | ↓↓ (<7 mg/dL) | ↓↓ | ↓↓ |
| **IgG Level** | **Normal** | ↓↓ | ↓↓ |
| **IgM Level** | **Normal** | ↓ | ↓↓ |
| **B-cell count** | **Normal** | Normal | ↓↓ (Absent) |
| **Vaccine response** | **Normal** | Impaired | Impaired |
| **Clinical severity**| Mild / Asymptomatic | Moderate / Severe | Severe (early onset) |

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
### Primary Immunodeficiencies
* **Common Variable Immunodeficiency (CVID)**: Affects multiple Ig classes
* **Ataxia-telangiectasia**: Frequently features IgA deficiency + cerebellar ataxia + telangiectasia
* **Transient hypogammaglobulinaemia of infancy**: Normalizes by age 2-4
* **IgG subclass deficiency**: Can co-exist with IgA deficiency

### Secondary IgA Deficiency Causes
| Category | Examples |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Medications** | **Phenytoin**, **Sulfasalazine**, **Penicillamine**, Gold salts |
| **Infections** | Congenital Rubella, EBV, CMV |
| **Malignancy** | Leukaemia, Lymphoma |

## 7. Tiered Investigations
### Bedside & Basic Bloods
* **Complete Blood Count (CBC)**: Usually normal (r/o cytopenias)
* **Peripheral Blood Smear**: Normal

### Gold Standard Diagnostic Investigations
* **Quantitative Serum Immunoglobulins**:
  * IgA <7 mg/dL (measured >4 years of age)
  * IgG = Normal
  * IgM = Normal
* **Specific Vaccine Antibody Titres** (Tetanus, Diphtheria, Pneumococcus):
  * **Normal** (+ve response confirms intact IgG memory, ruling out CVID)

### Condition-Specific Special Investigations
* **Coeliac Serology Screen**:
  * Standard tissue transglutaminase (tTG) IgA → **Falsely -ve** (due to total lack of IgA)
  * **MUST order tTG IgG** or deamidated gliadin peptide (DGP) IgG
* **GI Pathogen Stool Microscopy**:
  * Look for cysts/trophozoites of **Giardia lamblia**

## 8. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Acute Infection Management
* Low threshold for empirical Abx for respiratory/GI infections
* Standard HA antibiotic protocols apply (e.g., Amoxicillin-Clavulanate for sinusitis/otitis media)
* Prolonged Abx courses may be required for slow responders

### Chronic Prophylactic Management
* **Supportive Care**: Good hand hygiene, avoid sick contacts
* **Prophylactic Abx**: Rarely needed (only if severe recurrent sinopulmonary infections)
* **Vaccinations**:
  * Standard childhood schedule recommended
  * Safe to receive live vaccines (e.g., MMR, Varicella) since T-cell function & IgG are intact
  * Annual Influenza & Pneumococcal vaccines highly recommended

### Treatments STRICTLY CONTRAINDICATED
* **IVIG (Intravenous Immunoglobulin)**:
  * **NOT INDICATED**: IVIG contains >95% IgG and negligible IgA
  * Cannot replace mucosal sIgA via IV infusion
  * Risk of precipitating anaphylaxis if trace IgA is present in IVIG preparation

### HA Blood Transfusion Protocol for IgA Deficient Patients
* **CRITICAL**: High risk of fatal **Anaphylaxis (過敏性休克)**
* Due to pre-formed anti-IgA antibodies (IgE or IgG) attacking donor IgA
* Must transfuse:
  * **Washed Red Blood Cells** (plasma/IgA completely rinsed away)
  * OR blood products from a known **IgA-deficient donor**

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Allied Health & MSW Involvement
* **Medical Social Worker (MSW)**: Support for chronic illness costs if complications arise
* **Clinical Psychologist**: Address anxiety relating to recurrent infections or co-morbid autoimmune diseases

### Patient Education & Safety
* Provide a **Medical Alert Bracelet / Card** explicitly stating "Selective IgA Deficiency - REQUIRES WASHED BLOOD PRODUCTS"
* Education on early recognition of sinopulmonary / GI infections

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Non-Accidental Injury (NAI) Considerations
* N/A for this specific condition
* Ensure recurrent bruising/infections are not misattributed to NAI if an undiagnosed immunodeficiency or autoimmune coagulopathy (e.g., ITP) is present

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
### Iatrogenic Complications
* **Fatal Anaphylaxis (過敏性休克)** during transfusion of unwashed blood products / plasma / IVIG

### Disease-Related Complications
* **Autoimmune diseases**: Up to 25-30% develop an autoimmune condition (Coeliac, Thyroiditis, T1DM)
* **Progression to CVID**: Small minority (<5%) may eventually progress to Common Variable Immunodeficiency over their lifetime
* **Chronic Lung Disease**: Recurrent pneumonias → Bronchiectasis (rare in pure IgA deficiency, usually implies co-existing IgG subclass deficiency)

### Long-Term Prognosis
* Excellent prognosis for asymptomatic patients
* Normal life expectancy
* Most symptomatic patients manage well with prompt treatment of intercurrent infections

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Viva Question: An asthmatic 12-year-old child goes into hemorrhagic shock after trauma. 10 minutes into a packed RBC transfusion, he develops acute stridor, hives, and his blood pressure plummets. His parents mention he has a mild immune deficiency. What deficiency, and what went wrong?
  Answer: Selective IgA deficiency. He developed anaphylaxis because he has anti-IgA antibodies, and the donor blood contained small amounts of donor IgA. He needed washed cells.
* Trap: Ordering a standard tTG-IgA test to check for Coeliac disease in a child known to have IgA deficiency.
  Pearl: It will be falsely negative. You MUST order tTG-IgG or DGP-IgG to accurately screen for Coeliac disease in IgA-deficient patients.
* Pitfall: Prescribing IVIG to "boost immunity" in a patient with pure Selective IgA deficiency suffering from recurrent colds. Avoid this as IVIG consists mainly of IgG, cannot replace mucosal sIgA, and poses a severe risk of triggering anaphylaxis due to trace amounts of IgA in the preparation.
* Viva Question: A 2-year-old child is found to have an IgA level of 5 mg/dL. Should they be diagnosed with Selective IgA Deficiency?
  Answer: No. IgA levels are physiologically low in early childhood and do not reach adult levels until around age 4. Diagnosis requires the patient to be >4 years old.
* Trap: Assuming a patient with Selective IgA deficiency cannot receive live vaccines.
  Pearl: T-cell function and IgG/IgM production are completely normal in these patients. They can and should safely receive all routine live attenuated vaccines (e.g., MMR, Varicella).
