
# Paediatric Sepsis & Septicaemia (小兒敗血症)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Sepsis is life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection. Septicaemia refers specifically to the presence and multiplication of bacteria in the blood.
- **Local Epidemiology**: A leading cause of paediatric mortality. Common pathogens vary by age:
  - *Neonates*: Group B Streptococcus (GBS), E. coli, Listeria.
  - *Infants/Children*: Streptococcus pneumoniae, Neisseria meningitidis, Staphylococcus aureus, Group A Strep. (Note: Haemophilus influenzae type b is now rare in HK due to vaccination).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Mechanism**: Pathogen-Associated Molecular Patterns (PAMPs, e.g., endotoxin from Gram-negative bacteria) trigger a massive release of pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-alpha, IL-1, IL-6).
- This "cytokine storm" causes:
  1. **Vasodilation**: Leading to profound hypotension ("warm shock" initially).
  2. **Capillary Leak**: Endothelial damage causes fluid to leak into tissues (oedema, ARDS), worsening intravascular volume depletion.
  3. **Myocardial Depression**: Decreased cardiac output ("cold shock", more common in children than adults).
  4. **Coagulopathy**: Activation of the coagulation cascade leading to Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- See below.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Early Signs (Often non-specific)**: Fever (or hypothermia in neonates), lethargy, poor feeding, irritability, unexplained tachycardia, tachypnoea.
- **Late Signs (Shock)**:
  - *Cold Shock (Common in children)*: Cold/mottled peripheries, prolonged capillary refill time (>3 seconds), weak/thready pulses, oliguria, altered mental status.
  - *Warm Shock*: Bounding pulses, flash capillary refill, warm peripheries, wide pulse pressure.
- **Specific Clues**:
  - **Non-blanching petechial/purpuric rash**: Highly suggestive of Meningococcal septicaemia.
  - Bulging fontanelle/neck stiffness: Meningitis.
## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Simple Viral Infection**: A child with a viral URTI may have a high fever and tachycardia, but they usually remain alert, interactive, and have normal perfusion (capillary refill < 2s). A septic child looks "toxic" and lethargic.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Most Likely**:
  - Bacterial Sepsis (Pneumococcus, Meningococcus, Staph aureus).
- **Must-Not-Miss**:
  - **Meningococcal Septicaemia**: Can cause death within hours.
  - **Toxic Shock Syndrome**: (Staph or Strep) presents with fever, shock, and a diffuse macular erythroderma (sunburn-like rash).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**:
  - **DO NOT DELAY TREATMENT FOR INVESTIGATIONS**.
  - **Bedside Glucose**: Hypoglycaemia is common and dangerous in septic children.
  - **Blood Gas (VBG/ABG)**: Check Lactate (elevated > 2 mmol/L indicates tissue hypoperfusion) and acid-base status (metabolic acidosis).
- **Gold Standard**:
  - **The "Sepsis Six" (Adapted for Paediatrics - To be done within 1 hour)**:
1. **Blood Cultures**: Take BEFORE giving antibiotics (but do not delay antibiotics if access is difficult).
2. **Urine & Sputum/CSF Cultures**: If clinically indicated (do NOT do an LP if the child is in shock or has a purpuric rash/coagulopathy).
3. **Bloods**: CBC, U&E, LFT, CRP/Procalcitonin, Coagulation screen (for DIC).
- **Monitoring**:
  - Continuous ECG, SpO2, and frequent non-invasive BP monitoring.
  - Strict fluid balance (urinary catheter to monitor output; goal > 1 ml/kg/hr).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**:
  - The "Golden Hour"
  - **High-Flow Oxygen**: 100% via non-rebreather mask.
  - **IV/IO Access**: Obtain access within 5 minutes. If IV fails, insert an Intraosseous (IO) needle immediately.
  - **IV Antibiotics**: Administer broad-spectrum antibiotics **WITHIN 1 HOUR**. (e.g., Ceftriaxone/Cefotaxime. Add Ampicillin for neonates to cover Listeria. Add Vancomycin if MRSA suspected).
  - **Fluid Resuscitation**:
    - Give 10-20 ml/kg of 0.9% Normal Saline as a rapid bolus (over 5-10 minutes).
    - Reassess after each bolus (check heart rate, capillary refill, liver size, crackles).
    - Can repeat up to 40-60 ml/kg in the first hour if shock persists and no signs of fluid overload (hepatomegaly/crackles).
    - **Inotropes/Vasopressors**: If shock is fluid-refractory (persists after 40 ml/kg), start peripheral Adrenaline or Noradrenaline infusion and arrange urgent PICU transfer.
- **Chronic/Definitive**:
  - Source control (e.g., draining an abscess, removing an infected central line).
  - Tailor antibiotics once culture sensitivities are available.
## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- PICU admission is mandatory for fluid-refractory shock or organ dysfunction.
## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Public health notification (DH) is required for certain pathogens (e.g., Meningococcus) to arrange prophylaxis for close contacts.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Complications**: Multi-Organ Dysfunction Syndrome (MODS), DIC, ARDS, acute kidney injury, limb amputations (due to purpura fulminans in meningococcal disease), death.
- Prognosis depends heavily on the speed of recognition and administration of the first dose of antibiotics and fluids.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Waiting for a lumbar puncture before giving antibiotics to a child with a purpuric rash and shock. *Pearl*: NEVER delay antibiotics in suspected sepsis/meningitis. If the child is in shock or has a coagulopathy (purpura), an LP is absolutely CONTRAINDICATED as it can cause fatal coning or bleeding. Give IV Ceftriaxone immediately.
- **Viva Question**: "What is the most reliable clinical sign of shock in a child?" *Answer*: Tachycardia and prolonged capillary refill time (>3 seconds). Hypotension is a very LATE and pre-terminal sign in children, as their robust cardiovascular system compensates by increasing heart rate and systemic vascular resistance until they suddenly collapse.
