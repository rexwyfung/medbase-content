# Vascular Anomalies (血管異常)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Vascular Anomalies
* According to **ISSVA Classification**:
* **Vascular Tumours**: Endothelial cell hyperplasia (e.g., **Infantile Haemangioma [嬰兒血管瘤]**).
* **Vascular Malformations (血管畸形)**: Errors in vascular morphogenesis. Subdivided by flow (Low-flow vs High-flow) and vessel type (Capillary, Venous, Lymphatic, Arteriovenous).

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
* **Infantile Haemangioma (IH)**: Most common benign tumour in infancy (4-5% in HK).
* ↑ incidence in premature infants, low birth weight, females (3:1), multiple gestations.
* **Vascular Malformations**: Rare, occur in ~1% of births. Equal gender distribution.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics

### Pathophysiology of Vascular Tumours (IH)
* Endothelial cell proliferation.
* **Triphasic lifecycle**:
  * **Proliferation phase**: Rapid growth (0-1 yr).
  * **Involution phase**: Slow regression (1-7 yrs).
  * **Involuted phase**: Fibrofatty residuum.
* Express **GLUT-1** +ve.

### Pathophysiology of Vascular Malformations
* Defective vessel morphogenesis.
* Normal endothelial turnover.
* Grow commensurately with child; expand with trauma, puberty, or pregnancy.
* Express **GLUT-1** -ve.

### Genetic Syndromes & Associations
* **PHACE Syndrome**: Posterior fossa anomalies, Haemangiomas (large, facial), Arterial anomalies, Cardiac defects, Eye abnormalities.
* **Sturge-Weber Syndrome (斯德格-韋伯綜合徵)**: Capillary malformation (V1 trigeminal distribution) + ipsilateral leptomeningeal angioma + glaucoma (associated with **GNAQ** mutation).
* **Klippel-Trenaunay Syndrome**: Capillary malformation + venous varicosities + limb hypertrophy (associated with **PIK3CA** mutation).

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Symptoms & Signs of Infantile Haemangioma
* Absent at birth (or faint premonitory blanching/telangiectatic halo).
* Appears 1-4 weeks postnatal.
* **Superficial IH**: Bright red, bosselated, sharply demarcated ("strawberry").
* **Deep IH**: Bluish hue, deep subcutaneous nodule.
* Firm/rubbery on palpation.

### Symptoms & Signs of Capillary Malformations (Port Wine Stain / 葡萄酒色斑)
* Present at birth.
* Flat, sharply demarcated red/pink macule.
* Grows with child, deepens in colour, may become nodular/cobblestoned in adulthood.

### Symptoms & Signs of Venous Malformations
* Present at birth (may manifest later).
* Soft, compressible, bluish mass.
* ↑ size with Valsalva manoeuvre, crying, or dependent position.
* Frequent phleboliths (palpable hard nodules).

### Symptoms & Signs of Lymphatic Malformations (Cystic Hygroma / 囊狀水瘤)
* Present at birth.
* Fluctuant, soft, compressible mass.
* **+ve Transillumination**.
* Often located in posterior triangle of neck or axilla.
* Prone to sudden ↑ size secondary to intralesional haemorrhage or infection.

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)

### Discriminatory Features: Tumours vs Malformations
| Feature | Vascular Tumour (IH) | Vascular Malformation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Onset** | Postnatal (1-4 wks) | Present at birth |
| **Growth** | Rapid out of proportion to child | Commensurate with child |
| **Involution** | Spontaneous (years) | Never |
| **Endothelium** | Hyperplastic (GLUT-1 +ve) | Normal (GLUT-1 -ve) |
| **Palpation** | Firm, rubbery | Compressible, soft |

## 5. Differential Diagnosis

### Differential Diagnosis of Cutaneous Vascular Lesions
| Condition | Clinical Features | Distinguishing Trait |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Pyogenic Granuloma (化膿性肉芽腫)** | Rapidly growing friable red papule | Bleeds profusely with minor trauma; appears older infants/children |
| **Congenital Haemangioma (RICH/NICH)** | Fully formed at birth | RICH involutes rapidly (1 yr); NICH never involutes |
| **Salmon Patch / Naevus Simplex** | Faint pink macules on nape of neck/glabella | Fades with time; common (up to 50% neonates) |
| **Kaposiform Hemangioendothelioma** | Indurated, purpuric plaque | Associated with Kasabach-Merritt phenomenon (↓ platelets) |

## 6. Tiered Investigations

### Bedside & Clinical Investigations
* **Clinical Diagnosis**: Sufficient for typical IH and simple capillary malformations.
* **Serial Photography**: Essential for monitoring growth/involution and treatment response.

### Imaging Investigations
* **USG Doppler (首選超聲波)**: 1st line to differentiate high-flow (IH, AVM) vs low-flow (Venous, Lymphatic) and measure depth.
* **MRI with contrast**: Indicated for:
  * Large/segmental facial IH (rule out PHACE).
  * Lumbosacral IH (rule out occult spinal dysraphism).
  * Deep/extensive vascular malformations prior to intervention.

### Laboratory Investigations
* Usually not required for IH.
* **Coagulation profile / Platelet count**: If Kasabach-Merritt suspected or extensive venous malformation (consumptive coagulopathy).

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)

### Acute Management of Haemangioma Complications
* **Bleeding**: Direct prolonged pressure (5-10 mins). 
* **Ulceration**: Pain control, topical barrier creams, local wound care, topical antibiotics if secondary infection.
* **Airway Obstruction (Subglottic IH)**: Immediate HA PICU/ENT referral, intubation/tracheostomy if critical.

### Indications for Pharmacological Intervention in IH
* Visual axis obstruction (risk of amblyopia/astigmatism).
* Airway compromise (associated with "beard" distribution IH).
* Severe ulceration unresponsive to wound care.
* High-output cardiac failure (large/multiple hepatic IH).
* Significant cosmetic disfigurement (facial).

### Pharmacological Management (HA Protocols)
* **Oral Propranolol (普萘洛爾)**: 1st line systemic therapy. 
  * **Dose**: 2-3 mg/kg/day divided BD/TDS. 
  * **HA Protocol**: Inpatient admission for 1st dose/dose-escalation to monitor HR, BP, and blood glucose.
  * **Contraindications**: Asthma, bradycardia, heart block, hypoglycaemia.
* **Topical Timolol (0.5%)**: 1st line for small, superficial, uncomplicated IH.

### Interventional & Surgical Management
* **Vascular Malformations**: 
  * Venous/Lymphatic: **Sclerotherapy** (e.g., Bleomycin, Sodium Tetradecyl Sulfate) by Interventional Radiology.
  * AVM: Embolization ± surgical resection.
  * Capillary (Port Wine Stain): **Pulsed Dye Laser (PDL)**.
* **IH Residuum**: Surgical excision for loose skin/fibrofatty tissue post-involution.

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care

### Multidisciplinary Team Roles
* **Paediatric Dermatology/Surgery**: Primary coordinators of care.
* **Ophthalmology**: Urgent review for periocular lesions.
* **ENT**: Endoscopy for airway symptoms (stridor).
* **Interventional Radiology**: Sclerotherapy/embolization.

### Psychosocial Care
* Active parental reassurance regarding natural history of IH (spontaneous involution).
* Anticipatory guidance for school-aged children regarding peer questioning/bullying.
* Clinical Psychologist referral for significant parental anxiety or child body-image issues.

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags

### Safeguarding Considerations in Vascular Lesions
* **Mimics of Bruising**: Deep IH, venous malformations, or Mongolian spots (Slate grey naevus) can be misdiagnosed as bruises.
* **Distinguishing Features**: True bruises change colour (purple → green → yellow) and resolve rapidly. Vascular malformations are static; IH grows.
* Detailed birth history and old photographs are critical to establish chronicity.

## 10. Complications & Prognosis

### Complications of Vascular Anomalies
* **Ulceration**: Most common complication of IH (~15%), highly painful, predilection for lip/perineum.
* **Kasabach-Merritt Phenomenon (卡薩巴赫-梅里特綜合徵)**: Profound thrombocytopenia + consumptive coagulopathy. Trap: Associated with Kaposiform Hemangioendothelioma / Tufted Angioma, **NOT** classic Infantile Haemangioma.
* **High-Output Cardiac Failure**: Large hepatic haemangiomas or AVMs.
* **Amblyopia**: Periocular IH compressing globe or blocking visual axis.

### Prognosis & Natural History
* **Infantile Haemangioma**: Excellent prognosis. 50% complete involution by 5 yrs; 90% by 9 yrs. Leaves residual telangiectasia/fibrofatty tissue in ~50%.
* **Vascular Malformations**: Do not involute. Progressive enlargement over life. High recurrence rate post-intervention.

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: What is the first-line medical treatment for a large facial infantile haemangioma causing visual obstruction? Answer: Oral Propranolol, initiated under monitoring for bradycardia, hypotension, and hypoglycaemia.
* Viva Question: An infant has a large haemangioma covering the jawline and neck (beard distribution). What serious associated complication must you investigate? Answer: Subglottic/airway haemangioma.
* Trap: Assuming Kasabach-Merritt Phenomenon occurs in large classic infantile haemangiomas. Pearl: Kasabach-Merritt Phenomenon is exclusively associated with Kaposiform Hemangioendothelioma and Tufted Angioma.
* Pitfall: Reassuring parents that a Port Wine Stain will fade over time. Port Wine Stains are capillary malformations that persist and often darken/thicken; Salmon patches (Naevus simplex) are the ones that fade.
* Trap: Performing a biopsy on a classic strawberry haemangioma. Pearl: Diagnosis is clinical; biopsy is contraindicated due to bleeding risk and is unnecessary unless malignancy is suspected.
