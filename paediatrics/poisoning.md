
# Paediatric Poisoning (小兒中毒)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Exposure to a substance (ingestion, inhalation, dermal, or ocular) that causes harmful physiological effects.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Very common presentation to A&E in HK.
  - *Toddlers (1-4 years)*: Accidental ingestion. Usually single substance, small amount, found around the home (e.g., Paracetamol, household cleaners, cosmetics, grandparents' medications).
  - *Adolescents*: Intentional self-harm or recreational drug use. Often multiple substances, larger amounts, delayed presentation.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Pathophysiology depends entirely on the specific toxin.
- **Common Toxidromes**:
  - **Anticholinergic** (e.g., Antihistamines, TCAs): "Hot as a hare, blind as a bat, dry as a bone, red as a beet, mad as a hatter." (Tachycardia, dilated pupils, dry skin, flushed, delirium/seizures).
  - **Cholinergic** (e.g., Organophosphate insecticides): DUMBELS (Diarrhoea, Urination, Miosis, Bradycardia, Emesis, Lacrimation, Salivation).
  - **Opiate**: Pinpoint pupils, respiratory depression, CNS depression.
  - **Sympathomimetic** (e.g., Amphetamines, Cocaine): Tachycardia, hypertension, dilated pupils, agitation, sweating.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- See below.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- Presentation varies widely. Always suspect poisoning in a child with sudden, unexplained altered mental status, seizures, or multi-organ dysfunction.
- **Paracetamol (Acetaminophen)**: Often asymptomatic initially. Nausea/vomiting. Liver failure (jaundice, coagulopathy, encephalopathy) develops after 48-72 hours if untreated.
- **Corrosives (Alkalis/Acids)**: Drooling, oral burns, stridor, severe abdominal/chest pain (esophageal perforation).
## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Encephalitis/Meningitis**: Can mimic the altered mental status of poisoning, but usually has a fever and prodromal illness.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Most Likely**:
  - Accidental ingestion of household products or OTC medications (Paracetamol).
- **Must-Not-Miss**:
  - **"One Pill Can Kill"**: Medications where a single adult dose can be fatal to a toddler (e.g., Calcium Channel Blockers, Beta-blockers, Tricyclic Antidepressants, Opioids, Oral Hypoglycaemics).
  - **Corrosives**: Never induce vomiting.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**:
  - **Bedside Glucose**: Mandatory in ANY child with altered mental status (rule out hypoglycaemia from oral hypoglycaemics or alcohol).
  - **ECG**: Crucial to check for arrhythmias or prolonged QTc/QRS (classic for TCA overdose).
  - **Blood Gas (VBG/ABG)**: Check for metabolic acidosis (e.g., Salicylate/Aspirin overdose, toxic alcohols).
- **Gold Standard**:
  - **Specific Drug Levels**:
    - **Paracetamol level**: Check at 4 hours post-ingestion (or immediately if >4 hours). Plot on the Rumack-Matthew nomogram to determine the need for antidote.
    - Salicylate level, Iron level, Digoxin level (if suspected).
    - **Toxicology Screen (Urine/Blood)**: Often takes too long to guide acute management, but useful for confirming the substance in intentional overdoses.
- **Monitoring**:
  - Continuous cardiac monitoring for cardiotoxic drugs (TCAs, CCBs).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**:
  - **Airway**: Intubate if GCS < 8 or loss of airway reflexes.
  - **Breathing**: Oxygen.
  - **Circulation**: IV fluids for hypotension.
  - **Decontamination**:
    - *Skin/Eyes*: Copious irrigation.
    - *GI*: **Activated Charcoal** (1 g/kg oral/NG). Only useful if given within 1 hour of ingestion of a substance that binds to it (does NOT bind iron, lithium, alcohols, or corrosives).
    - *Gastric Lavage / Induced Emesis (Ipecac)*: **Obsolete and contraindicated** due to risk of aspiration and injury.
- **Chronic/Definitive**:
  - **Consult the Hong Kong Poison Information Centre (HKPIC)**: A vital resource for specific management advice.
  - **Specific Antidotes**:
    - **Paracetamol**: N-acetylcysteine (NAC) IV infusion. Highly effective if started within 8 hours.
    - **Opiates**: Naloxone (IV/IM/IN).
    - **Benzodiazepines**: Flumazenil (use with caution, can precipitate seizures).
    - **Iron**: Desferrioxamine.
    - **Organophosphates**: Atropine and Pralidoxime.
## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Psychiatric Assessment**: **MANDATORY** for all intentional overdoses (adolescents) before discharge.
## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- **Accidental**: Educate parents on safe storage (locked cabinets, child-proof caps). MSW referral if neglect is suspected.
- **Intentional**: MSW and Child Psychiatry referral.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Prognosis depends on the substance, dose, and time to presentation.
- Paracetamol overdose is the leading cause of acute liver failure requiring transplant if missed.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Suggesting gastric lavage or inducing vomiting for a child who swallowed bleach (alkali). *Pearl*: NEVER induce vomiting or use charcoal for corrosives. Vomiting re-exposes the oesophagus to the burn and increases the risk of aspiration. Management is supportive (NBM, endoscopy).
- **Viva Question**: "A 2-year-old is brought in 2 hours after swallowing a handful of his mother's Paracetamol tablets. He looks completely well. What is your immediate management?" *Answer*: Do not be reassured by his well appearance. Check a serum Paracetamol level at 4 hours post-ingestion, plot it on the nomogram, and start N-acetylcysteine (NAC) if it is above the treatment line to prevent fatal liver failure.
