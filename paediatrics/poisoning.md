# Paediatric Poisoning (小兒中毒)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition of Poisoning
* Exposure to substance (ingestion, inhalation, dermal, ocular) → harmful physiological effects

### Hong Kong Paediatric Poisoning Epidemiology
* Very common presentation to A&E in HK
* **Toddlers (1-4 years)**: Accidental ingestion. Single substance. Small amounts. Found in home (e.g., **Paracetamol** (撲熱息痛), household cleaners, cosmetics, grandparents' meds)
* **Adolescents**: Intentional self-harm / recreational drug use. Multiple substances. Larger amounts. Delayed presentation

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Paracetamol Pathophysiology
* Metabolized in liver via sulfation/glucuronidation
* Saturation → CYP450 pathway → toxic metabolite **NAPQI**
* Glutathione stores depleted → NAPQI binds macromolecules → **Hepatic necrosis** (肝壞死)

### Salicylate Pathophysiology
* Medullary stimulation → **Respiratory Alkalosis** (呼吸性鹼中毒)
* Uncoupling of oxidative phosphorylation → **Metabolic Acidosis** (代謝性酸中毒) → hyperthermia, ↑ lactate

### Organophosphate Pathophysiology
* Irreversible inhibition of **Acetylcholinesterase (AChE)**
* ↑ Acetylcholine at synapses → cholinergic overstimulation

### Iron Pathophysiology
* Direct corrosive effect on GI mucosa → haemorrhage
* Free iron → oxidative free radical damage → **Circulatory Shock** (休克) + **Hepatic Failure** (肝衰竭)

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Anticholinergic Toxidrome Signs
* Tachycardia
* Dilated pupils
* Dry skin / mucous membranes
* Flushed / red skin
* Delirium / Seizures (癲癇)

### Cholinergic Toxidrome Signs (DUMBELS)
* **D**iarrhoea (腹瀉)
* **U**rination
* **M**iosis (pinpoint pupils)
* **B**radycardia
* **E**mesis
* **L**acrimation
* **S**alivation

### Opiate Toxidrome Signs
* Pinpoint pupils
* Respiratory depression (呼吸抑制)
* CNS depression / Coma (昏迷)

### Sympathomimetic Toxidrome Signs
* Tachycardia
* Hypertension
* Dilated pupils
* Agitation
* Sweating

### Paracetamol Overdose Stages
* **0-24 hrs**: Nausea, vomiting, lethargy (often asymptomatic)
* **24-72 hrs**: RUQ pain, ↑ AST/ALT, ↑ PT/INR
* **72-96 hrs**: Jaundice (黃疸), hepatic encephalopathy, acute renal failure
* **>96 hrs**: Resolution or death

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Poisoning vs Central Nervous System Infections
* **Poisoning**: Sudden onset, lack of prodrome, apyrexial (usually), specific toxidrome signs
* **Encephalitis/Meningitis**: Fever +ve, prodromal illness +ve, neck stiffness +ve, gradual deterioration

### Poisoning vs Metabolic Encephalopathy
* **Poisoning**: Known access to drugs, empty blister packs, specific drug levels +ve
* **Metabolic Encephalopathy**: Hypoglycaemia +ve, underlying inborn errors of metabolism, ↑ ammonia

## 5. Differential Diagnosis
### Most Likely Accidental Ingestions
* OTC analgesics (**Paracetamol**, **Ibuprofen**)
* Household cleaning products (Bleach, detergents)
* Cosmetics (Perfume, nail polish remover)
* Vitamins / Iron supplements

### Must-Not-Miss "One Pill Can Kill" Agents
| Drug Class | Examples | Fatal Mechanism |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Calcium Channel Blockers** | Amlodipine | Refractory hypotension, bradycardia |
| **Beta-blockers** | Propranolol | Cardiogenic shock, hypoglycaemia |
| **Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs)** | Amitriptyline | Arrhythmias (↑ QTc), intractable seizures |
| **Opioids** | Methadone, Fentanyl | Respiratory arrest |
| **Oral Hypoglycaemics** | Sulfonylureas | Profound prolonged hypoglycaemia |

## 6. Tiered Investigations
### Bedside Investigations for Poisoning
* **Bedside Glucose**: Mandatory in ALL altered mental status (rule out hypoglycaemia)
* **ECG**: Check arrhythmias, prolonged QTc, wide QRS (classic for TCAs)
* **Blood Gas (VBG/ABG)**: Check metabolic acidosis (Salicylates, Toxic alcohols)

### Gold Standard Blood Investigations for Poisoning
* **Paracetamol Level**: Check at 4 hrs post-ingestion (or immediately if >4 hrs)
* **Salicylate Level**: If indicated
* **Iron Level**: If indicated
* **Liver Function Tests (LFTs)**: Baseline AST, ALT, Bilirubin
* **Coagulation Screen (PT/INR)**: Crucial for paracetamol hepatotoxicity
* **Renal Function Tests (U&Es)**: Monitor urea, creatinine, electrolytes

### Toxicology Screening Indications
* **Urine/Blood Tox Screen**: Useful for confirming intentional overdoses
* Note: Often too slow to guide acute management

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Acute Resuscitation for Poisoning (ABCDE)
* **Airway**: Intubate if GCS < 8 or loss of protective reflexes
* **Breathing**: High-flow O2
* **Circulation**: IV fluid bolus (20 mL/kg 0.9% NaCl) for hypotension
* **Disability**: Treat seizures (IV Lorazepam)

### Gastrointestinal Decontamination Protocols
* **Skin/Eyes**: Copious saline irrigation
* **Activated Charcoal** (活性碳) (1 g/kg oral/NG):
  * Give within 1 hr of ingestion
  * Does NOT bind: Iron, Lithium, Alcohols, Corrosives
* **Gastric Lavage / Ipecac**: Obsolete in HK HA protocols (↑ aspiration risk)

### HK Hospital Authority Paracetamol Overdose Protocol
* Check serum level at 4 hrs post-ingestion
* Plot on **Rumack-Matthew Nomogram**
* Start **IV N-acetylcysteine (NAC)** (乙酰半胱氨酸) if level crosses treatment line
* NAC highly effective if started within 8 hrs of ingestion

### Specific Antidotes Table
| Toxin | Specific Antidote | Mechanism of Action |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Paracetamol** | N-acetylcysteine (NAC) | Replenishes glutathione stores |
| **Opiates** | Naloxone | Competitive opioid receptor antagonist |
| **Benzodiazepines** | Flumazenil | Competitive GABA antagonist (Use caution: seizure risk) |
| **Iron** | Desferrioxamine | Chelates free iron |
| **Organophosphates** | Atropine + Pralidoxime | Antimuscarinic + reactivates AChE |
| **TCAs** | Sodium Bicarbonate | Overcomes Na-channel blockade |

### Corrosive Ingestion Management
* **DO NOT** induce vomiting (re-exposes oesophagus)
* **DO NOT** give activated charcoal
* Keep NBM (禁食)
* Urgent endoscopy within 24 hrs to assess burns

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Multidisciplinary Team Roles in Poisoning
* **HK Poison Information Centre (HKPIC)**: 24/7 specialist tox advice
* **Medical Social Worker (MSW)**: Assess home safety, neglect
* **Child Psychiatrist**: Mandatory review for intentional overdoses

### Psychiatric Assessment Protocols
* Mandatory for ALL intentional ingestions (adolescents) before discharge
* Assess suicide risk, underlying depression, family dynamics

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Accidental Poisoning Red Flags
* Inconsistent history from parents
* Delayed presentation > 24 hrs
* Multiple previous attendances for injuries/poisoning
* Lack of parental supervision / gross neglect
* Needs MSW referral + inform Police / SWD if high suspicion of NAI

### Intentional Poisoning & Neglect Indicators
* Fabricated or Induced Illness (Munchausen by proxy)
* Toxicological screen revealing unprescribed adult meds in toddler

## 10. Complications & Prognosis
### Short-term Complications of Poisoning
* Aspiration pneumonia (from depressed GCS or emesis)
* Hypoxic brain injury (from prolonged seizures/respiratory arrest)
* Acute Liver Failure (急性肝衰竭) (Paracetamol)

### Long-term Prognosis of Poisoning
* Dependent on substance, dose, time to antidote
* **Paracetamol**: Leading cause of liver transplant if missed
* **Corrosives**: High risk of strictures (狹窄) requiring dilatations/surgery

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Trap: Suggesting gastric lavage or inducing vomiting for a child who swallowed bleach (alkali). Pearl: NEVER induce vomiting or use charcoal for corrosives. Vomiting re-exposes the oesophagus to the burn and increases the risk of aspiration. Management is supportive (NBM, endoscopy).
* Viva Question: A 2-year-old is brought in 2 hours after swallowing a handful of his mother's Paracetamol tablets. He looks completely well. What is your immediate management? Answer: Do not be reassured by his well appearance. Check a serum Paracetamol level at 4 hours post-ingestion, plot it on the nomogram, and start N-acetylcysteine (NAC) if it is above the treatment line to prevent fatal liver failure.
* Pitfall: Administering Flumazenil routinely for Benzodiazepine overdose. Flumazenil lowers the seizure threshold and can precipitate intractable seizures, especially in mixed overdoses (e.g., TCAs).
* Viva Question: What are the clinical signs of an organophosphate overdose? Answer: DUMBELS (Diarrhoea, Urination, Miosis, Bradycardia, Emesis, Lacrimation, Salivation) due to cholinergic excess.
* Trap: Reassuring parents because a toddler who ingested iron tablets only has mild gastroenteritis symptoms initially. Pearl: Iron toxicity has a deceptive latent phase (6-24 hrs) where GI symptoms resolve, before progressing abruptly to shock, metabolic acidosis, and hepatic failure.
