# Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH) (發育性髖關節發育不良)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A spectrum of disorders affecting the proximal femur and acetabulum that leads to hip subluxation and dislocation. It ranges from mild acetabular dysplasia to a completely dislocated hip joint.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Incidence in Hong Kong is approximately 1-2 per 1000 live births. It is significantly more common in females (4:1 ratio). Routine screening is conducted by the Maternal and Child Health Centres (MCHC) and paediatricians at birth and subsequent visits.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Pathophysiology**: The hip joint is a ball-and-socket joint. In DDH, the acetabulum (socket) is shallow, allowing the femoral head (ball) to slip out partially (subluxation) or completely (dislocation). If left unreduced, the acetabulum fails to develop properly, leading to permanent deformity and early osteoarthritis.
- **Risk Factors (The 6 F's)**:
  - **F**emale sex (due to increased sensitivity to maternal relaxin hormone).
  - **F**amily history (genetic predisposition).
  - **F**irstborn (tighter uterine environment).
  - **F**rank breech presentation (the most significant risk factor).
  - **F**luid (Oligohydramnios - restricts fetal movement).
  - **F**oot abnormalities (e.g., clubfoot/talipes, packaging disorders).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Usually asymptomatic in neonates and infants. It is typically detected during routine clinical screening examinations.
- If missed early, a toddler may present with a painless limp, a waddling gait (if bilateral), or delayed walking.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Neonates (0-3 months)**:
  - **Barlow Test**: Attempts to *dislocate* an unstable hip. (Adduct the hip and apply posterior pressure). A palpable "clunk" indicates the hip has slipped out of the acetabulum.
  - **Ortolani Test**: Attempts to *reduce* a dislocated hip. (Abduct the hip and apply anterior pressure). A palpable "clunk" indicates the hip has slipped back into the acetabulum.
  - *Note: Clicks are common and usually benign (ligamentous snapping); a "clunk" is pathological.*
- **Older Infants (>3 months)**:
  - The Barlow and Ortolani tests become negative as the soft tissues tighten.
  - **Limited Hip Abduction**: The most reliable sign in older infants. The affected hip cannot be abducted fully when flexed to 90 degrees.
  - **Galeazzi Sign**: With the infant supine and knees flexed, the knee on the dislocated side is lower because the femoral head lies posterior to the acetabulum.
  - **Asymmetrical Skin Folds**: Asymmetry of the gluteal or thigh creases (a soft sign, common in normal infants too).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Septic Arthritis of the Hip**: Presents acutely with severe pain, fever, refusal to move the leg (pseudoparalysis), and the hip is held in flexion, abduction, and external rotation. DDH is painless in infancy.
- **Transient Synovitis**: Occurs in older children (3-8 years), usually after a viral illness, causing a painful limp.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Septic arthritis.
- Transient synovitis.
- Perthes disease (Legg-Calvé-Perthes).
- Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE).
- Neuromuscular disorders (e.g., Cerebral Palsy causing spastic hip subluxation).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Ultrasound of the Hips**: The imaging modality of choice for infants **< 4-6 months of age**. The femoral head is mostly cartilaginous and not visible on X-ray. Ultrasound assesses the depth of the acetabulum (alpha angle) and the stability of the femoral head.
  - *Screening*: Routine ultrasound is recommended at 6 weeks for infants with significant risk factors (e.g., breech presentation, positive family history), even if the clinical exam is normal.
- **X-ray of the Pelvis (AP view)**: The imaging modality of choice for infants **> 4-6 months of age**, once the ossification centre of the femoral head appears.
  - Look for a shallow acetabular index, lateral and superior displacement of the femoral head, and delayed ossification.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Goal**: To achieve and maintain concentric reduction of the femoral head within the acetabulum to stimulate normal joint development.
- **Age < 6 months**:
  - **Pavlik Harness**: The first-line treatment. A dynamic splint that holds the hips in flexion and abduction, keeping the femoral head reduced while allowing some movement. Usually worn 24/7 for 6-12 weeks. Success rate is >90%.
- **Age 6-18 months (or failed Pavlik)**:
  - **Closed Reduction and Spica Cast**: Performed under general anaesthesia with an arthrogram to confirm reduction. The child is immobilized in a plaster spica cast for several months.
- **Age > 18 months**:
  - **Open Reduction**: Surgical clearance of obstacles preventing reduction (e.g., inverted limbus, tight psoas tendon), often combined with a pelvic osteotomy (to reshape the acetabulum) or femoral osteotomy. Followed by spica casting.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Paediatric Orthopaedics**: For specialist management and follow-up.
- **MCHC**: Crucial role in the universal clinical screening programme in Hong Kong.
- **Nursing/Physiotherapy**: Educating parents on how to care for a baby in a Pavlik harness or spica cast (nappy changing, positioning, car seats).

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- N/A for this condition.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Avascular Necrosis (AVN) of the Femoral Head**: The most devastating iatrogenic complication. Caused by excessive forced abduction in a harness or cast, which compresses the fragile blood supply to the femoral head.
- **Early Osteoarthritis**: If the hip remains dysplastic or subluxated, premature wear and tear leads to severe osteoarthritis in young adulthood, requiring total hip replacement.
- **Prognosis**: Excellent if diagnosed and treated early (in the first few months of life). Poor if diagnosed late (after walking age).

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "Why do we use Ultrasound instead of X-ray to screen for DDH in a 6-week-old infant?" (Because the femoral head and much of the acetabulum are entirely cartilaginous at this age and are radiolucent on X-ray. The ossification centre of the femoral head does not appear until 4-6 months of age).
- *Viva*: "What is the most reliable clinical sign of DDH in a 9-month-old infant?" (Limited abduction of the flexed hip. The Barlow and Ortolani tests are no longer useful at this age).
- *Pitfall*: Ignoring a "clunk" on newborn examination or failing to arrange an ultrasound for a baby born via breech presentation.
