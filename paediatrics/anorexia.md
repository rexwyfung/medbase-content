# Anorexia in Childhood (Poor Appetite / Picky Eating) (兒童厭食 / 偏食)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A prolonged decrease or loss of appetite, or extreme selectivity in food choices (picky eating), leading to parental concern. *Note: This section covers general paediatric poor appetite, NOT the psychiatric eating disorder Anorexia Nervosa (covered separately).*
- **Local Epidemiology**: One of the most common presenting complaints in Hong Kong general paediatric and MCHC clinics. Often driven by intense parental anxiety regarding growth centiles and academic performance.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Physiological Anorexia**: Around 12-18 months of age, a toddler's growth rate naturally slows down significantly compared to their first year of life. Consequently, their caloric requirement and appetite decrease. This is a normal physiological stage, but often alarms parents.
- **Organic Causes**: Chronic illness (e.g., congenital heart disease, chronic kidney disease), gastrointestinal disorders (Coeliac disease, GORD, constipation), iron deficiency anaemia, or chronic infections.
- **Psychosocial/Behavioral Causes**: Coercive feeding practices (force-feeding), grazing (snacking constantly so the child is never hungry for meals), excessive milk intake (displacing solid foods), or sensory processing issues (aversion to certain textures/colors).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Parents complain that the child "eats nothing," "holds food in their mouth," or only eats 2-3 specific foods (e.g., only white rice and chicken nuggets).
- The consultation is often highly emotionally charged.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **The History is paramount**:
  - *Dietary Recall*: A 3-day food diary is essential. Often reveals that the child is actually consuming adequate calories through milk, juice, or constant snacking.
  - *Feeding Environment*: Are meals eaten at a table or chasing the child around with an iPad? Is there force-feeding or punishment?
  - *Red Flags*: Vomiting, chronic diarrhoea, blood in stool, developmental delay, recurrent infections.
- **Physical Examination**:
  - **Growth Charts (The most important objective tool)**: Plotting weight, height, and head circumference. If the child is tracking along their centiles (even if it's the 10th centile), they are getting enough calories.
  - Look for signs of organic disease: Pallor (anaemia), clubbing, murmurs, abdominal distension.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Faltering Growth (Failure to Thrive)**: The child's weight is crossing centiles downwards or is significantly below the 3rd centile. This indicates a true caloric deficit or organic disease, unlike physiological anorexia where the child maintains their centile.
- **Anorexia Nervosa**: An intense fear of gaining weight and a distorted body image, typically presenting in adolescence, not toddlerhood.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Physiological anorexia of toddlerhood (most common).
- Behavioral/Picky eating.
- Iron Deficiency Anaemia (poor appetite is a symptom).
- Constipation (a full rectum reduces appetite).
- Coeliac Disease.
- Chronic tonsillar hypertrophy (makes swallowing difficult).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **No investigations are needed** if the child is thriving (growing along their centiles), has a normal physical examination, and the history suggests behavioral/physiological anorexia.
- **Bloods (If red flags or faltering growth are present)**:
  - FBC and Iron Studies (to rule out Iron Deficiency Anaemia).
  - Coeliac screen (Anti-TTG IgA).
  - Thyroid Function Tests.
  - U&E, Creatinine, LFTs, CRP.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Reassurance and Education**: The cornerstone of management for physiological/behavioral anorexia. Show the parents the growth chart to prove the child is growing normally.
- **Behavioral Modification (The "Division of Responsibility" model)**:
  - *Parent's job*: Decide WHAT food is offered, WHEN it is offered, and WHERE it is eaten (at a table, no screens).
  - *Child's job*: Decide WHETHER to eat and HOW MUCH to eat.
  - Stop all force-feeding, bribing, or punishing.
  - Limit milk intake to < 500ml/day for toddlers.
  - Eliminate "grazing" (no snacks/juice within 2 hours of a main meal).
  - Limit meal times to 20-30 minutes. If the child hasn't eaten, calmly remove the plate without comment.
- **Treat Underlying Organic Causes**: e.g., Iron supplementation for anaemia, laxatives for constipation.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Dietitian**: For detailed nutritional assessment and practical advice on introducing new foods (food chaining).
- **Speech Therapist**: If there are oral-motor difficulties or severe sensory aversions to textures.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Severe faltering growth due to inadequate food provision or extreme, rigid dietary restrictions imposed by parents (e.g., strict vegan diets without supplementation leading to B12/iron deficiency) can constitute neglect or abuse.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Complications**: Nutritional deficiencies (Iron, Vitamin D, Zinc) if the diet is extremely restricted. Psychological trauma around mealtimes.
- **Prognosis**: Excellent for behavioral picky eating, provided parents can successfully implement behavioral changes and reduce their own anxiety.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "A mother is distraught because her 18-month-old 'eats like a bird' compared to when he was 9 months old. His weight is tracking perfectly along the 25th centile. What is the most likely explanation?" (Physiological anorexia of toddlerhood. Growth velocity slows significantly after the first year, leading to a natural and appropriate decrease in appetite).
- *Viva*: "What is the 'Division of Responsibility' in feeding?" (The parent is responsible for what, when, and where the child eats; the child is responsible for whether and how much they eat).
- *Pitfall*: Ordering a massive battery of blood tests for a picky eater who is perfectly healthy and growing along their centiles, instead of addressing the parental anxiety and feeding behaviors.
