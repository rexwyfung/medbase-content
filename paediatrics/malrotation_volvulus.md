# Malrotation with Volvulus (腸旋轉不良伴腸扭轉)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Malrotation is a failure of normal embryonic midgut rotation and fixation. The resulting narrow mesenteric base predisposes the bowel to twist upon itself (Volvulus), cutting off its arterial supply (SMA).
- **Local Epidemiology**: Most devastating pediatric surgical emergency. Incidence is ~1 in 500.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Normally, the bowel rotates 270 degrees counter-clockwise around the Superior Mesenteric Artery (SMA), fixing via a broad mesentery. In malrotation:
  - The Duodenojejunal (DJ) flexure is displaced downwards and rightwards.
  - The caecum is mobile and improperly located (usually high).
  - Ladd's bands (fibrous bands) run across the duodenum to the right colon, causing extrinsic duodenal obstruction.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Over 50% present in the first month of life (classic is day 2-3).
- **Bilious vomiting** in a previously healthy, full-term infant.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Early**: Sudden onset green (bilious) vomiting, inconsolable crying (colicky pain). The abdomen may initially be soft and non-distended.
- **Late**: Intractable shock, blood-stained stools, abdominal rigidity and erythema. This implies established bowel necrosis.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Must be assumed immediately in ANY neonate with bilious vomiting until definitively excluded.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Duodenal / Jejunal / Ileal Atresia
- Meconium Ileus
- Hirschsprung's disease

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Gold Standard Imaging**: **Upper GI Contrast Study**.
  - Findings: DJ flexure is on the right of the spine and lies inferior to the duodenal bulb. Classic "corkscrew" appearance of the twisted small bowel loops.
- **Bedside**: VBG (Severe metabolic acidosis / high lactate indicates bowel ischemia).
- **Emergency US**: "Whirlpool sign" (SMA and SMV twisted) – useful but operator dependent. UGI contrast is still king.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **HA Protocol**: Immediate NBM, large-bore IV access, 20ml/kg normal saline boluses, IV broad-spectrum antibiotics (Cefotaxime/Metronidazole), pass NG tube.
- **Surgery**: Emergency **Ladd's Procedure**:
  - Untwisting the volvulus (counter-clockwise).
  - Division of Ladd's bands.
  - Widening the mesenteric base.
  - Placing the small bowel on the right and large bowel on the left (non-anatomical but safe).
  - Prophylactic appendicectomy (due to final left-sided placement of caecum).

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Immediate MSW intervention required for families facing potential loss of an infant or catastrophic lifestyle changes due to short bowel syndrome.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Be vigilant for delayed presentation leading to extensive bowel death. Ensure meticulous safety netting for previously discharged infants returning with vomiting.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Ordering an AXR, seeing a normal gas pattern, and reassuring the parents. Volvulus can have a completely NORMAL plain AXR. Never delay an Upper GI contrast.
- **Viva Q**: "Why is an appendicectomy performed during a Ladd's Procedure?" Answer: Because the final surgical placement puts the caecum (and appendix) in the left upper quadrant. A future appendicitis would present with highly atypical signs, confusing doctors.
