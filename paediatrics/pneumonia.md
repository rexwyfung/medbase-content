
# Paediatric Pneumonia (小兒肺炎)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Infection and inflammation of the lung parenchyma (alveoli and bronchioles).
- **Local Epidemiology**: A leading cause of paediatric admission in HK.
  - *Viral*: Most common cause overall, especially in < 5 years (RSV, Influenza A/B, Parainfluenza, Adenovirus).
  - *Bacterial*: Streptococcus pneumoniae (most common typical), Mycoplasma pneumoniae (very common in school-aged children in HK, causing "walking pneumonia"), Group A Strep, Staph aureus.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Pathogens reach the lower respiratory tract via inhalation or aspiration.
- Inflammatory response leads to alveolar exudate, consolidation, and impaired gas exchange (V/Q mismatch -> hypoxia).
- **Mycoplasma**: An atypical bacterium lacking a cell wall. Causes a more indolent, interstitial inflammation.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- See below.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Symptoms**: Fever, cough (productive or dry), tachypnoea, increased work of breathing, lethargy, poor feeding (in infants).
- **Signs**:
  - Tachypnoea (the most sensitive sign in young children).
  - Respiratory distress (recessions, nasal flaring, grunting).
  - **Auscultation**: Focal crackles (crepitations), bronchial breathing, dullness to percussion (indicates consolidation or effusion).
## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Bronchiolitis**: Usually < 1 year old, presents with widespread wheeze and fine crackles, hyperinflated chest. Pneumonia is typically focal.
- **Asthma**: Presents with wheeze and responds to bronchodilators. Pneumonia crackles do not clear with Salbutamol.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Most Likely**:
  - Viral Pneumonia (infants/toddlers).
  - Mycoplasma Pneumonia (school-aged).
  - Strep pneumoniae (any age, classic lobar pneumonia).
- **Must-Not-Miss**:
  - **Complicated Pneumonia**: Empyema (pus in the pleural space), lung abscess, necrotising pneumonia (often Staph aureus or severe Pneumococcus).
  - Sepsis.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**:
  - **Pulse Oximetry**: Essential to assess severity.
  - **Chest X-ray (CXR)**: Not routinely needed for mild, uncomplicated community-acquired pneumonia treated as an outpatient. Indicated if admitted, severe distress, or suspecting complications.
  - *Typical*: Lobar consolidation.
  - *Atypical/Viral*: Bilateral perihilar infiltrates.
  - **Nasopharyngeal Aspirate (NPA) / Swab**: For respiratory virus multiplex PCR (RSV, Flu, Adenovirus, Mycoplasma). Very useful in HK HA hospitals for cohorting patients and guiding therapy.
- **Gold Standard**:
  - **Blood Culture**: If the child is toxic, febrile, or admitted to ICU.
  - **Pleural Fluid Analysis**: If a significant pleural effusion is present (tap to differentiate simple parapneumonic effusion from empyema).
- **Monitoring**:
  - Monitor SpO2, respiratory rate, and work of breathing.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**:
  - **Oxygen**: If SpO2 < 92%.
  - **IV Fluids**: If unable to maintain oral intake due to respiratory distress.
- **Chronic/Definitive**:
  - **Mild (Outpatient)**:
    - *Typical*: Oral Amoxicillin (high dose to cover resistant Pneumococcus).
    - *Atypical (School-aged)*: Oral Macrolide (e.g., Azithromycin or Clarithromycin) to cover Mycoplasma.
    - **Severe (Inpatient)**:
    - IV antibiotics (e.g., IV Ampicillin/Penicillin G, or IV Cefotaxime/Ceftriaxone if not fully immunised or severe). Add IV Macrolide if Mycoplasma suspected.
  - **Complicated (Empyema)**:
    - IV antibiotics (e.g., Ceftriaxone + Clindamycin to cover Staph aureus).
    - **Chest Drain**: Insertion of a pigtail catheter.
    - **Intrapleural Fibrinolytics (Urokinase)**: Often used in HK to break down loculations in empyema, avoiding the need for surgery.
    - **VATS (Video-Assisted Thoracoscopic Surgery)**: If fibrinolytics fail.
## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Physiotherapy is generally NOT helpful for uncomplicated pneumonia, but may be used for resolving empyema or atelectasis.
## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Advise parents that the cough may persist for weeks after the infection has cleared.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Excellent prognosis for uncomplicated pneumonia.
- Complications: Pleural effusion, empyema, lung abscess, pneumatocele, sepsis.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Prescribing Amoxicillin for a 10-year-old with a persistent dry cough and bilateral infiltrates. *Pearl*: Mycoplasma is the most likely cause in this age group in HK. Mycoplasma lacks a cell wall, so beta-lactams (Amoxicillin) are useless. You must prescribe a Macrolide (Azithromycin).
- **Viva Question**: "What is the most sensitive clinical sign of pneumonia in a young child?" *Answer*: Tachypnoea (raised respiratory rate).
