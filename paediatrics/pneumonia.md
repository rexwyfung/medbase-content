# Paediatric Pneumonia (小兒肺炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Paediatric Pneumonia
* Infection & inflammation of lung parenchyma (肺實質) (alveoli & bronchioles)
* Exudate consolidation (肺實變) → impaired gas exchange

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
* Leading cause of paediatric admission in HK Hospital Authority (HA) hospitals
* Highly seasonal: ↑ winter/spring (viral peaks), ↑ summer (parainfluenza)

### Common Viral Pathogens
* Most common overall cause, especially in children < 5 years
* **Respiratory Syncytial Virus (RSV)** (呼吸道合胞病毒)
* **Influenza A/B** (甲型/乙型流感)
* Parainfluenza
* Adenovirus

### Common Bacterial Pathogens
* **Streptococcus pneumoniae** (肺炎鏈球菌): Most common typical bacterial cause (all ages)
* **Mycoplasma pneumoniae** (肺炎支原體): Endemic & highly prevalent in HK school-aged children (>5 yrs); causes "walking pneumonia"
* Group A Streptococcus
* Staphylococcus aureus (金黃色葡萄球菌): Suspect in severe/necrotising cases

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics

### Typical Bacterial Pneumonia Pathophysiology
* Pathogen inhalation/aspiration → lower respiratory tract colonization
* Robust inflammatory cascade → neutrophil infiltration → alveolar exudate + consolidation (肺實變)
* Result: V/Q mismatch → intrapulmonary shunting → **hypoxia** (缺氧)

### Atypical (Mycoplasma) Pathophysiology
* *M. pneumoniae* lacks cell wall → inherently resistant to β-lactams (e.g., Penicillins)
* Attaches to respiratory epithelium via P1 adhesin
* Triggers indolent, primarily interstitial inflammation (間質性發炎) rather than dense alveolar exudate
* Often triggers extrapulmonary immune-mediated phenomena (e.g., erythema multiforme, cold agglutinin haemolysis)

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Key Symptoms of Paediatric Pneumonia
* **Fever** (發燒) (often high-grade in typical bacterial)
* **Cough** (咳嗽) (dry initially → productive of purulent sputum)
* Tachypnoea (氣促)
* ↑ Work of breathing
* Lethargy & irritability
* Poor feeding (especially in infants)
* Pleuritic chest pain / abdominal pain (lower lobe pneumonia referring to abdomen)

### General Signs of Respiratory Distress
* **Tachypnoea** (Most sensitive sign in young children!)
* Subcostal/intercostal recessions (凹陷)
* Nasal flaring (鼻翼扇動)
* Grunting (呻吟) (effort to maintain end-expiratory pressure)
* Cyanosis (發紺) (late/severe sign)

### Auscultatory Signs of Pneumonia
* Focal **crackles / crepitations** (喀喇音)
* Bronchial breathing (支氣管呼吸音) (over consolidated areas)
* ↓ Air entry
* Dullness to percussion (叩診濁音) (indicates consolidation or pleural effusion)
* ↑ Vocal resonance

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)

### Pneumonia vs Bronchiolitis
* **Age:** Bronchiolitis typically < 1 yr; Pneumonia any age
* **Auscultation:** Bronchiolitis = widespread bilateral wheeze + fine crackles; Pneumonia = focal crackles / consolidation
* **Chest Shape:** Bronchiolitis = hyperinflated; Pneumonia = normal shape, focal dullness

### Pneumonia vs Childhood Asthma
* **Auscultation:** Asthma = expiratory wheeze; Pneumonia = crackles/bronchial breathing
* **Reversibility:** Asthma responds to bronchodilators (Salbutamol); Pneumonia crackles DO NOT clear with bronchodilators
* **Systemic Signs:** Pneumonia typically presents with higher fever and toxic look

## 5. Differential Diagnosis

### Differential Diagnosis of Paediatric Respiratory Distress
| Category | Condition | Key Differentiating Features |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Most Likely (Infection)** | Viral Pneumonia | Age < 5, bilateral signs, coryza prodrome, low-grade fever |
| | Mycoplasma Pneumonia | School-aged, indolent dry cough, prominent extrapulmonary signs |
| | Typical Bacterial Pneumonia | Sudden onset, high fever, toxic look, focal lobar signs |
| **Must-Not-Miss (Complications)** | Empyema (膿胸) | Persistent fever despite Abx, swinging fever, stony dull percussion |
| | Necrotising Pneumonia | Severe toxicity, haemoptysis, prominent radiolucent cysts on CXR |
| **Anatomical/Other** | Foreign Body Aspiration | Sudden choking episode, unilateral wheeze, asymmetric expansion |
| | Heart Failure | Hepatomegaly, bilateral basal crackles, heart murmur |

## 6. Tiered Investigations

### Bedside Investigations for Pneumonia
* **Pulse Oximetry (SpO2):** Essential to assess severity/hypoxia
* Vital signs monitoring (HR, RR, Temp)

### Laboratory Blood Investigations
* **CBC:** ↑ WBC (neutrophilia in bacterial, lymphocytosis in viral)
* **CRP / Procalcitonin:** ↑ markedly in typical bacterial (>100 mg/L)
* **Blood Cultures:** Gold standard for bacteremia; take IF toxic, severe, or admitted to ICU (often -ve)
* **Mycoplasma Serology:** (IgM/IgG) if suspected, though NPA PCR is faster

### Microbiological Swabs & Aspirates
* **Nasopharyngeal Aspirate (NPA) / Swab Multiplex PCR:** * *Crucial in HK HA hospitals* for cohorting in wards
* Rapid detection of RSV, Influenza A/B, Adenovirus, Parainfluenza, *Mycoplasma*

### Imaging & Special Investigations
* **Chest X-ray (CXR):** * *NOT* routine for mild, uncomplicated community-acquired outpatient cases
* *Indicated if:* Admitted, severe distress, uncertain diagnosis, or suspecting complications (effusion)
* *Typical findings:* Lobar consolidation (bacterial), bilateral perihilar infiltrates (viral/atypical)
* **Pleural Fluid Analysis:** Tap if significant effusion to differentiate simple parapneumonic effusion vs empyema (膿胸)
* **Chest Ultrasound:** Highly sensitive for detecting & quantifying pleural fluid/loculations

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)

### Acute Resuscitation & Supportive Care
* **Oxygen Therapy:** If SpO2 < 92% (nasal cannula or high-flow nasal cannula)
* **Fluid Management:** IV fluids if poor oral intake or severe respiratory distress (avoid fluid overload → exacerbates pulmonary edema)
* **Antipyretics:** Paracetamol / Ibuprofen for fever and pleuritic pain

### Pharmacological Management Protocols (HK HA Focus)
| Severity / Type | First-Line Antibiotic Choice (HK Context) | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Mild / Outpatient (Typical)** | PO **Amoxicillin** (high dose: 80-90 mg/kg/day) | Overcomes intermediate penicillin-resistant *Strep. pneumoniae* |
| **Mild / Outpatient (Atypical)** | PO **Azithromycin** or **Clarithromycin** | Macrolides required as *Mycoplasma* lacks a cell wall |
| **Severe / Inpatient (Infant/Toddler)** | IV **Ampicillin** / Penicillin G (+/- IV Cefotaxime) | Broad typical coverage; escalate to 3rd Gen Ceph if unimmunised |
| **Severe / Inpatient (School-Aged)** | IV **Ampicillin** + IV **Macrolide** | Dual therapy to cover *Pneumococcus* + *Mycoplasma* |

### Complicated Pneumonia (Empyema) Management
* IV Antibiotics: Escalate to IV **Ceftriaxone** + **Clindamycin** (covers *Staph aureus* & anaerobes)
* **Chest Drain (Pigtail Catheter):** Essential for pus drainage
* **Intrapleural Fibrinolytics:** Intrapleural **Urokinase** (尿激酶) standard in HK HA to break down fibrinous loculations, highly effective at avoiding surgery
* **VATS (Video-Assisted Thoracoscopic Surgery):** Indicated if Urokinase fails or extensive organized peel forms

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care

### Allied Health & MDT Involvement
* **Physiotherapy:** *NOT* routinely helpful for uncomplicated pneumonia. Indicated for resolving empyema, atelectasis, or underlying neuromuscular weakness (to aid sputum clearance)
* **Medical Social Worker (MSW):** Assess if parents struggle with childcare during prolonged hospital stay or medication costs

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags

### Red Flags for Underlying Neglect or Abuse
* **Delayed Presentation:** Parents waiting several days while child is in severe respiratory distress
* **Recurrent Aspiration Pneumonia:** May indicate severe neglect, unsafe feeding practices, or unmanaged underlying cerebral palsy / neurological dysphagia
* **Non-Compliance:** Failure to complete oral antibiotic courses leading to recurrent admissions

## 10. Complications & Prognosis

### Prognosis of Paediatric Pneumonia
* Excellent for mild, uncomplicated community-acquired pneumonia
* Cough may persist for 2-4 weeks post-infection (advise parents to reassure)

### Major Pulmonary Complications
* **Parapneumonic Effusion:** Sterile fluid in pleural space
* **Empyema (膿胸):** Pus in pleural space, loculations
* **Necrotising Pneumonia:** Destruction of lung parenchyma, often *Pneumococcal* or *Staph aureus*
* **Lung Abscess (肺膿腫):** Cavitary lesion containing pus
* **Pneumatocele (肺氣腔):** Thin-walled air-filled cysts

### Major Systemic Complications
* Sepsis / Septic Shock
* SIADH (Syndrome of Inappropriate Antidiuretic Hormone) → hyponatraemia
* HUS (Haemolytic Uraemic Syndrome) secondary to *S. pneumoniae*

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Trap: Prescribing Amoxicillin for a 10-year-old with a persistent dry cough and bilateral infiltrates.
* Pearl: *Mycoplasma* is the most likely cause in this age group in HK. *Mycoplasma* lacks a cell wall, so beta-lactams (Amoxicillin) are useless. You must prescribe a Macrolide (Azithromycin/Clarithromycin).
* Viva Question: What is the most sensitive clinical sign of pneumonia in a young child?
* Answer: Tachypnoea (raised respiratory rate).
* Pitfall: Ordering a routine CXR for a well-looking 3-year-old with mild focal crackles who is tolerating oral intake. Avoid unnecessary radiation; CXR is not routinely indicated for mild, uncomplicated outpatient pneumonia.
* Trap: Assuming right lower quadrant abdominal pain is always appendicitis in a febrile child.
* Pearl: Right lower lobe pneumonia can irritate the diaphragm and refer pain to the abdomen. Always check respiratory rate and auscultate the lung bases!
* Viva Question: How do you medically manage an empyema with loculations in a Hong Kong public hospital?
* Answer: Insertion of a chest drain combined with intrapleural fibrinolytics (Urokinase) to break down the loculations, alongside IV antibiotics (e.g., Ceftriaxone + Clindamycin).
