# Acute Otitis Media (AOM) (急性中耳炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: An acute inflammation and infection of the middle ear space, characterized by the presence of middle ear effusion and rapid onset of signs and symptoms of middle ear inflammation.
- **Local Epidemiology**: One of the most common paediatric infections in Hong Kong. Peak incidence is between 6 and 24 months of age. Most children will have at least one episode before school age.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Pathophysiology**: 
  - Usually preceded by a viral upper respiratory tract infection (URTI).
  - The viral infection causes inflammation and oedema of the respiratory mucosa, including the Eustachian tube.
  - The Eustachian tube becomes blocked, leading to negative pressure in the middle ear and accumulation of sterile fluid.
  - Bacteria from the nasopharynx ascend the blocked Eustachian tube, colonize the fluid, and cause a purulent infection.
- **Anatomy**: Infants are more susceptible because their Eustachian tubes are shorter, wider, and more horizontal than in adults, making it easier for bacteria to enter and harder for fluid to drain.
- **Causative Organisms**:
  - **Streptococcus pneumoniae** (most common).
  - **Haemophilus influenzae** (non-typeable).
  - **Moraxella catarrhalis**.
  - Viruses (RSV, Rhinovirus) can cause AOM alone or as a co-infection.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- A young child (often 1-2 years old) who has had a runny nose and cough for a few days suddenly develops a high fever, becomes very irritable, and cries inconsolably, especially at night.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Symptoms**: Ear pain (otalgia) - infants may pull or tug at their ears, fever, irritability, poor feeding, vomiting.
- **Otoscopy Findings (The Key to Diagnosis)**:
  - **Bulging Tympanic Membrane (TM)**: The hallmark sign of AOM. The eardrum is pushed outward by pus under pressure.
  - **Erythema**: The TM is intensely red and inflamed.
  - **Loss of Landmarks**: The handle of the malleus and the light reflex are obscured.
  - **Otorrhoea**: Purulent discharge in the ear canal if the tympanic membrane has ruptured (this often relieves the severe pain).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Otitis Media with Effusion (OME / "Glue Ear")**: Fluid is present in the middle ear, but there are NO signs of acute inflammation (no fever, no severe pain, TM is not bulging or red). Often follows an episode of AOM.
- **Otitis Externa (Swimmer's Ear)**: Infection of the ear canal. Severe pain when pulling the pinna or pressing the tragus. The ear canal is swollen and erythematous, but the TM (if visible) is usually normal.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Otitis Media with Effusion (OME).
- Otitis Externa.
- Referred pain (from teething, tonsillitis, or mumps).
- Mastoiditis (a complication of AOM).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Diagnosis is entirely clinical** based on otoscopy.
- **Tympanometry**: Can confirm the presence of middle ear fluid (shows a flat Type B curve), but is not routinely needed for acute diagnosis.
- **Swab**: Only useful if the TM has ruptured and there is purulent discharge in the ear canal.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Analgesia**: The most important initial step. Paracetamol or Ibuprofen for pain and fever.
- **Antibiotic Strategy (HA Guidelines)**:
  - **"Watchful Waiting" (Observation)**: Recommended for mild cases in children > 6 months old without severe symptoms. Provide a "back-up" prescription to use if symptoms do not improve after 48-72 hours.
  - **Immediate Antibiotics**: Indicated for:
    - Infants < 6 months old.
    - Severe symptoms (toxic appearance, severe otalgia, fever > 39°C).
    - Bilateral AOM in children < 2 years old.
    - Otorrhoea (ruptured TM).
  - **First-line Antibiotic**: Oral **Amoxicillin** (high dose).
  - **Second-line (if no improvement in 48-72 hrs or recent amoxicillin use)**: Oral **Amoxicillin-Clavulanate (Augmentin)** to cover beta-lactamase producing H. influenzae and M. catarrhalis.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Prevention**: Breastfeeding (protective), avoiding secondhand smoke, and ensuring up-to-date vaccinations (Pneumococcal Conjugate Vaccine - PCV, and annual Influenza vaccine).

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Intratemporal Complications**:
  - **Mastoiditis**: Infection spreads to the mastoid air cells. Presents with post-auricular swelling, erythema, tenderness, and the pinna is pushed forward and outward. Requires urgent IV antibiotics and often surgical drainage.
  - TM perforation (usually heals spontaneously).
  - Hearing loss (conductive, usually temporary).
- **Intracranial Complications (Rare but life-threatening)**: Meningitis, brain abscess, cavernous sinus thrombosis.
- **Prognosis**: Excellent. Most cases resolve without complications.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "What is the most reliable otoscopic finding to diagnose Acute Otitis Media?" (A bulging tympanic membrane. Erythema alone is not sufficient, as a crying child can have a red eardrum).
- *Viva*: "A 1-year-old child with AOM is prescribed Amoxicillin but returns 3 days later with persistent high fever and a bulging TM. What is the most likely reason, and what is the next step?" (The infection is likely caused by a beta-lactamase producing organism, such as Haemophilus influenzae or Moraxella catarrhalis. The next step is to switch to Amoxicillin-Clavulanate / Augmentin).
- *Pitfall*: Prescribing antibiotics for every red eardrum. Many are viral or simply red from crying. Look for the bulging TM and loss of landmarks.
