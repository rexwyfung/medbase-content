# Acute Otitis Media (AOM) (急性中耳炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition
**Definition**: An acute inflammation and infection of the middle ear space, characterized by the presence of middle ear effusion and rapid onset of signs and symptoms of middle ear inflammation.

### Local Epidemiology
* One of the most common paediatric infections in Hong Kong.
* **Peak incidence**: **6 to 12 months** of age (up to 24 months).
* Most children will have at least one episode before school age, and up to **20%** will have **≥3 episodes**.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Pathophysiology
* Usually preceded by a **viral upper respiratory tract infection (URTI)**.
* Viral infection → inflammation and oedema of the respiratory mucosa, including the **Eustachian tube**.
* Eustachian tube becomes blocked → negative pressure in the middle ear → accumulation of sterile fluid.
* Bacteria from the nasopharynx ascend the blocked Eustachian tube, colonize the fluid, and cause a purulent infection.

### Anatomical Susceptibility
* Infants are more susceptible because their Eustachian tubes are **shorter, wider, and more horizontal** than in adults → easier for bacteria to enter and harder for fluid to drain.

### Causative Organisms
* **Streptococcus pneumoniae (肺炎鏈球菌)** (most common).
* **Haemophilus influenzae (流感嗜血桿菌)** (non-typeable).
* **Moraxella catarrhalis (卡他莫拉菌)**.
* **Viruses** (**RSV, Rhinovirus**) can cause AOM alone or as a co-infection.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Clinical Presentation
* A young child (often 1-2 years old) who has had a runny nose and cough for a few days suddenly develops a **high fever**, becomes very **irritable**, and **cries inconsolably**, especially at night.

### Symptoms
* **Ear pain (otalgia) (耳痛)** - infants may pull or tug at their ears.
* **Fever**.
* **Irritability**.
* **Poor feeding**.
* **Vomiting**.

### Otoscopy Findings (The Key to Diagnosis)
Every child with a fever should have their tympanic membranes examined.
* **Bulging Tympanic Membrane (TM) (鼓膜膨出)**: The hallmark sign of AOM. The eardrum is pushed outward by pus under pressure.
* **Erythema (紅斑)**: The TM is intensely red and inflamed.
* **Loss of Landmarks**: The handle of the malleus and the light reflex are obscured.
* **Otorrhoea (耳漏)**: Purulent discharge in the ear canal if the TM has ruptured (this often relieves the severe pain).

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Otitis Media with Effusion (OME / "Glue Ear") (膠耳)
* Fluid is present in the middle ear, but **NO signs of acute inflammation** (no fever, no severe pain, TM is dull/retracted, often with a visible fluid level, not bulging or red).
* Often follows an episode of AOM. Very common between 2-7 years of age.

### Otitis Externa (Swimmer's Ear) (外耳炎)
* Infection of the ear canal.
* Severe pain when pulling the pinna or pressing the tragus.
* Ear canal is swollen and erythematous, but the TM (if visible) is usually normal.

## 5. Differential Diagnosis
### Common Differentials Summary
| Condition | Key Differentiating Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Acute Otitis Media (AOM)** | Bulging, red TM, fever, severe pain, loss of landmarks. |
| **Otitis Media with Effusion (OME)** | Dull/retracted TM, visible fluid level, NO acute inflammation. |
| **Otitis Externa** | Swollen/red ear canal, severe pain on pulling pinna/tragus. |
| **Mastoiditis** | Post-auricular swelling/erythema, pinna pushed forward/outward. |
| **Referred Pain** | Normal ear exam; look for teething, tonsillitis, or mumps. |

## 6. Tiered Investigations
### Bedside Investigations
* Diagnosis is entirely **clinical** based on otoscopy.

### Special Investigations
* **Tympanometry**: Can confirm the presence of middle ear fluid (shows a flat Type B curve), but is not routinely needed for acute diagnosis.
* **Ear Swab**: Only useful if the TM has ruptured and there is purulent discharge in the ear canal.

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Acute Analgesia
* The most important initial step. **Paracetamol** or **Ibuprofen** for pain and fever (may be required for up to a week). Neither decongestants nor antihistamines are beneficial.

### Antibiotic Strategy (HA Guidelines)
* AOM usually resolves spontaneously. Antibiotics marginally shorten the duration of pain but do not reduce hearing loss.

| Indication | Management Strategy | Pharmacological Agent |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Mild cases in children > 6 months** | **"Watchful Waiting" (Observation)** for 48-72h. | Provide a "back-up" Abx prescription if no improvement. |
| **Infants < 6 months**, **Severe symptoms** (toxic, severe otalgia, T>39°C), **Bilateral AOM < 2 years**, or **Otorrhoea** | **Immediate Antibiotics** | 1st Line: Oral **Amoxicillin** (high dose). |
| **No improvement in 48-72 hrs** or recent Amoxicillin use | **Second-Line Antibiotics** | Oral **Amoxicillin-Clavulanate (Augmentin)** to cover beta-lactamase producers. |

### Chronic Management (OME)
* **Tympanostomy tubes (grommets)**: May be considered with or without adenoidectomy if hearing does not improve and causes speech/learning impairment, but benefits often last <12 months.

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Prevention Strategies
* **Breastfeeding** (protective).
* Avoiding secondhand smoke.
* Ensuring up-to-date vaccinations (**Pneumococcal Conjugate Vaccine - PCV**, and annual **Influenza vaccine**).

### Allied Health Involvement
* **Audiology and Speech Therapy**: Indicated for children with recurrent AOM leading to persistent OME and conductive hearing loss affecting speech/language development.

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### NAI Considerations
* N/A for this condition.

## 10. Complications & Prognosis
### Intratemporal Complications
* **Mastoiditis (乳突炎)**: Infection spreads to the mastoid air cells. Presents with post-auricular swelling, erythema, tenderness, and the pinna is pushed forward and outward. Requires urgent IV antibiotics and often surgical drainage.
* **TM perforation** (usually heals spontaneously).
* **Hearing loss** (conductive, usually temporary).

### Intracranial Complications (Rare)
* **Meningitis (腦膜炎)**.
* **Brain abscess (腦膿腫)**.
* **Cavernous sinus thrombosis (海綿竇血栓形成)**.

### Prognosis
* **Excellent**. Most cases resolve spontaneously without complications.

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Viva Question: What is the most reliable otoscopic finding to diagnose Acute Otitis Media?
Answer: A bulging tympanic membrane. Erythema alone is not sufficient, as a crying child can have a red eardrum.
* Viva Question: A 1-year-old child with AOM is prescribed Amoxicillin but returns 3 days later with persistent high fever and a bulging TM. What is the most likely reason, and what is the next step?
Answer: The infection is likely caused by a beta-lactamase producing organism, such as Haemophilus influenzae or Moraxella catarrhalis. The next step is to switch to Amoxicillin-Clavulanate (Augmentin).
* Pitfall: Prescribing antibiotics for every red eardrum. Many are viral or simply red from crying. Look for the bulging TM and loss of landmarks.
* Trap: Assuming antibiotics reduce the risk of long-term hearing loss in AOM. 
Pearl: Antibiotics only marginally shorten the duration of pain; they have not been shown to reduce the risk of hearing loss.
