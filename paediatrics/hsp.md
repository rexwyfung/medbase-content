# Henoch-Schönlein Purpura (HSP) / IgA Vasculitis (過敏性紫癜)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of HSP
* **Most common systemic vasculitis** in childhood.
* Immune-mediated, small-vessel **IgA vasculitis** (IgA 血管炎).
* Characterized by **IgA immune complex** deposition in target organs.

### Hong Kong Epidemiology
* **Highly prevalent** in HK paediatric wards.
* **Peak seasons**: Autumn & Winter (correlates with viral URTI surges).
* **Peak age**: 3–10 years.
* **Gender**: ♂ Boys > ♀ Girls.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics

### Immunopathogenesis
* **Trigger**: Preceding URTI (1–3 weeks prior) commonly **Group A Streptococcus (GAS)**, **Adenovirus**, or **Parvovirus B19**.
* **Mechanism**: Abnormal **IgA1** antibody production → Forms circulating **immune complexes** → Deposition in walls of small blood vessels (**capillaries, venules, arterioles**).

### Target Organ Pathophysiology
* Vessel wall deposition → complement activation → **Inflammation & Necrosis** (Vasculitis).
* **Skin** → Purpura (紫癜)
* **Joints** → Arthritis (關節炎)
* **GI Tract** → Bowel ischaemia / Haemorrhage / Abdominal pain (腹痛)
* **Kidneys** → Mesangial IgA deposition / Nephritis (腎炎)

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### The Classic Clinical Tetrad
* Diagnosis requires **Palpable Purpura** + ≥1 of the other 3 features:
* **1. Palpable Purpura** (可觸及的紫癜) (**100%**)
* **2. Arthritis/Arthralgia** (關節炎) (**75%**)
* **3. Abdominal Pain** (腹痛) (**50%**)
* **4. Renal Involvement** (HSP Nephritis) (**40-50%**)

### Dermatological Signs (Skin)
* Evolution: Erythematous macules/urticarial wheals → rapid progression to **non-blanching palpable purpura**.
* **Distribution**: Symmetrical & **gravity-dependent**.
* Target sites: Buttocks, extensor surfaces of lower limbs, ankles.

### Musculoskeletal Signs (Joints)
* **Transient, migratory, non-destructive** inflammation.
* Targets **large joints** (knees, ankles).
* Often causes refusal to bear weight / limp.

### Gastrointestinal Signs (Bowel)
* Severe, colicky, periumbilical pain (due to bowel wall ischaemia/oedema).
* Associated with vomiting.
* **GI Bleeding**: Melaena (黑糞) or Haematochezia (便血).

### Renal Signs (HSP Nephritis)
* Highly variable severity.
* Mild: Microscopic **haematuria** (血尿) +/- mild **proteinuria** (蛋白尿).
* Severe: Macroscopic haematuria, **Nephrotic Syndrome** (腎病綜合症), or **Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)**.

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)

### Distinguishing HSP from Immune Thrombocytopenic Purpura (ITP)
* **ITP** (免疫性血小板減少性紫癜): Flat (non-palpable) petechiae, child is clinically well, **Platelets markedly ↓**.
* **HSP**: Raised (palpable) purpura, systemic symptoms (pain), **Platelets Normal or ↑**.

### Distinguishing HSP from Meningococcaemia
* **Meningococcaemia** (腦膜炎雙球菌血症): Critically ill, toxic, shocked, rapidly spreading purpura, high fever.
* **HSP**: Non-toxic, hemodynamically stable, gravity-dependent rash.

## 5. Differential Diagnosis

### Key Differential Diagnoses for Childhood Purpura
| Condition | Differentiating Clinical & Lab Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Immune Thrombocytopenic Purpura (ITP)** | Well child, flat petechiae/purpura, **isolated severe thrombocytopenia** (↓ Plt). |
| **Meningococcal Septicaemia** | Toxic, febrile, hypotensive, rapidly spreading rash, abnormal coagulation / DIC. |
| **Acute Post-Streptococcal Glomerulonephritis (APSGN)** | Primary renal presentation (haematuria, hypertension, oedema), lacking classic HSP rash, ↓ C3 complement. |
| **Idiopathic Intussusception** | Typically infants (6-18 mo), isolated GI signs, no purpura, **ileo-colic** anatomy. |
| **Other Vasculitides (e.g., Kawasaki, ANCA)** | Kawasaki: Prolonged fever >5 days, mucosal changes, coronary aneurysms. ANCA: Pulmonary-renal syndrome, +ve ANCA. |

## 6. Tiered Investigations

### Mandatory Bedside Investigations
* **Urinalysis** (尿液測試): **Mandatory** screen for haematuria & proteinuria.
* **Blood Pressure (BP)**: Screen for renal hypertension.

### Blood Investigations
* **FBC**: **Platelets Normal or ↑** (rules out ITP/Leukaemia).
* **Renal Function (U&E, Cr)**: Assess for AKI.
* **Albumin**: Screen for nephrotic-range protein loss.
* **Coagulation Profile**: Normal (rules out bleeding diathesis/DIC).

### Imaging Studies
* **Abdominal Ultrasound** (腹部超聲波): Indicated for severe/worsening abdo pain to rule out **intussusception** (腸套疊).

### Invasive Special Investigations
* **Renal Biopsy** (腎活檢): Indicated ONLY for severe renal involvement (e.g., nephrotic-range proteinuria, AKI) to grade nephritis and guide immunosuppression.

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)

### Acute Supportive Management
* Mostly self-limiting (resolves in 4–6 weeks).
* Bed rest & adequate hydration.
* **Analgesia**: Paracetamol for joint pain.
* *Caution*: **NSAIDs** (e.g., Ibuprofen) contraindicated if significant GI bleeding or renal impairment.

### Pharmacological Management (Corticosteroids)
* **Oral Prednisolone** (口服類固醇) indications:
    * Severe abdominal pain.
    * Severe joint involvement preventing ambulation.
    * Significant renal disease.
* *Key Fact*: Steroids ↓ duration of acute GI pain but **DO NOT prevent long-term renal complications**.

### Severe Renal Disease Management
* Requires **Paediatric Nephrologist** input.
* Immunosuppression protocols: High-dose IV Methylprednisolone, **Cyclophosphamide**, **Azathioprine**, or **Mycophenolate Mofetil (MMF)**.

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care

### Multidisciplinary Team Involvement
* **Paediatric Nephrology**: Essential for patients with persistent proteinuria/haematuria or AKI.
* **Surgical Team**: Standby if abdominal US confirms intussusception or bowel ischaemia/perforation.

### Psychosocial Support & Education
* **Reassurance**: Educate parents that rash typically **waxes and wanes** in crops over several weeks before full resolution.

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags

### Distinguishing HSP from Non-Accidental Injury (NAI)
* Childhood bruising highly suspicious for NAI (虐兒).
* **HSP Medical hallmarks**: Classic symmetrical, **gravity-dependent** distribution of palpable purpura + **normal** coagulation/platelet profiles strongly confirm medical aetiology.

## 10. Complications & Prognosis

### Acute Gastrointestinal Complications
* **Intussusception** (腸套疊): Major life-threatening GI complication.
* Mechanism: Bowel wall vasculitic oedema acts as a **lead point**.
* Anatomy: Typically **Ileo-ileal** (迴腸-迴腸型) (unlike classic idiopathic ileo-colic).

### Chronic Renal Complications
* **Long-term Renal Impairment**: Most significant long-term morbidity.
* Severity of initial renal involvement dictates prognosis.
* Small % progress to **End-Stage Renal Failure (ESRF)**.

### Overall Prognosis
* Excellent for >90% of children. Complete recovery without sequelae.

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: What is the most important investigation to perform on a child presenting with a new diagnosis of HSP, and how long must you continue it? Answer: Urinalysis and blood pressure measurement. It must be continued regularly for at least 6-12 months after the rash resolves, as HSP nephritis can present late.
* Viva Question: How does the intussusception seen in HSP differ from typical idiopathic intussusception? Answer: HSP intussusception is typically ileo-ileal (driven by small bowel vasculitic oedema acting as a lead point), while idiopathic intussusception in infants is typically ileo-colic.
* Trap: Assuming oral steroids given during the acute phase will protect the kidneys. Pearl: Corticosteroids reduce the severity and duration of acute abdominal and joint pain, but evidence shows they do NOT prevent the future development of long-term HSP nephritis.
* Pitfall: Prescribing NSAIDs routinely for HSP joint pain. NSAIDs must be withheld until urinalysis and renal function are checked, as they can exacerbate underlying HSP nephritis or provoke active GI bleeding.
