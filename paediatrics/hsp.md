# Henoch-Schönlein Purpura (HSP) / IgA Vasculitis (過敏性紫癜)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: The most common systemic vasculitis in childhood. It is an immune-mediated, small-vessel vasculitis characterized by IgA immune complex deposition.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Very common in Hong Kong paediatric wards, particularly during the autumn and winter months. Peak incidence is between 3 and 10 years of age. More common in boys.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Pathophysiology**: Often triggered by an upper respiratory tract infection (e.g., Group A Streptococcus, Adenovirus, Parvovirus B19) 1-3 weeks prior. The immune system produces abnormal IgA1 antibodies, which form immune complexes that deposit in the walls of small blood vessels (capillaries, venules, arterioles).
- **Target Organs**: This deposition causes inflammation and necrosis of the vessels in the skin (purpura), joints (arthritis), gastrointestinal tract (abdominal pain/bleeding), and kidneys (nephritis).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- The child usually presents with a rash that begins as erythematous macules or urticarial wheals and rapidly progresses to palpable purpura.
- The rash is classically distributed symmetrically on gravity-dependent areas: the **buttocks, extensor surfaces of the lower limbs**, and ankles.
- Accompanied by joint pain, abdominal pain, and sometimes gross haematuria.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs (The Classic Tetrad)
1. **Palpable Purpura (100%)**: Non-blanching, raised, purple/red spots.
2. **Arthritis/Arthralgia (75%)**: Transient, migratory, non-destructive inflammation of large joints (knees, ankles). Causes refusal to bear weight.
3. **Abdominal Pain (50%)**: Colicky, severe periumbilical pain due to bowel ischaemia/vasculitis. Often associated with vomiting and GI bleeding (melaena or haematochezia).
4. **Renal Involvement (HSP Nephritis) (40-50%)**: Ranges from microscopic haematuria and mild proteinuria to nephrotic syndrome and acute kidney injury.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Immune Thrombocytopenic Purpura (ITP)**: Also presents with purpura/petechiae, but the child is usually completely well, the rash is NOT palpable (flat), and the platelet count is very low. In HSP, the platelet count is normal or high.
- **Meningococcal Septicaemia**: Presents with a rapidly spreading non-blanching rash, but the child is critically ill, toxic, and shocked.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Immune Thrombocytopenic Purpura (ITP).
- Meningococcal septicaemia.
- Acute Post-Streptococcal Glomerulonephritis (APSGN) - if presenting primarily with renal signs.
- Idiopathic Intussusception.
- Other vasculitides (e.g., Kawasaki disease, ANCA-associated vasculitis).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Diagnosis is primarily clinical**.
- **Urinalysis & BP**: Mandatory for all patients to screen for renal involvement (blood, protein). Measure blood pressure.
- **Bloods**: FBC (platelets are normal or high), U&E, Creatinine, Albumin.
- **Coagulation Profile**: Normal (to rule out bleeding disorders).
- **Abdominal Ultrasound**: Indicated if there is severe abdominal pain to rule out intussusception.
- **Renal Biopsy**: Indicated only if there is severe renal involvement (e.g., nephrotic-range proteinuria, acute kidney injury) to grade the nephritis and guide immunosuppressive therapy.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Supportive Care**: Most cases are self-limiting and resolve within 4-6 weeks.
  - Rest, hydration.
  - Analgesia: Paracetamol or NSAIDs (e.g., Ibuprofen) for joint pain. *Caution with NSAIDs if there is significant renal impairment or GI bleeding.*
- **Corticosteroids (Oral Prednisolone)**:
  - Indicated for severe abdominal pain, severe joint involvement preventing ambulation, or significant renal disease.
  - Steroids reduce the duration of abdominal pain but do NOT prevent the development of long-term renal complications.
- **Severe Renal Disease**: May require immunosuppressants (e.g., Cyclophosphamide, Azathioprine, Mycophenolate Mofetil) and involvement of a paediatric nephrologist.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Paediatric Nephrology**: For patients with significant or persistent renal involvement.
- **Education**: Reassure parents that the rash may wax and wane over several weeks before fully resolving.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Bruising in a child can raise suspicion of Non-Accidental Injury (NAI). However, the classic symmetrical, gravity-dependent distribution of palpable purpura in HSP, combined with a normal platelet count and coagulation profile, strongly points to a medical cause.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Intussusception**: A major GI complication. Due to bowel wall oedema acting as a lead point. *Crucial difference*: HSP intussusception is usually ileo-ileal, whereas idiopathic intussusception is usually ileo-colic.
- **Long-term Renal Impairment**: The most significant long-term complication. Renal involvement dictates the long-term prognosis. A small percentage progress to End-Stage Renal Failure (ESRF).
- **Prognosis**: Excellent for the vast majority.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "What is the most important investigation to perform on a child presenting with a new diagnosis of HSP, and how long must you continue it?" (Urinalysis and Blood Pressure measurement. It must be continued for at least 6-12 months after the rash resolves, as renal complications can present late).
- *Viva*: "How does the intussusception seen in HSP differ from typical idiopathic intussusception?" (HSP intussusception is typically ileo-ileal, while idiopathic is typically ileo-colic).
- *Pitfall*: Prescribing NSAIDs for joint pain without first checking a urinalysis and renal function, potentially exacerbating HSP nephritis.
