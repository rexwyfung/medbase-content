
# Acute Epiglottitis (急性會厭炎)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A rapidly progressive, life-threatening bacterial infection and inflammation of the epiglottis and surrounding supraglottic structures.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Now **extremely rare** in HK due to the success of the routine childhood immunisation programme (which includes the Haemophilus influenzae type b - Hib vaccine).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Pathogen**: Historically, almost exclusively caused by **Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib)**. Now, rare cases may be caused by Streptococcus pneumoniae, Group A Strep, or Staph aureus.
- **Mechanism**: Bacterial invasion of the supraglottic tissues causes intense, rapid swelling of the epiglottis (which becomes "cherry red" and swollen like a thumb). This swelling mechanically obstructs the airway just above the vocal cords.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- See below.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Onset**: Abrupt and rapid progression (over hours).
- **The "4 Ds"**:
  1. **Drooling**: Unable to swallow their own saliva due to severe pain.
  2. **Dysphagia**: Severe pain on swallowing.
  3. **Dysphonia**: Muffled, "hot potato" voice (not a hoarse, barking cough like croup).
  4. **Distress**: Severe respiratory distress, toxic appearance, high fever.
- **Posture**: The child will sit upright, leaning forward with the neck hyperextended and chin thrust forward (**Tripod position**) to maximise airway patency.
- **Stridor**: Soft, inspiratory stridor (a late and ominous sign of impending complete obstruction).
## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Croup**: Has a viral prodrome, barking cough, hoarse voice, and the child does not look toxic. Epiglottitis has NO barking cough, severe drooling, and the child looks extremely unwell.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Most Likely**:
  - Acute Epiglottitis (if classic presentation, especially if unimmunised).
- **Must-Not-Miss**:
  - **Epiglottitis IS the must-not-miss diagnosis**. It is a true medical emergency.
  - **Bacterial Tracheitis**: Can look similar (toxic child, stridor) but usually doesn't have the severe drooling/tripod posture.
  - **Foreign Body Aspiration**.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**:
  - **DO NOT EXAMINE THE THROAT**: Using a tongue depressor or making the child cry can precipitate sudden, complete, and irreversible airway obstruction.
  - **DO NOT perform any painful procedures** (e.g., blood tests, IV cannulation) until the airway is secured. Keep the child calm with parents.
- **Gold Standard**:
  - **Direct Laryngoscopy**: Performed ONLY in the Operating Theatre by a senior Anaesthetist/ENT surgeon ready to intubate or perform an emergency tracheostomy. Shows a "cherry red", swollen epiglottis.
  - **Lateral Neck X-ray**: NOT routinely recommended as it delays definitive airway management. If done, shows the classic **"Thumbprint Sign"** (swollen epiglottis).
  - **Blood Cultures & Epiglottic Swab**: Taken *after* the airway is secured.
- **Monitoring**:
  - Continuous visual observation and pulse oximetry (without distressing the child).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**:
  - **AIRWAY IS THE ONLY PRIORITY**.
  - **Call for Senior Help IMMEDIATELY**: Senior Paediatrician, Senior Anaesthetist, and ENT Surgeon.
  - **Transfer to Operating Theatre**: The child should be transferred to the OT accompanied by the senior team.
  - **Inhalational Induction**: The anaesthetist will induce anaesthesia using gas (e.g., Sevoflurane) while the child is sitting up. Once asleep, IV access is secured, and the child is **intubated**.
  - *Emergency Tracheostomy/Cricothyroidotomy*: Must be prepared as a backup if intubation fails.
- **Chronic/Definitive**:
  - **IV Antibiotics**: Once the airway is secure, start broad-spectrum IV antibiotics (e.g., Ceftriaxone or Cefotaxime) to cover Hib and other pathogens.
  - **Extubation**: Usually possible after 2-3 days of IV antibiotics when the swelling has subsided (confirmed by direct vision).
## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- PICU care is mandatory while intubated.
## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Check immunisation status. If unimmunised, arrange for catch-up vaccination after recovery.
- Prophylaxis (Rifampicin) may be required for close household contacts if the cause is Hib and there are vulnerable contacts (e.g., unimmunised infants).

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Prognosis**: Excellent IF the airway is secured in time.
- **Complications**: Death from asphyxia (if airway management is delayed or mishandled).

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Suggesting you would look in the throat with a tongue depressor to confirm the diagnosis. *Pearl*: This is an immediate fail in an OSCE. You must explicitly state: "I will NOT examine the throat, I will keep the child calm, and call for senior anaesthetic and ENT help immediately."
- **Viva Question**: "Why is epiglottitis so rare in Hong Kong today?" *Answer*: Because of the highly effective Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib) conjugate vaccine, which is part of the routine childhood immunisation schedule.
