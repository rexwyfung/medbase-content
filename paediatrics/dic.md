# Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC) (彌散性血管內凝血)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A consumptive coagulopathy. A devastating systemic syndrome characterized by the simultaneous, uncontrolled activation of systemic coagulation and subsequent exhaustion of clotting factors and platelets, leading to both massive thrombosis and massive hemorrhage.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Always secondary to an underlying catastrophe (severe sepsis, extreme trauma, massive burns, malignant leukemia). Not a disease in itself.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- A massive trigger (e.g., bacterial lipopolysaccharide in overwhelming sepsis) causes widespread release of Tissue Factor into the blood.
- Tissue Factor activates the clotting cascade everywhere at once. 
- **Phase 1 (Thrombosis)**: Millions of microscopic clots form in small vessels, causing widespread organ ischemia (kidney failure, liver failure).
- **Phase 2 (Hemorrhage)**: Fibrinolysis eventually kicks in to break down the clots. But you have now used up literally all the platelets and clotting factors in your body to make those micro-clots. Result: You bleed to death from everywhere.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- A critically ill neonate in the NICU with profound Gram-negative sepsis who begins profusely oozing blood from their IV lines, endotracheal tube, and heal-prick sites, while their toes turn black (ischemic).

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Bleeding**: Petechiae, ecchymoses, continuous oozing from puncture sites (venepuncture, surgical wounds), GI bleeds, massive pulmonary hemorrhage.
- **Thrombotic**: Acrocyanosis (blue/black fingers and toes), acute renal failure, gangrene.
- **Systemic**: Severe shock, multi-organ failure.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from typical ITP/TTP: ITP has isolated low platelets but absolutely normal PT/APTT. DIC has both low platelets AND profoundly deranged (long) PT/APTT because clotting factors are fully consumed.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Severe liver failure (Liver makes the clotting factors, so failure causes long PT/low fibrinogen, but D-Dimer isn't usually massively elevated).
- Vitamin K deficiency (prolonged PT/APTT, but platelets are normal).
- Idiopathic Thrombocytopenic Purpura (ITP - platelets low, clotting times normal).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **The Classic DIC Profile**:
  1.  **Platelets**: Very LOW
  2.  **PT / APTT**: Very PROLONGED (Takes much longer to clot)
  3.  **Fibrinogen**: Very LOW (It has all been converted into fibrin clots)
  4.  **D-Dimer**: Extremely HIGH (D-Dimer is a degradation product of fibrin; high levels prove thousands of clots are currently being broken down).
- **Blood Smear**: **Schistocytes** (Helmet cells). These are fragmented, torn red blood cells. As RBCs try to squeeze past the microscopic fibrin clots jammed in the capillaries, they get physically sliced in half.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **CRUCIAL FIRST STEP**: You MUST rapidly identify and reverse the underlying trigger. (e.g., broad-spectrum IV antibiotics and source control for sepsis). DIC will not stop until the trigger is removed.
- **Replacement Therapy (If bleeding)**:
  - **Fresh Frozen Plasma (FFP)**: To replace all the exhausted clotting factors.
  - **Cryoprecipitate**: Specifically to give massive amounts of Fibrinogen.
  - **Platelet transfusions**.
- **Heparin**: Highly controversial. Sometimes used purely in the early thrombotic phase for specific triggers (like promyelocytic leukemia or purpura fulminans) to stop the runaway clotting, but completely contraindicated if the child is actively bleeding.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Handled exclusively in the PICU/NICU. Extremely high mortality rate.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- N/A.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Death via intractable intracranial hemorrhage or severe multi-organ failure.
- Autoamputation of digits/limbs due to microvascular thrombotic gangrene.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Ordering Vitamin K in severe consumptive DIC and hoping it fixes the bleeding. Vitamin K takes 12-24 hours for the liver to synthesize new factors; a patient in DIC will die in minutes to hours. They need immediate FFP.
- **Viva Q**: "What specific blood smear finding is a hallmark of the microangiopathic hemolytic anemia seen in DIC?" Answer: Schistocytes (fragmented red blood cells). They are formed when RBCs are sheared apart by the widespread fibrin meshes blocking the microvasculature.
