# Septic Arthritis (化膿性關節炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: An acute, purulent infection within a joint space. It is a true orthopaedic emergency because the bacterial enzymes and inflammatory response can rapidly and irreversibly destroy the articular cartilage within days.
- **Local Epidemiology**: A relatively common paediatric emergency in Hong Kong. It most frequently affects children under 5 years of age. The hip and knee are the most commonly involved joints.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Pathogenesis**: 
  - **Haematogenous Spread (Most common)**: Bacteria from a transient bacteraemia (e.g., from a minor skin infection or URTI) lodge in the highly vascular synovial membrane.
  - **Direct Inoculation**: From a penetrating wound, intra-articular injection, or surgery.
  - **Contiguous Spread**: From adjacent osteomyelitis (especially in neonates, where blood vessels cross the growth plate into the epiphysis, or in joints where the metaphysis is intracapsular, like the hip and shoulder).
- **Cartilage Destruction**: The bacteria and the host's massive neutrophil response release proteolytic enzymes and cytokines that rapidly degrade the articular cartilage. The increased pressure from the pus within the joint capsule can also compromise the blood supply to the bone (e.g., causing avascular necrosis of the femoral head).
- **Causative Organisms**:
  - **Staphylococcus aureus**: The most common organism overall.
  - **Group A Streptococcus (S. pyogenes)**.
  - *Neonates*: Group B Streptococcus, E. coli.
  - *Unimmunized children*: Haemophilus influenzae type b (Hib) - now rare in HK due to vaccination.
  - *Adolescents*: Neisseria gonorrhoeae (consider in sexually active teens presenting with polyarthralgia/monoarthritis).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- A child presents with a very short history (hours to days) of high fever, severe joint pain, and an absolute refusal to use the affected limb.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Pain**: Exquisite, severe pain localized to a single joint.
- **Pseudoparalysis**: The child refuses to bear weight or move the limb at all.
- **Systemic Signs**: High fever, rigors, tachycardia, toxic appearance.
- **Local Joint Signs**:
  - Swelling, erythema, and warmth (obvious in superficial joints like the knee or ankle, but often absent in deep joints like the hip).
  - **Severely restricted Range of Motion (ROM)**: *Any* active or passive movement of the joint causes excruciating pain.
  - **Position of Comfort**: The joint is held in a position that maximizes capsular volume to reduce pressure (e.g., the hip is held flexed, abducted, and externally rotated).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Transient Synovitis of the Hip**: The most common differential. In transient synovitis, the child is usually afebrile or has a low-grade fever, looks well, and has only mild-to-moderate pain. They can often bear some weight. In septic arthritis, the child is toxic, highly febrile, and completely non-weight bearing.
- **Osteomyelitis**: The infection is in the bone (metaphysis). Palpating the bone is exquisitely painful, but gentle movement of the adjacent joint may be tolerated. In septic arthritis, the joint itself is the source of pain, and any movement is intolerable.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Transient Synovitis (Irritable hip).
- Osteomyelitis.
- Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA) - usually insidious onset, less toxic.
- Reactive Arthritis.
- Trauma / Occult fracture.
- Haemarthrosis (e.g., in a child with undiagnosed Haemophilia).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **The Kocher Criteria (For the Hip)**: Used to differentiate Septic Arthritis from Transient Synovitis.
  1. Non-weight bearing.
  2. ESR > 40 mm/hr.
  3. Fever > 38.5°C.
  4. WBC count > 12 x 10^9/L.
  *(3 or 4 criteria = >93% probability of septic arthritis).*
- **Bloods**: FBC (leukocytosis with left shift), ESR, CRP (markedly elevated). **Blood Cultures** (mandatory before antibiotics).
- **Imaging**:
  - **Ultrasound**: Highly sensitive for detecting a joint effusion (fluid in the joint). It is the best initial imaging test, especially for the hip. It can also guide needle aspiration.
  - **X-ray**: Usually normal in the early stages. Done to rule out fractures or tumours. May show widening of the joint space due to the effusion.
  - **MRI**: Excellent for assessing concurrent osteomyelitis or soft tissue abscesses, but takes time to arrange.
- **Joint Aspiration (Arthrocentesis) - The Gold Standard**:
  - Must be performed urgently (often under ultrasound guidance or in theatre) if septic arthritis is suspected.
  - *Synovial fluid analysis*: Appears purulent (pus). WBC count is typically **> 50,000 cells/mm3** (predominantly neutrophils). Send for Gram stain and culture.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Surgical Emergency**:
  - **Urgent Joint Washout (Arthrotomy and Lavage)**: The joint must be surgically opened, washed out with copious saline, and drained to remove the pus, reduce pressure, and stop the enzymatic destruction of the cartilage.
- **Intravenous Antibiotics**:
  - Started empirically *immediately after* joint aspiration and blood cultures are obtained.
  - *Empirical choice (HA guidelines)*: High-dose **IV Cloxacillin** (or Cefazolin) to cover MSSA. Add a 3rd generation cephalosporin (e.g., Cefotaxime) if the child is < 3 months old or unimmunized (to cover Gram-negatives/Hib).
  - *Duration*: Usually 2-4 weeks total (IV followed by high-dose oral), guided by clinical response and falling CRP levels.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Paediatric Orthopaedics**: The primary managing team.
- **Physiotherapy**: Crucial post-operatively to regain range of motion and prevent joint contractures.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- N/A for this condition.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Irreversible Joint Destruction**: If treatment is delayed by even 24-48 hours, the cartilage is destroyed, leading to early, severe osteoarthritis and permanent disability.
- **Avascular Necrosis (AVN)**: Especially of the femoral head, due to the high pressure of the pus compressing the blood supply.
- **Growth Arrest / Limb Length Discrepancy**: If the infection damages the adjacent growth plate.
- **Prognosis**: Excellent if diagnosed and washed out within the first 24 hours.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "A 3-year-old presents with a high fever and refuses to walk. He holds his right hip flexed and externally rotated. Ultrasound shows a hip effusion. What is the definitive diagnostic and therapeutic next step?" (Urgent joint aspiration and surgical washout (arthrotomy) to confirm the diagnosis, remove the destructive pus, and relieve pressure).
- *Viva*: "What are the four Kocher criteria used to assess the risk of septic arthritis in a child with a painful hip?" (Fever >38.5C, Non-weight bearing, ESR >40, WBC >12).
- *Pitfall*: Starting oral antibiotics for a "suspected infection" in a child with acute severe joint pain without performing a joint aspiration. This masks the symptoms, delays surgical washout, and guarantees permanent joint destruction.
