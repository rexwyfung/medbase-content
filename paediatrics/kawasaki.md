# Kawasaki Disease (川崎症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: An acute, self-limiting, systemic medium-sized vessel vasculitis of unknown aetiology. It has a striking predilection for the coronary arteries.
- **Local Epidemiology**: The leading cause of acquired heart disease in children in developed countries. Incidence is highest in East Asian populations (Japan, Korea, Taiwan, Hong Kong). In HK, the incidence is ~50-70 per 100,000 children under 5 years old. Peak age is 6 months to 5 years.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Pathophysiology**: Believed to be triggered by an unidentified infectious agent (viral or bacterial) in a genetically susceptible host, leading to an exaggerated immune response.
- **Vascular Damage**: Severe inflammation infiltrates the tunica media of medium-sized arteries. In the coronary arteries, this destroys the structural integrity of the vessel wall, leading to dilation and **aneurysm formation**.
- **Genetics**: Strong genetic link, particularly involving the *ITPKC* gene and HLA types common in Asian populations.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- The hallmark is a prolonged, high-spiking fever (>39°C) that is unresponsive to broad-spectrum antibiotics and typical antipyretics, accompanied by extreme irritability.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs (Diagnostic Criteria)
Diagnosis requires **Fever for >= 5 days** PLUS at least **4 of the 5** following clinical criteria (Mnemonic: **CREAM**):
- **C - Conjunctivitis**: Bilateral, non-purulent (no pus), bulbar conjunctival injection with *limbal sparing* (a clear ring around the iris).
- **R - Rash**: Polymorphous (maculopapular, erythema multiforme-like, or scarlatiniform), non-vesicular. Often accentuated in the perineal/groin area with early desquamation.
- **E - Extremity changes**: 
  - *Acute phase*: Erythema and firm induration (oedema) of the palms and soles.
  - *Subacute phase (week 2-3)*: Periungual desquamation (peeling of skin starting under the fingernails/toenails).
- **A - Adenopathy**: Cervical lymphadenopathy. Usually unilateral, non-fluctuant, and large (>1.5 cm).
- **M - Mucosal changes**: Erythema of the lips and oral mucosa, cracked/bleeding lips, and a prominent **"Strawberry tongue"**.
- *Incomplete (Atypical) Kawasaki Disease*: Fever >= 5 days but < 4 criteria. Diagnosed using supportive laboratory findings and echocardiogram. More common in infants < 6 months, who are at highest risk of coronary aneurysms.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Scarlet Fever**: Has a strawberry tongue and rash, but the rash feels like sandpaper, throat swab is positive for Group A Strep, and it responds rapidly to Penicillin. Eyes are usually normal.
- **Measles**: Has fever, rash, and conjunctivitis, but features a distinct prodrome (Cough, Coryza), Koplik spots in the mouth, and the rash starts on the face/hairline and moves down.
- **Adenovirus**: Can cause prolonged fever, red eyes, and rash, but conjunctivitis is often exudative (purulent), and pharyngitis is severe.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Scarlet Fever (Group A Streptococcus).
- Viral exanthems (Measles, Adenovirus, Enterovirus, EBV).
- Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS) / Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis (TEN).
- Toxic Shock Syndrome (Staphylococcal or Streptococcal).
- Systemic Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (sJIA).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bloods**:
  - *Acute*: Marked leukocytosis (neutrophilia), normocytic normochromic anaemia, significantly elevated acute phase reactants (CRP, ESR), elevated liver transaminases (ALT/AST), hypoalbuminaemia.
  - *Subacute (Week 2-3)*: **Thrombocytosis** (platelet count can exceed 1,000 x 10^9/L).
- **Urine**: Sterile pyuria (white cells in urine without bacterial growth, due to urethritis).
- **Imaging (CRITICAL)**: **Echocardiogram**. Mandatory for all suspected cases. Performed at diagnosis, 2 weeks, and 6-8 weeks. Assesses for coronary artery dilation or aneurysms (measured by Z-scores), pericardial effusion, and decreased LV function (myocarditis).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Goal**: Prevent coronary artery aneurysms. Treatment MUST be started within the first 10 days of illness.
- **Acute Therapy**:
  1. **Intravenous Immunoglobulin (IVIG)**: 2 g/kg as a single continuous infusion over 10-12 hours. Rapidly reduces systemic inflammation.
  2. **High-Dose Aspirin**: 30-50 mg/kg/day (anti-inflammatory dose) until the child is afebrile for 48 hours.
- **Subacute/Chronic Therapy**:
  - Switch to **Low-Dose Aspirin**: 3-5 mg/kg/day (antiplatelet dose) for 6-8 weeks to prevent thrombosis in potentially damaged coronary arteries. Discontinued if the 6-8 week ECHO is normal.
- **Refractory KD** (Persistent fever 36h after IVIG): 2nd dose of IVIG, IV Methylprednisolone, or biologic agents (e.g., Infliximab, Anakinra).

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Paediatric Cardiology**: Long-term follow-up is required for patients who develop aneurysms (may need lifelong aspirin, clopidogrel, or warfarin).
- **Vaccination Advice**: Live attenuated vaccines (MMR, Varicella) must be **delayed for 11 months** after IVIG administration, as the donor antibodies will neutralize the vaccine. Annual influenza vaccine is strongly recommended (especially while on Aspirin to prevent Reye's syndrome).

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Extreme irritability is a hallmark of KD. However, if the clinical picture is incomplete, always consider occult trauma, abusive head trauma, or meningitis as causes of unexplained irritability.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Coronary Artery Aneurysms (CAA)**: Occur in 25% of untreated patients, but reduced to < 5% with timely IVIG. Giant aneurysms (>8mm or Z-score >= 10) have the highest risk of thrombosis, myocardial infarction, and sudden cardiac death.
- **Other**: Myocarditis, pericarditis, macrophage activation syndrome (MAS).
- **Prognosis**: Excellent if treated early and no aneurysms develop.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "Why do we give Aspirin in Kawasaki disease when it is generally contraindicated in children due to Reye's syndrome?" (The risk of fatal coronary artery thrombosis far outweighs the rare risk of Reye's syndrome. We mitigate the risk by recommending the flu vaccine).
- *Pitfall*: Waiting for peeling of the fingers (desquamation) to diagnose KD. Desquamation is a *subacute* sign (week 2-3). If you wait for it, you have missed the 10-day window to give IVIG and prevent aneurysms.
- *Viva*: "What is the criteria for Incomplete Kawasaki Disease?" (Fever >= 5 days, 2-3 clinical criteria, plus supportive lab findings like high CRP, low albumin, sterile pyuria, or ECHO abnormalities).
