# Kawasaki Disease (川崎症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition
- **Definition**: An acute, self-limiting, systemic medium-sized vessel vasculitis of unknown aetiology.
- **Vascular Targeting**: Has a striking predilection for the coronary arteries.

### Local & Global Epidemiology
- **Incidence**: The leading cause of acquired heart disease in children in developed countries. 
- **Demographics**: Incidence is highest in East Asian populations (Japan, Korea, Taiwan, Hong Kong). In HK, the incidence is ~50-70 per 100,000 children under 5 years old. 
- **Age Distribution**: Peak age is 6 months to 5 years (especially at the end of the first year of life).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Pathophysiology
- **Trigger**: Believed to be triggered by an unidentified infectious agent (viral or bacterial) in a genetically susceptible host.
- **Immune Response**: Leads to an exaggerated immune response and severe systemic inflammation.

### Vascular Damage Mechanism
- **Arterial Destruction**: Severe inflammation infiltrates the tunica media of medium-sized arteries.
- **Aneurysm Formation**: In the coronary arteries, this destroys the structural integrity of the vessel wall, leading to dilation and aneurysm formation.

### Genetics
- **Susceptibility**: Strong genetic link, particularly involving the ITPKC gene and HLA types common in Asian populations.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs
### The Hallmark Presentation
- **Fever Profile**: Prolonged, high-spiking fever (>39°C) that is unresponsive to broad-spectrum antibiotics and typical antipyretics.
- **Temperament**: Accompanied by extreme irritability and misery (a striking clinical feature).

### Diagnostic Criteria (CREAM)
- **Rule**: Diagnosis requires Fever for >= 5 days PLUS at least 4 of the 5 following clinical criteria:
- **C - Conjunctivitis**: Bilateral, non-purulent (no exudate), bulbar conjunctival injection with limbal sparing (a clear ring around the iris).
- **R - Rash**: Polymorphous (maculopapular, diffuse erythroderma, or erythema multiforme-like), non-vesicular. Often accentuated in the perineal/groin area.
- **E - Extremity changes**: 
  - *Acute phase*: Erythema and firm induration (oedema) of the palms and soles.
  - *Subacute phase* (week 2-3): Periungual desquamation (peeling of skin starting under the fingernails/toenails).
- **A - Adenopathy**: Cervical lymphadenopathy. Usually unilateral, non-fluctuant, and large (>= 1.5 cm diameter).
- **M - Mucosal changes**: Erythema of the lips and oral/pharyngeal mucosa, cracked/bleeding lips, and a prominent "Strawberry tongue" (草莓舌).

### Incomplete (Atypical) Kawasaki Disease
- **Definition**: Fever >= 5 days but < 4 classic criteria.
- **Risk Group**: More common in infants < 6 months, who are at the highest risk of developing coronary aneurysms due to delayed diagnosis.
- **Diagnosis**: Relies heavily on high clinical suspicion, supportive laboratory findings (high CRP, low albumin, sterile pyuria), and early ECHO abnormalities.

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Differentiating Scarlet Fever
- **Features**: Has a strawberry tongue and rash.
- **Key differences**: The rash feels like sandpaper, throat swab is positive for Group A Strep, responds rapidly to Penicillin, and eyes are usually normal.

### Differentiating Measles
- **Features**: Has fever, rash, and conjunctivitis.
- **Key differences**: Features a distinct prodrome (Cough, Coryza), Koplik spots in the mouth, and the rash starts on the face/hairline and moves downwards.

### Differentiating Adenovirus
- **Features**: Can cause prolonged fever, red eyes, and rash.
- **Key differences**: Conjunctivitis is often exudative (purulent), and pharyngitis is severe.

## 5. Differential Diagnosis
### Common Infectious Differentials
- **Bacterial**: Scarlet Fever (Group A *Streptococcus*), Toxic Shock Syndrome (Staphylococcal or Streptococcal), deep abscesses, infective endocarditis.
- **Viral**: Viral exanthems (Measles, Adenovirus, Enterovirus, EBV).

### Autoimmune & Inflammatory Differentials
- **Rheumatological**: Systemic Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (sJIA), Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE), other vasculitides.
- **Severe Cutaneous**: Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS) / Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis (TEN).
- **Other**: Macrophage activation syndrome (MAS), drug fever.

## 6. Tiered Investigations
### Bedside Investigations
- **Urine**: Sterile pyuria (white cells in urine without bacterial growth, indicative of urethritis).

### Blood Investigations (Acute Phase)
- **Haematology**: Marked leukocytosis (neutrophilia), normocytic normochromic anaemia.
- **Inflammatory Markers**: Significantly ↑ acute phase reactants (CRP, ESR).
- **Biochemistry**: ↑ liver transaminases (ALT/AST), hypoalbuminaemia.

### Blood Investigations (Subacute Phase - Week 2-3)
- **Platelets**: Thrombocytosis (platelet count typically rises in the second week and can exceed 1,000 x 10^9/L).

### Imaging (CRITICAL)
- **Echocardiogram (ECHO)**: Mandatory for all suspected cases.
- **Timing**: Performed at diagnosis, and repeated at 4-6 weeks (and 6-8 weeks).
- **Purpose**: Assesses for coronary artery dilation or aneurysms (measured by Z-scores), pericardial effusion, decreased LV contractility (myocarditis), and endocardial disease (valve regurgitation). 
- **Advanced Imaging**: If coronary arteries are abnormal, angiography or MRI will be required.

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Acute Therapy Goals
- **Objective**: Prevent coronary artery aneurysms. 
- **Timing**: Treatment MUST be started within the first 10 days of illness.

### First-Line Acute Management
- **Intravenous Immunoglobulin (IVIG)**: 2 g/kg as a single continuous infusion over 10-12 hours. Rapidly reduces systemic inflammation and aneurysm risk.
- **High-Dose Aspirin**: 30-50 mg/kg/day (anti-inflammatory dose) until the child is afebrile for 48 hours.

### Refractory Disease Management
- **Definition**: Persistent fever 36h after the first dose of IVIG.
- **Options**: 2nd dose of IVIG, IV Methylprednisolone, or biologic agents (e.g., Infliximab, Anakinra).

### Subacute/Chronic Therapy
- **Low-Dose Aspirin**: Switch to 3-5 mg/kg/day (antiplatelet dose) for 6-8 weeks to prevent thrombosis in potentially damaged coronary arteries.
- **Discontinuation**: Discontinued if the 6-8 week ECHO is completely normal.

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Paediatric Cardiology Follow-up
- **Long-term Care**: Required for patients who develop aneurysms. These patients may need lifelong antiplatelet or anticoagulant therapy (Aspirin, Clopidogrel, or Warfarin).

### Immunization Advice
- **Live Vaccines**: Live attenuated vaccines (MMR, Varicella) MUST be delayed for 11 months after IVIG administration, as the donor antibodies will neutralize the vaccine.
- **Influenza Vaccine**: Annual influenza vaccine is strongly recommended (especially while on Aspirin to prevent Reye's syndrome).

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Investigating Unexplained Irritability
- **Red Flags**: Extreme irritability is a hallmark of KD. However, if the clinical picture is incomplete, ALWAYS consider occult trauma, abusive head trauma (shaken baby syndrome), or meningitis as causes of unexplained irritability.

## 10. Complications & Prognosis
### Cardiovascular Complications
- **Coronary Artery Aneurysms (CAA)**: Occur in ~25-33% of untreated patients within the first 6 weeks. Reduced to < 5% with timely IVIG.
- **Giant Aneurysms**: (>8mm in diameter or Z-score >= 10) carry the highest risk of thrombosis, myocardial infarction, and sudden cardiac death.
- **Other Cardiac**: Myocarditis, pericarditis, gallop rhythm, valve regurgitation.

### Other Complications
- **Systemic**: Macrophage activation syndrome (MAS).

### Prognosis
- **Outcome**: Excellent if treated early (<10 days) and no aneurysms develop. Remains the most common cause of acquired heart disease in children if missed.

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Viva Question: Why do we give Aspirin in Kawasaki disease when it is generally contraindicated in children due to Reye's syndrome? Answer: The risk of fatal coronary artery thrombosis far outweighs the rare risk of Reye's syndrome. We mitigate the risk by recommending the annual influenza vaccine.
* Trap: Waiting for peeling of the fingers (desquamation) to diagnose KD. Pearl: Desquamation is a subacute sign (week 2-3). If you wait for it, you have missed the 10-day window to give IVIG and prevent irreversible coronary aneurysms.
* Viva Question: What is the criteria for Incomplete Kawasaki Disease? Answer: Fever >= 5 days, 2-3 clinical criteria, plus supportive lab findings like high CRP, low albumin, sterile pyuria, or ECHO abnormalities. 
* Pitfall: Discharging an extremely irritable infant < 6 months old with prolonged fever as a "simple viral illness". Explanation: Infants < 6 months often present with incomplete KD and have the highest risk of giant coronary aneurysms. Always consider incomplete KD in this age group.
