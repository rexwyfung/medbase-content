# Anaphylaxis (過敏性休克)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A severe, life-threatening, generalized or systemic hypersensitivity reaction. It is characterized by rapidly developing airway, breathing, or circulation problems, usually associated with skin and mucosal changes.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Increasing incidence in Hong Kong. Common triggers in HK children include food (peanut, tree nuts, shellfish, egg, cow's milk), insect stings (bees, wasps), and medications (penicillin, NSAIDs).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **IgE-Mediated (Type I Hypersensitivity)**: The most common mechanism.
  - *Sensitization*: Initial exposure to an allergen causes production of specific IgE antibodies, which bind to high-affinity receptors on mast cells and basophils.
  - *Re-exposure*: The allergen cross-links the bound IgE, triggering massive, sudden degranulation of mast cells and basophils.
- **Mediators**: Release of histamine, tryptase, leukotrienes, and prostaglandins causes:
  - Vasodilation and increased capillary permeability (hypotension, distributive shock, urticaria, angioedema).
  - Smooth muscle spasm (bronchospasm, abdominal cramps).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Onset is usually rapid (minutes to hours) following exposure to a known or unknown trigger.
- The child may report a sense of "impending doom," tingling in the mouth, or sudden severe itching.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Airway (A)**: Pharyngeal/laryngeal oedema causing throat tightness, hoarse voice, stridor, or complete airway obstruction. Swelling of the tongue/lips (angioedema).
- **Breathing (B)**: Bronchospasm causing dyspnoea, tachypnoea, wheeze, cyanosis, respiratory arrest.
- **Circulation (C)**: Vasodilation and fluid shift causing tachycardia, profound hypotension, pale/clammy skin, dizziness, collapse, cardiac arrest.
- **Skin/Mucosa**: Urticaria (hives), erythema, pruritus, angioedema. *(Note: Skin changes may be absent in up to 20% of cases, especially if the patient is rapidly deteriorating into shock).*
- **Gastrointestinal**: Severe colicky abdominal pain, vomiting, diarrhoea.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Vasovagal Syncope**: Presents with bradycardia and pallor, without urticaria, wheeze, or angioedema. Recovers quickly upon lying flat. Anaphylaxis presents with tachycardia (initially) and skin/respiratory signs.
- **Acute Severe Asthma**: Presents with severe wheeze and respiratory distress, but lacks the sudden hypotension, urticaria, and angioedema seen in anaphylaxis.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Vasovagal syncope.
- Acute severe asthma.
- Panic attack / hyperventilation.
- Choking / foreign body aspiration.
- Hereditary angioedema (presents with swelling and abdominal pain, but no urticaria and does not respond to adrenaline).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Diagnosis is entirely CLINICAL and treatment must NEVER be delayed for investigations.**
- **Mast Cell Tryptase**: A blood test taken ideally within 1-2 hours of the reaction (and a baseline sample 24 hours later). Elevated levels confirm mast cell degranulation and support the diagnosis of anaphylaxis retrospectively.
- **Allergy Testing (Outpatient)**: Skin prick testing or specific IgE blood tests (RAST) to identify the trigger, performed weeks after the event.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute Management (Medical Emergency)**:
  1. **Remove Trigger**: Stop IV drug infusion, remove stinger, etc.
  2. **Call for Help & Resuscitation**: ABCDE approach. Lay the patient flat with legs elevated (do NOT allow them to stand up, as this can cause sudden fatal cardiac arrest due to empty ventricles).
  3. **ADRENALINE (Epinephrine) IM**: The single most important, life-saving treatment.
     - **Route**: Intramuscular (IM) injection into the anterolateral aspect of the middle third of the thigh.
     - **Dose (1:1000 solution)**: 
       - < 6 years: 0.15 ml (150 mcg)
       - 6-12 years: 0.3 ml (300 mcg)
       - > 12 years: 0.5 ml (500 mcg)
     - *Repeat every 5 minutes if no improvement.*
  4. **Oxygen**: High-flow 100% oxygen via non-rebreather mask.
  5. **IV Fluids**: Rapid fluid bolus (20 ml/kg 0.9% Saline) for hypotension.
  6. **Second-line Adjuncts**: IV/IM Chlorphenamine (antihistamine), IV Hydrocortisone (steroid), Nebulized Salbutamol (for persistent wheeze). *These do NOT treat the life-threatening features and must never delay adrenaline.*
- **Post-Acute Care**: Observe for at least 6-12 hours due to the risk of a **biphasic reaction** (a second wave of anaphylaxis occurring hours later without re-exposure).

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Paediatric Immunology/Allergy**: Referral for formal allergy testing and management plan.
- **Education**: The most critical long-term intervention.
  - Provide a written **Anaphylaxis Action Plan**.
  - Train the child, parents, and school staff on how to use an **Adrenaline Auto-Injector (e.g., EpiPen, Jext)**.
  - Strict allergen avoidance advice.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- N/A for this condition.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Complications**: Hypoxic brain injury, cardiac arrest, death.
- **Prognosis**: Excellent if adrenaline is administered promptly. Fatalities are almost always associated with delayed administration of adrenaline.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "What is the dose, route, and location for administering adrenaline in a 7-year-old child with anaphylaxis?" (0.3 ml of 1:1000 adrenaline, given intramuscularly into the anterolateral aspect of the middle third of the thigh).
- *Viva*: "Why must a patient with anaphylaxis be laid flat and not allowed to stand up?" (Anaphylaxis causes massive vasodilation. Standing up causes blood to pool in the legs, leading to an empty heart, sudden loss of cardiac output, and fatal cardiac arrest).
- *Pitfall*: Giving IV antihistamines or steroids first and delaying IM adrenaline. Adrenaline is the ONLY drug that reverses the life-threatening features of anaphylaxis (laryngeal oedema and shock).
