
# Paediatric Hypertension (小兒高血壓)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Blood pressure ≥ 95th percentile for age, sex, and height on 3 separate occasions. (Normal is < 90th percentile).
- **Local Epidemiology**: Increasingly common in HK due to the rising prevalence of childhood obesity. However, severe or prepubertal hypertension is often secondary to an underlying disease.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Primary (Essential) Hypertension**: Most common in adolescents. Associated with obesity, family history, high salt intake, and sedentary lifestyle.
- **Secondary Hypertension**: Most common in pre-adolescent children. The younger the child and the higher the BP, the more likely it is secondary.
  - **Renal Parenchymal Disease (70-80%)**: Glomerulonephritis (e.g., APSGN, IgA nephropathy), reflux nephropathy/renal scarring, polycystic kidney disease.
  - **Renovascular Disease**: Renal artery stenosis (e.g., Fibromuscular dysplasia, Neurofibromatosis Type 1).
  - **Cardiovascular**: Coarctation of the Aorta.
  - **Endocrine**: Phaeochromocytoma, Cushing's syndrome, Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia, Hyperthyroidism.
  - **Medications**: Corticosteroids, stimulants (ADHD meds), oral contraceptives.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- See below.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- Often asymptomatic (the "silent killer").
- **Symptoms (if severe)**: Headache, epistaxis, visual changes, dizziness, poor school performance.
- **Signs of Underlying Cause**:
  - *Coarctation*: Radio-femoral delay, weak/absent femoral pulses, BP higher in right arm than legs.
  - *Renal*: Oedema, haematuria, palpable abdominal mass (polycystic kidneys).
  - *Endocrine*: Cushingoid facies, tachycardia/tremor (hyperthyroidism), episodic sweating/palpitations (phaeochromocytoma).
  - *Neurofibromatosis*: Café-au-lait spots.
## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **White Coat Hypertension**: BP is >95th percentile in the clinic but normal at home or on 24-hour Ambulatory BP Monitoring (ABPM).

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Most Likely**:
  - Primary hypertension (in an obese adolescent).
  - Renal parenchymal disease (in a young child).
- **Must-Not-Miss**:
  - **Hypertensive Emergency**: Severe hypertension with target organ damage (e.g., hypertensive encephalopathy causing seizures/coma, heart failure, acute kidney injury).
  - Coarctation of the Aorta.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**:
  - **Accurate BP Measurement**: Use the correct cuff size (bladder width should cover 40% of arm circumference). Measure in all 4 limbs once to rule out Coarctation.
  - **Urinalysis**: Dipstick for protein and blood (screening for glomerulonephritis).
- **Gold Standard**:
  - **24-hour Ambulatory BP Monitoring (ABPM)**: The gold standard to confirm the diagnosis, rule out white coat hypertension, and assess nocturnal dipping.
  - **Renal Ultrasound with Doppler**: To check for renal scarring, size asymmetry, and assess renal artery flow (for stenosis).
  - **Bloods**: U&E, Creatinine (eGFR), Lipid profile, HbA1c (to screen for metabolic syndrome in obese patients).
  - **Echocardiogram**: To assess for Left Ventricular Hypertrophy (target organ damage) and rule out Coarctation.
- **Monitoring**:
  - Regular BP checks and monitoring for side effects of antihypertensives.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**:
  - **Hypertensive Emergency**: Admit to PICU. Lower BP *gradually* (e.g., reduce by 25% of the planned reduction in the first 8 hours) using IV infusions (e.g., Labetalol, Sodium Nitroprusside). Dropping BP too fast can cause cerebral ischaemia.
- **Chronic/Definitive**:
  - **Primary Hypertension**:
    - **Lifestyle Modification**: First-line. Weight loss, DASH diet (low sodium, high potassium), regular aerobic exercise, avoid smoking/alcohol.
    - **Secondary / Symptomatic / Target Organ Damage**:
    - **Pharmacotherapy**:
      - *ACE Inhibitors (e.g., Enalapril) or ARBs*: First-line for children, especially if they have proteinuria or diabetes (renoprotective). **CONTRAINDICATED** in bilateral renal artery stenosis.
      - *Calcium Channel Blockers (e.g., Amlodipine)*: Good alternative.
      - *Beta-blockers*: Less commonly used as first-line now.
  - **Surgical**: Angioplasty for renal artery stenosis, surgical repair for Coarctation.
## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Dietitian referral is crucial for primary hypertension.
## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Ensure compliance with medication, as it is often a lifelong requirement for secondary causes.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Target Organ Damage**: Left ventricular hypertrophy, hypertensive retinopathy, chronic kidney disease, early coronary artery disease, stroke.
- Prognosis depends on the underlying cause and compliance with treatment.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Starting an ACE inhibitor in a child with a renal bruit. *Pearl*: A bruit suggests renal artery stenosis. ACE inhibitors are contraindicated in bilateral renal artery stenosis as they can precipitate acute renal failure by removing the efferent arteriolar constriction needed to maintain GFR.
- **Viva Question**: "What is the most important physical examination step when you find high blood pressure in a child's arm?" *Answer*: Measure the blood pressure in the legs and palpate the femoral pulses simultaneously with the radial pulse to rule out Coarctation of the Aorta.
