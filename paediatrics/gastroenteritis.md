# Acute Gastroenteritis (急性腸胃炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition of Gastroenteritis
* Inflammation of stomach & intestines
* Acute onset diarrhoea (≥3 loose/watery stools/24h)
* ± Vomiting (嘔吐), fever (發燒), crampy abdominal pain (腹痛)

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
* Extremely common in HK paediatric admissions
* **Viral (Most Common):**
  * **Norovirus (諾如病毒):** Commonest cause currently; winter/spring outbreaks in schools/nurseries
  * **Rotavirus (輪狀病毒):** Incidence ↓ due to private vaccination; previously caused severe disease
  * Sapovirus, enteric adenovirus, astrovirus
* **Bacterial (Less Common):**
  * **Salmonella (沙門氏菌):** Often from undercooked eggs/meat
  * **Campylobacter jejuni (空腸彎曲菌)**
  * **Shigella (志賀氏菌)**
  * **E. coli:** ETEC, STEC/O157:H7
* **Protozoan:**
  * Giardia, Cryptosporidium (Rare, suspect in persistent diarrhoea or travel history)

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Viral Pathophysiology
* Viruses invade/destroy enterocytes in small intestine villi
* → Blunting of villi → ↓ absorptive surface area
* → Transient secondary lactose intolerance (乳糖不耐症)
* → Osmotic diarrhoea

### Bacterial Pathophysiology (Toxin-Mediated vs Invasive)
* **Toxin-mediated:**
  * Cholera, ETEC
  * → Enterotoxins stimulate cAMP/cGMP → Secretory diarrhoea (watery)
* **Invasive:**
  * Salmonella, Shigella, Campylobacter
  * → Invade mucosa → Inflammation, ulceration
  * → Bloody/mucous diarrhoea (Dysentery - 痢疾)

### Pathophysiology of Dehydration & Fluid Shifts
* **Isonatraemic Dehydration (等鈉性脫水):** Proportionate loss of Na+ & H2O
* **Hyponatraemic Dehydration (低鈉性脫水):** Net loss of Na+ > H2O (often due to hypotonic fluid replacement) → Water shifts intracellularly → ↑ brain volume → Seizures (抽搐)
* **Hypernatraemic Dehydration (高鈉性脫水):** Net loss of H2O > Na+ (high insensible losses or low-Na+ diarrhoea) → Extracellular fluid hypertonic → Water drawn out of brain → Cerebral shrinkage → Irritability/Abnormal neuro signs. *NB: Extracellular volume relatively preserved → Skin turgor/sunken eyes less prominent per unit fluid loss.*

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs
### High-Risk Groups for Dehydration
* Infants <6 months old
* Low birthweight infants
* ≥5 diarrhoeal stools/24h
* >2 episodes vomiting/24h
* Unable to tolerate supplementary fluids
* Malnourished or immunocompromised

### Gastrointestinal Symptoms
* **Diarrhoea (腹瀉):** Watery (viral/toxin), bloody/mucous (invasive bacterial)
* **Vomiting (嘔吐):** Often precedes diarrhoea in viral causes
* Crampy abdominal pain (腹痛)
* Tenesmus (里急後重) in dysentery

### Assessment of Dehydration Severity
| Clinical Feature | No Dehydration (<5% weight loss) | Clinical Dehydration (5-10% weight loss) | Shock (>10% weight loss) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Appearance** | Well, alert | Unwell, irritable, lethargic | ↓ Conscious level |
| **Urine Output** | Normal | ↓ Decreased | Anuria |
| **Skin Colour** | Normal | Normal | Pale or mottled |
| **Extremities** | Warm | Warm | Cold |
| **Eyes** | Normal | Sunken | Grossly sunken, tearless |
| **Mucous Membranes**| Moist | Dry | Very dry |
| **Heart Rate** | Normal | Tachycardia (心動過速) | Severe Tachycardia |
| **Respiratory Rate** | Normal | Tachypnoea (呼吸急促) | Acidotic/Kussmaul breathing |
| **Capillary Refill** | Normal (<2s) | Normal | Prolonged (>2s) |
| **Peripheral Pulses** | Normal | Normal | Weak / thready |
| **Skin Turgor** | Normal | ↓ Reduced | Very slow recoil |
| **Blood Pressure** | Normal | Normal | **Hypotension** (Decompensated shock) |

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Distinguishing Surgical Abdomen (e.g., Appendicitis/Intussusception)
* Vomiting is often bilious (green) in obstruction
* Severe, localized, persistent abdominal pain
* Pain precedes diarrhoea
* Diarrhoea is NOT the prominent feature

### Distinguishing Systemic Infections
* UTI, sepsis, or meningitis can present with vomiting/diarrhoea
* Persistent high fever, rigors, bulging fontanelle, neck stiffness, or dysuria suggest non-GI source

## 5. Differential Diagnosis
### Most Likely Diagnoses
* Viral gastroenteritis (Norovirus, Rotavirus)
* Bacterial gastroenteritis (Salmonella)
* Transient food intolerance/allergy

### Must-Not-Miss Diagnoses (Red Flags)
* **Hypovolaemic Shock (低血容量性休克):** Decompensated dehydration
* **Intussusception (腸套疊):** Colicky pain, pallor, bilious vomiting, "red currant jelly" stools
* **Haemolytic Uraemic Syndrome (HUS):** Triad of Microangiopathic Haemolytic Anaemia (MAHA), Thrombocytopenia, AKI; follows E. coli O157:H7 (STEC)
* **Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA):** Polyuria/polydipsia, deep acidotic breathing, vomiting, abdo pain
* **Surgical Emergencies:** Appendicitis, volvulus, Hirschsprung enterocolitis

## 6. Tiered Investigations
### Bedside Investigations
* Usually clinical diagnosis; no investigations for mild/moderate cases
* **Bedside Glucose (血糖):** Mandatory in lethargic/severely dehydrated child to rule out hypoglycaemia
* **Weight Measurement:** Serial daily weights are gold standard for monitoring fluid balance

### Blood Tests (U&E, VBG, FBC)
* Indicated IF:
  * Severe dehydration / requiring IV fluids
  * Altered conscious level
  * Suspected hypernatraemic dehydration
  * Suspected HUS or sepsis
* **Check:** Plasma Na+, K+, urea, creatinine, lactate, acid-base status (VBG)

### Stool Investigations
* **Stool Microscopy, Culture & Virology:**
  * Indicated IF:
    * Bloody/mucous diarrhoea
    * Septic appearance
    * Immunocompromised child
    * Recent travel abroad
    * Prolonged symptoms (>7 days)
    * Diagnostic uncertainty

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Acute Management of Shock
* **IV Fluid Resuscitation:**
  * **Bolus:** 10-20 ml/kg of 0.9% Normal Saline
  * Repeat as necessary until shock reversed
  * Reassess circulation after each bolus

### Definitive Management of Mild to Moderate Dehydration
* **Oral Rehydration Therapy (ORT - 口服補液療法):**
  * Cornerstone of management
  * Use low-osmolarity Oral Rehydration Salts (ORS)
  * Promotes active Na+ & passive H2O absorption via Na+/Glucose cotransporter
  * Dose: 50-100 ml/kg over 4 hours + replacement for ongoing losses
  * Give frequently in small amounts (via NGT if refusing oral)
  * Do NOT use Coca-Cola or apple juice (high osmolarity, low Na+)
* **Anti-emetics:**
  * **Ondansetron (昂丹司瓊):** Single oral/SL dose considered in A&E for persistent vomiting to facilitate ORT and prevent admission

### Management of Severe or Hypernatraemic Dehydration
* **IV Fluid Deficit Replacement:**
  * Indicated if ORT fails or severe dehydration present
  * Deficit replaced over 24 hours
  * **Hypernatraemic Dehydration (Na+ >150 mmol/L):**
    * Deficit MUST be replaced slowly over **48 hours**
    * Use 0.9% Saline (± 5% Dextrose)
    * Target Na+ reduction: **<0.5 mmol/L per hour**
    * Rapid ↓ Na+ → Water shifts into brain cells → Cerebral oedema & seizures

### Medications to AVOID in Paediatric Gastroenteritis
* **Anti-motility agents (e.g., Loperamide):**
  * **CONTRAINDICATED** in children
  * Risk of paralytic ileus, toxic megacolon, CNS depression
* **Antibiotics:**
  * Not routinely recommended (even for Salmonella)
  * Does not shorten course; prolongs carriage
  * **Exceptions:** Shigella, severe Campylobacter, C. difficile, sepsis, immunocompromised, infants <6 months with Salmonella

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Infection Control & Nursing Care
* Strict isolation/contact precautions in ward (single room if possible)
* Input/Output charting (weigh nappies)
* Daily weight monitoring

### Post-Gastroenteritis Dietary Advice
* Continue breastfeeding throughout
* Reintroduce normal solid food & milk immediately after rehydration
* Avoid fruit juices and high-sugar carbonated drinks
* If lactose intolerance suspected post-illness, brief trial of lactose-free formula may be considered (dietitian input)

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Hygiene & Public Health Education
* Educate parents on strict handwashing (soap & water; alcohol gel ineffective for Norovirus/C. diff)
* **School Exclusion:** Exclude from school/nursery until **48 hours** after last episode of diarrhoea/vomiting

### Non-Accidental Injury (NAI) Red Flags
* Consider NAI / Neglect if:
  * Extreme, unexplained hypernatraemia
  * Factitious illness (deliberate poisoning/laxative abuse)
  * Severe delay in seeking medical attention despite obvious shock

## 10. Complications & Prognosis
### Acute Complications
* Hypovolaemic shock (低血容量性休克)
* Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)
* Hypernatraemic dehydration → Seizures (抽搐), cerebral oedema, intracranial haemorrhage
* Electrolyte derangements (Hypokalaemia, metabolic acidosis)

### Late or Post-Infective Complications
* Transient secondary lactose intolerance
* Post-infectious irritable bowel syndrome (IBS)
* Haemolytic Uraemic Syndrome (post-STEC)
* Reactive arthritis, Guillain-Barré syndrome (post-Campylobacter)

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Trap: Prescribing Loperamide to stop diarrhoea in a young child. Pearl: Never prescribe anti-motility drugs to children due to the high risk of paralytic ileus and fatal CNS depression; this is a major safety failure in an OSCE.
* Pitfall: Rehydrating a hypernatraemic child too quickly. Rapid correction of plasma sodium causes rapid fluid shifts into the relatively hypertonic brain cells, leading to cerebral oedema and seizures.
* Viva Question: A 10-month-old presents with vomiting and diarrhoea, looking lethargic with a capillary refill time of 4 seconds and a Na+ of 156 mmol/L. What is the fluid management plan? Answer: Fluid bolus of 10-20 ml/kg 0.9% Saline for shock, followed by slow deficit replacement over 48 hours using 0.9% Saline to ensure Na+ falls by less than 0.5 mmol/L/hour.
* Viva Question: A previously well 2-year-old presents with acute bloody diarrhoea and abdominal pain. What are the key bacterial pathogens to suspect? Answer: Campylobacter jejuni, Shigella, Salmonella, and Shiga-toxin producing E. coli (STEC/O157).
* Trap: Giving a fluid bolus of 0.45% Saline or 5% Dextrose for shock. Pearl: Always use isotonic crystalloids (0.9% Normal Saline or Plasmalyte) for acute fluid resuscitation boluses.
