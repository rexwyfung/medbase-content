
# Acute Gastroenteritis (急性腸胃炎)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Inflammation of the stomach and intestines, typically presenting with acute onset of diarrhoea (≥3 loose/watery stools in 24 hours) with or without vomiting, fever, and abdominal pain.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Extremely common in HK.
  - *Viral*: Rotavirus (incidence decreasing due to private vaccination), Norovirus (common in winter/spring outbreaks in schools/nurseries), Adenovirus.
  - *Bacterial*: Salmonella (often from undercooked eggs/meat), Campylobacter, Shigella, E. coli.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Viral**: Invade and destroy enterocytes in the small intestine villi, leading to blunting of villi, decreased absorptive surface area, and transient secondary lactose intolerance. Osmotic diarrhoea.
- **Bacterial**:
  - *Toxin-mediated*: Cholera, ETEC (secretory diarrhoea).
  - *Invasive*: Salmonella, Shigella, Campylobacter (invade mucosa causing inflammation, ulceration, and bloody/mucous diarrhoea - dysentery).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- See below.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- Vomiting (often precedes diarrhoea in viral causes).
- Diarrhoea (watery in viral; may be bloody/mucous in invasive bacterial).
- Crampy abdominal pain, fever.
- **Assessment of Dehydration (CRITICAL)**:
  - *Mild (<5%)*: Normal examination, thirsty.
  - *Moderate (5-10%)*: Restless/irritable, sunken eyes, dry mucous membranes, absent tears, tachycardia, delayed capillary refill (2-3s), decreased urine output.
  - *Severe (>10%)*: Lethargic/unconscious, deep acidotic breathing (Kussmaul), weak/thready pulse, prolonged capillary refill (>3s), skin pinch retracts very slowly, anuria.
## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Surgical Abdomen (Appendicitis/Intussusception)**: Vomiting is often bilious (in obstruction). Abdominal pain is severe, localised, and precedes diarrhoea. Diarrhoea is not the prominent feature.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Most Likely**:
  - Viral gastroenteritis (Norovirus, Rotavirus).
- **Must-Not-Miss**:
  - Severe dehydration leading to hypovolaemic shock.
  - Intussusception (can present with vomiting and "red currant jelly" stools mimicking dysentery).
  - Haemolytic Uraemic Syndrome (HUS) following E. coli O157:H7 infection.
  - Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) (can present with vomiting and abdominal pain).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**:
  - **Usually a clinical diagnosis**. No investigations needed for mild/moderate cases.
  - **Bedside Glucose**: Mandatory in any lethargic or severely dehydrated child to rule out hypoglycaemia.
- **Gold Standard**:
  - **Stool Culture & Virology**: Indicated if: bloody diarrhoea, severe disease, immunocompromised, recent travel, or prolonged symptoms (>7 days).
  - **Bloods (U&E, VBG)**: Indicated if: severe dehydration requiring IV fluids, altered conscious level, or suspected hypernatraemic dehydration.
- **Monitoring**:
  - Strict fluid balance (input/output chart). Daily weights.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**:
  - **Severe Dehydration / Shock**: IV fluid bolus (20 ml/kg of 0.9% Normal Saline). Repeat as necessary until shock is reversed.
- **Chronic/Definitive**:
  - **Mild to Moderate Dehydration**:
    - **Oral Rehydration Therapy (ORT)**: The cornerstone of management. Use low-osmolarity Oral Rehydration Salts (ORS). Give 50-100 ml/kg over 4 hours, plus replacement for ongoing losses.
    - *Ondansetron*: A single oral dose can be considered in A&E for children with persistent vomiting to facilitate ORT and prevent admission.
    - **Failure of ORT / Severe Dehydration**:
    - IV fluid deficit replacement (usually over 24 hours, or 48 hours if hypernatraemic to prevent cerebral oedema) + maintenance fluids.
  - **Medications to AVOID**:
    - **Anti-motility agents (e.g., Loperamide)**: **CONTRAINDICATED** in children. Can cause paralytic ileus, toxic megacolon, and CNS depression.
    - **Antibiotics**: Not routinely recommended, even for bacterial causes (e.g., Salmonella), as they do not shorten the course and may prolong carriage. *Exceptions*: Shigella, severe Campylobacter, sepsis, immunocompromised.
## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Infection control (contact precautions in the ward).
## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Educate parents on hygiene (handwashing). Advise exclusion from school/nursery until 48 hours after the last episode of diarrhoea/vomiting.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Hypovolaemic shock, acute kidney injury.
- Hypernatraemic dehydration (doughy skin, jittery, risk of seizures/cerebral oedema during rapid rehydration).
- Transient lactose intolerance (post-gastroenteritis).

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Prescribing Loperamide for a child with diarrhoea. *Pearl*: Never prescribe anti-motility drugs to children. It is a major safety failure in an OSCE.
- **Viva Question**: "A 1-year-old with gastroenteritis has a Na of 155 mmol/L. How do you manage the fluids?" *Answer*: This is hypernatraemic dehydration. The deficit must be replaced slowly over 48 hours (not 24 hours) using 0.9% Normal Saline or 0.45% Saline to prevent rapid fluid shifts into the brain causing cerebral oedema and seizures.
