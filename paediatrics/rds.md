# Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS) (呼吸窘迫綜合症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A respiratory disorder of premature neonates caused by a deficiency of pulmonary surfactant, leading to alveolar collapse and impaired gas exchange. Also known as Hyaline Membrane Disease.
- **Local Epidemiology**: The most common cause of respiratory distress in premature infants in Hong Kong NICUs. Incidence is inversely proportional to gestational age (affects ~60% of infants born <28 weeks, and ~30% of those born 28-34 weeks).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Surfactant Deficiency**: Surfactant (a complex of phospholipids and proteins, mainly DPPC) is produced by Type II pneumocytes. It reduces alveolar surface tension, preventing alveolar collapse at the end of expiration.
- **Pathogenesis**: Without sufficient surfactant, alveoli collapse (atelectasis). This leads to:
  1. Decreased lung compliance (stiff lungs).
  2. Ventilation-perfusion (V/Q) mismatch and intrapulmonary shunting -> **Hypoxaemia**.
  3. Increased work of breathing -> **Hypercapnia** and respiratory acidosis.
- **Hyaline Membranes**: The hypoxia and acidosis damage the alveolar epithelium, causing proteinaceous exudate to leak into the alveoli, forming "hyaline membranes" that further impair gas exchange.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Presents immediately at birth or within the first few hours of life in a premature infant.
- The infant exhibits progressively worsening signs of respiratory distress.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Tachypnoea**: Respiratory rate > 60 breaths/min.
- **Expiratory Grunting**: The infant exhales against a partially closed glottis to generate intrinsic PEEP (Positive End-Expiratory Pressure) to keep alveoli open.
- **Retractions**: Subcostal, intercostal, and sternal recessions due to highly compliant chest wall and stiff lungs.
- **Nasal Flaring**: An attempt to decrease airway resistance.
- **Cyanosis**: If hypoxaemia is severe and untreated.
- **Auscultation**: Decreased air entry bilaterally, fine inspiratory crackles.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Transient Tachypnoea of the Newborn (TTN)**: Usually occurs in term or near-term infants (especially post-Caesarean section without labor). Caused by delayed clearance of fetal lung fluid. Presents with tachypnoea but usually mild retractions and rapid improvement within 24-48 hours. CXR shows fluid in the fissures.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Transient Tachypnoea of the Newborn (TTN).
- Neonatal Pneumonia / Sepsis (e.g., Group B Streptococcus).
- Meconium Aspiration Syndrome (MAS) - usually in term/post-term infants.
- Congenital Diaphragmatic Hernia (CDH).
- Pneumothorax.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Chest X-ray (CXR)**: The diagnostic gold standard.
  - *Classic findings*: Bilateral, diffuse **"ground-glass" appearance** (reticulogranular pattern) due to microatelectasis, and **air bronchograms** (air-filled bronchi standing out against the opaque, collapsed alveoli). Low lung volumes.
- **Arterial Blood Gas (ABG)**: Hypoxaemia, hypercapnia, and respiratory/mixed acidosis.
- **Sepsis Screen**: Blood culture, CBP, CRP. (It is clinically impossible to distinguish RDS from early-onset neonatal pneumonia, so antibiotics are always started).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Antenatal Prevention**: **Maternal Corticosteroids** (IM Dexamethasone or Betamethasone) given to mothers at risk of preterm delivery (<34 weeks) to accelerate fetal lung maturity and surfactant production.
- **Delivery Room**: Delayed cord clamping. Early stabilization with **CPAP** (Continuous Positive Airway Pressure) to prevent alveolar collapse.
- **Respiratory Support**:
  - *Non-invasive*: CPAP or High-Flow Nasal Cannula (HFNC) is preferred.
  - *Invasive*: Intubation and mechanical ventilation if CPAP fails (FiO2 > 0.40, severe acidosis, or frequent apnoeas).
- **Surfactant Replacement Therapy**:
  - Exogenous surfactant (e.g., Curosurf, Survanta) administered directly into the trachea via an endotracheal tube.
  - *INSURE method*: INtubate, SURfactant, Extubate to CPAP (minimizes mechanical ventilation time).
  - *LISA/MIST method*: Less Invasive Surfactant Administration (using a thin catheter while the baby remains on CPAP).
- **Supportive**: IV fluids, thermoregulation (incubator), empirical IV antibiotics (Penicillin + Gentamicin) until blood cultures are negative.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **NICU Team**: Neonatologists, specialized neonatal nurses, respiratory therapists.
- **Parental Support**: Having a premature baby in the NICU is highly traumatic. Regular updates and involvement in care (e.g., kangaroo care) are essential.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- N/A for this condition.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
**Acute**: Pneumothorax, Pulmonary Haemorrhage, Intraventricular Haemorrhage (IVH), Patent Ductus Arteriosus (PDA) - hypoxia keeps the ductus open.
- **Chronic**: **Bronchopulmonary Dysplasia (BPD)** / Chronic Lung Disease of prematurity (oxygen dependency beyond 36 weeks corrected gestational age).
- **Prognosis**: Survival has improved dramatically with antenatal steroids and exogenous surfactant. Long-term respiratory and neurodevelopmental outcomes depend on the degree of prematurity and complications.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "What is the physiological purpose of expiratory grunting in a neonate with RDS?" (The infant exhales against a partially closed glottis to create intrinsic Positive End-Expiratory Pressure (PEEP), which helps prevent alveolar collapse at the end of expiration).
- *Viva*: "Describe the classic chest X-ray findings in RDS." (Low lung volumes, a diffuse bilateral ground-glass or reticulogranular appearance, and prominent air bronchograms).
