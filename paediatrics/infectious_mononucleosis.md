
# Infectious Mononucleosis (傳染性單核白血球增多症)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A clinical syndrome typically caused by primary infection with the Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV).
- **Local Epidemiology**: Very common in HK. Often occurs in early childhood in Asia (often asymptomatic or mild), but classic "glandular fever" presentation is more common in adolescents and young adults.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Pathogen**: Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV), a human herpesvirus (HHV-4).
- **Transmission**: Saliva ("kissing disease"), sharing utensils.
- **Mechanism**: EBV infects B lymphocytes in the oropharynx. The profound immune response (proliferation of cytotoxic T cells) causes the massive lymphadenopathy and hepatosplenomegaly. The atypical lymphocytes seen on a blood film are actually the reactive T cells, not the infected B cells.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- See below.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Classic Triad**:
  1. **Fever**: Often prolonged (1-2 weeks).
  2. **Pharyngitis/Tonsillitis**: Severe, often with thick, white/grey exudate. Can cause airway compromise.
  3. **Lymphadenopathy**: Symmetrical, particularly involving the **posterior cervical** nodes.
- **Other Signs**:
  - **Splenomegaly**: Occurs in ~50% of cases. Peaks in the 2nd week. Risk of splenic rupture.
  - Hepatomegaly and mild jaundice.
  - Severe fatigue and malaise (can last for months).
  - **Maculopapular Rash**: Occurs in >90% of patients erroneously given **Amoxicillin or Ampicillin** (thought to have Strep throat).
## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Streptococcal Pharyngitis**: Usually lacks significant splenomegaly or posterior cervical lymphadenopathy. Resolves quickly with antibiotics.
- **CMV Infection**: Can cause a "mononucleosis-like syndrome" but typically has less severe pharyngitis and negative heterophile antibodies.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Most Likely**:
  - EBV Infectious Mononucleosis.
- **Must-Not-Miss**:
  - **Airway Obstruction**: Massive tonsillar hypertrophy.
  - **Splenic Rupture**: A rare but life-threatening complication.
  - **Acute HIV Infection**: Can present with a mononucleosis-like illness.
  - **Leukaemia/Lymphoma**: Can present with fever, lymphadenopathy, and splenomegaly.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**:
  - **Complete Blood Count (CBC)**: Lymphocytosis with >10% **atypical lymphocytes** (large, irregular nuclei, abundant cytoplasm).
  - **Liver Function Tests (LFTs)**: Mild to moderate transaminitis (elevated ALT/AST) is very common.
- **Gold Standard**:
  - **Monospot Test (Heterophile Antibodies)**: Rapid latex agglutination test. Often negative in children < 4 years old and in the first week of illness.
  - **EBV Specific Serology**: Definitive test.
  - *Acute Infection*: VCA IgM (+), VCA IgG (+), EBNA IgG (-).
  - *Past Infection*: VCA IgM (-), VCA IgG (+), EBNA IgG (+).
- **Monitoring**:
  - Not usually required unless complications arise.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**:
  - **Airway**: If massive tonsillar hypertrophy threatens the airway (stridor, difficulty breathing), a short course of **Corticosteroids** (e.g., IV Dexamethasone) is indicated.
- **Chronic/Definitive**:
  - **Supportive Care**: The mainstay of treatment. Rest, hydration, antipyretics/analgesics (Paracetamol/Ibuprofen).
  - **Antivirals (Aciclovir)**: Not routinely recommended as they do not significantly alter the clinical course.
## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Reassure adolescents that fatigue may persist for weeks to months (post-viral fatigue).
## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- **CRITICAL Safety Netting**: Advise strictly **AGAINST contact sports** (e.g., rugby, martial arts) or heavy lifting for at least **4 weeks** (or until splenomegaly resolves on ultrasound) due to the risk of splenic rupture.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Generally self-limiting with complete recovery.
- **Complications**:
  - Splenic rupture (highest risk in weeks 2-3).
  - Airway obstruction.
  - Neurological: Guillain-Barré syndrome, meningoencephalitis, facial nerve palsy.
  - Haematological: Autoimmune haemolytic anaemia, thrombocytopenia.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Prescribing Amoxicillin for a teenager with a sore throat and exudate without considering EBV. *Pearl*: If you give Amoxicillin to a patient with EBV, they will almost certainly develop a florid, pruritic maculopapular rash.
- **Viva Question**: "What is the most important advice to give a 16-year-old rugby player diagnosed with infectious mononucleosis?" *Answer*: "He must avoid all contact sports for at least 4 weeks due to the risk of a fatal splenic rupture."
