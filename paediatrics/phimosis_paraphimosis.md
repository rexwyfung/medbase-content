# Phimosis & Paraphimosis (包莖及嵌頓包莖)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Definition of Phimosis
* **Phimosis (包莖)**: Inability to retract **foreskin (prepuce)** over **glans penis (龜頭)**.
* **Physiological Phimosis**: Normal in infants/young children; non-scarring.
* **Pathological Phimosis**: 2° to scarring/fibrosis.
### Definition of Paraphimosis
* **Paraphimosis (嵌頓包莖)**: **TRUE UROLOGICAL EMERGENCY**.
* Retracted foreskin trapped behind coronal sulcus → acts as a tourniquet.
### Local Epidemiology
* Common pediatric presentation in HA EDs & **MCHCs (母嬰健康院)**.
* ~90% of physiological phimosis resolves spontaneously by age 5.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Pathophysiology of Physiological Phimosis
* Foreskin naturally fused to glans at birth.
* Separation occurs naturally via:
  * Spontaneous erections.
  * Keratinization.
  * Accumulation of epithelial debris (**Smegma (包皮垢)**).
### Pathophysiology of Pathological Phimosis
* Chronic inflammation/infection (**Balanoposthitis (龜頭包皮炎)**) or forceful premature retraction → microtears → dense fibrotic scarring of preputial ring.
* **Balanitis Xerotica Obliterans (BXO) (乾燥性閉塞性龜頭炎)**: Penile lichen sclerosus → whitish plaque & stricture.
### Pathophysiology of Paraphimosis
* Tight foreskin retracted & left proximally → restricts venous/lymphatic return → massive glans/preputial **oedema (水腫)** → arterial compromise → ischemic necrosis of glans.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
### Presentation of Phimosis
* **Physiological**: 
  * Asymptomatic.
  * Massively "ballooning" of foreskin during voiding.
* **Pathological**: 
  * Painful erections.
  * Recurrent balanoposthitis.
  * Weak urinary stream.
### Presentation of Paraphimosis
* Extreme penile pain.
* Often post-caregiver cleaning or post-urinary catheterization.
* Distressed, screaming child holding crotch.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Signs of Pathological Phimosis
* Visible dense, white, unyielding fibrous ring at tip of foreskin (pathognomonic for BXO).
* Pinpoint meatus.
* Localized erythema.
* Purulent discharge.
### Signs of Paraphimosis
* Dusky, **cyanotic (發紺)** glans.
* Massive oedematous ring of fluid-filled foreskin proximal to glans.
* Exquisite tenderness on palpation.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Phimosis vs Paraphimosis Comparison
| Feature | **Phimosis (包莖)** | **Paraphimosis (嵌頓包莖)** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Foreskin Position** | Stuck forward (over glans) | Stuck backward (behind glans) |
| **Pain Level** | Painless (unless infected) | Exquisite, acute pain |
| **Glans Appearance** | Hidden | Exposed, swollen, dusky |
| **Urgency** | Elective | **UROLOGICAL EMERGENCY** |

### Paraphimosis vs Balanitis
* **Balanitis (龜頭炎)**: 
  * Pure inflammation/infection.
  * Glans is red/warm.
  * NO tight stricture ring.
  * NO ischemic changes.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
### Differentials for Penile Swelling & Pain
* **Insect Bite / Allergic Swelling**: 
  * Painless or pruritic.
  * Generalized swelling.
  * NO tight mechanical ring.
* **Hair Tourniquet Syndrome (頭髮纏繞綜合症)**: 
  * Strand of hair wrapped around coronal sulcus mimicking paraphimosis.
  * Requires meticulous inspection under magnification.
* **Zipper Injury**: 
  * Clear history of mechanical trauma.
  * Foreskin caught in zipper teeth.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
### Bedside Investigations
* **Clinical Diagnosis**: Purely clinical based on visual inspection.
* **CRITICAL**: DO NOT delay manual reduction for any investigations.
### Bloods & Imaging
* Not routinely indicated.
* Consider urine dipstick/culture ONLY if concurrent UTI suspected (post-reduction).

## 8. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Acute Management of Paraphimosis
* **Analgesia (止痛)**: 
  * Generous & immediate.
  * Penile nerve block (NO adrenaline) or IV **Morphine/Ketamine**.
* **Osmotic Therapy (滲透治療)**: 
  * Apply granulated sugar, 20% Mannitol-soaked gauze, or ice packs tightly around glans.
  * Leave for 20-30 mins.
  * Mechanism: Osmotically extracts fluid → ↓ edema → facilitates reduction.
* **Manual Reduction**: 
  * Place both thumbs firmly on glans.
  * Place index/middle fingers behind swollen foreskin.
  * Push glans inwards while pulling foreskin forwards (like turning sock inside out).
* **Surgical Intervention**: 
  * Emergency **Dorsal Slit (背側切開術)** under LA/GA by urology if manual reduction fails.
  * Elective circumcision subsequently.
### Management of Physiological Phimosis
* **Conservative**: 
  * Reassurance.
  * Advise caregivers NEVER to forcibly retract.
  * Normal gentle hygiene.
### Management of Pathological Phimosis
* **Medical (1st Line)**: 
  * Topical steroid cream (e.g., 0.05% **Betamethasone (倍他米松)**) daily for 4-8 weeks.
  * Softens fibrotic skin.
* **Surgical (2nd Line)**: 
  * Elective **Circumcision (割包皮)** if steroid fails or severe BXO present.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
### MCHC & Psychosocial Support
* **MCHC (母嬰健康院)** nurses provide vital education on normal foreskin hygiene.
* Counseling parents to alleviate anxiety regarding physiological ballooning.
* Avoidance of unnecessary surgical interventions.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Non-Accidental Injury (NAI) Indicators
* Severe bleeding, tearing, or bruising in young infant from forcible retraction.
* Unexplained/recurrent genital trauma.
* Requires gentle questioning of caregivers & potential **MSW/SWD** referral.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
### Complications of Paraphimosis
* **Gangrene (壞疽)** of glans penis.
* Autoamputation of glans (if ischemia prolonged).
### Complications of Phimosis
* Recurrent balanoposthitis.
* Increased risk of UTI.
* Obstructive uropathy (extremely rare).
### Prognosis
* Excellent if Paraphimosis is reduced promptly.
* Phimosis mostly resolves conservatively.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Trap: Examining a male patient, pulling back the foreskin to insert a Foley catheter, and forgetting to push the foreskin back forward when you are done. Pearl: Always immediately reduce the foreskin over the glans after examination or catheterization. This is the single most common iatrogenic cause of Paraphimosis and is entirely preventable.
* Viva Question: If manual reduction of paraphimosis fails despite adequate analgesia, what simple substance found in a hospital pantry can be used to shrink the swelling? Answer: Granulated sugar. It acts as a powerful osmotic agent to rapidly draw edema fluid out of the glans, facilitating reduction.
* Pitfall: Recommending circumcision for physiological phimosis in a toddler. Avoid unnecessary surgery; ~90% of physiological phimosis resolves spontaneously by 5 years of age.
