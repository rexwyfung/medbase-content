# Phimosis & Paraphimosis (包莖及嵌頓包莖)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Phimosis**: The inability to retract the foreskin (prepuce) over the glans penis. Physiologically normal in infants; pathological if driven by scarring.
- **Paraphimosis**: A TRUE UROLOGICAL EMERGENCY. The foreskin is retracted *behind* the glans (corona), gets hopelessly trapped, and acts as a tight tourniquet.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Phimosis**: At birth, the foreskin is naturally fused to the glans. Over the first few years of life, epithelial debris (smegma) helps naturally separate them. By age 5, ~90% of foreskins are fully retractable. Pathological phimosis occurs when trauma (forcing it back too early) or chronic infection causes dense fibrotic scarring of the preputial opening.
- **Paraphimosis**: Someone (often a caregiver cleaning the child, or a healthcare worker inserting a urinary catheter) retracts a tight foreskin but forgets to pull it back forward. The tight ring of foreskin severely compresses the venous and lymphatic return. The glans rapidly swells (edema), which traps the foreskin even tighter, eventually cutting off the arterial blood supply leading to ischemic necrosis of the glans.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- **Phimosis**: A 7-year-old whose foreskin "balloons" massively during voiding and cannot be pulled back.
- **Paraphimosis**: A 10-year-old presenting to the ED screaming in horrific pain, holding his crotch. His glans penis is dark purple, and there is a massive ring of swollen fluid-filled tissue just behind it.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Paraphimosis**: Intense pain. The glans is dusky, cyanotic, and extremely swollen. The foreskin forms a tight, hard ring proximal to the glans.
- **Pathological Phimosis**: A visible dense, white, unyielding fibrous ring at the tip of the foreskin.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from Balanitis: Balanitis is purely inflammation/infection without the mechanical "strangulation" seen in paraphimosis.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Insect bite / Allergic swelling (painless/pruritic, not a tight ring).
- Hair tourniquet syndrome (A strand of hair wraps around the coronal sulcus, mimicking paraphimosis. Must look very closely for a hair!).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- Purely a clinical diagnosis based entirely on inspection. Do not delay reduction for any investigations.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **PARAPHIMOSIS (Immediate Action)**:
  1.  **Analgesia**: Generous pain relief (often penile nerve block or IV morphine).
  2.  **Osmotic Therapy**: Apply granulated sugar, mannitol-soaked gauze, or ice packs tightly wrapped around the glans for 20-30 minutes. This osmotically drastically pulls fluid *out* of the glans, shrinking it enough to slip the foreskin over.
  3.  **Manual Reduction**: Place both thumbs firmly on the glans and index/middle fingers behind the swollen foreskin, and firmly push the glans *into* the foreskin like turning a sock inside out.
  4.  **Surgical**: If manual reduction fails, an emergency **Dorsal Slit** (surgically cutting the tight band under local anesthetic) is performed by urology, followed eventually by elective circumcision.
- **PHIMOSIS**:
  - Physiological: Leave it alone. Advise parents NEVER to forcibly retract it.
  - Pathological: Topical steroid cream (e.g., 0.05% Betamethasone) daily for 4-8 weeks helps soften the skin. If failed, circumcision.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Forcible retraction injuries causing severe bleeding in a young infant may warrant gentle questioning regarding caregivers' actions.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Paraphimosis: Gangrene and autoamputation of the glans penis if not reduced in time.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Examining a male patient, pulling back the foreskin to insert a Foley catheter, and forgetting to push the foreskin back forward when you are done. This is the single most common iatrogenic cause of Paraphimosis and is entirely preventable medical negligence.
- **Viva Q**: "If manual reduction of paraphimosis fails despite adequate analgesia, what simple substance found in a hospital pantry can be used to shrink the swelling?" Answer: Granulated sugar. It acts as a powerful osmotic agent to rapidly draw edema fluid out of the glans, facilitating reduction.
