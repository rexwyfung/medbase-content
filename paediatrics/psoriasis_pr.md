# Psoriasis & Pityriasis Rosea (銀屑病及玫瑰糠疹)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Psoriasis**: A chronic, genetically-predisposed, immune-mediated inflammatory skin disease causing epidermal hyperproliferation. Occurs in ~1-2% of children.
- **Pityriasis Rosea (PR)**: An acute, self-limiting, likely viral-triggered papulosquamous skin eruption. Very common in healthy adolescents and young adults (peaks at age 10-35).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Psoriasis**: Driven by heavily dysregulated T-cells (specifically Th17) releasing cytokines (TNF-alpha, IL-17, IL-23). This causes the skin cells (keratinocytes) to multiply wildly. Normal skin turns over in 28 days; psoriatic skin turns over in 3-5 days, piling up rapidly into thick silvery scales.
- **PR**: The exact etiology is unknown, but strongly associated with reactivation of Human Herpesvirus 6 (HHV-6) or HHV-7.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- **Psoriasis**: A 12-year-old girl with thick, itchy, red scaly patches persistently stuck over both her elbows and knees.
- **PR**: A 15-year-old boy who noticed a single large red scaly patch on his stomach a week ago. Yesterday, he suddenly broke out in a massive, sweeping rash of hundreds of smaller oval patches all over his back and chest. He feels perfectly well otherwise.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **PSORIASIS**:
  - **Plaque Psoriasis**: Well-demarcated, erythematous plaques covered in thick, **silvery-white scales**.
  - Classic distribution: **Extensor surfaces** (knees, elbows), scalp margin, and umbilicus.
  - **Auspitz Sign**: If you purposefully pick off a scale, it reveals tiny, pinpoint bleeding spots (due to dilated capillaries shooting up close to the surface in the hyperproliferative skin).
  - **Nail changes**: Pitting (tiny ice-pick holes in the nails), onycholysis (nail lifting), and "oil drop" discoloration.
  - **Guttate Psoriasis**: A specific pediatric variant triggered universally by a recent **Streptococcal throat infection**. Presents as a sudden "rain-drop" shower of hundreds of tiny scaly teardrop plaques all over the trunk.
- **PITYRIASIS ROSEA**:
  - **The Herald Patch**: The infection always starts with a single, large (2-5cm) oval, salmon-pink scaly plaque.
  - **The Eruption**: 1 to 2 weeks later, a massive secondary eruption occurs on the trunk. 
  - **Christmas Tree Pattern**: The secondary oval plaques align themselves perfectly along the skin cleavage lines (Langer's lines) on the back, angling downward like the branches of a pine tree.
  - Usually completely spares the face, palms, and soles.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from Tinea Corporis (Ringworm): The Herald patch of PR looks exactly like a large ringworm. However, PR is KOH negative, does not clear in the center as cleanly, and ringworm does not erupt into a massive Christmas tree pattern a week later.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Secondary Syphilis (Also causes a diffuse scaly rash, but critically involves the palms and soles! Adolescents with a diffuse rash should be checked for syphilis).
- Atopic Eczema (Involves flexures, not extensors).
- Tinea Corporis.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- Mostly clinical diagnoses.
- For Guttate Psoriasis: Antistreptolysin O (ASO) titer or throat swab to confirm the preceding Strep infection.
- Syphilis serology (VDRL/RPR) in sexually active adolescents if the PR rash involves palms/soles.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **PITYRIASIS ROSEA**:
  - **Active Reassurance**: The rash is entirely benign, not highly contagious, and **will completely self-resolve without any treatment in 6 to 8 weeks**. 
  - Symptomatic treatment for itch: Emollients, mild topical steroids, or oral antihistamines.
- **PSORIASIS**:
  - Mild: Emollients, **Topical Corticosteroids**, Vitamin D analogues (Calcipotriol).
  - Moderate/Severe: Narrowband UVB Phototherapy.
  - Refractory/Systemic: Methotrexate, Ciclosporin, or highly targeted Biologics (e.g., Adalimumab, Secukinumab).

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Severe impact on self-esteem for adolescents with psoriasis due to the highly visible, flaking skin lesions.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- N/A.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Psoriatic Arthritis (develops in up to 30% of psoriasis patients later in life).
- PR leaves no scars, but may leave temporary post-inflammatory hypopigmentation.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Giving a teenager with PR intensive oral antifungals for weeks because you mistook the Herald Patch for severe Tinea. Know the history (the Herald Patch always precedes the breakout).
- **Viva Q**: "A 9-year-old child presents with a sudden, full-body 'raindrop' shower of tiny scaly plaques. What vital piece of history from the past 2 weeks must you elicit to confirm the diagnosis?" Answer: You must ask if the child recently had a severe sore throat or tonsillitis. This is Guttate Psoriasis, which is classically triggered by a recent Group A Streptococcal (Strep) infection.
