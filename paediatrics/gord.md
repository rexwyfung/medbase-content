# Gastro-oesophageal Reflux Disease (GORD) (胃酸倒流 / 胃食管反流病)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: The involuntary passage of gastric contents into the esophagus. Becomes a "Disease" (GORD) when it causes troublesome symptoms or complications (e.g., esophagitis, poor weight gain, respiratory issues).
- **Local Epidemiology**: Extremely common. ~50% of infants <3 months have physiological reflux. Only a small fraction develops pathological GORD.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Generally due to transient lower esophageal sphincter relaxations (TLESR), an underdeveloped His angle, and a strictly liquid diet in infants lying mostly supine.
- Pathological GORD involves acidic burn to the esophageal mucosa.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- **Infants**: Frequent regurgitation after feeds, extreme crying/arching back during feeds (Sandifer syndrome), faltering growth.
- **Older Children**: Heartburn, epigastric pain, water brash, chronic night cough.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- Crying/irritability explicitly linked to feeding.
- Blood-tinged vomitus (sign of erosive esophagitis).
- Recurrent chest infections or aspiration pneumonia.
- Sandifer syndrome: spasmodic torsional dystonia with arching of the back (an attempt to stretch the esophagus to reduce acid burn).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Unlike pyloric stenosis, the vomiting in GORD is typically *not* projectile and usually start earlier than the classic 3-6 week window of PS. The baby with GORD is distressed; the baby with PS is immediately hungry again ("hungry vomiter").

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Pyloric Stenosis (projectile)
- Cow's Milk Protein Allergy (CMPA)
- Intestinal Malrotation / Volvulus (Rule out if bilious vomiting)
- Eosinophilic Oesophagitis

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**: Clinical diagnosis primarily using a validated questionnaire (I-GERQ).
- **Imaging**: Upper GI contrast study (Barium meal) - **NOT** to diagnose GORD, but specifically to *rule out* malrotation or gastric outlet obstruction.
- **Special**: 24-hour esophageal pH/impedance monitoring (Gold standard). Endoscopy if suspected esophagitis.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Step 1 (Conservative)**: Upright positioning post-feed. Feed thickeners (e.g., carob bean gum). Smaller, more frequent feeds. Trial of extensively hydrolyzed formula to rule out CMPA.
- **Step 2 (Medical)**: Acid suppression using PPIs (Omeprazole). H2 receptor antagonists are second line.
- **Step 3 (Surgical)**: Nissen fundoplication (wrapping the gastric fundus around the lower esophagus), usually reserved for severe neurodisability patients failing maximal therapy.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Dietetic input is vital for growth monitoring. MCHC monitors height/weight trajectories meticulously.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Force-feeding a resistant, screaming baby (secondary to GORD pain) can escalate to abuse. Parental frustration is immense; ask directly about coping.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Erosive esophagitis, strictures, Barrett's esophagus (rare in kids), aspiration pneumonia.
- 90% outgrow physiological reflux completely by 1 year as they spend more time upright and eat solid foods.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Ordering a Barium swallow to 'diagnose' reflux. The examiner will fail you. Barium swallows capture a 5-minute window and are useless for diagnosing intermittent GORD; they are used solely to rule out anatomical anomalies.
- **Viva Q**: "What is Sandifer syndrome?" Answer: Abnormal posturing and torticollis in infants with GORD, theorized as a reflex to protect the airway or clear esophageal acid.
