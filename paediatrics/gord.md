# Gastro-oesophageal Reflux Disease (GORD) (胃酸倒流 / 胃食管反流病)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition of Reflux vs. Disease
- **Physiological Reflux (生理性胃酸倒流)**: Involuntary passage of gastric contents → esophagus. Extremely common, benign, and self-limiting.
- **Pathological GORD (病理性胃食管反流病)**: Becomes a disease when reflux causes troublesome symptoms or complications (e.g., esophagitis, poor weight gain, respiratory issues).

### Local Epidemiology & Natural History
- **Prevalence**: ~50% of infants <3 months experience regular physiological regurgitation.
- **Resolution**: ~90% outgrow it spontaneously by 12 months (driven by ↑ upright posture, introduction of solid foods, and maturation of the lower esophageal sphincter).
- **Pathology**: Only a small fraction develops actual GORD requiring Hong Kong Hospital Authority (HA) specialist intervention.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Primary Pathophysiological Mechanisms
- **Sphincter Dysfunction**: Driven by frequent transient lower esophageal sphincter relaxations (TLESR) and an underdeveloped His angle.
- **Infant-Specific Exacerbators**: Strictly liquid diet, predominantly supine posture, and a short intra-abdominal esophagus.

### Pathological Tissue Damage
- **Mucosal Injury**: Prolonged exposure to gastric acid + pepsin → acidic burn to esophageal mucosa → **Esophagitis (食管炎)**.

### High-Risk Pediatric Populations
- **Neurological**: Cerebral palsy, severe neurodisability (very high risk for severe GORD).
- **Anatomical**: Post-repair esophageal atresia, diaphragmatic hernia, hiatus hernia.
- **Other**: Prematurity, childhood obesity.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Classic Infant Presentation
- Frequent non-forceful regurgitation/vomiting post-feed.
- Extreme crying/irritability explicitly linked to feeding times.
- **Faltering Growth (生長遲緩)** / poor weight gain.

### Classic Older Child Presentation
- Heartburn, epigastric pain, water brash.
- Chronic night cough, hoarseness.

### Red Flag Complication Signs
- **Hematemesis (吐血)** / blood-tinged vomitus → indicates erosive esophagitis.
- **Respiratory Signs**: Recurrent pulmonary aspiration (肺吸入) → recurrent pneumonia, chronic wheeze, apnoea (in preterms).
- **Sandifer Syndrome (山迪弗氏綜合徵)**: Spasmodic torsional dystonia / extreme arching of the back during feeds (a reflex to stretch the esophagus and relieve acid pain).
- **BRUE**: Brief Resolved Unexplained Events (formerly ALTE).

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Distinguishing GORD from Pyloric Stenosis
- **Timing**: GORD starts very early; **Pyloric Stenosis (幽門狹窄)** classically presents at 2–8 weeks of age.
- **Nature of Vomitus**: GORD is effortless, non-projectile regurgitation; PS is increasingly forceful, projectile vomiting.
- **Infant Demeanor**: The GORD baby is distressed and in pain; the PS baby is a "hungry vomiter" who immediately wants to feed again.
- **Physical Signs**: PS presents with visible gastric peristalsis and a palpable "olive" mass in the right upper quadrant.

## 5. Differential Diagnosis
### Common Differential Diagnoses Table
| Condition | Key Distinguishing Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Pyloric Stenosis (幽門狹窄)** | Projectile vomiting, hungry infant, palpable olive mass, hypochloremic hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis. |
| **Cow's Milk Protein Allergy (CMPA) (牛奶蛋白敏感)** | Eczema, bloody/mucus stools, wheeze, symptoms resolve on extensively hydrolyzed formula. |
| **Intestinal Malrotation / Volvulus (腸扭轉)** | **Bilious (green) vomiting (膽汁性嘔吐)**, acute surgical abdomen, hemodynamic shock. *Medical emergency!* |
| **Eosinophilic Oesophagitis (EoE) (嗜酸性粒細胞食管炎)** | Bolus dysphagia, food impaction, strong personal/family atopic history (asthma, eczema). |
| **Raised Intracranial Pressure (ICP)** | Bulging fontanelle, early morning vomiting, lethargy, seizures. |

## 6. Tiered Investigations
### Bedside Diagnostics
- **Clinical Diagnosis**: Primarily diagnosed clinically (e.g., using validated I-GERQ questionnaire). Routine investigations are NOT required for uncomplicated infant reflux.

### Imaging Modalities
- **Upper GI Contrast Study (Barium Meal) (鋇餐造影)**: NOT used to diagnose GORD. Used specifically to rule out anatomical anomalies (e.g., malrotation, strictures, hiatal hernia).

### Special / Gold Standard Investigations
- **24-hour pH/Impedance Monitoring (24小時食管pH及阻抗監測)**: The gold standard to quantify acid and non-acid reflux. Highly useful for atypical presentations, neurodisability, or treatment failure. Wireless pH capsules available.
- **Upper GI Endoscopy + Biopsy (胃鏡檢查)**: Indicated to identify macroscopic esophagitis and exclude EoE or *H. pylori*.

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Step 1: Conservative & Dietary Interventions
- **Positioning**: Maintain upright positioning post-feed.
- **Feeding Regimen**: Smaller, more frequent feeds. Avoid overfeeding.
- **Thickeners**: Add inert feed thickeners (e.g., Carobel / carob bean gum).
- **Alginates**: 1–2 week trial of alginate therapy (e.g., Gaviscon) to form a protective gel above stomach contents.
- **Dietary Trial**: 2-week trial of extensively hydrolyzed formula to rule out underlying CMPA.

### Step 2: Medical Therapy
- **Acid Suppression**: **Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs) (質子泵抑制劑)** e.g., Omeprazole are first-line for true GORD. H2 receptor antagonists (e.g., Ranitidine) act as a second-line alternative.
- **Prokinetics**: e.g., Domperidone. *HA Protocol Note*: Routine use is strongly discouraged due to poor efficacy evidence and significant risk of fatal cardiac arrhythmias (QT prolongation).

### Step 3: Surgical Options
- **Nissen Fundoplication (尼森胃底摺疊術)**: Wrapping the gastric fundus around the intra-abdominal esophagus (open or laparoscopic).
- **Indications**: Reserved for severe, life-threatening complications unresponsive to maximal medical therapy (e.g., recurrent aspiration pneumonia, severe strictures), typically in patients with severe neurodisability.

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Dietetic & Community Support
- **Dietitian (營養師)**: Vital for prescribing appropriate feed thickeners, ensuring caloric adequacy, and managing specialized hypoallergenic formulas.
- **MCHC (母嬰健康院)**: Essential for meticulous ongoing monitoring of height/weight trajectories to quickly detect faltering growth.

### Parental Education & Support
- **Reassurance**: Validate that the mess and smell of physiological reflux is frustrating but emphasize its benign, self-limiting nature to prevent maternal anxiety.

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Recognizing Parental Burnout and Abuse Risk
- **The Pain-Refusal Cycle**: Esophagitis causes severe pain → baby refuses feeds and screams → escalating parental frustration.
- **NAI Risk**: Exhausted caregivers force-feeding a resistant, screaming infant can rapidly escalate to physical abuse or Shaken Baby Syndrome. Directly assess parental coping mechanisms and aggressively involve Medical Social Workers (MSW) or SWD if burnout is evident.

## 10. Complications & Prognosis
### Major GORD Complications
- **Esophageal**: Erosive esophagitis, stricture formation, iron-deficiency anemia, Barrett's esophagus (rare in pediatrics).
- **Respiratory**: Recurrent aspiration pneumonia, chronic cough, apnoea (especially in premature neonates).

### Long-Term Prognosis
- **Excellent**: Uncomplicated physiological reflux carries a near 100% resolution rate by 12-18 months of age.
- **Refractory**: Neurodisabled children often suffer from lifelong, refractory GORD necessitating permanent PEG feeding or fundoplication.

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Trap: Ordering a Barium swallow to 'diagnose' reflux. Pearl: Barium swallows capture a 5-minute window and are completely useless for diagnosing intermittent GORD; they are used strictly to rule out anatomical anomalies like malrotation or strictures.
* Pitfall: Prescribing prokinetics (e.g., domperidone) routinely for reflux. This is outdated practice; they lack solid efficacy evidence and carry a significant risk of QT prolongation. PPIs remain the gold standard medical therapy.
* Trap: Attributing bilious (green) vomiting to severe GORD. Pearl: Bilious vomiting is NEVER caused by GORD. It is an absolute red flag for mechanical intestinal obstruction (e.g., malrotation/volvulus) and requires immediate surgical referral.
* Viva Question: What is Sandifer syndrome? Answer: Abnormal torsional neck posturing and arching of the back in infants with GORD, theorized as a physiological reflex to stretch the esophagus and clear painful gastric acid.
