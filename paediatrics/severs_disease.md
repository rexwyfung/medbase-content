# Sever's Disease (跟骨骨骺炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition of Sever's Disease
*   **Definition**: Traction apophysitis (**牽引性骨骺炎**) of the calcaneal tuberosity at the insertion of the Achilles tendon (**跟腱**).
*   **Nature**: Most common cause of paediatric heel pain (**足跟痛**); a self-limiting overuse injury.

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
*   **Demographics**: ↑ prevalence in growing, active children.
*   **Age of Onset**: Boys typically 10–14 yrs; Girls typically 8–12 yrs (correlates with pubertal growth spurts).
*   **HK Context**: Highly prevalent in students participating in high-impact school sports (e.g., basketball, athletics, football). Commonly presents to HA General Outpatient Clinics (GOPC) or Student Health Service (**學生健康服務**).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Pathophysiology of Calcaneal Apophysitis
*   **Bone vs. Soft Tissue Growth Mismatch**: During growth spurt, long bone (tibia/fibula) growth rate > muscle/tendon lengthening.
*   **Biomechanical Stress**: → Tight calf muscles (gastrocnemius/soleus) → ↑ baseline tension on Achilles tendon.
*   **Microtrauma**: Repetitive running/jumping → ↑ traction forces on the open, immature calcaneal apophysis (**生長板**) → repetitive microtrauma (**微小創傷**) & localized inflammation (**發炎**).

### Genetic and Biomechanical Predisposition
*   **Anatomical Factors**: Flat feet (pes planus / **扁平足**), high arches (pes cavus), or hindfoot valgus ↑ biomechanical stress.
*   **Genetics**: No direct Mendelian inheritance, but familial structural traits (e.g., foot biomechanics) contribute.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Principal Clinical Symptoms
*   Insidious onset posterior heel pain.
*   Pain ↑ during/after weight-bearing activities (running, jumping).
*   Pain ↓ with rest.
*   No systemic symptoms (no fever, no night sweats).

### Essential Clinical Signs
*   **+ve Calcaneal Squeeze Test (跟骨擠壓測試)**: Pain reproduced by medial-lateral compression of the posterior calcaneus.
*   Point tenderness directly over the Achilles tendon insertion.
*   Tightness of Achilles tendon (↓ ankle dorsiflexion <10°).
*   Antalgic gait (**避痛性步態**) or toe-walking to avoid heel strike.
*   No visible erythema or severe swelling (mild swelling occasionally present).

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Differentiating Sever's Disease from Other Heel Pain
*   **vs. Plantar Fasciitis (足底筋膜炎)**: Sever's pain is posterior and reproduced by medial-lateral calcaneal squeeze; Plantar fasciitis pain is inferior/plantar (medial calcaneal tubercle) and worse with first steps in the morning.
*   **vs. Achilles Tendinopathy (跟腱病變)**: Sever's pain is exactly at the bony insertion; Tendinopathy pain is 2-6 cm proximal to the insertion within the tendon body.
*   **vs. Calcaneal Stress Fracture (跟骨應力性骨折)**: Stress fractures present with abrupt ↑ in pain, constant pain, and +ve squeeze test but may have rest/night pain.
*   **vs. Osteomyelitis (骨髓炎)**: Osteomyelitis presents with fever, constant severe pain, inability to bear weight, erythema, and warmth.

## 5. Differential Diagnosis
### Differential Diagnosis Table for Paediatric Heel Pain
| Condition | Key Differentiating Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Sever's Disease (跟骨骨骺炎)** | Activity-related, posterior heel pain, +ve squeeze test, healthy growing child. |
| **Plantar Fasciitis (足底筋膜炎)** | Plantar heel pain, morning stiffness/pain on first steps. |
| **Achilles Tendinopathy (跟腱病變)** | Pain localized to tendon body, rare in pre-teens. |
| **Calcaneal Stress Fracture** | Sudden ↑ training, rest pain, exquisite focal tenderness. |
| **Osteomyelitis (骨髓炎)** | Fever, systemic toxicity, constant pain, erythema, warmth, non-weight bearing. |
| **Bone Tumour/Cyst (骨腫瘤)** | Unrelenting pain, night pain, visible mass (rare). |

## 6. Tiered Investigations
### Gold Standard Diagnostic Approach
*   Strictly a **Clinical Diagnosis** based on history and typical physical exam findings (+ve squeeze test).

### Indications for Imaging (X-ray)
*   **Routine X-ray (X光)**: **NOT** indicated for typical Sever's disease.
*   *Note*: Fragmentation and sclerosis of the calcaneal apophysis on X-ray is a **NORMAL physiological variant** in growing children and does *not* diagnose Sever's.
*   **Indications for X-ray**: Red flags present (night pain, severe rest pain, unilateral severe swelling, systemic symptoms) to rule out fractures, bone cysts, or tumours.

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### HA Primary Care Management Protocol
*   Managed primarily in GOPC/Primary Care.
*   Orthopaedic referral (**骨科轉介**) ONLY indicated if: conservative management fails >6 months, red flags present, or atypical presentation (e.g., unilateral severe night pain).

### Acute Symptom Relief (1st Line Management)
*   **Activity Modification (減少運動)**: Relative rest. Cease high-impact sports (jumping/running) until pain-free. Swimming/cycling permitted.
*   **Cryotherapy**: Ice massage (**冰敷**) for 15-20 mins post-activity.
*   **Analgesia**: Short course oral NSAIDs (**非類固醇消炎藥**) (e.g., Ibuprofen) or topical NSAID gel for acute flare-ups.

### Chronic / Rehabilitative Management
*   **Orthotics/Footwear**: 
    *   Silicone heel cups / Heel wedges (**足跟墊**) (lifts heel by ~1cm to ↓ Achilles tension).
    *   Supportive athletic shoes with shock-absorbing soles.
*   **Physiotherapy (物理治療)**: 
    *   Aggressive stretching (**拉筋**) of gastrocnemius-soleus complex and hamstrings.
    *   Eccentric calf strengthening once acute pain settles.

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Multidisciplinary Team (MDT) Involvement
*   **Physiotherapist**: Core for rehabilitation and biomechanical correction.
*   **Podiatrist (足病診療師)**: For custom orthotics if severe pes planus/cavus contributes to refractory pain.
*   **PE Teachers / Sports Coaches**: Essential to communicate the need for training modification and avoidance of punitive running.

### Psychosocial Support and Reassurance
*   **Parental Reassurance**: Crucial to explain the condition is benign, strictly self-limiting (**自限性**), and leaves no long-term deformity or disability.
*   **Child Support**: Manage frustration regarding temporary sports restriction. Set realistic return-to-play timelines.

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Non-Accidental Injury (NAI) Considerations in Heel Pain
*   Sever's disease itself is an overuse injury and rarely flags safeguarding concerns.
*   **Red Flags**: Consider NAI if there is an unexplained calcaneal fracture in a non-ambulant child, delayed presentation of severe trauma, or inconsistent history regarding the mechanism of severe heel injury.

## 10. Complications & Prognosis
### Natural History and Prognosis
*   **Excellent Prognosis**: 100% resolution.
*   **Timeline**: Symptoms fluctuate but permanently resolve once the calcaneal growth plate fuses (**生長板癒合**) (typically by age 15 in boys, age 13 in girls).

### Potential Complications
*   No long-term skeletal complications.
*   Short-term: Deconditioning or weight gain secondary to sports restriction if alternative low-impact activities are not encouraged.

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Viva Question: What is the most sensitive physical examination finding for Sever's disease? Answer: A positive calcaneal squeeze test (reproducing pain by compressing the medial and lateral aspects of the posterior calcaneus).
* Trap: Ordering bilateral heel X-rays for classic activity-related heel pain in a 10-year-old. Pearl: Sever's disease is a clinical diagnosis; X-rays expose the child to unnecessary radiation unless red flags are present.
* Pitfall: Misinterpreting a fragmented and sclerotic calcaneal apophysis on an X-ray as pathological. The fragmented appearance is a normal physiological variant in multiple ossification centers of a growing child and does not confirm Sever's disease.
* Viva Question: How do you differentiate the location of pain in Sever's disease versus plantar fasciitis? Answer: Sever's pain is posterior at the Achilles insertion, whereas plantar fasciitis pain is inferior/plantar at the medial calcaneal tubercle.
