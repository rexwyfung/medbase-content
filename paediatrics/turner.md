# Turner Syndrome (特納氏綜合症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A chromosomal disorder affecting females, caused by the complete or partial absence of one of the X chromosomes.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Affects approximately 1 in 2,500 live female births. It is a common cause of short stature and primary amenorrhoea in girls in Hong Kong.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Genetics**:
  - **Classic Turner Syndrome (45,X0)**: ~50% of cases. The entire second sex chromosome is missing. Usually due to a non-disjunction event during paternal meiosis.
  - **Mosaicism (e.g., 45,X0 / 46,XX)**: ~30% of cases. Some cells have a normal karyotype, others are missing an X. These girls often have a milder phenotype and may even have spontaneous puberty.
  - **Structural Abnormalities**: e.g., Isochromosome X or ring X chromosome.
- **Pathophysiology**: The absence of specific genes on the X chromosome (particularly the **SHOX gene**) leads to the characteristic physical features, short stature, and accelerated loss of ovarian follicles (gonadal dysgenesis).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Can present at various stages of life:
  - *Antenatal*: Cystic hygroma, hydrops fetalis on ultrasound.
  - *Neonatal*: Puffy hands and feet (lymphoedema), webbed neck, heart murmur (coarctation).
  - *Childhood*: Unexplained short stature (falling off the growth centiles).
  - *Adolescence*: Delayed puberty, primary amenorrhoea.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Growth**: **Short stature** is the most consistent feature (>95%).
- **Facial/Neck Features**: Webbed neck (pterygium colli), low posterior hairline, high-arched palate, micrognathia, prominent low-set ears.
- **Chest/Limbs**: Broad chest with widely spaced nipples (shield chest), increased carrying angle of the elbows (cubitus valgus), short fourth metacarpals.
- **Neonatal**: Congenital lymphoedema of the hands and feet.
- **Reproductive**: Streak ovaries (fibrous tissue replacing normal ovaries), leading to primary ovarian failure, lack of secondary sexual characteristics, and infertility.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Noonan Syndrome**: Often called "Male Turner Syndrome," but affects both sexes. Shares many physical features (webbed neck, short stature, cubitus valgus). However, Noonan syndrome is autosomal dominant (PTPN11 mutation), karyotype is normal (46,XX or 46,XY), and the classic cardiac defect is Pulmonary Stenosis (whereas Turner is Coarctation/Bicuspid Aortic Valve).

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Familial short stature.
- Constitutional delay of growth and puberty.
- Noonan Syndrome.
- Hypothyroidism.
- Growth Hormone Deficiency.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Karyotype (Chromosomal Analysis)**: **The definitive diagnostic test**. A blood test to confirm 45,X0 or mosaicism.
- **Echocardiogram**: Mandatory for all patients at diagnosis to screen for congenital heart defects (present in ~30%).
  - *Classic defects*: **Bicuspid Aortic Valve** (most common) and **Coarctation of the Aorta**.
- **Renal Ultrasound**: To screen for structural anomalies (e.g., horseshoe kidney, present in ~30%).
- **Bloods**:
  - Thyroid function tests (high risk of autoimmune Hashimoto's thyroiditis).
  - FSH and LH (will be markedly elevated in adolescence due to primary ovarian failure - hypergonadotropic hypogonadism).
  - Coeliac screen (increased risk).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Growth Hormone (GH) Therapy**: Started early in childhood (often around 4-6 years) to maximize final adult height.
- **Oestrogen Replacement Therapy**: Started around 11-12 years of age to induce puberty (breast development) and promote bone health (prevent osteoporosis). Progesterone is added later to induce regular withdrawal bleeds and protect the endometrium.
- **Fertility**: Most women are infertile. Pregnancy is possible via IVF with donor eggs, but carries a very high risk of aortic dissection during pregnancy.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Paediatric Endocrinology**: The primary managing specialty.
- **Cardiology**: Lifelong monitoring is required due to the ongoing risk of aortic root dilation and fatal aortic dissection, even in adulthood.
- **Psychology**: Support for issues related to self-esteem, short stature, and infertility. Intelligence is usually normal, but there may be specific learning difficulties (e.g., spatial awareness, mathematics).

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Cardiovascular**: Aortic root dilation, aortic dissection, hypertension.
- **Endocrine**: Hypothyroidism, Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus, Osteoporosis.
- **ENT**: Recurrent otitis media leading to conductive hearing loss.
- **Prognosis**: Life expectancy is slightly reduced, primarily due to cardiovascular complications. With appropriate hormone replacement, patients lead normal, productive lives.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "What are the two most common congenital heart defects associated with Turner Syndrome?" (Bicuspid Aortic Valve and Coarctation of the Aorta).
- *Viva*: "A 14-year-old girl is investigated for primary amenorrhoea. She is below the 3rd centile for height. Her FSH and LH levels are markedly elevated. What is the most likely diagnosis, and what is the definitive test?" (Turner Syndrome. The definitive test is a Karyotype).
- *Pitfall*: Forgetting to arrange a baseline Echocardiogram and Renal Ultrasound in a newly diagnosed patient with Turner Syndrome, as structural anomalies in these organs are extremely common and potentially life-threatening.
