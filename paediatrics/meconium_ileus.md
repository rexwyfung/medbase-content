# Meconium Ileus (胎糞性腸阻塞)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Neonatal bowel obstruction caused by impaction of thick, viscid meconium in the terminal ileum.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Exceedingly rare in the Chinese population of HK compared to Caucasians, but Cystic Fibrosis (CF) still exists and must be suspected.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Highly sensitive, early marker of **Cystic Fibrosis (CF)**.
- Mutations in the CFTR gene reduce chloride and water secretion in the intestine. Pancreatic insufficiency limits digestive enzymes. Thus, meconium becomes extremely dehydrated, protein-rich, and putty-like, physically obstructing the ileum.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Usually within the first 24-48 hours of life. 
- Failure to pass meconium, followed by explosive bilious vomiting and immense abdominal distension (shiny, tight abdomen).

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- Palpable doughy loops of bowel in the right iliac fossa.
- Rectal examination yields an empty ampulla with normal tone, often passing only a "meconium plug" of grey/white mucus.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Hirschsprung's disease presents similarly, but rectal examination in HD often yields a "squirt" of liquid stool/gas, unlike the firm rubbery pellets in meconium ileus.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Ileal Atresia
- Hirschsprung's Disease
- Meconium Plug Syndrome (associated with prematurity or maternal diabetes, resolves with enema, not CF).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Imaging**: Plain AXR shows dilated loops with variable air-fluid levels. Characteristic "soap-bubble" or "ground-glass" appearance (Neuhauser's sign) in the right lower quadrant due to gas mixing with tenacious meconium.
- **Special**: 
  - Water-soluble contrast enema (Gastrografin): Confirms diagnosis (shows a microcolon from disuse, and pellet-like meconium in terminal ileum).
  - Confirmatory tests for CF: Sweat chloride test (once large enough), CFTR genetic screening.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Medical (1st line for uncomplicated cases)**: Hyperosmolar Gastrografin enema. It draws fluid into the bowel lumen, breaking up the meconium. Often repeated sequentially over days. Requires intense IV fluid support.
- **Surgical**: Required if perforation, peritonitis, or failed enema. Involves enterotomy to wash out meconium or establishing an temporary ileostomy.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Requires immediate referral to a specialized Cystic Fibrosis MDT (Pulmonologist, Physiotherapist, Dietitian, MSW). 
- **Psychosocial**: The dual shock of neonatal surgery and a lifelong limiting genetic diagnosis requires intense, structured bereavement and coping counseling.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Not NAI related, but parental adherence to complex future chest physiotherapy rules must be monitored.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Immediate: Bowel perforation (meconium peritonitis), volvulus.
- Long-term: Dictated by the underlying Cystic Fibrosis (progressive lung disease, pancreatic exocrine insufficiency).

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Viva Q**: "Why must a baby receiving a Gastrografin enema have extensive IV access?" Answer: Because Gastrografin is highly concentrated (hyperosmolar). It rapidly shifts intravascular water into the bowel lumen, which can cause profound life-threatening hypovolaemic shock in a neonate if they aren't aggressively pre-hydrated.
