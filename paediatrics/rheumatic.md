# Acute Rheumatic Fever (ARF) (急性風濕熱)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A multisystem, autoimmune inflammatory disease that occurs as a delayed, non-suppurative complication of a Group A Beta-Haemolytic Streptococcus (GAS) pharyngeal infection.
- **Local Epidemiology**: The incidence in Hong Kong has plummeted over the past few decades due to improved living conditions and prompt antibiotic treatment of "strep throat." It is now rare, but remains a major cause of acquired heart disease globally in developing nations.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Molecular Mimicry**: The core mechanism. 
  - A susceptible host contracts a GAS pharyngitis (Note: GAS skin infections/impetigo do *not* typically cause ARF, unlike APSGN).
  - The immune system produces antibodies against the M protein of the Streptococcus.
  - These antibodies cross-react with structurally similar human tissues, particularly the myocardium, heart valves, synovium, skin, and basal ganglia.
  - This leads to severe, sterile inflammation in these target organs 2-4 weeks after the initial throat infection.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- A child (typically 5-15 years old) presents with fever, severe joint pain, and possibly a rash or abnormal movements, 2 to 4 weeks after an episode of sore throat.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs (The Revised Jones Criteria)
Diagnosis requires evidence of preceding GAS infection PLUS either 2 Major criteria OR 1 Major + 2 Minor criteria.
- **Major Criteria (J♥NES)**:
  1. **J**oints (Migratory Polyarthritis): Extremely painful, swollen, red, hot large joints (knees, ankles, wrists). The inflammation moves from one joint to another over days. Exquisitely responsive to NSAIDs/Aspirin.
  2. **♥** (Carditis): The most serious manifestation. Can involve the endocardium (valvulitis, classically mitral regurgitation), myocardium, or pericardium. Presents with a new murmur, tachycardia out of proportion to fever, or heart failure.
  3. **N**odules (Subcutaneous): Firm, painless nodules over extensor surfaces of joints or the spine. Rare.
  4. **E**rythema Marginatum: A classic, non-pruritic, transient, pink rash with pale centres and serpiginous (snake-like) margins, usually on the trunk. Rare.
  5. **S**ydenham Chorea (St. Vitus Dance): A late manifestation (months later). Involuntary, purposeless, rapid, jerking movements of the face and limbs, often accompanied by emotional lability.
- **Minor Criteria**:
  - Fever.
  - Arthralgia (if arthritis is not a major criterion).
  - Elevated acute phase reactants (ESR or CRP).
  - Prolonged PR interval on ECG.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA)**: Arthritis lasts > 6 weeks, is not typically migratory, and lacks the preceding strep infection, carditis, or chorea.
- **Septic Arthritis**: Usually monoarticular (one joint), extremely high fever, and joint aspiration yields pus. ARF is polyarticular and sterile.
- **Post-Streptococcal Reactive Arthritis (PSRA)**: Arthritis following a strep infection but does not fulfill the Jones criteria (no carditis, chorea, etc.).

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Septic arthritis.
- Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA).
- Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE).
- Kawasaki Disease.
- Infective Endocarditis.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Evidence of Preceding GAS Infection (Mandatory)**:
  - Elevated or rising **Anti-Streptolysin O Titre (ASOT)** or Anti-DNase B.
  - Positive throat swab culture or rapid antigen test for GAS.
- **Bloods**: Markedly elevated ESR and CRP. FBC (leukocytosis).
- **Echocardiogram**: Mandatory for all suspected cases to detect subclinical carditis (valvulitis, usually mitral or aortic regurgitation).
- **ECG**: To check for a prolonged PR interval or signs of pericarditis.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Eradicate the Streptococcus**: A single dose of IM Benzathine Penicillin G or a 10-day course of oral Penicillin V, regardless of throat swab results.
- **Anti-inflammatory Therapy**:
  - *Arthritis only*: High-dose **NSAIDs** (e.g., Naproxen) or Aspirin. The arthritis responds dramatically within 48 hours; failure to respond should make you question the diagnosis.
  - *Severe Carditis*: High-dose **Corticosteroids** (Prednisolone) are used to suppress severe cardiac inflammation and heart failure.
- **Management of Chorea**: Rest, calm environment. Haloperidol or Valproic acid for severe, disabling movements.
- **Secondary Prophylaxis (Crucial)**:
  - Patients who have had ARF are at extremely high risk of recurrence with subsequent strep infections, which causes cumulative, irreversible valve damage.
  - **IM Benzathine Penicillin G every 3-4 weeks** (or daily oral Penicillin V).
  - *Duration*: 
    - No carditis: Until age 21 or for 5 years (whichever is longer).
    - Mild carditis: Until age 21 or for 10 years.
    - Severe carditis/valve damage: Until age 40 or lifelong.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Paediatric Cardiology**: Long-term follow-up for Rheumatic Heart Disease.
- **Compliance**: Ensuring compliance with painful monthly IM penicillin injections for years is a major challenge requiring significant nursing and psychosocial support.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Rheumatic Heart Disease (RHD)**: The long-term, permanent consequence of ARF carditis. Chronic scarring and fibrosis of the valves lead to **Mitral Stenosis** (the classic late lesion, often presenting in adulthood), mitral regurgitation, or aortic valve disease. May require valve replacement surgery.
- **Prognosis**: Depends entirely on the severity of the initial carditis and compliance with secondary prophylaxis to prevent recurrences.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "What are the five major Jones criteria for diagnosing Acute Rheumatic Fever?" (Migratory polyarthritis, Carditis, Subcutaneous nodules, Erythema marginatum, Sydenham chorea).
- *Viva*: "A 10-year-old is diagnosed with ARF with mild carditis. How long must they receive secondary penicillin prophylaxis?" (Until they are 21 years old, or for 10 years after the attack, whichever is longer, to prevent recurrent attacks and cumulative valve damage).
- *Pitfall*: Confusing the pathogenesis of ARF with APSGN. ARF is caused by molecular mimicry (antibodies attacking host tissue), whereas APSGN is caused by immune complex deposition in the kidneys.
