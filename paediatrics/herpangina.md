# Herpangina (疱疹性咽峽炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: An acute febrile viral illness causing painful vesicular eruptions on the posterior oropharynx.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Extremely common in Hong Kong toddlers, especially clustered in summer/autumn outbreaks at kindergartens.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Typically caused by **Coxsackie A viruses** (A1-A10, A16, A22) and other Enteroviruses.
- Transmitted via the fecal-oral route and respiratory droplets.
- The virus selectively infects the squamous epithelium of the posterior oropharynx (tonsils, uvula, soft palate).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- A 2-year-old child who abruptly spikes a high fever (up to 40°C), cries inconsolably during meals, and starts drooling because swallowing is too painful.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- Sudden high fever.
- **Oral examination**: 1-2 mm greyish vesicles on an erythematous base exclusively on the **posterior** structures: anterior tonsillar pillars, soft palate, uvula, and posterior pharynx. (These vesicles rapidly rupture into shallow ulcers).
- Drooling, outright refusal to eat or drink.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Unlike Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD)**: HFMD (often Coxsackie A16 or EV71) involves the *anterior* mouth (tongue, buccal mucosa, hard palate) PLUS lesions on the palms, soles, and buttocks. Herpangina is ONLY the throat.
- **Unlike Herpetic Gingivostomatitis (HSV-1)**: HSV-1 causes horrific, bleeding, swollen gums (gingivitis) and lesions on the lips and anterior tongue. Herpangina spares the gums and lips.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD)
- Herpetic Gingivostomatitis (HSV)
- Streptococcal Pharyngitis / Tonsillitis
- Aphthous ulcers

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- Purely a clinical diagnosis based on the classic anatomical location of the ulcers.
- Throat swab for Enterovirus PCR if diagnosis is unclear or the child is profoundly unwell.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Supportive**: Hydration is the absolute key. 
- **Analgesia**: High-dose oral Paracetamol/Ibuprofen given 30 mins before attempting feeds. "Magic mouthwash" (e.g., Difflam/Benzydamine spray if older) or topical teething gels (lignocaine based, use cautiously to avoid toxicity).
- **Diet**: Cold, soft foods (ice cream, popsicles, cold milk). Avoid acidic citrus juices or salty broths which burn the ulcers.
- IV fluids only if the child completely stops drinking and becomes clinically dehydrated.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- High infectivity requires strict school exclusion until the fever subsides and ulcers heal (usually about 1 week).

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- N/A.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Excellent prognosis. The ulcers heal without scarring in 7-10 days.
- Main complication is moderate-to-severe dehydration requiring hospital admission.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Calling it "HSV infection" because it has "Herp" in the name. It is an Enterovirus.
- **Viva Q**: "A mother brings her 3-year-old who has high fever and ulcers in the mouth. How does your physical exam differentiate between the three main causes?" Answer: If the gums are bleeding and swollen, it's HSV. If the ulcers are on the tonsils/uvula and the child has a normal body, it's Herpangina. If there are also spots on the palms, soles, or buttocks, it's HFMD.
