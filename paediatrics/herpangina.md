# Herpangina (疱疹性咽峽炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Herpangina
* Acute, self-limiting febrile viral illness
* Characterized by painful vesicular eruptions & shallow ulcers localized to the **posterior oropharynx (後咽部)**

### Hong Kong Local Epidemiology
* **Peak Seasons**: Endemic in HK; major peaks in Summer & Autumn (May → October)
* **Target Demographic**: Highly contagious among toddlers & preschoolers (typically 1–5 yrs)
* **Outbreak Clusters**: Frequently causes institutional outbreaks in HK **kindergartens (幼稚園)** & childcare centres
* **Public Health**: Significant institutional outbreaks require notification to HK **Centre for Health Protection (CHP) (衞生防護中心)**

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics

### Causative Pathogens
* **Primary**: **Coxsackie A viruses (柯薩奇病毒 A型)** (predominantly A1–A10, A16, A22)
* **Secondary**: Coxsackie B viruses, Enterovirus 71 (EV71), Echoviruses

### Transmission & Incubation
* **Incubation Period**: 3–5 days
* **Routes of Transmission**:
  * **Fecal-oral route (糞口傳播)** (most common)
  * Respiratory droplets
  * Direct contact with vesicular fluid or contaminated fomites
* **Viral Shedding**: Can shed in feces for weeks post-infection

### Viral Pathogenesis
* Virus enters via oral/respiratory tract → local replication in lymphoid tissue (tonsils/pharynx)
* Selectively targets & destroys squamous epithelium of **posterior oropharynx structures** → vesicle formation → rapid rupture → painful shallow ulcerations

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Classic Clinical Presentation
* Abrupt onset of **high fever (高燒)** (often up to 39–40°C)
* **Odynophagia (吞嚥痛)** → refusal to feed/drink, inconsolable crying during meals
* Excessive drooling due to pain
* Constitutional symptoms: Malaise, headache, vomiting, neck pain

### Pathognomonic Oral Signs
* **Early Stage**: 1–2 mm greyish papules/vesicles on an erythematous base
* **Late Stage**: Rupture rapidly into shallow, punched-out ulcers (up to 5 mm) with red halos
* **Anatomical Distribution (CRITICAL)**: Strictly confined to posterior structures:
  * **Anterior tonsillar pillars (扁桃體前柱)**
  * **Soft palate (軟腭)**
  * **Uvula (懸雍垂)**
  * **Posterior pharyngeal wall (咽後壁)**

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)

### Herpangina vs Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD)
* **Herpangina**: Lesions strictly isolated to **posterior** oropharynx. NO skin rash.
* **HFMD (手足口病)**: Lesions involve **anterior** mouth (tongue, buccal mucosa, hard palate) PLUS characteristic maculopapular/vesicular rash on **palms, soles, buttocks**.

### Herpangina vs Herpetic Gingivostomatitis (HSV-1)
* **Herpangina**: Spares gums and lips. Caused by Enterovirus.
* **Herpetic Gingivostomatitis (疱疹性齒齦口腔炎)**: Caused by HSV-1. Severe **gingivitis (牙齦炎)** (swollen, bleeding, friable gums), lesions on lips, anterior tongue, and perioral skin.

## 5. Differential Diagnosis

### Key Differentials for Pediatric Oral Ulcers
| Diagnosis | Pathogen | Key Differentiating Clinical Features |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Herpangina** | Coxsackie A | Ulcers on **posterior** pharynx only. High fever. No body rash. |
| **HFMD** | Coxsackie A16, EV71 | Ulcers in **anterior** mouth. Vesicles on **palms, soles, buttocks**. |
| **Herpetic Gingivostomatitis** | HSV-1 | Bleeding/swollen gums. Ulcers on lips/anterior tongue. Perioral crusting. |
| **Streptococcal Pharyngitis** | Group A Strep | Exudative tonsillitis. Petechiae on palate. Cervical lymphadenopathy. No vesicles. |
| **Aphthous Ulcers (Canker sores)** | Idiopathic / Autoimmune | Usually afebrile. 1–2 painful ulcers on buccal mucosa/lips. Non-infectious. |

## 6. Tiered Investigations

### Gold Standard Clinical Diagnosis
* Purely a **clinical diagnosis** based on classic anatomical distribution of ulcers and history.
* Routine bloods (CBP, CRP) generally NOT indicated unless ruling out bacterial sepsis.

### Confirmatory Virological Tests (If severe/atypical)
* **Throat Swab / Stool Sample**: Multiplex PCR for Enterovirus / Coxsackie typing
* **Indication in HA**: Only performed if child is severely unwell, suspected EV71 neurological complications (e.g., encephalitis), or for CHP outbreak tracing.

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)

### First-Line Acute Management (Supportive)
* **Hydration**: Absolute priority. Encourage frequent, small sips of fluid.
* **Dietary Modification**: 
  * +ve: Cold, soft foods (ice cream, jelly, cold milk)
  * -ve: Avoid acidic, citrus, spicy, or salty foods (exacerbates ulcer pain)

### Pharmacological Analgesia
* **Systemic**: Regular high-dose oral **Paracetamol (撲熱息痛)** & **Ibuprofen (布洛芬)** (administer 30 mins prior to feeds)
* **Topical**: 
  * "Magic Mouthwash" / **Benzydamine (Difflam) spray**: For older children (anesthetic/anti-inflammatory)
  * Topical lignocaine gels (use with extreme caution in infants → risk of systemic toxicity/seizures if overdosed)

### Indications for Hospital Admission & IV Fluids
* Clinical signs of moderate-to-severe **dehydration (脫水)** (↓ skin turgor, sunken eyes, dry mucous membranes)
* Complete refusal to drink → prolonged ↓ urine output (<1ml/kg/hr)
* Uncontrollable fever or lethargy
* **HA Protocol**: Admit for **IV fluid rehydration (靜脈輸液)** (e.g., D5 0.45% NaCl + KCl) until oral intake is re-established.

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care

### Infection Control & CHP School Exclusion
* Highly contagious. Strict hand hygiene (soap & water; Enteroviruses are somewhat resistant to alcohol sanitizers).
* **School Exclusion Protocol**: Child MUST be excluded from kindergarten/nursery until fever subsides completely AND all vesicular lesions have healed (usually minimum 7 days).

### Parental Education & Reassurance
* Educate parents on monitoring warning signs of dehydration (number of wet diapers).
* Reassure that symptoms peak at days 2-3 and spontaneously resolve.

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags

### NAI Considerations in Oral Lesions
* While Herpangina is definitively organic, always carefully document oral lesions.
* **Red Flag**: Traumatic mouth ulcers or palatal burns (e.g., from forced feeding of scalding food/liquids) can superficially mimic infectious ulcers. Look for geometric burn borders or concurrent frenulum tears.

## 10. Complications & Prognosis

### Clinical Prognosis
* Excellent. Self-limiting disease.
* Fever resolves in 2–4 days; ulcers heal completely without scarring in 7–10 days.

### Potential Complications
* **Dehydration**: Most common complication requiring HA ward admission.
* **Neurological (Rare)**: Aseptic meningitis, encephalitis, or acute flaccid paralysis (extremely rare in Herpangina; much higher risk if HFMD caused by EV71).

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: A 3-year-old presents with fever and drooling. On examination, there are greyish vesicles on the soft palate and anterior tonsillar pillars. The buccal mucosa, lips, and gums are clear. What is the most likely pathogen? Answer: Coxsackie A virus (causing Herpangina).
* Trap: Prescribing Acyclovir or viewing it as a "Herpes" virus infection because of the prefix "Herp-". Pearl: Herpangina is caused by an Enterovirus (Coxsackie A); Acyclovir is entirely ineffective. Treatment is strictly supportive.
* Pitfall: Mistaking Herpangina for Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease in clinical charting. Always explicitly document the absence of palm, sole, and buttock lesions, and confirm the anterior mouth is spared to secure the Herpangina diagnosis.
* Viva Question: What is the most crucial clinical sign to differentiate Herpetic Gingivostomatitis from Herpangina during an oral examination? Answer: The presence of severely inflamed, bleeding gums (gingivitis) and lesions on the lips/anterior tongue strictly points to HSV-1, whereas Herpangina spares the gums and is confined to the posterior oropharynx.
