# Global Developmental Delay (GDD) (整體發展遲緩)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A significant delay (defined as performance >= 2 standard deviations below the age-appropriate mean) in **two or more** developmental domains: Gross/Fine Motor, Speech/Language, Cognition, Social/Personal, and Activities of Daily Living.
- **Terminology**: The term "GDD" is reserved for children **under 5 years of age**. For children over 5, the term "Intellectual Disability" (ID) is used, as IQ testing becomes more reliable.
- **Local Epidemiology**: A very common reason for referral to Child Assessment Centres (CAC) under the Department of Health in Hong Kong. Affects ~1-3% of children.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
The aetiology is highly heterogeneous and can occur at any stage of development:
- **Prenatal (Most common)**: 
  - *Genetic/Chromosomal*: Down syndrome, Fragile X syndrome, Microdeletions/duplications.
  - *Congenital Infections*: TORCH (Toxoplasmosis, Rubella, CMV, HSV, Syphilis).
  - *Teratogens*: Fetal Alcohol Spectrum Disorder, maternal anticonvulsant use.
  - *Brain Malformations*: Cortical dysplasia, holoprosencephaly.
- **Perinatal**: Hypoxic Ischaemic Encephalopathy (HIE), extreme prematurity, intraventricular haemorrhage, severe neonatal jaundice (Kernicterus).
- **Postnatal**: CNS infections (Meningitis/Encephalitis), traumatic brain injury, severe psychosocial deprivation/neglect, metabolic disorders (e.g., Congenital Hypothyroidism, Inborn Errors of Metabolism).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Usually identified by parents or Maternal and Child Health Centre (MCHC) nurses during routine developmental surveillance.
- The child fails to meet expected milestones (e.g., not sitting unsupported by 9 months, not walking independently by 18 months, no meaningful single words by 18 months).

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
The physical examination aims to find clues to the underlying aetiology:
- **Neurological**: Hypotonia (common in genetic syndromes), spasticity/asymmetry (Cerebral Palsy).
- **Dysmorphic Features**: Suggests a genetic or chromosomal syndrome.
- **Neurocutaneous Stigmata**: Café-au-lait macules (Neurofibromatosis type 1), Ash-leaf spots (Tuberous Sclerosis).
- **Growth Parameters**: Microcephaly (congenital infection, genetic) or Macrocephaly (hydrocephalus, specific syndromes).
- **Organomegaly**: Hepatosplenomegaly suggests a storage disorder (e.g., Mucopolysaccharidosis).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Specific Language Impairment**: Delay is isolated *only* to speech and language. Motor, cognitive, and social skills are normal.
- **Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)**: Primary deficits are in social communication and the presence of restricted/repetitive behaviors. While GDD and ASD frequently co-exist, a child with pure GDD is usually socially engaged and attempts to communicate (e.g., pointing, eye contact) despite lacking words.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Isolated developmental delay (e.g., isolated motor delay, isolated speech delay due to hearing loss).
- Autism Spectrum Disorder.
- Cerebral Palsy.
- **Progressive Neurodegenerative Disorder**: *Crucial to differentiate.* GDD is a failure to acquire milestones. Losing previously acquired milestones (Regression) is a major red flag for a progressive metabolic or neurodegenerative disease.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **First-Line (Mandatory for ALL)**:
  - **Formal Hearing (Audiology) and Vision Assessment**. You cannot diagnose a central delay until you prove the sensory inputs are intact.
- **Genetic Testing**:
  - **Chromosomal Microarray (CMA)**: The first-line genetic test for unexplained GDD/ID.
  - **Fragile X DNA testing**: Especially in boys.
- **Metabolic/Bloods**:
  - Thyroid Function Tests (TFTs) - treatable cause.
  - Creatine Kinase (CK) - to rule out Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy in boys presenting with gross motor delay.
  - Basic metabolic panel, lead levels (if risk factors).
- **Neuroimaging (MRI Brain)**: Indicated if there are focal neurological signs, microcephaly, macrocephaly, or seizures.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Treat the Underlying Cause**: If identified (e.g., Thyroxine for hypothyroidism, dietary restriction for PKU).
- **Early Intervention (The Cornerstone)**:
  - Referral to a **Child Assessment Centre (CAC)** for formal multidisciplinary evaluation.
  - Placement in an Early Education and Training Centre (EETC) or Special Child Care Centre (SCCC) for intensive therapy.
  - **Therapies**: Physiotherapy (motor skills), Occupational Therapy (fine motor, sensory integration, ADLs), Speech Therapy (communication, swallowing).

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Medical Social Worker (MSW)**: Essential for navigating the Social Welfare Department (SWD) system for school placements and applying for the Disability Allowance.
- **Psychological Support**: Parents often experience a grieving process (denial, anger, acceptance) over the loss of the "perfect child." Support groups are vital.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Severe psychosocial deprivation, neglect, or abuse can cause profound developmental delay (an environmental aetiology). Assess the home environment and parent-child interaction carefully.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Prognosis**: Depends entirely on the underlying aetiology. Early, intensive intervention maximizes the child's neuroplasticity and long-term potential.
- **Complications**: Behavioral issues, learning difficulties, epilepsy, feeding difficulties.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "A 2-year-old boy presents with no speech. What is the absolute most important first investigation?" (A formal hearing test / Audiology assessment. Never assume it's autism or GDD without ruling out deafness).
- *Viva*: "What is the difference between developmental delay and developmental regression?" (Delay means the child is slow to acquire milestones. Regression means the child is losing milestones they previously had. Regression is a massive red flag indicating a progressive neurodegenerative or metabolic disorder, requiring urgent investigation).
- *Pitfall*: Reassuring parents that a child will "catch up" without arranging proper hearing tests and CAC referral.
