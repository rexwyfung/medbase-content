# Neuroblastoma (神經母細胞瘤)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: An embryonal malignancy of the sympathetic nervous system arising from neuroblasts (pluripotent sympathetic cells). It is the most common extracranial solid tumour in childhood.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Accounts for ~8-10% of childhood cancers. The median age at diagnosis is 1-2 years. It is infamous for its highly variable clinical course, ranging from spontaneous regression to relentless, fatal progression.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Pathophysiology**: Tumours can arise anywhere along the sympathetic chain. 
  - *Abdomen (70%)*: Most commonly from the adrenal medulla, or paraspinal sympathetic ganglia.
  - *Chest (20%)*: Posterior mediastinum.
  - *Neck/Pelvis (10%)*.
- **Metastasis**: Highly aggressive; >50% of patients have metastatic disease at presentation. Spreads via blood and lymphatics to bone marrow, bone (cortex), liver, and skin.
- **Genetics (Crucial for Prognosis)**:
  - **MYCN Amplification**: The most important negative prognostic marker. Indicates highly aggressive disease and poor survival, regardless of age or stage.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Presentation is notoriously diverse ("the great mimicker") and depends entirely on the site of the primary tumour and the presence of metastases.
- Unlike Wilms tumour, children with neuroblastoma usually look systemically unwell (weight loss, lethargy, fever).

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Abdominal Primary**: A firm, irregular, nodular abdominal mass that **frequently crosses the midline**.
- **Thoracic Primary**: Often asymptomatic, picked up on CXR. May cause respiratory distress or Horner syndrome (ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis) if compressing the cervical sympathetic chain.
- **Paraspinal Primary**: Can grow through the intervertebral foramina into the spinal canal ("dumbbell tumour"), causing spinal cord compression (back pain, lower limb weakness, bowel/bladder dysfunction).
- **Metastatic Signs (Very Common)**:
  - *Bone/Bone Marrow*: Severe bone pain (limp, refusal to walk), pancytopenia.
  - *Orbital Metastases*: Proptosis and periorbital ecchymosis (**"Raccoon eyes"**).
  - *Skin*: Subcutaneous bluish nodules (**"Blueberry muffin baby"** - usually seen in infants with stage 4S disease).
- **Paraneoplastic Syndromes**:
  - *Opsoclonus-Myoclonus-Ataxia Syndrome (OMAS)*: "Dancing eyes, dancing feet." An autoimmune response to the tumour.
  - *Secretory Diarrhoea*: Due to VIP (Vasoactive Intestinal Peptide) secretion by the tumour.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Wilms Tumour**: Usually a smooth, unilateral mass that does not cross the midline. The child is typically well. Neuroblastoma is irregular, crosses the midline, and the child is sick.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Wilms tumour.
- Leukaemia (if presenting with bone pain and pancytopenia).
- Lymphoma.
- Hepatoblastoma.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Urine Catecholamines**: **Elevated VMA (Vanillylmandelic acid) and HVA (Homovanillic acid)** in a 24-hour urine collection or spot urine. Highly sensitive and specific diagnostic markers.
- **Imaging**:
  - *Ultrasound/CT/MRI*: To define the primary tumour and local invasion. Tumours often show **calcification** (unlike Wilms).
  - **MIBG Scan (Iodine-123 metaiodobenzylguanidine)**: A radiotracer taken up specifically by sympathetic tissue. The gold standard for identifying the primary tumour and all metastatic sites.
- **Biopsy**: Core biopsy of the primary tumour for histology and MYCN amplification testing.
- **Bone Marrow Aspirate & Trephine (BMAT)**: Mandatory to stage the disease (check for marrow infiltration).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- Treatment is highly risk-stratified based on age, stage, and MYCN status.
- **Low Risk (e.g., localized tumour, infant)**: Surgery alone, or even "watch and wait" (as some infant tumours spontaneously regress).
- **High Risk (e.g., metastatic disease, or any MYCN amplified tumour)**: Extremely intensive multimodal therapy.
  1. Induction chemotherapy.
  2. Surgical resection of the primary tumour.
  3. High-dose chemotherapy with Autologous Stem Cell Rescue (Transplant).
  4. Local Radiotherapy.
  5. Maintenance therapy: Isotretinoin and Anti-GD2 Immunotherapy (Dinutuximab) to eradicate minimal residual disease.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Paediatric Oncology**.
- **Palliative Care**: Early involvement is crucial for high-risk patients, as the relapse rate is high and treatment is exceptionally grueling.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- "Raccoon eyes" (periorbital ecchymosis) from orbital metastases can be misdiagnosed as bilateral black eyes from physical abuse (basal skull fracture or direct trauma). Always consider neuroblastoma if the history doesn't fit or there are other systemic signs.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Complications**: Spinal cord compression (an oncological emergency requiring urgent steroids and neurosurgery/radiotherapy).
- **Prognosis**: 
  - *Infants (<18 months)*: Generally excellent prognosis, even with metastatic disease (Stage 4S often regresses spontaneously).
  - *Older children (>18 months) with high-risk disease*: Poor prognosis (survival < 50%) despite aggressive therapy.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "A 2-year-old presents with weight loss, a nodular abdominal mass crossing the midline, and bilateral periorbital bruising ('raccoon eyes'). What is the most likely diagnosis, and what urine test would confirm it?" (Neuroblastoma. Elevated urine VMA and HVA).
- *Viva*: "What is the most important genetic marker for risk stratification in Neuroblastoma?" (MYCN amplification. Its presence indicates high-risk, aggressive disease).
