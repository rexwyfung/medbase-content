# Neuroblastoma (神經母細胞瘤)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition
* **Embryonal malignancy (胚胎性惡性腫瘤)** of the sympathetic nervous system (交感神經系統).
* Arises from **neuroblasts (神經母細胞)** (pluripotent sympathetic cells).
* Most common extracranial solid tumour in childhood.

### Local Epidemiology (Hong Kong)
* Accounts for **~8-10%** of HK childhood cancers.
* Managed centrally at **Hong Kong Children's Hospital (香港兒童醫院)**.
* Median age at diagnosis: **1-2 years**.
* Highly variable clinical course: ranges from spontaneous regression → relentless, fatal progression.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Anatomical Pathophysiology
* Tumours can arise anywhere along the sympathetic chain.
* **Abdomen (腹部)** (70%): Most commonly from **adrenal medulla (腎上腺髓質)** or paraspinal sympathetic ganglia.
* **Chest (胸部)** (20%): Posterior mediastinum.
* **Neck/Pelvis (頸部/盆腔)** (10%).

### Metastatic Spread
* Highly aggressive; **>50%** present with metastatic disease.
* Spreads via blood/lymphatics → **bone marrow (骨髓)**, **bone cortex (骨皮質)**, **liver (肝臟)**, **skin (皮膚)**.

### Molecular Genetics & Prognosis
* **MYCN Amplification (MYCN 基因擴增)**: Most critical **-ve prognostic marker**. Indicates highly aggressive disease & poor survival, regardless of age or stage.
* Hyperdiploidy: Generally associated with better prognosis (common in infants).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
### General Clinical Presentation
* "The great mimicker" → notoriously diverse presentation.
* Depends entirely on primary tumour site & presence of metastases.
* **Systemically unwell (全身不適)**: Unlike Wilms tumour, NB patients look sick (weight loss, lethargy, fever).

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Primary Tumour Signs
* **Abdominal Primary**: Firm, irregular, nodular abdominal mass → frequently **crosses midline (跨越中線)**.
* **Thoracic Primary**: Often asymptomatic (incidental finding on CXR). May cause respiratory distress or **Horner syndrome (霍納氏綜合症)** (ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis) if compressing cervical sympathetic chain.
* **Paraspinal Primary**: "Dumbbell tumour" growing through intervertebral foramina → **spinal cord compression (脊髓受壓)** (back pain, lower limb weakness, bowel/bladder dysfunction).

### Metastatic Signs
* **Bone/Bone Marrow**: Severe bone pain (limp, refusal to walk), pancytopenia.
* **Orbital Metastases**: Proptosis & periorbital ecchymosis → **"Raccoon eyes" (浣熊眼)**.
* **Skin**: Subcutaneous bluish nodules → **"Blueberry muffin baby" (藍莓鬆餅嬰兒)** (typically infants with stage 4S disease).
* **Liver**: Massive hepatomegaly (especially stage 4S).

### Paraneoplastic Syndromes
* **Opsoclonus-Myoclonus-Ataxia Syndrome (OMAS)**: "Dancing eyes, dancing feet". Autoimmune response against cerebellar tissue.
* **Secretory Diarrhoea (分泌性腹瀉)**: Due to **VIP (Vasoactive Intestinal Peptide)** secretion by the tumour.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Neuroblastoma vs. Wilms Tumour
| Feature | Neuroblastoma (神經母細胞瘤) | Wilms Tumour (腎母細胞瘤) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Systemic Health** | Unwell (sick, fever, weight loss) | Generally well, asymptomatic |
| **Abdominal Mass** | Irregular, nodular, **crosses midline** | Smooth, firm, unilateral, rarely crosses midline |
| **Calcification on CT** | Frequently present (>80%) | Rare (10-15%) |
| **Urine Catecholamines**| **↑ VMA / ↑ HVA** | Normal |
| **Bone pain/Metastases**| Common (Bone, marrow, raccoon eyes) | Rare at presentation (Lungs main site) |

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
### Main Paediatric Solid Tumour Differentials
| Diagnosis | Differentiating Clues |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Wilms Tumour (腎母細胞瘤)** | Well child, unilateral smooth mass, normal VMA/HVA. |
| **Hepatoblastoma (肝母細胞瘤)** | Liver mass, ↑ α-fetoprotein (AFP). |
| **Leukaemia (白血病)** | Bone pain, pancytopenia, hepatosplenomegaly, ↑ blasts on smear. No discrete primary solid mass. |
| **Lymphoma (淋巴瘤)** | Lymphadenopathy, mediastinal mass, B symptoms (night sweats, fever, weight loss). |

## 7. Tiered Investigations
### Bedside Investigations & Bloods
* **Urine Catecholamines (尿兒茶酚胺)**: **↑ VMA (Vanillylmandelic acid)** & **↑ HVA (Homovanillic acid)** (24hr or spot urine). Highly sensitive & specific diagnostic markers.
* **Bloods**: FBC (pancytopenia?), LFTs/U&Es, LDH, Ferritin, Neuron-specific enolase (NSE) (↑ correlates with disease bulk).

### Imaging Modalities
* **Ultrasound Abdomen**: Initial assessment of primary mass & local invasion.
* **CT/MRI Chest/Abdomen/Pelvis**: Define local invasion, vascular encasement, intraspinal extension. Tumours often show **calcification (鈣化)** (unlike Wilms).
* **MIBG Scan (Iodine-123 metaiodobenzylguanidine)**: Radiotracer taken up specifically by sympathetic tissue. **Gold standard** for identifying primary tumour & all metastatic sites.

### Special/Invasive Investigations
* **Core Biopsy**: Of primary tumour for histology & **MYCN amplification** testing.
* **Bone Marrow Aspirate & Trephine (BMAT)**: Mandatory for staging (check for marrow infiltration).

## 8. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Acute Oncological Emergencies
* **Spinal Cord Compression**: Urgent high-dose **Dexamethasone (地塞米松)**, urgent MRI Spine → Neurosurgery / local Radiotherapy.

### Risk-Stratified Management Protocols (HKCH)
Treatment is highly risk-stratified based on age, stage (INSS/INRGSS), and MYCN status.
* **Low Risk (Localized, Infant)**: Surgery alone. "Watch and wait" for infants (Stage 4S) as tumours frequently spontaneously regress.
* **Intermediate Risk**: Surgery + moderate-dose chemotherapy.
* **High Risk (Metastatic >18mo OR MYCN amplified)**: Extremely intensive multimodal therapy.
    1. **Induction Chemotherapy (誘導化療)**: Rapid tumour debulking.
    2. **Surgery**: Resection of primary tumour.
    3. **Consolidation**: High-dose chemotherapy + Autologous Stem Cell Rescue (Transplant).
    4. **Radiotherapy (放射治療)**: Local therapy to primary tumour bed.
    5. **Maintenance**: **Isotretinoin (異維A酸)** + **Anti-GD2 Immunotherapy (Dinutuximab)** to eradicate minimal residual disease.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Multidisciplinary Team Involvement
* **HK Children's Hospital Oncology Team**: Lead coordinators.
* **Paediatric Surgery**: For biopsy, definitive resection, and central venous line insertion.
* **Medical Social Worker (MSW) / SWD**: Financial assistance for expensive Anti-GD2 immunotherapy (e.g., CCF/Samaritan Fund), housing/transport support.
* **Clinical Psychology / Play Therapy**: Coping with prolonged hospitalization & painful procedures.
* **Palliative Care (紓緩治療)**: Early involvement crucial for high-risk patients due to ↑ relapse rate & exceptionally grueling treatment toxicities.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Non-Accidental Injury (NAI) Mimicry
* **"Raccoon eyes" (浣熊眼)**: Periorbital ecchymosis from orbital metastases closely mimics bilateral black eyes from physical abuse (basal skull fracture or direct trauma).
* **Clinical Action**: Always consider NB if history lacks congruent trauma, features systemic illness, or abdominal mass is present. Perform full examination before making abuse allegations.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
### Disease & Treatment Complications
* **Spinal Cord Compression**: Permanent paraplegia, neurogenic bladder/bowel if delayed.
* **Chemotherapy Toxicity**: Neutropenic sepsis, ototoxicity (platinum drugs), cardiotoxicity (anthracyclines).
* **Long-Term**: Secondary malignancies, growth failure, endocrine dysfunction, infertility.

### Prognostic Stratification
* **Infants (<18 months)**: Generally excellent prognosis, even with metastatic disease. Stage 4S (primary tumour + liver/skin/marrow metastases <10%) frequently regresses spontaneously.
* **Older children (>18 months) with high-risk/MYCN+ disease**: Poor prognosis. Overall survival < 50% despite aggressive therapy. Relapse is typically fatal.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Viva Question: A 2-year-old presents with weight loss, a nodular abdominal mass crossing the midline, and bilateral periorbital bruising ('raccoon eyes'). What is the most likely diagnosis, and what urine test would confirm it?
* Answer: Neuroblastoma. Elevated urine VMA and HVA.
* Viva Question: What is the most important genetic marker for risk stratification in Neuroblastoma?
* Answer: MYCN amplification. Its presence indicates high-risk, aggressive disease regardless of age or stage.
* Viva Question: An infant presents with subcutaneous blue nodules and massive hepatomegaly, but is otherwise stable. What is the likely staging and management?
* Answer: Stage 4S Neuroblastoma. Often managed with observation alone ("watch and wait") as it frequently regresses spontaneously.
* Trap: Assuming every unilateral abdominal mass in a child is a Wilms tumour without checking for systemic signs.
* Pearl: Always perform a full physical exam. If the child is systemically unwell (fever, weight loss) and the mass crosses the midline, highly suspect Neuroblastoma.
* Pitfall: Misdiagnosing periorbital ecchymosis in a toddler solely as physical abuse (NAI). Always check for an abdominal mass or systemic signs to rule out orbital metastases from Neuroblastoma.
