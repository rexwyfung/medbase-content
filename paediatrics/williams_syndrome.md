# Williams Syndrome (威廉氏綜合症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A sporadic multisystem neurodevelopmental disorder caused by a microdeletion on chromosome 7q11.23.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Rare, occurring in approx 1 in 7,500 births.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Microdeletion**: Encompasses ~26 genes, crucially including the **ELN (elastin) gene**.
- Haploinsufficiency of elastin leads to the classic connective tissue and cardiovascular abnormalities.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Often presentation is delayed until early childhood.
- Infants often present with severe hypercalcaemia, causing extreme irritability, vomiting, refusing feeds, and constipation.
- Toddlers present with developmental and speech delay, though highly sociable.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Facies**: "Elfin" face (broad forehead, short nose with broad tip, full cheeks, wide mouth with full lips, stellate iris pattern).
- **Behavioral**: Overly friendly "cocktail party" demeanor, highly conversational but lacks social nuance. Phobias to loud sounds (hyperacusis).
- **Cardiovascular**: Supravalvular Aortic Stenosis (SVAS), peripheral pulmonary branch stenosis. Let examiner know to listen at the *supra* aortic area (right sternal border, neck radiation).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from autism: these children have an obsessive drive to socially interact, whereas individuals with autism may avoid eye contact and lack social reciprocity.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Autism Spectrum Disorder (early language delay presentation)
- Noonan Syndrome
- Fetal Alcohol Syndrome

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**: ECG (LVH signs for SVAS), Blood pressure (check all 4 limbs for renal artery stenosis).
- **Bloods**: Calcium profile (Hypercalcaemia in infancy).
- **Special (Definitive)**: **FISH** or Microarray specifically for the 7q11.23 region (ELN deletion).
- Echocardiogram: Essential to define the extent of SVAS.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Infancy Hypercalcaemia**: Low calcium/vitamin D diet. Usually resolves by age 2.
- **Cardiovascular**: Regular cardiology reviews. Severe SVAS requires surgical patch repair (HA cardiothoracic protocols) to prevent sudden cardiac death.
- **Developmental**: Early intervention PT/OT/ST.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Support system must emphasize specialized education. MSW aids with schooling placement. MCHC ongoing surveillance for failure to thrive.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Highly vulnerable children due to extreme sociability towards strangers without boundary awareness. Requires intensive safety grooming counseling.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Sudden cardiac death (from severe SVAS or anaesthetic complications).
- Renal artery stenosis leading to secondary hypertension.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Assuming their strong expressive verbal skills reflect good overall IQ. They often have profound deficits in visuospatial skills.
- **Viva Q**: "Why are they hypercalcaemic as infants?" Answer: It is not entirely understood but suspected to be tied to abnormal vitamin D sensitivity or genes contiguous to the elastin deletion.
