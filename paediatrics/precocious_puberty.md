
# Precocious Puberty (性早熟)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Onset of secondary sexual characteristics before age 8 in girls and before age 9 in boys.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Much more common in girls. In girls, it is usually idiopathic. In boys, it is much rarer and more likely to have an underlying pathological cause (e.g., CNS tumour).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **1. Central Precocious Puberty (CPP) / Gonadotropin-Dependent**: Early activation of the Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Gonadal (HPG) axis.
  - *Causes*: Idiopathic (90% of girls), CNS lesions (tumours like hypothalamic hamartoma, astrocytoma; hydrocephalus, previous meningitis/irradiation).
- **2. Peripheral Precocious Puberty (PPP) / Gonadotropin-Independent**: Excess sex steroids from the gonads or adrenal glands, independent of pituitary LH/FSH.
  - *Causes*: Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia (CAH), Ovarian cysts/tumours, Testicular tumours (Leydig cell), McCune-Albright Syndrome.
- **3. Benign Normal Variants**:
  - *Premature Thelarche*: Isolated breast development (usually < 2 years old), non-progressive.
  - *Premature Adrenarche*: Isolated pubic/axillary hair and body odour due to early adrenal androgen production.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- See below.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Girls**: Early breast development (thelarche), pubic hair, rapid growth spurt, early menarche.
- **Boys**: Early testicular enlargement (volume > 4 mL indicates central cause), pubic hair, rapid growth spurt, deepening voice.
- **Growth**: Accelerated growth velocity (tall for age initially), but early epiphyseal fusion leads to **short final adult height**.
## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Central vs. Peripheral in Boys**:
  - *Central*: Testes are enlarged (>4 mL) bilaterally (driven by LH/FSH).
  - *Peripheral*: Testes are small (prepubertal) because the androgens are coming from the adrenals (e.g., CAH), OR one testis is enlarged (testicular tumour).

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Most Likely**:
  - Girls: Idiopathic Central Precocious Puberty.
  - Boys: CNS lesion (Central) or CAH (Peripheral).
- **Must-Not-Miss**:
  - CNS Tumour (Hypothalamic hamartoma, glioma).
  - Adrenal or Gonadal malignancy.
  - Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia (Late-onset).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**:
  - **Bone Age X-ray**: Markedly advanced in true precocious puberty. Normal in benign variants (premature thelarche).
- **Gold Standard**:
  - **Basal Bloods**: LH, FSH, Oestradiol (girls), Testosterone (boys).
  - *High/Pubertal LH/FSH* -> Central.
  - *Low/Suppressed LH/FSH with high sex steroids* -> Peripheral.
  - **GnRH Stimulation Test (LHRH test)**: Definitive test for Central PP. Administer synthetic GnRH; a brisk, pubertal rise in LH confirms central activation.
  - **Pelvic Ultrasound (Girls)**: Assesses uterine size and ovarian volume/cysts.
  - **MRI Brain**: **MANDATORY** in all boys with Central PP, and in girls with Central PP onset < 6 years or with neurological signs, to rule out CNS tumours.
  - **Adrenal Androgens (17-OHP, DHEAS)**: If peripheral cause (CAH) is suspected.
- **Monitoring**:
  - Monitor growth velocity and bone age progression.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**:
  - Not applicable.
- **Chronic/Definitive**:
  - **Central Precocious Puberty**:
    - **GnRH Agonists (e.g., Triptorelin, Leuprorelin)**: Given as monthly or 3-monthly depot injections. Paradoxically down-regulates pituitary GnRH receptors, suppressing LH/FSH and halting puberty.
    - *Goal*: Stop menses, halt rapid bone maturation to preserve final adult height, and alleviate psychosocial distress.
    - Treatment is stopped at a normal age of puberty (e.g., 11 years) to allow normal progression.
    - **Peripheral Precocious Puberty**: Treat the underlying cause (e.g., Hydrocortisone for CAH, surgical removal of ovarian/testicular tumours).
    - **Benign Variants**: Reassurance and observation.
## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Psychological support for the child who looks much older than their chronological age and may face inappropriate social expectations or early menarche distress.
## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Ensure parents understand that the child's cognitive and emotional maturity matches their chronological age, not their physical appearance.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Without treatment, the main complication is significantly compromised final adult height due to early epiphyseal fusion.
- With GnRH agonist treatment, final height is usually preserved.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Assuming a boy with early pubic hair and a growth spurt has Central PP without checking the testes. *Pearl*: If the testes are small (<4mL), it is NOT Central PP. The androgens are coming from elsewhere (e.g., CAH).
- **Viva Question**: "Why do we use a GnRH *agonist* to stop puberty?" *Answer*: Continuous, non-pulsatile administration of a GnRH agonist down-regulates the pituitary GnRH receptors, leading to a profound suppression of LH and FSH release.
