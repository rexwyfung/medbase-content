# Exomphalos & Gastroschisis (臍膨出與腹裂)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition of Exomphalos (臍膨出)
* Congenital abdominal wall defect involving the umbilical ring.
* Viscera herniate inside a translucent membranous sac (peritoneum + amnion).
* Umbilical cord inserts directly into the apex of the sac.

### Clinical Definition of Gastroschisis (腹裂)
* Full-thickness abdominal wall defect, typically to the right of an intact umbilical cord.
* Viscera freely eviscerate into the amniotic cavity → chemical serositis ("peel").
* NO protective sac.

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
* ↑ global & local incidence of Gastroschisis, strongly associated with young maternal age.
* Exomphalos incidence remains stable; high rate of Termination of Pregnancy (TOP) in HK due to strong association with lethal chromosomal anomalies.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Pathophysiology of Exomphalos
* Failure of embryonic gut to return from yolk sac to the abdominal cavity by week 10.
* **Genetics**: >50% associated with chromosomal anomalies [Trisomy 13 (13三體綜合症), 18, 21] & Beckwith-Wiedemann Syndrome (貝克威思－威德曼症候群).

### Pathophysiology of Gastroschisis
* Likely a localized vascular accident involving the right umbilical vein.
* **Genetics**: Rare association with extra-intestinal syndromes.
* ↑ rates of intra-intestinal complications [Intestinal Atresia (腸閉鎖), Stenosis (腸狹窄), Volvulus (腸扭轉)].

## 3. Clinical Presentation
### Antenatal Presentation
* >90% detected during 12-20 week routine antenatal US anomaly scan.
* ↑ Maternal Serum Alpha-Fetoprotein (MSAFP) (母體血清甲胎蛋白).

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Postnatal Signs of Gastroschisis
* Visually striking at birth; exposed bowel loops outside the abdomen.
* Eviscerated bowel appears matted, contracted, and covered by a thick fibrous peel (due to unprotected exposure to amniotic fluid).

### Postnatal Signs of Exomphalos
* Intact translucent sac containing liver/bowel at umbilicus.
* Note: Sac may rupture during delivery (obstetric emergency).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Exomphalos vs. Gastroschisis Comparison

| Feature | Exomphalos (臍膨出) | Gastroschisis (腹裂) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Location** | Central (umbilical ring) | Lateral (usually right of cord) |
| **Sac** | Present (amnion + peritoneum) | Absent |
| **Cord Insertion** | Apex of sac | Intact, separate from defect |
| **Bowel Appearance** | Normal | Matted, thick fibrous peel |
| **Genetic Anomalies** | >50% (Trisomies 13/18/21, BWS) | Rare |
| **Intestinal Anomalies** | Rare | High risk (atresia, volvulus) |

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
### Differential Diagnosis of Abdominal Wall Defects
* **Ruptured Exomphalos**: Can mimic gastroschisis, but the umbilical cord inserts into the remnants of the sac.
* **Umbilical Hernia (臍疝)**: Defect covered by normal skin, completely reducible, usually resolves spontaneously.
* **Prune-Belly Syndrome (梅乾腹綜合症)**: Lax abdominal musculature mimicking a defect, but not a true full-thickness evisceration.

## 7. Tiered Investigations
### Antenatal Investigations
* **US Scan**: Identifies defect, assesses fetal growth, detects concurrent anomalies.
* **Karyotyping/Microarray**: Critical for Exomphalos due to genetic associations.
* **Fetal Echocardiogram**: High incidence of concurrent Congenital Heart Disease (先天性心臟病).

### Postnatal Bedside & Imaging Investigations
* **Urgent Echocardiogram**: Rule out cardiac anomalies before surgery (esp. Exomphalos).
* **Renal US**: Detect concurrent genitourinary anomalies.

## 8. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Acute Delivery Room Management
* **Delivery**: Tertiary HA center (e.g., QMH/HKCH) with NICU & Paediatric Surgery on-site.
* **Silo Bag**: Place lower half of baby in a sterile drawstring plastic bag (Silo) immediately to prevent evaporative heat & massive fluid loss, and protect from infection.
* **Supportive**: NBM (禁食), NG tube (to decompress stomach), broad-spectrum IV antibiotics.
* **Resuscitation**: Aggressive IV fluid resuscitation (esp. Gastroschisis due to huge 3rd space losses).

### Surgical Management Protocols
* **Exomphalos**: 
  * Small: Primary fascial closure.
  * Giant: Delayed closure to avoid respiratory compromise. Paint the sac with astringents (e.g., mercurochrome or silver sulfadiazine) to epithelialise it, allowing gradual reduction.
* **Gastroschisis**:
  * Staged reduction utilizing a preformed silo, slowly squeezing the bowel in over several days on the ward → final fascial closure in OT.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Multidisciplinary Team (MDT) Involvement
* Paediatric Surgery, Neonatology, Clinical Genetics, Dietitians (for TPN management).
* Medical Social Worker (MSW) & Clinical Psychologist: Support for prolonged NICU stay & parental distress.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Social & Safeguarding Risks
* Gastroschisis strongly associated with young maternal age (<20 yrs), smoking, and low socioeconomic status.
* SWD (Social Welfare Department) referral mandated if maternal drug abuse, neglect, or lack of support identified.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
### Complications & Prognosis of Gastroschisis
* Universal bowel dysmotility → delayed enteral feeding.
* High reliance on Total Parenteral Nutrition (TPN) (全靜脈營養) for weeks/months → ↑ risk of TPN cholestasis & central line sepsis.
* Prognosis: >90% survival; primarily depends on bowel viability & presence of Short Bowel Syndrome (短腸綜合症).

### Complications & Prognosis of Exomphalos
* Mortality strictly dictated by severity of associated anomalies (e.g., lethal congenital heart defects or chromosomal disorders).
* Giant exomphalos → ↑ risk of Pulmonary Hypoplasia (肺發育不全) & prolonged mechanical ventilation.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Trap: Assuming the exposed bowel in gastroschisis is the primary immediate threat to life. Pearl: The actual immediate threats are profound hypothermia and hypovolaemic shock from massive evaporative fluid loss across the uncoated bowel. Prompt bagging handles this.
* Viva Question: What is Beckwith-Wiedemann Syndrome (貝克威思－威德曼症候群)? Answer: An overgrowth syndrome characterized by Exomphalos, Macroglossia, Gigantism (LGA), and Neonatal Hypoglycaemia (hyperinsulinism), with a high risk of Wilms tumor (腎母細胞瘤).
* Pitfall: Attempting primary surgical closure on a giant exomphalos. This acutely increases intra-abdominal pressure (abdominal compartment syndrome), causing respiratory splinting and decreased venous return.
