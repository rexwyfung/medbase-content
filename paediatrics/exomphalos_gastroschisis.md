# Exomphalos & Gastroschisis (臍膨出與腹裂)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- Both are congenital abdominal wall defects.
- **Exomphalos (Omphalocele)**: Defect involving the umbilical ring. Viscera herniate inside a translucent membranous sac consisting of peritoneum and amnion. Umbilical cord inserts into the apex of the sac.
- **Gastroschisis**: Full-thickness abdominal wall defect to the right of a normal, intact umbilical cord. Viscera freely eviscerate into the amniotic cavity, causing chemical serositis ("peel"). No protective sac.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Exomphalos**: Failure of the embryonic gut to return from the yolk sac to the abdominal cavity by week 10. High incidence (>50%) of associated genetic anomalies (Trisomy 13, 18, 21, and Beckwith-Wiedemann syndrome).
- **Gastroschisis**: Likely a localized vascular accident involving the right umbilical vein. Rare association with extra-intestinal syndromes, but high rates of intra-intestinal complications (atresia, stenosis, volvulus). Usually seen in younger mothers.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Virtually all cases are detected during the 12 to 20-week antenatal ultrasound anomaly scan.
- Elevated maternal serum alpha-fetoprotein (MSAFP).

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- Visually striking at birth.
- Gastroschisis bowel appears matted, contracted, and covered by a thick fibrous peel (due to unprotected exposure to amniotic fluid).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Exomphalos = Central, has a sac, cord on sac.
- Gastroschisis = Lateral (usually right), NO sac, cord is separate.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Not applicable; clinical diagnosis is obvious, but must actively hunt for the associated deadly systemic anomalies.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Antenatal**: Karyotyping/microarray (critical for Exomphalos). Fetal echocardiogram (high incidence of congenital heart defects).
- **Postnatal**: Urgent Echocardiogram and Renal US (for Exomphalos).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Delivery**: Tertiary center with NICU and Paediatric Surgery on-site.
- **Acute (Labor Ward)**: 
  - Place lower half of baby in a sterile drawstring plastic bag (Silo bag) to prevent evaporative heat and massive fluid loss, and protect from infection.
  - NBM, NG tube (to decompress stomach), broad spectrum IV antibiotics.
  - Aggressive IV fluid resuscitation (especially for Gastroschisis due to huge third space losses).
- **Surgical**:
  - Exomphalos: Delayed closure often used (painting the sac with astringents like mercurochrome to epithelialise it), to avoid respiratory compromise if the abdominal cavity is too small.
  - Gastroschisis: Staged reduction utilizing a preformed silo, slowly squeezing the bowel in over several days, followed by final fascial closure.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **MDT**: Paediatric Surgery, Neonatology, Clinical Genetics.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Strong counseling regarding young maternal age and socioeconomic risk factors often associated with gastroschisis.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Gastroschisis**: Bowel dysmotility is universal. They may require weeks of parenteral nutrition (TPN) before taking full enteral feeds. Prognosis is generally good if bowel viable.
- **Exomphalos**: Mortality is strictly dictated by the severity of associated anomalies (e.g., lethal heart defects or chromosomal disorders).

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Assuming the exposed bowel in gastroschisis is the primary threat to life. Actually, it's profound hypothermia and hypovolaemic shock from massive evaporative loss across the uncoated bowel. Prompt bagging handles this.
- **Viva Q**: "What is Beckwith-Wiedemann Syndrome?" Answer: An overgrowth syndrome characterized by Exomphalos, Macroglossia, Gigantism (LGA), and Neonatal Hypoglycaemia (hyperinsulinism). High risk of Wilms tumor.
