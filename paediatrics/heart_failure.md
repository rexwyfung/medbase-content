

# Paediatric Heart Failure (小兒心臟衰竭)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: The inability of the heart to pump blood at a rate commensurate with the requirements of the metabolizing tissues, or doing so only from an elevated filling pressure.
- **Local Epidemiology**: In HK, congenital heart disease (CHD) is the leading cause in infants. Cardiomyopathies (e.g., viral myocarditis, genetic) and rheumatic heart disease are more common in older children.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Aetiology by Age**:
  - *Neonate*: Structural CHD (e.g., Hypoplastic Left Heart Syndrome, severe coarctation), arrhythmias (SVT).
  - *Infant*: Left-to-right shunts (VSD, PDA, AVSD), anomalous left coronary artery from pulmonary artery (ALCAPA).
  - *Child/Adolescent*: Viral myocarditis, cardiomyopathies (dilated, hypertrophic), rheumatic fever, anthracycline toxicity (oncology patients).
- **Compensatory Mechanisms**: Sympathetic nervous system activation (tachycardia, diaphoresis), RAAS activation (fluid retention).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- **Infants**: Poor feeding (tiring easily, sweating during feeds), failure to thrive, tachypnoea.
- **Older Children**: Exercise intolerance, fatigue, orthopnoea, abdominal pain (hepatic congestion).

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Tachycardia** and **Tachypnoea** (earliest signs).
- **Hepatomegaly**: A very reliable sign of right-sided heart failure in infants (JVP is hard to assess).
- Gallop rhythm (S3/S4).
- Cardiomegaly (displaced apex beat).
- Peripheral oedema (less common in infants, more in older children).
- Cool peripheries, prolonged capillary refill time (in severe low cardiac output).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Respiratory Infections (e.g., Bronchiolitis)**: Can present with tachypnoea and feeding difficulties, but usually have prominent wheeze/crepitations, fever, and NO hepatomegaly.
- **Sepsis**: Tachycardia and poor perfusion, but usually febrile with an infective focus.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Respiratory distress (pulmonary origin).
- Sepsis / Septic shock.
- Metabolic disorders (e.g., mitochondrial disease presenting with cardiomyopathy).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
**Bedside**: ECG (arrhythmias, hypertrophy, ischaemia).
- **Bloods**: FBC, U&E, LFTs (hepatic congestion), BNP/NT-proBNP (elevated), Troponin (if myocarditis suspected), VBG (lactate).
- **Imaging**: CXR (cardiomegaly, pulmonary oedema/plethora).
**Gold Standard**: **Echocardiogram**: Determines structural cause, ventricular function (Ejection Fraction, Fractional Shortening), and chamber sizes.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute Resuscitation**:
  - Prop up (semi-recumbent).
  - Oxygen (use with caution in duct-dependent systemic circulation).
  - IV Diuretics (Furosemide).
  - Inotropes (e.g., Milrinone, Dobutamine) in PICU for cardiogenic shock.
- **Chronic Management**:
  - **Diuretics**: Furosemide, Spironolactone.
  - **ACE Inhibitors**: Captopril or Enalapril (afterload reduction).
  - **Beta-blockers**: Carvedilol or Bisoprolol (introduced carefully once stable).
  - Nutritional support (high-calorie formula for infants).
- **Definitive**: Treat the underlying cause (e.g., surgical repair of VSD).

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Dietitian**: Crucial for optimizing caloric intake in infants with failure to thrive.
- **MSW**: Support for families facing chronic illness, frequent hospitalizations, or potential need for heart transplant.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Ensure compliance with medications and follow-up. Medical neglect can exacerbate heart failure.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Failure to thrive, recurrent respiratory infections, arrhythmias, sudden cardiac death.
- Prognosis depends entirely on the underlying aetiology (excellent for repairable shunts, guarded for severe cardiomyopathies).

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Looking for raised JVP and ankle oedema in an infant. *Pearl*: Hepatomegaly is the most reliable sign of systemic venous congestion in infants.
- **Viva**: "Why do infants with heart failure sweat during feeds?" *Answer*: Feeding is their equivalent of exercise. The sympathetic nervous system is hyperactivated to maintain cardiac output, leading to diaphoresis.
