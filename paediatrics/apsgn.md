# Acute Post-Streptococcal Glomerulonephritis (APSGN) (急性鏈球菌感染後腎小球腎炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: The most common cause of acute nephritic syndrome in childhood. It is an immune complex-mediated glomerulonephritis that occurs after an infection with specific nephritogenic strains of Group A Beta-Haemolytic Streptococcus (GAS).
- **Local Epidemiology**: Common in Hong Kong, particularly in school-aged children (5-12 years). Incidence peaks in winter/spring (following pharyngitis) and summer (following impetigo).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Pathophysiology**: 
  - Infection with a nephritogenic strain of GAS (e.g., causing pharyngitis or impetigo).
  - 1-3 weeks later (for pharyngitis) or 3-6 weeks later (for impetigo), the body produces antibodies against the streptococcal antigens.
  - These antigen-antibody immune complexes circulate and deposit in the glomerular basement membrane (GBM) of the kidneys.
  - This triggers complement activation and severe inflammation of the glomeruli, leading to decreased Glomerular Filtration Rate (GFR), fluid retention, and leakage of red blood cells and protein into the urine.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- A classic history: A school-aged child who had a sore throat 2 weeks ago (or skin infection a month ago) now presents with sudden onset of facial puffiness, dark urine, and headache.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Macroscopic Haematuria**: "Cola-coloured" or "tea-coloured" urine.
- **Oedema**: Typically mild to moderate, often presenting as periorbital puffiness in the morning.
- **Hypertension**: Can be severe and lead to hypertensive encephalopathy (headache, vomiting, visual disturbances, seizures).
- **Oliguria**: Decreased urine output due to reduced GFR.
- **Signs of Fluid Overload**: Raised JVP, basal crepitations (pulmonary oedema), hepatomegaly.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **IgA Nephropathy**: Also presents with macroscopic haematuria after a respiratory infection, but the haematuria occurs *concurrently* (within 1-2 days) with the infection, whereas APSGN has a latent period of 1-3 weeks. Complement C3 is normal in IgA nephropathy but low in APSGN.
- **Nephrotic Syndrome**: Presents with massive proteinuria and severe generalized oedema, but usually normal BP and no macroscopic haematuria.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- IgA Nephropathy.
- Lupus Nephritis (SLE).
- Henoch-Schönlein Purpura (HSP) Nephritis.
- Membranoproliferative Glomerulonephritis (MPGN).
- Alport Syndrome (hereditary nephritis with deafness).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Urinalysis**: Blood (+++), Protein (+ to ++).
- **Urine Microscopy**: **Red Blood Cell (RBC) casts** (pathognomonic for glomerular bleeding) and dysmorphic RBCs.
- **Bloods**:
  - **Complement C3**: **Significantly LOW** (consumed by the immune complex activation). C4 is usually normal. *Crucial: C3 must return to normal within 6-8 weeks; if it remains low, reconsider the diagnosis (e.g., SLE, MPGN).*
  - **Evidence of recent Strep infection**: Elevated Anti-Streptolysin O Titre (ASOT) or Anti-DNase B.
  - U&E, Creatinine: May show acute kidney injury (elevated urea and creatinine, hyperkalaemia).
- **Throat/Skin Swab**: For GAS culture (often negative by the time nephritis presents).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Supportive Care (The Mainstay)**:
  - **Fluid Restriction**: Strict fluid restriction (e.g., 400ml/m2/day + previous day's urine output) to manage fluid overload and hypertension.
  - **Low Sodium Diet**.
- **Medical Therapy**:
  - **Diuretics**: IV or Oral Furosemide is the first-line treatment for hypertension and fluid overload in APSGN.
  - **Antihypertensives**: Calcium channel blockers (e.g., Amlodipine) if BP remains high despite diuresis.
  - **Antibiotics**: A 10-day course of Oral Penicillin V (or Amoxicillin) to eradicate any remaining nephritogenic streptococci and prevent spread to others. *Note: This does NOT alter the course of the current nephritis.*
- **Indications for Dialysis**: Severe hyperkalaemia, refractory pulmonary oedema, or severe uraemia.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Paediatric Nephrology**: Involvement if there is severe AKI or atypical features.
- **Education**: Reassure parents that the macroscopic haematuria will resolve in a few weeks, but microscopic haematuria can persist for up to a year.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- N/A for this condition.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Acute Complications**: Hypertensive encephalopathy (seizures), acute pulmonary oedema, acute kidney injury requiring dialysis.
- **Prognosis**: Excellent in children. >95% recover completely with no long-term renal impairment. Recurrence is extremely rare.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "What is the most important blood test to confirm the diagnosis of APSGN, and what is its expected trajectory?" (Complement C3. It should be low at presentation and must return to normal within 6-8 weeks. If it doesn't, you must suspect a chronic glomerulonephritis like MPGN or Lupus).
- *Viva*: "A child with APSGN presents with a blood pressure of 150/100. What is the first-line pharmacological treatment?" (Furosemide. The hypertension is driven by fluid and sodium retention, so diuresis is the most effective treatment).
- *Pitfall*: Prescribing steroids for APSGN. Unlike Nephrotic Syndrome, steroids have no role in APSGN and may worsen fluid retention and hypertension.
