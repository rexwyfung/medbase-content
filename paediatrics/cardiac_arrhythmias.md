# Cardiac Arrhythmias (心律不正)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Any abnormality in the rate, regularity, origin, or conduction of the cardiac electrical impulse. 
- **Local Epidemiology**: Supraventricular Tachycardia (SVT) is the most common symptomatic arrhythmia in children requiring emergency attendance.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **SVT**: Almost always due to an accessory re-entry pathway (AVRT, e.g., Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome) or AV nodal re-entry (AVNRT). 
- **Long QT Syndrome (LQTS)**: Genetic channelopathies (Romano-Ward, Jervell and Lange-Nielsen) causing delayed ventricular repolarization, risking Torsades de Pointes.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- **Infants**: Poor feeding, pallor, extreme lethargy, sweating, tachypnea. Often unrecognized until they enter heart failure.
- **Older Children**: Palpitations ("heart racing"), chest pain, dizziness, syncope during exercise.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- Heart rate continuously >220 bpm in infants, or >180 bpm in children.
- The rate in SVT is rigidly regular and does *not* vary with crying or sleep (unlike sinus tachycardia).
- Signs of cardiogenic shock if the arrhythmia has been prolonged (>24 hours): hepatomegaly, poor perfusion, mottling.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from Sinus Tachycardia: Sinus tachycardia usually has a visible P wave before every QRS, the rate fluctuates with activity/fever, and is typically <220 in infants. SVT has absent or retrograde P waves, and the rate is completely fixed.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Sinus Tachycardia (due to fever, sepsis, pain, dehydration)
- Ventricular Tachycardia (VT - incredibly rare in children unless underlying structural heart disease or severe systemic derangement).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Definitive**: 12-lead ECG DURING the event. (Classic SVT: Narrow complex, regular, no P waves, HR >220).
- **Post-termination ECG**: Crucial to look for delta waves (Wolff-Parkinson-White), prolonged QTc, or underlying structural changes.
- **Special**: 24-hour Holter monitor or an implantable loop recorder if palpitations are fleeting and not captured in A&E. Echocardiogram to rule out structural heart disease (e.g., Ebstein anomaly associated with WPW).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Unstable (Shock, poor perfusion, decreased consciousness)**: Immediate synchronized DC Cardioversion (start at 0.5 - 1 J/kg).
- **Stable Acute SVT**:
  1. Vagal Maneuvers: Application of ice-water bag directly to the face (simulating diving reflex) for ~10 seconds in infants. Valsalva (blowing into a syringe) for older kids. *Never press on baby's eyeballs.*
  2. Medical: Fast IV Push of **Adenosine** (start 100-150 mcg/kg) via a large vein closest to the heart, followed IMMEDIATELY by a rapid saline flush. (Explain to parents: "The baby's heart will stop for a few seconds, they will turn grey, then it will restart normally.")
- **Chronic**: Amiodarone, Flecainide, or Sotalol. 
- **Curative**: Radiofrequency Catheter Ablation of the accessory pathway (usually deferred until the child is >15kg/older to reduce complication risks).

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Severe parental anxiety regarding future episodes. Training parents to check heart rates and teaching vagal maneuvers for home use.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Consider toxicology/drug screening (amphetamines, excessive caffeine/energy drinks, cold medicines with pseudoephedrine) in older children/teens presenting with new-onset unexplained arrhythmias.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Heart failure / Cardiogenic shock if SVT is unrecognized for >24-48 hours in infants.
- Sudden Cardiac Death (especially in Long QT syndrome or unchecked WPW).
- Many infants with SVT "grow out of it" by age 1.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Attempting carotid massage in an infant for vagal manoeuvres. It is completely contraindicated. Always use the facial ice technique.
- **Viva Q**: "Why must Adenosine be given as a rapid bolus followed by a rapid flush?" Answer: Adenosine has an extraordinarily short half-life (less than 10 seconds). If pushed slowly, it will be broken down by red blood cell enzymes in the arm before it ever reaches the AV node in the heart.
