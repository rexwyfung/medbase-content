# Acute Appendicitis (急性盲腸炎/闌尾炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Acute inflammation of the vermiform appendix. It is the most common surgical emergency in childhood.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Peak incidence is in the second decade of life (10-19 years). It is relatively rare in infants and toddlers, but when it occurs in this age group, the perforation rate is extremely high due to delayed diagnosis.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Obstruction**: The primary event is obstruction of the appendiceal lumen. 
  - *Causes*: Faecolith (hard stool), lymphoid hyperplasia (often secondary to a viral URTI or gastroenteritis), or rarely parasites (e.g., *Enterobius vermicularis* / pinworm).
- **Inflammation Cascade**: Obstruction leads to mucus accumulation, increasing intraluminal pressure. This causes venous engorgement, ischaemia of the appendiceal wall, and bacterial overgrowth.
- **Perforation**: If untreated, transmural necrosis occurs, leading to perforation and spillage of infected contents into the peritoneal cavity (peritonitis).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- **The Classic Sequence**:
  1. **Anorexia** (loss of appetite) - often the very first symptom.
  2. **Vague Periumbilical Pain**: Visceral pain caused by distension of the appendix (midgut structure).
  3. **Vomiting**: Usually occurs *after* the onset of pain.
  4. **Migration of Pain**: After 12-24 hours, the inflammation irritates the overlying parietal peritoneum, causing the pain to localize to the **Right Lower Quadrant (RLQ)** as a sharp, somatic pain.
  5. Low-grade fever.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **McBurney's Point Tenderness**: Maximal tenderness located 1/3 of the distance from the Anterior Superior Iliac Spine (ASIS) to the umbilicus.
- **Signs of Peritonitis**: Guarding (voluntary contraction), Rigidity (involuntary board-like abdomen), and Rebound tenderness (pain on sudden release of pressure).
- **Rovsing's Sign**: Palpation of the Left Lower Quadrant (LLQ) elicits pain in the RLQ.
- **Psoas Sign**: Pain on passive extension of the right hip (indicates a retrocaecal appendix irritating the psoas muscle).
- **Obturator Sign**: Pain on internal rotation of the flexed right hip (indicates a pelvic appendix irritating the obturator internus muscle).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Gastroenteritis**: Vomiting and diarrhoea typically precede the abdominal pain. Pain is diffuse and crampy, without localized RLQ peritonism.
- **Mesenteric Adenitis**: Often preceded by a viral URTI. High fever occurs early. Tenderness may shift when the patient rolls onto their side.
- **Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)**: Dysuria, frequency, and high fever without the classic periumbilical-to-RLQ pain migration.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Mesenteric adenitis.
- Acute gastroenteritis.
- Urinary tract infection / Pyelonephritis.
- Meckel's diverticulitis (clinically indistinguishable from appendicitis).
- Intussusception (in younger children).
- *In females*: Ovarian torsion, ruptured ovarian cyst, pelvic inflammatory disease (PID), ectopic pregnancy.
- Lower lobe pneumonia (can cause referred abdominal pain).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Diagnosis is primarily clinical**.
- **Bloods**: CBP (mild leukocytosis with a "left shift" / neutrophilia), CRP (elevated).
- **Urine**: Urine dipstick and microscopy to rule out UTI. *Note: A mildly inflamed appendix lying near the ureter or bladder can cause sterile pyuria (WBCs in urine without bacteria).*
- **Imaging**:
  - **Ultrasound Abdomen**: The first-line imaging modality in children (avoids ionizing radiation). *Positive findings*: Non-compressible, dilated appendix (>6 mm outer diameter), target sign, appendicolith, periappendiceal fluid.
  - **CT Abdomen**: Reserved for cases where US is equivocal, the patient is obese, or complications (abscess) are suspected.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute Resuscitation**: Keep NPO (nil by mouth). IV fluid hydration. Adequate analgesia (IV Paracetamol, Morphine - does not mask clinical signs).
- **Antibiotics**: IV broad-spectrum antibiotics (e.g., Cefuroxime + Metronidazole, or Amoxicillin-Clavulanate) to cover Gram-negative enteric organisms and anaerobes.
- **Surgical Management**:
  - **Uncomplicated**: Urgent Laparoscopic Appendicectomy (the gold standard).
  - **Perforated with localized abscess**: May be managed conservatively initially with IV antibiotics and percutaneous drainage (IR-guided), followed by an "interval appendicectomy" 6-8 weeks later.
  - **Generalized peritonitis**: Emergency laparotomy/laparoscopy and peritoneal washout.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Urgent Paediatric Surgery consultation.
- Pain management team involvement post-operatively.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Significant delay in seeking medical attention for a child with severe abdominal pain and vomiting, leading to a ruptured appendix and septic shock (Medical neglect).
- Abdominal trauma causing bowel injury mimicking appendicitis.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Perforation**: The risk is highest in children < 5 years old (up to 80%) because they have atypical presentations (leading to delayed diagnosis), a thinner appendiceal wall, and a short, underdeveloped omentum that cannot effectively "wall off" the infection.
- **Other Complications**: Intra-abdominal abscess, pelvic abscess, generalized peritonitis, sepsis, paralytic ileus, and future adhesive small bowel obstruction.
- **Prognosis**: Excellent with timely surgical intervention.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "Why is the perforation rate so high in toddlers?" (Atypical presentation, inability to communicate pain, thin appendiceal wall, and an underdeveloped omentum).
- *Viva*: "What is the classic sequence of symptoms in appendicitis?" (Anorexia -> Periumbilical pain -> Vomiting -> RLQ pain).
- *Pitfall*: Forgetting to examine the scrotum in a boy with abdominal pain (to rule out testicular torsion) or failing to consider a pregnancy test in an adolescent female.
