# Acute Appendicitis (急性盲腸炎/闌尾炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition of Acute Appendicitis
* **Acute Appendicitis (急性闌尾炎)**: Acute inflammation of the **vermiform appendix (闌尾)**.
* It is the most common surgical emergency in childhood.

### Hong Kong Epidemiology
* Peak incidence: 2nd decade of life (10-19 years).
* Rare in infants and toddlers, but when it occurs, the **perforation rate (穿孔率)** is extremely high (~80% in <5 years) due to atypical presentation and delayed diagnosis.
* HK HA context: Major cause of acute pediatric surgical admissions in HA hospitals.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Primary Pathophysiological Event
* **Luminal Obstruction (管腔阻塞)**: The primary initiating event.
* Causes:
  * **Lymphoid hyperplasia (淋巴組織增生)**: Most common in children (often 2° to a viral URTI or gastroenteritis).
  * **Faecolith / Appendicolith (糞石)**: Hard stool, more common in older children and adults.
  * Rare: Parasites (e.g., *Enterobius vermicularis* / pinworm), foreign bodies.

### Inflammation Cascade
* Obstruction → Mucus accumulation → ↑ Intraluminal pressure.
* Venous engorgement → Lymphatic stasis → Ischaemia of appendiceal wall.
* Bacterial overgrowth (mixed enteric flora: *E. coli*, *Bacteroides fragilis*).

### Progression to Perforation
* Untreated ischaemia → Transmural necrosis.
* **Perforation (穿孔)** → Spillage of infected contents into the peritoneal cavity → **Peritonitis (腹膜炎)** or localized abscess formation.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
### The Classic Symptom Sequence
* **Anorexia (食慾不振)**: Often the very first symptom.
* **Periumbilical pain (肚臍周圍痛)**: Vague, visceral pain caused by distension of the appendix (midgut structure).
* **Vomiting (嘔吐)**: Usually occurs *after* the onset of pain (distinguishes from gastroenteritis).
* **Pain Migration (轉移性痛楚)**: After 12-24 hours, inflammation irritates the overlying parietal peritoneum → Sharp, somatic pain localized to the **Right Lower Quadrant (RLQ) / Right Iliac Fossa (RIF) (右下腹)**.

### Associated Symptoms
* Low-grade **fever (發燒)**.
* Nausea.
* Constipation or mild diarrhoea (due to an inflamed pelvic appendix irritating the rectum).

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Abdominal Examination Signs
* **McBurney's Point Tenderness (麥氏點壓痛)**: Maximal tenderness located 1/3 of the distance from the Anterior Superior Iliac Spine (ASIS) to the umbilicus.
* **Guarding (防衛性收縮)**: Voluntary muscle contraction upon palpation.
* **Rigidity (僵硬)**: Involuntary board-like abdomen (indicates generalized peritonitis).
* **Rebound Tenderness (反跳痛)**: Pain on sudden release of pressure (indicates parietal peritoneal irritation).

### Eponymous Signs
* **Rovsing's Sign (羅氏徵)**: Palpation of the Left Lower Quadrant (LLQ) elicits pain in the RLQ.
* **Psoas Sign (腰大肌徵)**: Pain on passive extension of the right hip (indicates a retrocaecal appendix irritating the psoas muscle).
* **Obturator Sign (閉孔肌徵)**: Pain on internal rotation of the flexed right hip (indicates a pelvic appendix irritating the obturator internus muscle).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Appendicitis vs. Gastroenteritis
* **Gastroenteritis (腸胃炎)**: Vomiting and diarrhoea typically *precede* the abdominal pain. Pain is diffuse, crampy, and lacks localized RLQ peritonism.

### Appendicitis vs. Mesenteric Adenitis
* **Mesenteric Adenitis (腸繫膜淋巴結炎)**: Often preceded by a viral URTI. High fever occurs early. Tenderness may shift when the patient rolls onto their side.

### Appendicitis vs. Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)
* **UTI (尿道炎)**: Dysuria, frequency, and high fever without the classic periumbilical-to-RLQ pain migration.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
### Summary of Differential Diagnoses
| Category | Conditions |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Gastrointestinal** | **Mesenteric adenitis (腸繫膜淋巴結炎)**, **Acute gastroenteritis (急性腸胃炎)**, Meckel's diverticulitis (clinically indistinguishable), Intussusception (younger children), Constipation. |
| **Urological** | **UTI / Pyelonephritis (腎盂腎炎)**, Right ureteric colic. |
| **Gynaecological (Females)** | **Ovarian torsion (卵巢扭轉)**, Ruptured ovarian cyst, Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID), Ectopic pregnancy. |
| **Respiratory** | Lower lobe pneumonia (referred abdominal pain). |
| **Surgical (Males)** | **Testicular torsion (睪丸扭轉)**. |

## 7. Tiered Investigations
### Bedside & Laboratory Tests
* **Clinical Diagnosis**: Diagnosis is primarily clinical (Pediatric Appendicitis Score (PAS) or Alvarado score may be used).
* **Bloods**:
  * CBP: Mild leukocytosis with a "left shift" (neutrophilia).
  * CRP: ↑ (Elevated).
* **Urinalysis (尿液測試)**:
  * Urine dipstick and microscopy to rule out UTI.
  * *Note*: A mildly inflamed appendix lying near the ureter/bladder can cause **Sterile pyuria (無菌性膿尿)** (WBCs in urine without bacteria).
* **Pregnancy Test**: Urine hCG is mandatory for all post-menarchal females.

### Imaging Modalities
* **Ultrasound Abdomen (腹部超聲波)**:
  * 1st-line imaging modality in children (avoids ionizing radiation).
  * +ve findings: Non-compressible, dilated appendix (>6 mm outer diameter), target sign, appendicolith, periappendiceal fluid.
* **CT Abdomen (腹部電腦掃描)**:
  * Reserved for cases where US is equivocal, the patient is obese, or complications (e.g., abscess) are suspected.

## 8. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Acute Resuscitation & Ward Management
* Keep NPO (Nil by mouth).
* **IV Fluid Hydration (靜脈輸液)**: Correct deficits and provide maintenance.
* **Analgesia (止痛藥)**: IV Paracetamol, short-acting opioids (e.g., IV Morphine). *Note*: Adequate analgesia does NOT mask clinical signs.

### Antibiotic Protocols (HA Guidelines)
* IV broad-spectrum antibiotics to cover Gram-negative enteric organisms and anaerobes.
* Typical HA regimens: **IV Cefuroxime (頭孢呋辛) + IV Metronidazole (甲硝唑)** OR **IV Amoxicillin-Clavulanate (阿莫西林/克拉維酸)**.

### Surgical Interventions
* **Uncomplicated Appendicitis**: Urgent **Laparoscopic Appendicectomy (腹腔鏡闌尾切除術)** (Gold standard).
* **Perforated with Localized Abscess (闌尾膿腫)**: May be managed conservatively initially with IV antibiotics ± percutaneous drainage (IR-guided) → **Interval appendicectomy (延期闌尾切除術)** 6-8 weeks later.
* **Generalized Peritonitis (泛發性腹膜炎)**: Emergency laparoscopy/laparotomy + copious peritoneal washout.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Multidisciplinary Team Involvement
* **Paediatric Surgery (小兒外科)**: Primary managing team.
* **Paediatrics (兒科)**: Co-management for infants or medically complex children.
* **Pain Team (APS)**: Post-operative pain management (PCA or NCA).

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Recognizing Non-Accidental Injury (NAI)
* **Medical Neglect (疏忽照顧)**: Significant, unexplained delay in seeking medical attention for a child with severe abdominal pain and vomiting, leading to a ruptured appendix and septic shock.
* **Trauma Mimic**: Abdominal trauma (e.g., punch/kick to the abdomen) causing bowel/mesenteric injury that mimics appendicitis. Look for bruising or an inconsistent history.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
### Major Complications
* **Perforation (穿孔)**: Risk is highest in children < 5 years old (up to 80%).
* **Intra-abdominal / Pelvic Abscess (腹腔/盆腔膿腫)**: Suspect if swinging fever occurs post-operatively.
* Surgical Site Infection (SSI).
* Paralytic ileus.
* **Adhesive Small Bowel Obstruction (SBO) (腸黏連阻塞)**: Long-term risk post-abdominal surgery.

### Prognosis
* Excellent with timely surgical intervention. Mortality is extremely rare in modern settings.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Viva Question: Why is the perforation rate so high in toddlers?
  Answer: Atypical presentation, inability to communicate pain, thin appendiceal wall, and a short, underdeveloped omentum that cannot effectively "wall off" the infection.
* Viva Question: What is the classic sequence of symptoms in appendicitis?
  Answer: Anorexia → Periumbilical pain → Vomiting → RLQ pain.
* Trap: Believing that giving strong analgesia (Morphine) to a child with an acute abdomen will mask peritoneal signs.
  Pearl: Adequate analgesia actually improves the reliability of the abdominal exam by relaxing the child and reducing voluntary guarding.
* Pitfall: Forgetting to examine the external genitalia in a boy with lower abdominal pain. Always examine the scrotum to rule out testicular torsion.
* Pitfall: Ruling out appendicitis just because there are WBCs in the urine. An inflamed appendix lying adjacent to the ureter or bladder can cause sterile pyuria.
* Pitfall: Omitting a pregnancy test in an adolescent female with lower abdominal pain. Always perform a urine hCG to rule out an ectopic pregnancy.
