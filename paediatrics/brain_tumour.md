
# Paediatric Brain Tumours (小兒腦腫瘤)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Neoplasms of the central nervous system.
- **Local Epidemiology**: The most common solid tumours in childhood, and the second most common paediatric cancer overall (after leukaemia). Managed in tertiary paediatric oncology centres (HKCH).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Location**: In children, ~60% of brain tumours are **infratentorial** (in the posterior fossa, e.g., cerebellum, brainstem), unlike adults where most are supratentorial.
- **Common Types**:
  1. **Astrocytoma**: Most common overall. Often low-grade (Pilocytic Astrocytoma, WHO Grade I), frequently in the cerebellum. Excellent prognosis if completely resected.
  2. **Medulloblastoma**: Most common *malignant* brain tumour. Arises in the cerebellar vermis. Highly cellular, tends to seed through the CSF ("drop metastases" to the spinal cord).
  3. **Ependymoma**: Arises from the ependymal lining of the ventricles (often the 4th ventricle), causing hydrocephalus.
  4. **Craniopharyngioma**: Supratentorial, arises from Rathke's pouch remnants near the pituitary. Causes visual and endocrine deficits.
- **Genetics**: Associated with neurocutaneous syndromes (NF1 -> Optic pathway glioma; TSC -> SEGA).

- See below.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- Symptoms depend on location and the rate of growth.
- **Signs of Raised Intracranial Pressure (ICP)**:
  - **Headache**: Typically worse in the morning, waking the child from sleep, worsened by coughing/straining.
  - **Vomiting**: Especially early morning, often without nausea.
  - **Papilloedema**: Swelling of the optic disc (may be absent in infants whose sutures can split).
  - **Infants**: Macrocephaly (rapidly increasing head circumference), bulging fontanelle, "sun-setting" eyes (upward gaze paresis).
- **Focal Neurological Signs**:
  - *Cerebellar (Infratentorial)*: Ataxia, broad-based gait, dysmetria, nystagmus.
  - *Brainstem*: Cranial nerve palsies (e.g., CN VI palsy causing diplopia), hemiparesis.
  - *Supratentorial*: Seizures, focal weakness.
  - *Pituitary/Hypothalamic*: Visual field defects (bitemporal hemianopia), growth failure, diabetes insipidus, precocious puberty.
## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Migraine**: Headaches are usually episodic, not progressively worsening every morning, and neurological exam is normal between attacks.
- **Gastroenteritis**: Vomiting is usually accompanied by diarrhoea and fever, not morning headaches.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Most Likely**:
  - Brain tumour (if classic morning headache/vomiting and focal signs).
- **Must-Not-Miss**:
  - **Brain Herniation**: A sudden deterioration in GCS, pupillary changes (blown pupil), and Cushing's triad (hypertension, bradycardia, irregular respirations) indicate impending herniation. Requires immediate neurosurgical intervention.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**:
  - **Thorough Neurological Exam**: Including fundoscopy (for papilloedema) and plotting head circumference.
  - **CT Brain (Non-contrast)**: Often the first imaging done in A&E for a child presenting with acute neurological deficit or severe raised ICP symptoms. Good for identifying haemorrhage, hydrocephalus, and large masses.
- **Gold Standard**:
  - **MRI Brain and Spine (with Gadolinium)**: The gold standard. Provides detailed anatomy, tumour extent, and checks for spinal "drop metastases" (essential for Medulloblastoma and Ependymoma).
  - **Lumbar Puncture**: For CSF cytology (to check for spread). **CONTRAINDICATED** if there is raised ICP or an obstructive mass, due to the risk of fatal coning (herniation). Must do imaging first.
  - **Biopsy/Resection**: For definitive histological and molecular diagnosis.
- **Monitoring**:
  - Endocrine workup (TFTs, Cortisol, GH, sex steroids) if a supratentorial/pituitary tumour is suspected.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**:
  - **Raised ICP Management**: Elevate head of bed to 30 degrees, IV Dexamethasone (reduces tumour-associated vasogenic oedema), Mannitol/Hypertonic saline if herniating. Urgent neurosurgical consultation for possible external ventricular drain (EVD) or VP shunt if obstructive hydrocephalus is present.
- **Chronic/Definitive**:
  - **Surgery**: Maximal safe resection is the primary goal for most tumours.
  - **Radiotherapy**: Highly effective for Medulloblastoma and Ependymoma.
  - *CRITICAL RULE*: **Avoided in children < 3 years old** (if possible) due to devastating effects on the developing brain (severe cognitive impairment, endocrine dysfunction).
  - **Chemotherapy**: Used extensively, especially in young children to delay the need for radiotherapy, or as an adjunct for malignant tumours.
## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Complex MDT at HKCH: Neurosurgeons, Oncologists, Radiation Oncologists, Endocrinologists, Rehabilitation team.
## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Devastating diagnosis for families. Requires immense MSW and psychological support (e.g., Children's Cancer Foundation).

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Prognosis**: Varies wildly. Pilocytic astrocytoma has >90% cure rate. High-grade gliomas or brainstem gliomas (DIPG) are almost universally fatal.
- **Late Effects of Treatment**: Endocrine deficits (panhypopituitarism), cognitive impairment (from radiation), secondary malignancies, hearing loss (from platinum-based chemo).

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Performing an LP to look for meningitis in a child with a 2-week history of morning headaches and vomiting. *Pearl*: ALWAYS do a CT/MRI brain before an LP if there are signs of raised ICP or focal neurological deficits to avoid fatal coning.
- **Viva Question**: "Why do we try to avoid cranial radiotherapy in a 2-year-old with a brain tumour?" *Answer*: The developing brain is highly sensitive to radiation. It causes severe, irreversible cognitive impairment and neuroendocrine deficits. We use chemotherapy to delay radiation until the child is older (usually >3 years).
