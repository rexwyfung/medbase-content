# Thalassaemia (地中海貧血)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Definition
* **Thalassaemia (地中海貧血)**: Group of inherited **autosomal recessive** blood disorders.
* Characterized by ↓ or absent synthesis of one or more globin chains (α or β) of haemoglobin.
* Result: Ineffective erythropoiesis, haemolysis, and severe **microcytic anaemia (小細胞性貧血)**.

### Local Epidemiology (Hong Kong)
* Extremely prevalent in Southern China & HK.
* **Alpha-thalassaemia trait**: ~5% of HK population.
* **Beta-thalassaemia trait**: ~3% of HK population.
* **Public Health**: Pre-marital & antenatal screening heavily promoted by Family Planning Association / HA in HK.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics

### Normal Haemoglobin Physiology
* **Adult Haemoglobin (HbA)**: α₂β₂ (2 alpha, 2 beta chains).
* **Fetal Haemoglobin (HbF)**: α₂γ₂ (2 alpha, 2 gamma chains). Predominates until 4-6 months of life.

### Alpha-Thalassaemia (甲型地中海貧血) Genetics
* Mutation: Deletion of α-globin genes (Normally 4 copies on chromosome 16).
* **1 Deletion (-α/αα)**: Silent carrier. Asymptomatic, normal CBP.
* **2 Deletions (--/αα or -α/-α)**: Alpha-thalassaemia trait. Mild microcytic anaemia.
* **3 Deletions (--/-α)**: **HbH disease**. Moderate-to-severe haemolytic anaemia, splenomegaly.
* **4 Deletions (--/--)**: **Hb Barts Hydrops Fetalis (胎兒水腫)**. Massively high oxygen affinity → tissue hypoxia → massive fetal oedema, heart failure, intrauterine/perinatal death. Incompatible with life.

### Beta-Thalassaemia (乙型地中海貧血) Genetics
* Mutation: Point mutations in β-globin gene (Normally 2 copies on chromosome 11). Results in reduced (β⁺) or absent (β⁰) chain production.
* **Beta-Thalassaemia Minor/Trait (β/β⁺ or β/β⁰)**: Heterozygous. Mild, asymptomatic microcytic anaemia.
* **Beta-Thalassaemia Major / Cooley's Anaemia (β⁰/β⁰)**: Homozygous. Severe, transfusion-dependent anaemia.

### Pathophysiology of Beta-Thalassaemia Major
* **Ineffective Erythropoiesis**: Excess unmatched α-chains precipitate in RBC precursors → destruction in bone marrow.
* **Haemolysis (溶血)**: Surviving abnormal RBCs destroyed by spleen.
* **Delayed Onset**: Normal at birth (protected by HbF α₂γ₂). Presents at 4-6 months when physiological switch (γ → β chains) fails.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Clinical Presentation Timing
* Normal at birth.
* Onset of symptoms at **4 to 6 months of age** as HbF ↓ and body fails to produce HbA.

### Symptoms of Severe Anaemia
* Progressive **pallor (蒼白)**.
* Lethargy, poor feeding, failure to thrive (FTT).
* Tachycardia, flow murmur.
* High-output heart failure.

### Signs of Extramedullary Haematopoiesis (骨髓外造血)
* **Massive Hepatosplenomegaly (肝脾腫大)**: Extreme abdominal distension.
* **Bone Expansion**: Marrow hyperplasia leads to "**Thalassaemic facies**" (frontal bossing, prominent maxilla, depressed nasal bridge, malocclusion of teeth).
* Skull X-ray: Classic "**hair-on-end**" appearance.

### Signs of Iron Overload (Haemosiderosis / 鐵質積聚)
* Result of chronic blood transfusions + ↑ intestinal iron absorption.
* Bronze/grey skin pigmentation.
* Cardiac/endocrine/hepatic dysfunction (see Complications).

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)

### Thalassaemia Trait vs. Iron Deficiency Anaemia (IDA)
| Feature | Thalassaemia Trait | Iron Deficiency Anaemia (IDA) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **RBC Count** | Normal or ↑ (compensatory) | ↓ |
| **MCV** | Very low, disproportionate to mild anaemia | Low, proportionate to degree of anaemia |
| **Ferritin** | Normal or ↑ | ↓ |
| **TIBC** | Normal | ↑ |
| **Mentzer Index (MCV/RBC)** | **< 13** | **> 13** |

## 5. Differential Diagnosis

### Causes of Microcytic Hypochromic Anaemia
* Iron Deficiency Anaemia (缺鐵性貧血).
* Sideroblastic Anaemia.
* Lead poisoning (鉛中毒) (rare in HK, but possible).
* Anaemia of Chronic Disease.

### Causes of Haemolytic Anaemia in Childhood
* G6PD Deficiency (G6PD缺乏症) (Very common in HK).
* Hereditary Spherocytosis (遺傳性球形紅血球症).
* Autoimmune Haemolytic Anaemia (AIHA).

## 6. Tiered Investigations

### Bedside & Initial Bloods
* **Complete Blood Picture (CBP)**: Severe microcytic (↓ MCV), hypochromic (↓ MCH) anaemia.
* **Peripheral Blood Smear (血塗片)**: Target cells (靶形紅血球), nucleated RBCs, basophilic stippling, extreme anisopoikilocytosis.
* **Mentzer Index**: MCV/RBC < 13.

### Gold Standard Diagnostics
* **Haemoglobin Pattern (Hb Electrophoresis / HPLC)**:
    * *Beta-Thal Major*: Absent/very low HbA, massively elevated HbF (>90%), elevated HbA2.
    * *Beta-Thal Trait*: Elevated HbA2 (>3.5%).
* **Genetic Testing (DNA Analysis)**: Definitive diagnosis, identifies specific mutations. Mandatory for family screening / prenatal diagnosis.

### Monitoring Investigations (For Iron Overload & Complications)
* **Iron Studies**: Serum ferritin (regular monitoring, target < 1000 µg/L).
* **Imaging**: Liver/Cardiac MRI T2* (Gold standard for quantifying tissue iron loading).
* **Endocrine panel**: Fasting glucose, TFTs, calcium/phosphate, sex hormones (monitoring for haemosiderosis damage).
* **Cardiac**: ECG, Echocardiogram (monitoring for cardiomyopathy/heart failure).

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)

### 1st Line Chronic Management: Hypertransfusion Therapy
* **Regimen**: Lifelong regular packed RBC transfusions every 3-4 weeks.
* **Target**: Maintain pre-transfusion Hb > 9.5 g/dL.
* **Goal**: Suppress extramedullary haematopoiesis (prevents bony deformities/splenomegaly), promote normal growth.

### Mandatory Iron Chelation Therapy (除鐵治療)
* Initiated after ~10-20 transfusions or when Serum Ferritin > 1000 µg/L.
* **Deferasirox (Exjade / Jadenu)**: 1st line in HA. Oral, once daily. Preferred for compliance.
* **Deferoxamine (Desferal)**: SC infusion over 8-12 hours/night (3-7 nights/week). Often requires an infusion pump.
* **Deferiprone (Ferriprox)**: Oral, 3 times daily. Good for cardiac iron removal, but risk of agranulocytosis.

### Surgical Management
* **Splenectomy (脾臟切除術)**: Considered if severe hypersplenism (transfusion requirement escalates massively > 200-250 ml/kg/year).
    * *Pre-op*: Pneumococcal, meningococcal, Hib vaccines.
    * *Post-op*: Lifelong prophylactic Penicillin V.

### Curative Therapy
* **Haematopoietic Stem Cell Transplant (HSCT / 骨髓移植)**: Only definitive cure.
* Requires HLA-matched sibling or fully matched unrelated donor.
* Best performed early in childhood before end-organ iron damage occurs.

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care

### Multidisciplinary Team
* **Paediatric Haematologist**: Lead coordinator of care.
* **Endocrinologist**: Management of iron-overload sequelae (DM, hypogonadism).
* **Cardiologist**: Monitoring for myocardial haemosiderosis.
* **Medical Social Worker (MSW) / Clinical Psychologist**: Enormous psychosocial burden (monthly hospital visits, daily medications, chronic illness fatigue).
* **Patient Support Groups**: Children's Thalassaemia Foundation (HK).

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags

### Medical Neglect
* Failure to attend scheduled blood transfusions.
* Non-compliance with iron chelation therapy → rapidly fatal iron overload.
* If repeated non-compliance endangers the child, HA protocols mandate involvement of MSW and potentially Social Welfare Department (SWD) for medical neglect.

## 10. Complications & Prognosis

### Iron Overload Toxicity (Haemosiderosis)
* *The primary cause of morbidity and mortality.*
* **Cardiac**: Heart failure, fatal arrhythmias (Leading cause of death).
* **Hepatic**: Cirrhosis, portal hypertension, Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC).
* **Endocrine**: Hypogonadotropic hypogonadism (delayed puberty/infertility), Diabetes Mellitus (iron deposition in pancreas), Hypothyroidism, Hypoparathyroidism.

### Transfusion-Related Complications
* Alloimmunization (development of red cell antibodies making future cross-matching difficult).
* Blood-borne infections (Hep B/C, HIV) - very rare now due to HKRCBTS screening.

### Prognosis
* With rigorous compliance to modern hypertransfusion and chelation protocols, survival extends well into the 40s-50s and beyond. Poor compliance drastically reduces life expectancy (often fatal in 20s due to cardiac failure).

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: Why does a baby with Beta-Thalassaemia Major appear completely normal at birth and only present at 6 months of age?
* Answer: At birth, the predominant haemoglobin is Fetal Haemoglobin (HbF), which consists of alpha and gamma chains (α₂γ₂). The beta-globin defect does not manifest until the physiological switch from gamma to beta chain production occurs around 4-6 months of age.
* Viva Question: What is the leading cause of death in patients with Beta-Thalassaemia Major?
* Answer: Cardiac failure or fatal arrhythmias secondary to myocardial iron overload (haemosiderosis).
* Trap: Confusing the Mentzer index rules for Thalassaemia vs IDA.
* Pearl: Mentzer index = MCV / RBC. If < 13, it points to Thalassaemia trait (RBC count is robust but cells are tiny). If > 13, it points to IDA (RBC count is depleted).
* Pitfall: Performing a splenectomy without addressing infectious risks. Splenectomy carries a high risk of overwhelming post-splenectomy infection (OPSI). Ensure pneumococcal, meningococcal, and Hib vaccinations are given pre-operatively, followed by lifelong prophylactic penicillin.
