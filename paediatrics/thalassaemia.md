# Thalassaemia (地中海貧血)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A group of inherited autosomal recessive blood disorders characterized by reduced or absent synthesis of one or more of the globin chains (alpha or beta) that make up haemoglobin. This leads to ineffective erythropoiesis, haemolysis, and severe microcytic anaemia.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Extremely prevalent in Southern China and Hong Kong. 
  - ~5% of the HK population are carriers of Alpha-thalassaemia trait.
  - ~3% are carriers of Beta-thalassaemia trait.
  - Pre-marital and antenatal screening is a major public health initiative in HK (Family Planning Association / HA).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Normal Haemoglobin (HbA)**: Consists of 2 alpha and 2 beta chains (α2β2).
- **Alpha-Thalassaemia**: Deletion of alpha-globin genes (normally 4 copies on chromosome 16).
  - *1 deletion*: Silent carrier.
  - *2 deletions*: Alpha-thalassaemia trait (mild microcytic anaemia).
  - *3 deletions*: HbH disease (moderate to severe haemolytic anaemia, splenomegaly).
  - *4 deletions*: Hb Barts Hydrops Fetalis (incompatible with life, massive fetal oedema, intrauterine death).
- **Beta-Thalassaemia**: Point mutations in the beta-globin gene (normally 2 copies on chromosome 11) causing reduced (β+) or absent (β0) production.
  - *Beta-Thalassaemia Minor (Trait)*: Heterozygous. Mild, asymptomatic microcytic anaemia.
  - *Beta-Thalassaemia Major (Cooley's Anaemia)*: Homozygous (β0/β0). Severe, transfusion-dependent anaemia presenting at 6 months of age (when fetal haemoglobin, HbF, switches to adult HbA). The excess unmatched alpha chains precipitate, destroying red blood cells in the marrow (ineffective erythropoiesis) and spleen (haemolysis).

## 3. Clinical Presentation (Focus on Beta-Thalassaemia Major)
- **Timing**: Normal at birth (protected by HbF, which is α2γ2). Presents at **4 to 6 months of age** as HbF levels fall and the body fails to produce HbA.
- **Presentation**: Progressive pallor, lethargy, poor feeding, failure to thrive, and a distended abdomen (due to massive hepatosplenomegaly).

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Severe Anaemia**: Profound pallor, tachycardia, flow murmur, heart failure.
- **Extramedullary Haematopoiesis**: The body desperately tries to make red blood cells outside the marrow.
  - *Massive Hepatosplenomegaly*.
  - *Bone Expansion*: "Thalassaemic facies" (frontal bossing, prominent maxilla, depressed nasal bridge, malocclusion of teeth).
- **Iron Overload (Haemosiderosis)**: A consequence of both chronic transfusions and increased intestinal iron absorption. Leads to bronze/grey skin pigmentation.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Iron Deficiency Anaemia (IDA)**: Both cause microcytic, hypochromic anaemia. 
  - *Thalassaemia Trait*: Normal/high RBC count, very low MCV out of proportion to the anaemia, normal/high ferritin.
  - *IDA*: Low RBC count, low ferritin, high TIBC. Mentzer index (MCV/RBC) > 13 suggests IDA; < 13 suggests Thalassaemia trait.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Iron Deficiency Anaemia.
- Sideroblastic Anaemia.
- Lead poisoning.
- Other haemolytic anaemias (e.g., G6PD deficiency, Hereditary Spherocytosis).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Complete Blood Picture (CBP)**: Severe microcytic, hypochromic anaemia.
- **Blood Smear**: Target cells, nucleated red blood cells, basophilic stippling, extreme anisopoikilocytosis.
- **Haemoglobin Pattern (Hb Electrophoresis or HPLC)**: 
  - *Beta-Thal Major*: Absent or very low HbA, massively elevated HbF (>90%), elevated HbA2.
  - *Beta-Thal Trait*: Elevated HbA2 (>3.5%).
- **Iron Studies**: Ferritin is high (to rule out IDA and monitor iron overload).
- **Genetic Testing (DNA Analysis)**: Definitive diagnosis and essential for family screening/prenatal diagnosis.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Beta-Thalassaemia Major)
- **Chronic Blood Transfusions**: The mainstay of therapy. 
  - Regular packed RBC transfusions every 3-4 weeks to maintain a pre-transfusion Hb > 9.5 g/dL.
  - *Goal*: Suppress extramedullary haematopoiesis (preventing bone deformities and massive splenomegaly) and allow normal growth.
- **Iron Chelation Therapy**: Absolutely mandatory to prevent fatal iron overload.
  - Started after ~10-20 transfusions or when Ferritin > 1000 µg/L.
  - *Agents*: Deferoxamine (SC infusion over 8-12 hours/night), Deferasirox (oral, preferred), Deferiprone (oral).
- **Splenectomy**: Considered if transfusion requirements escalate massively (hypersplenism). Requires pneumococcal/meningococcal/Hib vaccination and lifelong prophylactic penicillin.
- **Curative**: **Haematopoietic Stem Cell Transplant (HSCT)** from an HLA-matched sibling is the only definitive cure.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Paediatric Haematology**.
- **Endocrinology**: To manage complications of iron overload.
- **Psychological Support**: Huge burden of chronic illness, monthly hospital visits, and nightly chelation infusions. Children's Thalassaemia Foundation provides support.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Medical neglect: Failure to attend regular transfusions or non-compliance with iron chelation therapy is life-threatening and constitutes neglect.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Iron Overload (The main cause of morbidity and mortality)**:
  - *Cardiac*: Heart failure, arrhythmias (the leading cause of death).
  - *Endocrine*: Hypogonadotropic hypogonadism (delayed puberty), Diabetes Mellitus, Hypothyroidism, Hypoparathyroidism.
  - *Hepatic*: Cirrhosis, Hepatocellular carcinoma.
- **Transfusion Risks**: Alloimmunization, blood-borne infections (Hep B/C, HIV - rare now).
- **Prognosis**: With rigorous transfusion and chelation therapy, patients can survive well into adulthood (40s-50s+), but compliance is a major challenge.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "Why does a baby with Beta-Thalassaemia Major appear completely normal at birth and only present at 6 months of age?" (At birth, the predominant haemoglobin is Fetal Haemoglobin (HbF), which consists of alpha and gamma chains (α2γ2). The beta-globin defect does not manifest until the physiological switch from gamma to beta chain production occurs around 4-6 months of age).
- *Viva*: "What is the leading cause of death in patients with Beta-Thalassaemia Major?" (Cardiac failure or fatal arrhythmias secondary to myocardial iron overload / haemosiderosis).
