# Neonatal Jaundice (初生嬰兒黃疸)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Yellowish discoloration of the skin, sclera, and mucous membranes due to hyperbilirubinaemia. Clinically visible when Total Serum Bilirubin (TSB) > 85 µmol/L.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Extremely common in Hong Kong. ~60-80% of Chinese newborns develop visible jaundice. Hong Kong has a high prevalence of G6PD deficiency (4.5% of males, 0.5% of females) and ABO incompatibility, making pathological jaundice a significant local burden.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Physiological**: Neonatal RBCs have a shorter lifespan (70-90 days) leading to increased bilirubin load. The neonatal liver has immature UGT1A1 enzyme activity (decreased conjugation) and there is increased enterohepatic circulation due to sterile gut and high beta-glucuronidase in breast milk.
- **Pathological**:
  - *Increased Production*: Haemolysis (ABO/Rh incompatibility, G6PD deficiency, Hereditary Spherocytosis), Polycythaemia, Cephalhaematoma/bruising.
  - *Decreased Clearance*: Crigler-Najjar syndrome, Gilbert syndrome, Congenital Hypothyroidism.
  - *Increased Enterohepatic Circulation*: Breastfeeding jaundice (dehydration/low intake), Bowel obstruction.
  - *Conjugated Hyperbilirubinaemia (>20% of TSB)*: Biliary Atresia, Neonatal Hepatitis (TORCH), Choledochal cyst.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- **Timing is crucial**:
  - **< 24 hours**: ALWAYS pathological (Haemolysis, Sepsis).
  - **24-72 hours**: Usually physiological, but can be early onset of pathological causes.
  - **> 72 hours to 2 weeks**: Breast milk jaundice, infection, cephalhaematoma.
  - **> 2 weeks (Prolonged Jaundice)**: Biliary atresia, Hypothyroidism, Breast milk jaundice, UTI.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Cephalocaudal Progression**: Jaundice progresses from head to toe. Kramer's rule (Zone 1: head/neck ~100 µmol/L; Zone 5: palms/soles >250 µmol/L).
- **Dehydration signs**: Sunken fontanelle, dry mucous membranes, poor skin turgor, < 6 wet nappies/day (Breastfeeding jaundice).
- **Lethargy, poor feeding, high-pitched cry**: Early signs of Acute Bilirubin Encephalopathy (ABE).
- **Opisthotonos, seizures**: Late signs of ABE / Kernicterus.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Breastfeeding vs. Breast Milk Jaundice**:
  - *Breastfeeding Jaundice*: Early onset (first week), due to suboptimal milk intake leading to dehydration and delayed meconium passage. Weight loss >10%.
  - *Breast Milk Jaundice*: Late onset (peaks around 2 weeks), due to factors in breast milk (e.g., beta-glucuronidases) increasing enterohepatic circulation. Baby is thriving and gaining weight normally.
- **Unconjugated vs. Conjugated**: Conjugated jaundice presents with pale/chalky stools and dark urine. Unconjugated has normal yellow/green stools.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Unconjugated**: Physiological, Breast milk/feeding, G6PD deficiency, ABO/Rh incompatibility, Cephalhaematoma, Sepsis/UTI, Congenital Hypothyroidism.
- **Conjugated**: Biliary Atresia, Neonatal Hepatitis (CMV, Rubella), Alpha-1 Antitrypsin deficiency, Galactosaemia.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
**Bedside**: Transcutaneous Bilirubinometry (TcB) - screening tool. Stool colour card (DH protocol).
- **Bloods (Jaundice Screen)**:
  - Total and Direct Bilirubin (SBR).
  - Maternal and infant blood group & Rh status.
  - Direct Antiglobulin Test (DAT / Coombs test).
  - Complete Blood Count (CBC) & Reticulocyte count.
  - G6PD screening (Routine cord blood screening in HK).
- **Prolonged Jaundice Screen (>14 days term, >21 days preterm)**:
  - Split bilirubin (Total & Direct).
  - Thyroid Function Tests (TFTs).
  - Urine routine & culture (to rule out UTI).
  - Liver Function Tests (LFTs).
- **Imaging**: Ultrasound abdomen (if conjugated, to look for biliary tree/choledochal cyst).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **HA Protocol**: Plot TSB on the NICE or AAP Bilirubin Nomogram based on gestational age and hours of life.
- **Phototherapy**:
  - Indicated when TSB crosses the phototherapy threshold.
  - Mechanism: Photoisomerization of bilirubin to water-soluble lumirubin.
  - Care: Eye protection, expose maximal skin, monitor temperature and hydration.
- **Exchange Transfusion**:
  - Indicated if TSB is >= 85 µmol/L above phototherapy line, or signs of ABE, or rapidly rising TSB despite intensive phototherapy.
  - Removes bilirubin and antibody-coated RBCs.
- **IVIG**: Consider in isoimmune haemolytic disease (ABO/Rh) if TSB is rising rapidly despite phototherapy.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **MCHC**: Crucial role in community follow-up. Routine day 3-5 checks for jaundice and weight. MCHC uses TcB and refers to HA Paediatric A&E/Ward if above threshold.
- **Lactation Consultants**: Support for breastfeeding jaundice to improve latch and milk supply.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Delayed presentation to medical care despite severe jaundice and lethargy (Medical neglect).
- Inconsistent history regarding feeding and output.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Kernicterus (Chronic Bilirubin Encephalopathy)**: Permanent neurological damage due to bilirubin deposition in the basal ganglia. Features: Choreoathetoid cerebral palsy, sensorineural hearing loss, upward gaze palsy, dental enamel dysplasia.
- **Prognosis**: Excellent if treated promptly. Physiological and breast milk jaundice resolve without sequelae.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Pitfall*: Never ignore jaundice in the first 24 hours. It is haemolysis or sepsis until proven otherwise.
- *Viva*: "How do you differentiate breastfeeding from breast milk jaundice?" (Weight gain and hydration status).
- *Viva*: "What is the stool colour in biliary atresia?" (Pale/acholic. Mention the DH stool colour card given to all parents in HK).
