# Hyperthyroidism (甲狀腺機能亢進)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Hyperthyroidism
* State of excessive thyroid hormone production and resultant hypermetabolic state.
* **Thyrotoxicosis (甲狀腺毒症)**: Clinical syndrome of excess circulating thyroid hormones (irrespective of source).

### Local Epidemiology of Paediatric Hyperthyroidism
* Less common than hypothyroidism in children.
* **Incidence peak**: Adolescence (11–15 years).
* **Gender**: Female predominant (F:M ≈ 5:1).
* **Most common cause**: **Graves' Disease (格雷夫斯病)** accounts for >95% of paediatric/adolescent cases.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics

### Pathophysiology of Graves' Disease
* Autoimmune disorder.
* Autoantibodies (TSH-Receptor Antibodies [**TRab**] / Thyroid Stimulating Immunoglobulins [**TSI**]) bind to & stimulate the TSH receptor.
* → Unregulated overproduction of **T3** and **T4**.
* → Negative feedback severely suppresses central **TSH**.

### Genetics & Associated Autoimmune Conditions
* Strong familial clustering; HLA-DR3 association.
* Frequently co-exists with other autoimmune disorders (Polyglandular Autoimmune Syndromes):
  * **Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus (一型糖尿病)**
  * **Coeliac Disease (乳糜瀉)**
  * **Vitiligo (白癜風)**
  * **Addison's Disease (阿狄森氏病)**
* ↑ Prevalence in **Down Syndrome (唐氏綜合症)** and **Turner Syndrome**.

### Pathophysiology of Neonatal Thyrotoxicosis
* Caused by transplacental passage of maternal **IgG TRab**.
* Typically transient (resolves in 2–3 months as maternal antibodies clear).
* Can be life-threatening → heart failure, craniosynostosis (premature suture fusion), neurodevelopmental impairment.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Key Symptoms of Paediatric Hyperthyroidism
* **Constitutional**: Weight loss despite ↑ appetite (polyphagia), heat intolerance, diaphoresis.
* **Neurological/Psychiatric**: Emotional lability, irritability, poor school performance, sleep disturbances.
* **Gastrointestinal**: Frequent bowel movements / diarrhoea.
* **Reproductive**: Oligomenorrhoea / secondary amenorrhoea in post-menarchal girls.

### Key Physical Signs of Paediatric Hyperthyroidism
* **Neck**: **Goitre (甲狀腺腫)** → Diffuse, smooth, non-tender enlargement ± audible bruit (hypervascularity).
* **Ophthalmopathy**: **Exophthalmos / Proptosis (突眼)**, lid lag, lid retraction, periorbital oedema (less severe in children vs adults).
* **Cardiovascular**: Tachycardia (persists during sleep — highly specific!), widened pulse pressure, systolic flow murmur.
* **Neuromuscular**: Fine action tremor, brisk deep tendon reflexes (hyperreflexia), proximal myopathy.
* **Growth**: Accelerated linear growth (tall stature temporarily) + advanced bone age.

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)

### Discriminatory Features against ADHD and Anxiety
* **ADHD / Anxiety Disorders** often mimic the hyperactivity, restlessness, and emotional lability of hyperthyroidism.
* **Key Discriminators**: Psychiatric mimics lack the objective physical hypermetabolic signs:
  * NO goitre.
  * NO true weight loss despite hyperphagia.
  * NO resting/sleeping tachycardia.
  * NO advanced bone age.

## 5. Differential Diagnosis

### Differential Diagnosis Table for Paediatric Hyperthyroidism
| Differential | Aetiology | Distinguishing Features |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Graves' Disease** | Autoimmune (TRab +ve) | Diffuse goitre, exophthalmos, TRab/TSI positive. |
| **Hashitoxicosis** | Early Hashimoto's Thyroiditis | Transient hyperthyroidism followed by hypothyroidism. Anti-TPO +ve. |
| **Neonatal Thyrotoxicosis** | Maternal TRab transfer | Maternal history of Graves' (even if currently euthyroid/post-ablation). |
| **Toxic Multinodular Goitre** | Autonomous nodules | Rare in children. Palpable nodules. "Hot" nodules on radioisotope scan. |
| **Factitious Thyrotoxicosis** | Exogenous thyroxine intake | Absent goitre, ↓ Thyroglobulin, psychiatric/weight-loss history. |

## 6. Tiered Investigations

### Bedside Investigations for Hyperthyroidism
* **Vital Signs**: Resting/sleeping tachycardia, widened pulse pressure, ↑ BP.
* **Growth Parameters**: Plot on HK growth charts (↑ height centile, ↓ weight centile).
* **ECG**: Sinus tachycardia (Atrial fibrillation is rare in paediatrics).

### Blood Investigations for Hyperthyroidism
* **TFTs (Gold Standard)**: ↓ TSH (<0.05 mU/L), ↑ Free T4, ↑ Free T3.
* **Autoantibodies**: 
  * **TRab / TSI**: Diagnostic for Graves' disease.
  * **Anti-TPO & Anti-Tg**: May also be +ve.
* **Pre-Treatment Baseline Bloods**: **CBC** (baseline WBC) and **LFTs** (baseline transaminases) are mandatory before initiating anti-thyroid drugs.

### Imaging Investigations for Hyperthyroidism
* **Ultrasound Thyroid**: Diffusely enlarged, heterogenous, hypervascular gland (Classic "**thyroid inferno**" on colour Doppler).
* **Radionuclide Scintigraphy (Tc-99m / I-123)**: Diffuse ↑ uptake in Graves'. Localised ↑ uptake in toxic nodules. ↓ uptake in thyroiditis/factitious.

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)

### Acute Management of Hyperthyroidism Symptoms
* **Beta-blockers**: **Propranolol (普萘洛爾)**.
* 1st-line for rapid symptomatic relief of adrenergic symptoms (tachycardia, tremor, anxiety).
* Weaned off once euthyroid state achieved with anti-thyroid drugs.

### Chronic Medical Management (HK HA Protocol)
* **Anti-Thyroid Drugs (ATDs)**: 1st-line definitive therapy in children.
* **Carbimazole (卡比馬唑)** (or Methimazole): Drug of choice.
* **Treatment Strategies**:
  1. **Titration Regimen**: Start high dose → titrate down as fT4 normalises. (Preferred in HK to minimise drug doses).
  2. **Block-and-Replace Regimen**: High dose Carbimazole to completely block intrinsic production + Levothyroxine supplementation. (Ensures stable TFTs but higher risk of side effects).
* **Duration**: Long course (often 2–4+ years) aiming for spontaneous remission.

### Second-Line / Definitive Surgical & Ablative Management
| Option | Indications | Pros / Cons |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Radioactive Iodine (RAI - I-131)** | ATD failure/relapse, severe adverse reactions to ATDs. Usually reserved for >10 yrs old. | Avoids surgery. **Cons**: Permanent hypothyroidism requiring lifelong thyroxine. Worsens ophthalmopathy. |
| **Total Thyroidectomy (甲狀腺全切除術)** | Massive goitre with compressive symptoms, severe ophthalmopathy, <10 yrs old, patient choice. | Immediate cure. **Cons**: Risks of recurrent laryngeal nerve palsy & hypoparathyroidism (hypocalcaemia). Requires pre-op Lugol's iodine to ↓ vascularity. |

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care

### Multidisciplinary Team Care for Paediatric Hyperthyroidism
* **Paediatric Endocrinologist**: Lead physician for long-term ATD titration.
* **Ophthalmologist**: Mandatory referral for Graves' ophthalmopathy. (Smoking/second-hand smoke severely worsens eye disease).
* **Clinical Psychologist / Medical Social Worker (MSW)**: Crucial for managing school performance decline, behavioural changes, and chronic disease burden.

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags

### Safeguarding Against Carbimazole Toxicity (Agranulocytosis)
* **Agranulocytosis** is a rare but fatal complication of Carbimazole.
* **CRITICAL Safety Netting**: Parents must be explicitly counselled.
* **Protocol**: If child develops **fever, sore throat, or mouth ulcers** → STOP Carbimazole immediately → attend A&E for STAT **Complete Blood Count (CBC)** to rule out severe neutropenia.

### Hepatotoxicity Safeguarding
* **Propylthiouracil (PTU)** is generally **CONTRAINDICATED** in paediatric practice (except in acute thyroid storm).
* High risk of unpredictable, idiosyncratic, fulminant liver failure.

## 10. Complications & Prognosis

### Prognosis of Paediatric Graves' Disease
* **Remission Rates**: Lower in children than adults (only ~20-30% achieve remission after 2 years of ATDs).
* Most patients eventually require definitive therapy (RAI or Surgery).

### Acute Complication: Thyroid Storm (甲狀腺風暴)
* Life-threatening hypermetabolic crisis triggered by stress, infection, or surgery.
* **Features**: Hyperthermia, severe tachycardia, delirium, heart failure, shock.
* **Management (ICU)**: 
  * PTU (blocks T4→T3 peripheral conversion)
  * Propranolol (blocks beta-adrenergic receptors)
  * Lugol's Iodine (inhibits hormone release - Wolff-Chaikoff effect)
  * Hydrocortisone (prevents adrenal crisis & blocks T4→T3 conversion).

## 12. Examiner's Pearls

* Trap: Prescribing PTU for a newly diagnosed 12-year-old with Graves' disease. Pearl: PTU is completely contraindicated in routine paediatric practice due to the risk of fulminant liver failure requiring transplantation. Always choose Carbimazole/Methimazole.
* Viva Question: "What is the most important discharge advice to give a patient starting Carbimazole?"? Answer: "If you develop a fever, sore throat, or mouth ulcers, stop the medication immediately and go to A&E for an urgent white cell count to rule out agranulocytosis."
* Pitfall: Attributing sleeping tachycardia to anxiety or night terrors. In a child with weight loss and behavioural changes, tachycardia that persists during deep sleep is a pathognomonic red flag for thyrotoxicosis and mandates TFTs.
* Viva Question: "How do you prepare a patient with Graves' disease for a total thyroidectomy to minimise surgical bleeding risk?"? Answer: "Administer Lugol's iodine for 10-14 days pre-operatively to decrease the vascularity and friability of the thyroid gland."
