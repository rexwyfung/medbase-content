
# Hyperthyroidism (甲狀腺機能亢進)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A state of excessive thyroid hormone production.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Less common than hypothyroidism in children, but incidence increases in adolescence. Graves' disease accounts for >95% of cases in children and adolescents. More common in females.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Graves' Disease**: An autoimmune disorder where Thyroid Stimulating Immunoglobulins (TSI) or TSH-Receptor Antibodies (TRab) bind to and activate the TSH receptor on the thyroid gland, causing unregulated overproduction of T3 and T4.
- Often associated with other autoimmune conditions (Type 1 DM, Coeliac disease, Vitiligo) and Down syndrome.
- **Neonatal Graves'**: Caused by transplacental passage of maternal TRab. Usually transient (resolves in 2-3 months as maternal antibodies clear) but can be life-threatening.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- See below.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Symptoms**: Weight loss despite increased appetite, heat intolerance, palpitations, tremor, emotional lability, poor school performance, frequent bowel movements, oligomenorrhoea.
- **Signs**:
  - **Goitre**: Diffuse, smooth, non-tender enlargement. Often with a bruit.
  - **Eyes**: Exophthalmos (proptosis), lid lag, lid retraction (Graves' ophthalmopathy is less severe in children than adults).
  - **Cardiovascular**: Tachycardia (even during sleep), widened pulse pressure.
  - **Neurological**: Fine tremor, brisk reflexes.
  - **Growth**: Accelerated growth and advanced bone age.
## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Anxiety/ADHD**: Can mimic the hyperactivity and emotional lability, but lack the physical signs (goitre, weight loss, tachycardia).

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Most Likely**:
  - Graves' Disease.
- **Must-Not-Miss**:
  - **Thyroid Storm**: A rare, life-threatening hypermetabolic state (fever, severe tachycardia, delirium, heart failure).
  - Neonatal thyrotoxicosis (can cause heart failure, craniosynostosis).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**:
  - **ECG**: Sinus tachycardia.
  - **Weight and Height**: Plot on growth charts.
- **Gold Standard**:
  - **TFTs**: Suppressed TSH (<0.05 mU/L), elevated Free T4 and Free T3.
  - **Autoantibodies**: TSH-Receptor Antibodies (TRab) or Thyroid Stimulating Immunoglobulins (TSI) are diagnostic for Graves'. Anti-TPO and Anti-Tg may also be positive.
  - **Ultrasound Thyroid**: Diffusely enlarged, hypervascular gland ("thyroid inferno").
- **Monitoring**:
  - Regular TFTs to titrate medication.
  - CBC and LFTs (to monitor for drug side effects).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**:
  - **Beta-blockers (Propranolol)**: First-line for rapid symptomatic relief of adrenergic symptoms (tachycardia, tremor) while waiting for anti-thyroid drugs to work.
- **Chronic/Definitive**:
  - **1. Anti-Thyroid Drugs (ATDs)**: First-line therapy in children.
  - **Carbimazole** (or Methimazole): Preferred drug. Given for a prolonged course (often 2-4 years or longer) in hopes of achieving remission.
  - *Propylthiouracil (PTU)*: Generally AVOIDED in children due to the risk of severe, unpredictable hepatotoxicity.
  - **2. Radioactive Iodine (RAI - I-131)**: Definitive treatment if ATDs fail, relapse occurs, or severe side effects develop. Usually reserved for older children/adolescents. Results in permanent hypothyroidism requiring lifelong Thyroxine.
  - **3. Total Thyroidectomy**: Definitive surgical option. Indicated for very large goitres, severe ophthalmopathy, or if RAI is contraindicated/refused.
## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Ophthalmology review if significant eye signs.
## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- **CRITICAL Safety Netting for Carbimazole**: Educate parents on the risk of **Agranulocytosis**. They MUST stop the drug and seek immediate medical attention (A&E) for a CBC if the child develops a fever, sore throat, or mouth ulcers.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Remission rates with ATDs in children are lower than in adults (~20-30% after 2 years). Many eventually require definitive therapy (RAI or surgery).
- Untreated: Arrhythmias, heart failure, osteoporosis.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Prescribing PTU for a child. *Pearl*: PTU is contraindicated in paediatric practice due to the risk of fulminant liver failure. Always choose Carbimazole.
- **Viva Question**: "What is the most important advice to give a patient starting Carbimazole?" *Answer*: "If you develop a fever or sore throat, stop the medication immediately and go to A&E for an urgent white cell count to rule out agranulocytosis."
