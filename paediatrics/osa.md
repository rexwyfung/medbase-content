# Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA) (阻塞性睡眠窒息症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition
- **Disorder of breathing** during sleep → prolonged partial upper airway obstruction and/or intermittent complete obstruction (**obstructive apnoea**) → disrupts normal ventilation & sleep architecture.

### Local Epidemiology (HK Context)
- **Prevalence**: Affects ~1-4% of children in HK (up to 12% have primary snoring).
- **Age Peaks**: 
  - 1st peak: **2-8 years** (coincides with peak adenotonsillar hypertrophy).
  - 2nd peak: **Adolescence** (associated with rising childhood **Obesity (肥胖症)** rates).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Pathophysiology Mechanism
- During **REM sleep**, normal physiological relaxation of upper airway & chest voluntary muscles occurs.
- In children with anatomical/functional narrowing → normal hypotonia leads to excessive airway collapse → ↑ work of breathing & recurrent hypoxia/hypercapnia → frequent cortical arousals → sleep fragmentation.

### Primary Causes & Risk Factors
- **Adenotonsillar Hypertrophy (腺樣體及扁桃腺肥大)**: Most common cause in children.
- **Obesity**: Fat deposition in parapharyngeal tissues narrows airway.
- **Craniofacial Anomalies**: **Micrognathia (小頜畸形)** (e.g., Pierre Robin sequence), midface hypoplasia (e.g., **Down Syndrome (唐氏綜合症)**, Crouzon syndrome).
- **Neuromuscular Disorders**: Hypotonia/dystonia (e.g., **Cerebral Palsy (腦性麻痺)**, Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy, SMA) → poor airway tone.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Nighttime Symptoms
- Loud, habitual **snoring (打鼻鼾)** (hallmark).
- Witnessed **apnoeas (窒息)** followed by gasping/snorting/choking.
- Restless sleep, frequent awakenings, unusual sleeping positions (neck hyperextended).
- Secondary **enuresis (尿床)**.

### Daytime Symptoms
- **Neurobehavioural**: Hyperactivity, poor concentration, inattention, poor school performance (frequently mimics **ADHD**).
- **Somnolence**: Excessive daytime sleepiness (more common in adults/adolescents).
- Mouth breathing (due to adenoidal hypertrophy), morning headaches (due to CO2 retention).

### Clinical Signs
- **Adenoidal facies**: Open mouth, long face, high-arched palate.
- Enlarged tonsils ("kissing tonsils"), nasal speech.
- **Faltering growth (生長遲緩)**: Due to ↑ nocturnal work of breathing & ↓ growth hormone release during sleep.

## 4. Discriminatory Features
### OSA vs Primary Snoring vs ADHD
| Feature | OSA | Primary Snoring | ADHD |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Nighttime Apnoeas/Gasping** | +ve | -ve | -ve |
| **Daytime Hyperactivity** | +ve (secondary to sleep debt) | -ve | +ve (primary neurodevelopmental) |
| **Oximetry/PSG Desaturations** | +ve | -ve | -ve |

## 5. Differential Diagnosis
### Common & Life-Threatening Differentials
- **Primary Snoring**: Snoring without obstructive apnoeas, hypoxia, or daytime symptoms.
- **Central Sleep Apnoea (中樞性睡眠窒息症)**: Lack of respiratory effort (e.g., Congenital Central Hypoventilation Syndrome, brainstem lesions).
- **ADHD**: Misdiagnosis due to overlapping daytime behavioural symptoms.
- **Cor Pulmonale (肺心病)**: Right heart failure due to chronic hypoxia-induced pulmonary hypertension (must-not-miss complication of severe untreated OSA).

## 6. Tiered Investigations
### Bedside & Clinical Screening
- **Clinical Assessment**: Sleep history (Paediatric Sleep Questionnaire - PSQ), tonsil size examination (Brodsky grading).
- **Overnight Pulse Oximetry (通宵血氧監測)**: 
  - 1st-line screening tool (can be done at home).
  - Quantifies frequency/severity of desaturations.
  - *Caveat*: Normal oximetry does **NOT** rule out mild OSA (misses EEG arousals/hypercapnia), but excludes severe hypoxia.

### Gold Standard Investigation
- **Polysomnography (PSG / 睡眠多導圖)**: 
  - Measures: EEG (sleep staging), EOG, EMG, airflow, respiratory effort (chest/abdo belts), SpO2, ECG, ETCO2.
  - Distinguishes obstructive vs central events.
  - **Diagnostic cutoff**: Obstructive Apnoea-Hypopnoea Index (OAHI) **> 1 event/hour** is abnormal in children (>5 in adults).

### Specialist Imaging & Bloods
- **Echocardiogram (心臟超聲波)**: Screen for **Pulmonary Hypertension (肺高壓)** or RV hypertrophy in severe OSA.
- **Lateral Neck X-ray**: To assess adenoid size (rarely needed if endoscopy available).

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Surgical Management (1st Line)
- **Adenotonsillectomy (T&A / 腺樣體及扁桃腺切除術)**: 
  - Definitive treatment for adenotonsillar hypertrophy.
  - Highly effective (cures ~80% of uncomplicated cases). Dramatically improves symptoms.

### Medical & Non-Surgical Management
- **Intranasal Corticosteroids** (e.g., Fluticasone) & **Leukotriene Receptor Antagonists** (e.g., Montelukast): For mild OSA or residual symptoms post-op.
- **CPAP / BiPAP (無創呼吸機)**: 
  - Used for severe obesity-related OSA, neuromuscular disorders, craniofacial syndromes, or persistent OSA post-T&A.
- **Weight Management**: Dietitian referral for obese children.

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Interdisciplinary Collaboration
- **Paediatric Respiratory / Sleep Medicine**: PSG interpretation, CPAP titration.
- **ENT Surgeons**: Adenotonsillectomy evaluation.
- **Dietitians**: Weight reduction for obesity-associated OSA.
- **Educational Psychologists**: Support for poor school performance/ADHD-like behaviours.

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### High-Risk Perioperative Monitoring
- Children with severe OSA (severe hypoxaemia on PSG), obesity, craniofacial syndromes, or Down syndrome are at **high risk of perioperative respiratory compromise** post-T&A.
- **Mandatory HA Protocol**: Require extended monitoring in **HDU / PICU (兒童深切治療部)** post-operatively rather than general ward.

## 10. Complications & Prognosis
### Prognosis
- **Excellent** post-T&A for uncomplicated adenotonsillar hypertrophy. Neurobehavioural & growth issues typically reverse.

### Complications of Untreated OSA
- **Cardiovascular**: Pulmonary hypertension, right ventricular hypertrophy, **Cor Pulmonale**.
- **Neurocognitive**: Permanent learning deficits, persistent behavioural issues.
- **Metabolic**: Insulin resistance, exacerbation of obesity.
- **Growth**: Faltering growth (failure to thrive).

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Trap: Assuming a child with hyperactivity and poor concentration has primary ADHD without taking a sleep history.
* Pearl: Always ask about snoring and sleep quality in any child presenting with behavioural or learning difficulties. OSA is a fully reversible cause of "ADHD-like" symptoms.
* Viva Question: How does the clinical presentation and primary cause of OSA in children differ from adults?
* Answer: Adults typically present with daytime somnolence and the primary cause is obesity. Children often present paradoxically with daytime hyperactivity/inattention, and the primary cause is adenotonsillar hypertrophy.
* Pitfall: Reassuring parents that OSA is ruled out because an overnight pulse oximetry was normal. Oximetry only detects severe desaturations; it misses frequent cortical arousals and hypercapnia. A formal PSG is needed if clinical suspicion remains high.
