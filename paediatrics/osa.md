
# Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA) (阻塞性睡眠窒息症)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A disorder of breathing during sleep characterised by prolonged partial upper airway obstruction and/or intermittent complete obstruction (obstructive apnoea) that disrupts normal ventilation and sleep patterns.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Increasingly recognised in HK. Affects ~1-4% of children. Peak age is 2-8 years (coinciding with peak adenotonsillar hypertrophy). A second peak occurs in adolescence, associated with the rising rates of childhood obesity in HK.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Mechanism**: During sleep, muscle tone in the upper airway relaxes. In children with anatomical narrowing, this relaxation leads to airway collapse.
- **Primary Cause (Children)**: **Adenotonsillar Hypertrophy**. Enlarged tonsils and adenoids physically block the airway.
- **Other Risk Factors**:
  - **Obesity**: Fat deposition in the parapharyngeal tissues narrows the airway.
  - **Craniofacial Anomalies**: Micrognathia (small jaw, e.g., Pierre Robin sequence), midface hypoplasia (e.g., Down syndrome, Crouzon syndrome).
  - **Neuromuscular Disorders**: Hypotonia (e.g., Cerebral Palsy, SMA) leads to excessive airway collapse.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- See below.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Nighttime Symptoms**:
  - **Loud, habitual snoring** (the hallmark symptom).
  - Witnessed apnoeas (pauses in breathing) followed by gasping or snorting.
  - Restless sleep, frequent awakenings, unusual sleeping positions (e.g., neck hyperextended).
  - Secondary enuresis (bedwetting).
- **Daytime Symptoms**:
  - Mouth breathing (due to adenoidal hypertrophy).
  - Morning headaches.
  - **Neurobehavioural issues**: Unlike adults who present with daytime sleepiness, children often present with **hyperactivity, poor concentration, and poor school performance** (mimicking ADHD).
- **Signs**: "Adenoidal facies" (open mouth, long face, high-arched palate), enlarged tonsils ("kissing tonsils"), failure to thrive (in severe cases due to increased work of breathing at night).
## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Primary Snoring**: Snoring without apnoeas, gasping, or daytime neurobehavioural symptoms. (Requires a sleep study to definitively differentiate from OSA).
- **ADHD**: Children with OSA are frequently misdiagnosed with ADHD due to their daytime hyperactivity and inattention.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Most Likely**:
  - OSA secondary to adenotonsillar hypertrophy.
- **Must-Not-Miss**:
  - Severe OSA leading to **Cor Pulmonale** (Right heart failure due to chronic hypoxia causing pulmonary hypertension). Rare now, but a classic complication.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**:
  - **Clinical Assessment**: Detailed sleep history (often using questionnaires like the Paediatric Sleep Questionnaire - PSQ). Examine tonsil size (Brodsky grading).
  - **Overnight Oximetry**: A screening tool. Can show clusters of desaturations, but a normal oximetry does NOT rule out OSA (it doesn't detect arousals or mild apnoeas).
- **Gold Standard**:
  - **Polysomnography (PSG - Sleep Study)**: The gold standard diagnostic test.
  - Measures EEG (sleep staging), EOG, EMG, airflow (nasal cannula/thermistor), respiratory effort (chest/abdominal belts), SpO2, and ECG.
  - **Diagnostic Criteria**: Obstructive Apnoea-Hypopnoea Index (OAHI) > 1 event per hour is considered abnormal in children (unlike adults where >5 is the cutoff).
- **Monitoring**:
  - Echocardiogram if severe OSA is diagnosed, to screen for pulmonary hypertension/cor pulmonale.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**:
  - Not applicable for chronic presentation.
- **Chronic/Definitive**:
  - **Surgical (First-line for most)**:
    - **Adenotonsillectomy (T&A)**: The definitive treatment for children with adenotonsillar hypertrophy. Highly effective (cures ~80% of uncomplicated cases).
    - **Medical/Non-Surgical**:
    - **Weight Management**: Crucial for obese children.
    - **Intranasal Corticosteroids (e.g., Fluticasone)** and/or **Leukotriene Receptor Antagonists (Montelukast)**: Can be used for mild OSA to reduce adenotonsillar inflammation.
    - **CPAP (Continuous Positive Airway Pressure)**: Used if surgery is contraindicated, if OSA persists after surgery, or for severe obesity-related OSA.
## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Joint care between Paediatric Respiratory specialists and ENT surgeons.
## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Post-operative risk: Children with severe OSA, obesity, or syndromes are at high risk of post-operative respiratory compromise after T&A and require HDU/PICU monitoring.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Prognosis**: Excellent after T&A for uncomplicated cases. Neurobehavioural symptoms often improve dramatically.
- **Complications of untreated OSA**: Poor academic performance, behavioural problems, growth failure, pulmonary hypertension, cor pulmonale.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Assuming a child with hyperactivity and poor concentration has ADHD without asking about sleep. *Pearl*: Always ask about snoring and sleep quality in any child presenting with behavioural or learning difficulties. OSA is a reversible cause of "ADHD-like" symptoms.
- **Viva Question**: "How does the presentation of OSA in children differ from adults?" *Answer*: Adults typically present with daytime somnolence (sleepiness). Children often present paradoxically with daytime hyperactivity, inattention, and behavioural problems. Also, the primary cause in children is adenotonsillar hypertrophy, whereas in adults it is usually obesity/anatomy.
