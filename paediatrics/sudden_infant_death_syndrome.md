# Sudden Infant Death Syndrome (SIDS) (嬰兒猝死症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: The sudden, unexpected death of an infant under 1 year of age, which remains unexplained after a thorough case investigation, including performance of a complete autopsy, examination of the death scene, and review of the clinical history.
- **Local Epidemiology**: The incidence in Hong Kong is relatively low compared to Western countries, but it remains a leading cause of post-neonatal mortality. Peak incidence is between **2 and 4 months of age**.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **The "Triple Risk Model"**: SIDS is thought to occur when three factors intersect:
  1. **Vulnerable Infant**: An underlying, undetected abnormality in the brainstem network that controls arousal, respiration, and autonomic cardiovascular responses.
  2. **Critical Developmental Period**: The rapid growth and developmental changes occurring in the first 6 months of life (especially the transition in sleep-wake patterns).
  3. **Exogenous Stressor**: Environmental factors that the vulnerable infant cannot adequately respond to (e.g., prone sleeping, hyperthermia, airway obstruction).
- When a normal infant experiences hypoxia or hypercapnia during sleep (e.g., face covered by a blanket), they wake up, cry, and move. A SIDS infant fails to arouse, leading to progressive hypoxia, bradycardia, and death.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Tragically, there is no clinical presentation. The infant is typically put to sleep healthy and is later found dead in their cot by the parents.

## 4. Key Risk Factors (Crucial for Prevention)
- **Modifiable Risk Factors (The focus of public health campaigns)**:
  - **Prone Sleeping (Sleeping on the stomach)**: The single greatest risk factor.
  - **Maternal Smoking**: During pregnancy and postnatal exposure to second-hand smoke.
  - **Bed-sharing (Co-sleeping)**: Especially dangerous if the parents smoke, have consumed alcohol/drugs, or if the infant is premature.
  - **Soft bedding**: Pillows, heavy blankets, bumper pads, stuffed toys in the cot.
  - **Overheating**: High room temperature or over-wrapping the infant.
- **Non-Modifiable Risk Factors**:
  - Prematurity and low birth weight.
  - Male sex.
  - Multiple births (twins/triplets).
  - Recent mild viral illness.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- SIDS is a diagnosis of exclusion. It can only be diagnosed *after* an autopsy rules out other causes of Sudden Unexpected Death in Infancy (SUDI).

## 6. Differential Diagnosis (Causes of SUDI that must be ruled out)
- **Infection**: Undetected meningitis, pneumonia, myocarditis, or septicaemia.
- **Congenital Anomalies**: Undiagnosed congenital heart disease or brain anomalies.
- **Inborn Errors of Metabolism**: e.g., Medium-chain acyl-CoA dehydrogenase (MCAD) deficiency (can cause fatal hypoglycaemia during a fast).
- **Accidental Suffocation**: Wedging between a mattress and wall, or overlaying by a parent.
- **Non-Accidental Injury (NAI)**: Intentional suffocation or abusive head trauma.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Post-Mortem)
- **Coronial Investigation**: All sudden, unexpected infant deaths in Hong Kong must be reported to the Coroner.
- **Complete Autopsy**: Mandatory. Includes histology, toxicology, microbiology, and metabolic screening.
- **Death Scene Investigation**: Police and forensic pathologists examine the cot, bedding, and room temperature.
- **Skeletal Survey**: X-rays to look for healing fractures (indicative of NAI).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Prevention is the ONLY management)
- **The "Back to Sleep" Campaign (Safe Sleep Guidelines)**:
  - **Always place the baby on their BACK to sleep** for every sleep (naps and night).
  - Use a firm, flat mattress with a fitted sheet.
  - **Keep the cot completely empty**: No pillows, blankets, bumper pads, or toys. Use a well-fitted infant sleeping bag instead of blankets.
  - **Room-sharing without bed-sharing**: The baby should sleep in their own cot in the same room as the parents for the first 6 months.
  - Keep the baby in a smoke-free environment.
  - Avoid overheating (keep the room at a comfortable temperature, do not put hats on indoors).
  - Breastfeeding and pacifier use at sleep time are associated with a reduced risk of SIDS.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Bereavement Support**: The death of an infant is an unimaginable tragedy. The parents require immense psychological support, counseling, and compassion. They often feel profound, misplaced guilt.
- **Care in Subsequent Pregnancies (CONI - Care of Next Infant)**: Parents will be highly anxious with their next child. They require extra support, frequent MCHC visits, and sometimes apnoea monitors (though monitors do not prevent SIDS, they provide parental reassurance).

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Differentiating SIDS from intentional suffocation (infanticide) is extremely difficult, as both leave few physical signs.
- Red flags for NAI: Previous unexplained infant deaths in the family, inconsistent history from parents, presence of blood at the nose/mouth, or evidence of old fractures on the skeletal survey.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- N/A (Fatal condition).

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "What is the single most important modifiable risk factor for Sudden Infant Death Syndrome?" (Prone sleeping / sleeping on the stomach).
- *Viva*: "A mother asks if she should share a bed with her newborn to make breastfeeding easier. What is your advice regarding SIDS risk?" (You must strongly advise against bed-sharing, as it significantly increases the risk of SIDS and accidental suffocation. The safest place for the baby is in their own cot, in the same room as the parents).
- *Pitfall*: Diagnosing SIDS in the AED. SIDS is a post-mortem diagnosis of exclusion. In the AED, the presentation is "Sudden Unexpected Death in Infancy (SUDI)," and a full coronial investigation is required.
