
# Haemophilia A and B (血友病 甲型及乙型)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Inherited bleeding disorders caused by a deficiency of coagulation Factor VIII (Haemophilia A) or Factor IX (Haemophilia B / Christmas Disease).
- **Local Epidemiology**: Haemophilia A is more common (~1 in 5,000 males) than B (~1 in 30,000 males). Managed in specialised comprehensive care centres in HK (e.g., PWH, QEH).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Genetics**: X-linked recessive. Almost exclusively affects males. Females are carriers (though lyonization can rarely cause mild bleeding in carriers).
- **Mechanism**: Deficiency of FVIII or FIX disrupts the intrinsic pathway of the coagulation cascade, leading to impaired thrombin generation and poor clot formation.
- **Severity**: Correlates with factor levels:
  - *Severe*: <1% factor activity (frequent spontaneous bleeds).
  - *Moderate*: 1-5% (bleeds with minor trauma).
  - *Mild*: 5-40% (bleeds only with major trauma or surgery).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- See below.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Neonates**: Prolonged bleeding from heel pricks or circumcision, large cephalhaematoma, or intracranial haemorrhage (rare but devastating).
- **Infants/Toddlers**: Easy bruising as they start to crawl/walk.
- **Hallmark: Haemarthrosis**: Spontaneous bleeding into joints (knees, ankles, elbows). Presents as a swollen, warm, painful joint with restricted movement.
- **Muscle Haematomas**: Deep muscle bleeds (e.g., iliopsoas bleed, which can present as groin pain and hip flexion contracture).
- **Prolonged bleeding**: After dental extractions or surgery.
## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Von Willebrand Disease (vWD)**: Typically presents with *mucosal* bleeding (epistaxis, menorrhagia) rather than deep joint/muscle bleeds. Autosomal dominant (affects males and females equally).

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Most Likely**:
  - Haemophilia A or B (clinically indistinguishable).
- **Must-Not-Miss**:
  - **Intracranial Haemorrhage**: Any head trauma in a haemophiliac must be treated with factor replacement *before* imaging.
  - **Airway Bleeding**: Neck/throat bleeds can rapidly compromise the airway.
  - **Compartment Syndrome**: From deep muscle bleeds (e.g., calf or forearm).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**:
  - **Coagulation Profile**:
    - **APTT**: Prolonged (tests the intrinsic pathway).
    - **PT**: Normal (extrinsic pathway is intact).
    - **Platelet count**: Normal.
    - **Bleeding time**: Normal (primary haemostasis/platelet plug is normal).
- **Gold Standard**:
  - **Factor Assays**: Specific assays for Factor VIII and Factor IX levels to confirm the diagnosis and determine severity.
  - **Inhibitor Screen**: To check for the development of alloantibodies against the replaced factor (a major complication of treatment).
- **Monitoring**:
  - Regular monitoring of factor levels and inhibitor titres.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**:
  - **Suspected severe bleed (Head, Neck, Abdomen, severe joint)**: **REPLACE FACTOR IMMEDIATELY**. Do not wait for imaging or lab results.
  - *Haemophilia A*: Recombinant Factor VIII.
  - *Haemophilia B*: Recombinant Factor IX.
- **Chronic/Definitive**:
  - **Prophylaxis**: Standard of care for severe haemophilia in HK. Regular IV infusions of recombinant factor (e.g., 2-3 times a week) to keep trough levels >1% and prevent spontaneous haemarthroses and joint damage.
  - **Emicizumab (Hemlibra)**: A bispecific monoclonal antibody that mimics FVIII. Given subcutaneously (weekly/monthly). Increasingly used in HK for Haemophilia A with or without inhibitors, revolutionising care by reducing the burden of IV infusions.
  - **Desmopressin (DDAVP)**: Useful for *mild* Haemophilia A (releases stored FVIII and vWF from endothelial cells). Ineffective for severe Haemophilia A or any Haemophilia B.
  - **Tranexamic Acid**: Antifibrinolytic, useful adjunct for mucosal bleeds (e.g., dental work).
## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Comprehensive care involves Haematologists, Specialist Nurses, Physiotherapists (crucial for joint rehabilitation), and Orthopaedic surgeons.
## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- **Safety Netting**: MedicAlert bracelet. Parents taught home IV infusion. Avoid NSAIDs/Aspirin and IM injections.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Haemophilic Arthropathy**: Chronic, disabling joint damage from recurrent haemarthroses.
- **Inhibitors**: Development of IgG antibodies against the infused factor (occurs in ~30% of severe Haemophilia A), rendering standard treatment ineffective. Requires bypass agents (e.g., rFVIIa) or immune tolerance induction.
- **Infection**: Historically, HIV/HCV from contaminated blood products. Now extremely rare with recombinant products.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Sending a child with haemophilia and a head injury for a CT scan before giving factor. *Pearl*: Factor first, CT second. Delaying factor replacement for imaging can be fatal.
- **Viva Question**: "What is the coagulation profile of a patient with Haemophilia A?" *Answer*: Prolonged APTT, normal PT, normal platelet count, normal bleeding time.
