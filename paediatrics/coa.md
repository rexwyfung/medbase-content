

# Coarctation of the Aorta (CoA) (主動脈縮窄)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A congenital narrowing of the descending aorta, usually located opposite the insertion of the ductus arteriosus (juxtaductal).
- **Local Epidemiology**: Accounts for ~5-8% of CHD. More common in males. Strongly associated with Turner Syndrome (45,X).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Narrowing increases afterload on the left ventricle (LV), leading to LV hypertrophy and heart failure.
- Decreased perfusion to the lower body and kidneys.
- **Duct-dependent systemic circulation**: In severe neonatal coarctation, the lower body is perfused by right-to-left shunting through the PDA. When the PDA closes, the infant rapidly decompensates into cardiogenic shock.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- **Neonates**: Present with acute cardiovascular collapse, severe acidosis, and shock when the ductus arteriosus closes (usually days 2-7 of life).
- **Older Children/Adolescents**: Often asymptomatic. Picked up incidentally due to hypertension in the upper extremities or a murmur. May complain of leg claudication or headaches.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Radio-femoral delay**: A classic sign. The femoral pulse is felt later than the radial pulse.
- **Absent or weak femoral pulses**.
- **Four-limb Blood Pressure**: Hypertension in the upper limbs (right arm) and lower blood pressure in the lower limbs (gradient >20 mmHg).
- **Murmur**: Ejection systolic murmur heard best at the left infraclavicular area and radiating to the back (interscapular area).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Aortic Stenosis**: Ejection systolic murmur radiating to the neck, but femoral pulses are present and equal to upper limb pulses (though both may be weak).
- **Interrupted Aortic Arch**: More severe, always presents in the neonatal period with shock.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Neonatal shock: Sepsis, other left-sided obstructive lesions (Hypoplastic Left Heart Syndrome, critical aortic stenosis).
- Essential hypertension (in older children).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
**Bedside**: Four-limb BP measurement. ECG (RVH in neonates, LVH in older children).
- **Imaging**: **CXR**: May show cardiomegaly and pulmonary oedema in neonates. In older children, classic "figure of 3" sign (indentation of the aorta) and **rib notching** (due to collateral vessel erosion of the inferior rib margins - rarely seen <5 years old).
**Gold Standard**: **Echocardiogram**: Visualizes the narrowing and assesses LV function.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Neonates (Acute)**:
  - **Prostaglandin E1 (PGE1)**: Re-open the ductus arteriosus to restore perfusion to the lower body and reverse shock.
  - Inotropic support and mechanical ventilation if in severe shock.
- **Definitive Treatment**:
  - **Surgical Repair**: Resection and end-to-end anastomosis is the standard for neonates and infants.
  - **Transcatheter Balloon Angioplasty/Stenting**: Often preferred for older children or for re-coarctation after previous surgery.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Genetic counseling, especially if Turner Syndrome is suspected (karyotype should be considered in females with CoA).

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Not directly applicable.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Untreated: Severe hypertension, premature coronary artery disease, stroke, ruptured aortic aneurysm, heart failure.
- Post-repair: Good prognosis, but lifelong follow-up is required due to the risk of re-coarctation and persistent hypertension.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Forgetting to palpate femoral pulses in a baby. *Pearl*: Palpating femoral pulses is a mandatory part of the newborn examination to rule out CoA.
- **OSCE Pearl**: If you find hypertension in a child, explicitly state that you would check the blood pressure in the lower limbs and palpate the femoral pulses.
