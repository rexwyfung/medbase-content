# Coarctation of the Aorta (CoA) (主動脈縮窄)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Coarctation of the Aorta
* **Definition**: Congenital narrowing (縮窄) of descending aorta (降主動脈).
* **Location**: Typically juxtaductal (opposite insertion of ductus arteriosus).

### Local Epidemiology of CoA
* **Prevalence**: ~5-8% of all Congenital Heart Disease (CHD) (先天性心臟病).
* **Gender**: ♂ > ♀ (2:1 ratio).
* **Local HK Context**: All severe neonatal cases centralized to **Hong Kong Children's Hospital (HKCH) (香港兒童醫院)** for cardiothoracic intervention.

### Genetic & Anatomic Associations of CoA
* **Turner Syndrome (特納氏綜合症) (45,X)**: Present in 15-20% of Turner patients. (All ♀ w/ CoA need karyotype).
* **Bicuspid Aortic Valve (二葉主動脈瓣)**: Present in up to 85% of CoA cases.
* **Other CHD**: Ventricular Septal Defect (VSD) (心室中隔缺損), Patent Ductus Arteriosus (PDA), Mitral valve anomalies.
* **Berry Aneurysms (漿果動脈瘤)**: ↑ risk of Circle of Willis aneurysms.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics

### Pathophysiology of Neonatal Duct-Dependent CoA
* Severe juxtaductal narrowing → ↑↑ Left Ventricular (LV) afterload.
* **Duct-dependent systemic circulation (導管依賴性全身循環)**: Lower body perfusion relies entirely on Right-to-Left (R→L) shunt via PDA.
* **Day 2-7 PDA Closure**: PDA constricts → sudden cessation of lower body blood flow → rapid decompensation → profound cardiogenic shock (心源性休克), severe metabolic acidosis, acute kidney injury (AKI), necrotizing enterocolitis (NEC).

### Pathophysiology of Older Child/Adult CoA
* Less severe narrowing → gradual compensation via extensive collateral circulation (側支循環) (intercostal, internal mammary, scapular arteries).
* Chronic ↑ LV afterload → Left Ventricular Hypertrophy (LVH) (左心室肥厚) → eventual Left Heart Failure (左心衰竭).
* Renal hypoperfusion → ↑ Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System (RAAS) activation → secondary Upper Limb Hypertension (上肢高血壓).

## 3. Clinical Presentation

### Neonatal Clinical Presentation (Acute Presentation)
* Present at Day 2-7 of life (coincides w/ PDA closure).
* Acute cardiovascular collapse / Cardiogenic shock.
* Severe tachypnoea, grunting, respiratory distress.
* Profound metabolic acidosis & oliguria.
* Poor feeding, extreme lethargy.

### Older Child/Adolescent Clinical Presentation (Chronic Presentation)
* Often asymptomatic; picked up incidentally during school health screening (Student Health Service, HK) or pre-participation sports exams.
* Headaches, epistaxis (secondary to ↑ BP).
* Leg claudication (間歇性跛行) during exercise (secondary to ↓ lower limb perfusion).
* Cold lower extremities.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Key Physical Signs of CoA
* **Radio-femoral delay (橈股動脈搏動延遲)**: Classic pathognomonic sign. Femoral pulse palpated distinctly later than radial pulse.
* **Absent/Weak Femoral Pulses (股動脈搏動減弱/消失)**: Mandatory check in all neonates.
* **Four-limb Blood Pressure Discrepancy**:
    * ↑ BP in upper limbs (Right arm > Left arm depending on exact stricture location).
    * ↓ BP in lower limbs.
    * Significant if Upper-to-Lower gradient **>20 mmHg**.
* Visible/palpable collateral arterial pulsations around scapulae.

### Auscultation Findings in CoA
* **Murmur**: Ejection systolic murmur (ESM) (收縮期噴射性雜音).
* **Best Heard**: Left infraclavicular area.
* **Radiation**: Radiates strongly to the back (interscapular area).
* May also hear apical click (if associated bicuspid aortic valve) or gallop rhythm (if heart failure).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)

### Differentiating CoA from Aortic Stenosis (AS)
| Feature | Coarctation of the Aorta (CoA) | Aortic Stenosis (AS) (主動脈瓣狹窄) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Murmur Radiation** | Radiates to back (interscapular) | Radiates to neck (carotids) |
| **Femoral Pulses** | Weak/absent, radio-femoral delay | Present, equal to upper limbs (but slow-rising) |
| **Upper Limb BP** | Hypertension | Normal or low (narrow pulse pressure) |

### Differentiating CoA from Interrupted Aortic Arch (IAA)
| Feature | Coarctation of the Aorta | Interrupted Aortic Arch (IAA) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Anatomy** | Narrowing of aorta | Complete anatomical discontinuity of aorta |
| **Presentation** | Neonate OR older child | ALWAYS severe neonatal shock |
| **VSD Association** | Sometimes | Almost ALWAYS associated with VSD |
| **Genetic Link** | Turner Syndrome (45,X) | DiGeorge Syndrome (22q11.2 deletion) |

## 6. Differential Diagnosis

### Differential Diagnosis for Neonatal Shock
| Aetiology Category | Key Differentials |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Cardiogenic (Duct-dependent)** | Hypoplastic Left Heart Syndrome (HLHS), Critical Aortic Stenosis, Severe CoA. |
| **Septic** | Group B Streptococcus (GBS), E. coli sepsis, Meningitis. |
| **Metabolic** | Inborn errors of metabolism (e.g., Urea cycle defects). |
| **Hypovolaemic** | Severe twin-twin transfusion syndrome, massive feto-maternal haemorrhage. |

### Differential Diagnosis for Paediatric Hypertension
* **Renal**: Renal artery stenosis, Polycystic kidney disease, Glomerulonephritis.
* **Endocrine**: Phaeochromocytoma, Cushing's syndrome, Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia (CAH), Hyperthyroidism.
* **Cardiac**: Coarctation of the Aorta.
* **Essential**: Primary hypertension (obesity-related).

## 7. Tiered Investigations

### Bedside Investigations for CoA
* **Four-limb BP**: Document >20mmHg gradient between right arm & lower limbs.
* **Pre & Post-ductal SpO2**: Detect differential cyanosis (lower half cyanotic if PDA supplies lower body w/ deoxygenated PA blood).
* **ECG**:
    * Neonates: Right Ventricular Hypertrophy (RVH) (Right heart pumps systemic circulation via PDA in utero).
    * Older children: Left Ventricular Hypertrophy (LVH).

### Laboratory Investigations for CoA (Neonatal Shock)
* **Arterial Blood Gas (ABG)**: Severe metabolic acidosis, ↑ lactate (due to tissue hypoperfusion).
* **Renal Function Test (RFT)**: ↑ Urea, ↑ Creatinine (AKI from renal ischemia).
* **Septic Workup**: Blood culture, CRP (must rule out sepsis in shocked neonate).

### Imaging Investigations for CoA
* **Chest X-Ray (CXR)**:
    * Neonates: Cardiomegaly (心臟肥大), pulmonary venous congestion/oedema.
    * Older children (Usually >5 yrs):
        * **"Figure of 3" sign (「3」字徵)**: Pre-stenotic aortic dilation, the coarctation indent, and post-stenotic dilation.
        * **Rib Notching (肋骨切跡)**: Inferior rib margin erosion bilaterally (due to engorged, tortuous intercostal collateral arteries).
	    ![image](https://pub-f0606dedf6d94e4bb5b1999cbd065914.r2.dev/paediatrics/6c2cb7695518c33bf54d57ab5e29130f.png)
* **CT/MR Angiogram**: Excellent for pre-operative 3D anatomical planning.

### Gold Standard Investigation for CoA
* **Echocardiogram (ECHO)**: Transthoracic echocardiogram (TTE).
    * Visualizes exact site and severity of stricture.
    * Measures Doppler pressure gradient across coarctation.
    * Assesses LV function, bicuspid aortic valve, and other structural defects.

## 8. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)

### Acute Neonatal Management (Cardiogenic Shock Protocol)
* **Resuscitation**: ABC approach. Intubation & mechanical ventilation to ↓ metabolic demand.
* **Prostaglandin E1 (PGE1) Infusion (前列腺素E1)**:
    * **Drug**: Alprostadil IV continuous infusion.
    * **Dose**: 0.01 - 0.05 mcg/kg/min (titrate to effect).
    * **Goal**: Re-open/maintain patency of Ductus Arteriosus to restore lower body perfusion.
    * **Side Effect Watch**: Apnoea (requires intubation readiness), fever, flushing, hypotension.
* **Inotropes/Vasopressors**: Dopamine or Milrinone to support failing myocardium.
* **Acidosis Correction**: Correct underlying perfusion first; judicious sodium bicarbonate if profound.

### Definitive Surgical Management
* **Timing**: Urgent once stabilized (for neonates).
* **Procedure of Choice (Neonates/Infants)**: Resection and extended end-to-end anastomosis (切除及端對端吻合術) via left thoracotomy.
* Subclavian flap aortoplasty or patch aortoplasty (less common now).

### Transcatheter Management
* **Indications**: Older children, adolescents, or patients with post-surgical re-coarctation.
* **Procedure**: Balloon angioplasty (球囊血管成形術) ± Stent placement (支架植入).

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care

### Multidisciplinary Team (MDT) Involvement
* **Paediatric Cardiology & Cardiothoracic Surgery**: Centralized care at HKCH.
* **Clinical Genetics**: Karyotype/microarray testing for all females to rule out Turner Syndrome (45,X); genetic counseling for parents.
* **Medical Social Worker (MSW)**: Support for prolonged ICU stay, financial assistance (e.g., Samaritan Fund for specific off-label interventions/devices if applicable).
* **Maternal and Child Health Centre (MCHC)**: Long-term developmental monitoring and routine vaccinations (Synagis/Palivizumab for RSV prophylaxis in significant CHD <2 yrs).

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags

### Safeguarding Considerations in CHD
* Not a direct presentation of Non-Accidental Injury (NAI).
* However, chronic medical conditions ↑ parental stress → ↑ risk of neglect.
* Ensure follow-up compliance; failure to attend (DNA) specialist appointments for severe cardiac conditions is a medical neglect red flag.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis

### Complications of Untreated CoA
* Severe early-onset essential-like hypertension.
* Premature coronary artery disease (CAD) & Myocardial Infarction.
* Congestive Heart Failure (CHF).
* Ruptured cerebral berry aneurysm → Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH).
* Aortic dissection/rupture.
* Infective Endocarditis (IE).

### Long-Term Prognosis & Follow-Up
* **Post-Repair Prognosis**: Excellent survival rate if repaired early.
* **Long-Term Risks**:
    * **Re-coarctation (再縮窄)**: Risk is highest if repaired in neonatal period.
    * **Persistent Hypertension**: Occurs in up to 30% of patients despite successful anatomical repair; requires lifelong BP monitoring and antihypertensive therapy.
    * Lifelong Paediatric/Adult Congenital Heart Disease (ACHD) follow-up is mandatory.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls

* Trap: Forgetting to palpate femoral pulses during routine newborn examination. Pearl: Palpating and assessing the quality of femoral pulses is a mandatory, non-negotiable step in the newborn physical exam to rule out CoA.
* Viva Question: What is the classic X-ray sign of Coarctation of the Aorta in an older child, and what causes it? Answer: Rib notching on the inferior rib margins, caused by erosion from enlarged, pulsatile intercostal collateral arteries.
* Pitfall: Discharging a neonate with a seemingly innocent murmur on day 1 of life without follow-up. A duct-dependent CoA may have no murmur or a very soft murmur initially; the patient will crash when the PDA closes at home on day 2-7. Always ensure robust safety netting and SpO2 screening prior to discharge.
* Viva Question: A female child presents with short stature, primary amenorrhoea, and an ejection systolic murmur radiating to the back. What is the unifying diagnosis? Answer: Turner Syndrome complicated by Coarctation of the Aorta.
* OSCE Pearl: If you discover hypertension in a paediatric patient during an OSCE station, explicitly state to the examiner: "I would now check the blood pressure in the lower limbs and palpate the radio-femoral delay to screen for coarctation of the aorta."
* Trap: Delaying PGE1 infusion while waiting for echocardiogram confirmation in a crashing neonate. Pearl: If duct-dependent structural heart disease is suspected clinically (shock, severe cyanosis), start Alprostadil (PGE1) infusion immediately; do not wait for the ECHO to confirm.
