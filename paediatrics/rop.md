# Retinopathy of Prematurity (ROP) (早產兒視網膜病變)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A vasoproliferative disorder of the developing retina in premature infants.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Most common in extremely preterm (<28 weeks) and extremely low birth weight (<1000g) infants. HA and DH protocols emphasize strict oxygen targeting to minimize incidence.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Develops in two phases:
  - **Phase 1 (Vaso-obliteration)**: Premature birth interrupts normal retinal angiogenesis. Hyperoxia downregulates VEGF, causing vessel cessation.
  - **Phase 2 (Vasoproliferation)**: As the retina matures, it becomes hypoxic, upregulating VEGF and causing abnormal neovascularization.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- **Silent disease** initially; solely detected via structured screening and fundoscopy.
- Late signs (if missed): Leukocoria, strabismus, reduced vision, blindness.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- Abnormal vessel growth at the boundary of vascularised and avascular retina (from demarcation line to ridge to extraretinal fibrovascular proliferation).
- **Plus disease**: Increased venous dilation and arteriolar tortuosity in the posterior pole (a sign of severe activity).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinguished from Retinoblastoma by history of extreme prematurity and NICU care.
- Distinguished from Congenital Cataract which lacks retinal vascular pathology.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Retinoblastoma
- Familial Exudative Vitreoretinopathy (FEVR)
- Persistent Fetal Vasculature (PFV)

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**: Pupil dilation (Cyclopentolate/Phenylephrine) and fundoscopy by an ophthalmologist.
- **Special**: RetCam digital imaging (often utilized in HA NICUs for documentation and grading).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Prevention**: Strict targeting of SpO2 (usually 91-95% in NICU) to prevent Phase 1.
- **Screening (HA Protocol)**: Babies $le$ 32 weeks gestation or birth weight $le$ 1500g screened at 31-32 weeks PCA or 4 weeks postnatal.
- **Treatment**: Laser photocoagulation of avascular retina (gold standard) or Intravitreal Anti-VEGF (e.g., Bevacizumab/Ranibizumab) for aggressive posterior ROP.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **MDT**: Neonatologist, Paediatric Ophthalmologist, Specialized NICU nurses.
- **MSW & MCHC**: Parents require intense counseling due to risk of blindness. MCHC continues developmental follow-up.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Not typically associated with NAI, but neglecting screening follow-ups after discharge requires MSW intervention to safeguard the child's vision.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Retinal detachment, myopia, strabismus, amblyopia, glaucoma.
- Prompt treatment of threshold disease prevents blindness in most cases.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Forgetting the timing of the first screening. It's 31-32 weeks PCA or 4 weeks of age (choose the later one).
- **Viva Q**: "What is Plus disease and why does it matter?" Answer: It indicates florid disease and mandates urgent treatment within 48-72 hours.
