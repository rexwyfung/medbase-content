# Ventricular Septal Defect (VSD) (心室中隔缺損)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A congenital defect in the ventricular septum, allowing blood to communicate between the left and right ventricles.
- **Local Epidemiology**: It is the most common congenital heart disease (CHD) globally and in Hong Kong, accounting for approximately 30% of all CHD cases.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Anatomical Types**:
  - *Perimembranous* (Most common, ~80%): Located in the membranous septum, near the aortic and tricuspid valves.
  - *Muscular* (15%): Located in the muscular septum. Highly likely to close spontaneously.
  - *Inlet*: Associated with Down syndrome (part of an AVSD).
  - *Outlet / Supracristal*: Located just below the pulmonary valve. High risk of aortic valve prolapse.
- **Haemodynamics**: 
  - The Left Ventricle (LV) has a much higher pressure than the Right Ventricle (RV).
  - Blood shunts from Left-to-Right during systole.
  - This leads to increased pulmonary blood flow (pulmonary overcirculation) -> increased venous return to the Left Atrium (LA) and LV -> LA and LV volume overload and dilation -> **Left-sided Heart Failure**.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
Presentation depends entirely on the size of the defect and the pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR).
- **Small VSD**: Asymptomatic. Often picked up as an incidental murmur during a routine MCHC check. Normal growth and development.
- **Moderate/Large VSD**: 
  - *Crucial Timing*: Does NOT present at birth. Presents at **4 to 8 weeks of life**. At birth, PVR is high, so shunting is minimal. As PVR naturally falls over the first few weeks, the L-to-R shunt increases massively.
  - *Presentation*: Signs of **Heart Failure**. Poor feeding, diaphoresis (sweating) during feeds, tachypnoea, failure to thrive (poor weight gain), and recurrent respiratory infections.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Murmur**: A harsh, blowing, **pan-systolic murmur** heard best at the lower left sternal border. 
  - *Paradox*: A smaller VSD often produces a louder, harsher murmur due to higher turbulence across a small hole. A very large VSD may have a softer murmur.
- **Signs of a Large Shunt / Heart Failure**:
  - Tachypnoea, tachycardia.
  - Active precordium, palpable apical thrill, displaced apex beat (LV dilation).
  - Hepatomegaly (due to systemic venous congestion).
  - **Mid-diastolic rumble at the apex**: Indicates a massive shunt causing increased relative flow across the mitral valve.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Atrial Septal Defect (ASD)**: Usually asymptomatic in childhood. Has a soft ejection systolic murmur (flow across pulmonary valve) and a **fixed, widely split S2**.
- **Patent Ductus Arteriosus (PDA)**: Produces a continuous "machinery" murmur heard best below the left clavicle, accompanied by bounding pulses and a wide pulse pressure.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Innocent murmur (e.g., Still's murmur - soft, vibratory, changes with posture, no heart failure signs).
- Atrial Septal Defect (ASD).
- Patent Ductus Arteriosus (PDA).
- Mitral Regurgitation (also pan-systolic, but radiates to axilla).
- Tetralogy of Fallot (has a VSD, but presents with cyanosis due to right-to-left shunting from pulmonary stenosis).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Echocardiogram**: The gold standard diagnostic test. Confirms the size, location, and number of VSDs, assesses the haemodynamic significance (chamber dilation), and estimates pulmonary artery pressure.
- **ECG**: 
  - Small VSD: Normal.
  - Large VSD: Left Ventricular Hypertrophy (LVH) and Left Atrial Enlargement (LAE). Biventricular hypertrophy if pulmonary hypertension develops.
- **Chest X-ray (CXR)**: 
  - Large VSD shows cardiomegaly and increased pulmonary vascular markings (pulmonary plethora).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Small VSD**: Reassurance and routine clinical follow-up. ~75% of small muscular VSDs and many perimembranous VSDs close spontaneously by 2 years of age.
- **Moderate/Large VSD with Heart Failure**:
  - **Medical Management (Bridge to Surgery)**:
    - *Diuretics*: Furosemide + Spironolactone to reduce preload and pulmonary congestion.
    - *ACE Inhibitors*: Captopril to reduce systemic afterload, promoting forward flow into the aorta rather than shunting to the lungs.
    - *Nutrition*: High-calorie formula (e.g., Infatrini) via NG tube if necessary. Optimizing growth is crucial before surgery.
  - **Surgical Management**:
    - Indicated if there is failure to thrive despite maximal medical therapy, or development of pulmonary hypertension.
    - Usually performed at 3-6 months of age.
    - Procedure: Surgical patch closure (open heart) or transcatheter device closure (if anatomy is suitable).

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Paediatric Cardiology & Cardiothoracic Surgery**.
- **Dietitian**: Feeding an infant with heart failure is extremely challenging; they tire easily and have high metabolic demands. Specialized high-energy feeds are required.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Failure to thrive is a hallmark of a large VSD. However, clinicians must always ensure that adequate formula is actually being offered and prepared correctly by the caregivers to rule out concurrent neglect.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Eisenmenger Syndrome**: The most feared complication. If a large VSD is left untreated, chronic pulmonary overcirculation leads to irreversible pulmonary vascular remodeling and severe Pulmonary Hypertension. The pressure in the RV eventually exceeds the LV, causing the shunt to reverse (Right-to-Left). The patient becomes cyanotic. This is inoperable and eventually fatal.
- **Aortic Regurgitation**: Especially common with supracristal/outlet VSDs due to the Venturi effect pulling the aortic valve cusp into the defect.
- **Infective Endocarditis**: Rare in childhood, but a lifelong risk.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "Why does a baby with a large VSD not present with heart failure on day 1 of life, but rather at 4-6 weeks?" (At birth, Pulmonary Vascular Resistance (PVR) is high, equal to Systemic Vascular Resistance (SVR), so there is minimal pressure gradient and minimal shunting. As PVR naturally falls over the first few weeks of life, the Left-to-Right shunt increases massively, leading to volume overload and heart failure).
- *Viva*: "What is Eisenmenger syndrome?" (Irreversible pulmonary hypertension causing reversal of a left-to-right shunt into a right-to-left shunt, resulting in cyanosis).
