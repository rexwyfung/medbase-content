# Iron Deficiency Anaemia (缺鐵性貧血)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Definition of Iron Deficiency Anaemia
* **Microcytic, hypochromic anaemia** (小細胞低色素性貧血)
* ↓ iron stores → ↓ normal red blood cell (RBC) production
* Most common nutritional deficiency worldwide

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
* Highly prevalent in **Hong Kong toddlers** (1-3 yrs) & **adolescent females**
* Common co-morbidity: **Thalassaemia Trait** (地中海貧血基因攜帶者) (High prevalence in HK; requires careful differentiation)

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Pathophysiology of Iron Deficiency
* **Iron** = crucial component of **haemoglobin (Hb)** (血紅蛋白)
* ↓ Iron stores → bone marrow produces smaller (**microcytic**) & paler (**hypochromic**) RBCs → ↓ oxygen-carrying capacity

### Causes in Infants (6-24 months)
* **Excessive cow's milk intake** (>500-600ml/day) → poor iron source + high calcium inhibits iron absorption + microscopic GI bleeding
* **Delayed weaning** (加固) onto iron-rich solid foods
* **Prematurity / low birth weight** → depleted neonatal iron stores (normally acquired in 3rd trimester)
* **Delayed cord clamping** at birth → missed physiological transfusion of placental blood

### Causes in Adolescent Females
* **Menorrhagia** (經血過多) → chronic blood loss
* **Poor dietary intake** → vegetarian/vegan diets without supplementation

### Causes at Any Age
* **Chronic blood loss** → Meckel's diverticulum, inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), peptic ulcer disease
* **Malabsorption** → Coeliac disease (乳糜瀉)

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs
### General Symptoms of IDA
* Often asymptomatic → incidental finding on routine **Complete Blood Picture (CBP)** (全血細胞計數)
* Insidious onset of lethargy, fatigue, weakness
* Poor appetite, poor concentration
* **Pica** (異食癖) → craving non-food items (ice, dirt, paper)

### Clinical Signs of IDA
* **Pallor** (蒼白) → conjunctivae, palmar creases, nail beds
* Tachycardia, flow murmur → if severe anaemia (Hb <60-70 g/L)
* Rare/Chronic signs: **Koilonychia** (匙狀甲) (spoon-shaped nails), angular stomatitis, glossitis

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Differentiating IDA from Thalassaemia Trait
| Feature | Iron Deficiency Anaemia (IDA) | Thalassaemia Trait (地中海貧血) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **RBC Count** | ↓ Low | Normal / ↑ High |
| **RDW** | ↑ High (varying sizes) | Normal (uniformly small) |
| **Mentzer Index (MCV/RBC)** | **> 13** | **< 13** |
| **Ferritin** | ↓ Low | Normal / ↑ High |
| **Hb Pattern (HPLC)** | Normal | Abnormal (e.g., ↑ HbA2 in β-thal) |

## 5. Differential Diagnosis
### Differential Diagnoses for Microcytic Anaemia
* **Thalassaemia Trait** (Alpha or Beta)
* **Anaemia of Chronic Disease** (慢性疾病貧血) (e.g., JIA, IBD, chronic renal failure)
* **Lead poisoning** (鉛中毒)
* **Sideroblastic anaemia** (鐵粒幼細胞貧血)

## 6. Tiered Investigations
### Bedside Investigations
* **Growth parameters** → plot weight/height to detect faltering growth
* **Dietary assessment** → 3-day food diary, accurately assess daily milk intake volume

### Blood Investigations (CBP & Smear)
* **Complete Blood Picture (CBP)**:
  * ↓ Hb
  * ↓ MCV (**Microcytic**)
  * ↓ MCH (**Hypochromic**)
  * ↑ RDW
  * ↓ Reticulocyte count (unless responding to treatment)
* **Blood Smear**:
  * Microcytic, hypochromic RBCs
  * Pencil cells (Target cells usually seen in Thalassaemia; Spherocytes in HS)

### Blood Investigations (Iron Studies)
* **Serum Ferritin** (鐵蛋白) → **LOW** (Definitive test; most sensitive/specific for total body iron stores. *Note: Ferritin is an acute-phase reactant, can be falsely normal/↑ in infection*)
* **Serum Iron** → ↓ Low
* **Total Iron Binding Capacity (TIBC) / Transferrin** → ↑ High (compensatory ↑ to capture available iron)
* **Transferrin Saturation** → ↓ Low (<15%)

### Special Investigations (If cause unclear or refractory)
* **Hb HPLC/Electrophoresis** → to rule out Thalassaemia
* **Coeliac serology** (IgA anti-tTG) → if malabsorption suspected
* **Meckel's scan / Endoscopy** → if occult GI bleeding suspected

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Acute Management of Severe IDA
* **Blood transfusion** → *Rarely necessary* for dietary IDA, even if Hb 20-30 g/L (chronically adapted). ONLY indicated if haemodynamic instability or heart failure.

### Chronic Management: Dietary Modification (MCHC / HA Protocol)
* **Toddlers**:
  * Restrict cow's milk → **< 500ml/day**
  * Introduce iron-rich solids → red meat, dark green leafy vegetables, fortified cereals
  * Vitamin C (e.g., orange juice) with meals → ↑ iron absorption
  * Avoid giving tannin (tea) / phytates with meals → ↓ iron absorption

### Chronic Management: Oral Iron Supplementation
* **Medications**: **Ferrous Sulphate** (硫酸亞鐵) or **Ferrous Fumarate** drops/syrup. (Alternative: sodium iron edetate - does not stain teeth)
* **Dosage**: **3-6 mg/kg/day** of elemental iron
* **Duration**: Continue for **at least 3 months AFTER** Hb normalizes → fully replenish bone marrow iron stores
* **Expected Response**: Reticulocytosis in 1 week; Hb ↑ by ~10 g/L per week
* **Side Effects**: Constipation, dark/black stools, abdominal pain, teeth staining (use straw or brush teeth after)

### Management of Refractory IDA
* **Non-compliance** → Most common cause (taste, GI side effects)
* **Co-existing Thalassaemia** → DO NOT blindly prescribe iron for low MCV; MUST check Iron Studies/Ferritin first
* Investigate for chronic blood loss / malabsorption if compliant and diet is adequate

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### MDT Involvement in IDA
* **Dietitian** (營養師) → Detailed dietary assessment/advice (esp. vegetarian/vegan families, picky eaters)
* **MCHC (Maternal and Child Health Centre)** → Routine growth monitoring, weaning advice during early childhood visits
* **Medical Social Worker (MSW)** → If severe financial constraints preventing adequate nutrition

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Safeguarding Concerns in IDA
* Severe, untreated IDA in a toddler despite repeated MCHC/medical advice → raises concerns for **dietary neglect** (疏忽照顧)
* Consider referral to **Social Welfare Department (SWD)** (社會福利署)

## 10. Complications & Prognosis
### Complications of Untreated IDA
* **Neurodevelopmental** → Chronic iron deficiency in infancy/toddlerhood → long-term, irreversible deficits in cognitive & motor development, behavioural problems, poor school performance
* **Physical** → Faltering growth, ↑ susceptibility to infections
* **Cardiovascular** → Heart failure (only in extremely severe, prolonged anaemia)

### Prognosis of IDA
* **Excellent** → with appropriate dietary modification and compliant oral iron supplementation

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Viva Question: A 2-year-old boy presents with a Hb of 8.5 g/dL and an MCV of 65 fL. His mother says he is a picky eater and drinks 4 bottles of cow's milk a day. What is the most likely diagnosis, and what is the mechanism?
* Answer: Iron Deficiency Anaemia. Excessive cow's milk intake displaces iron-rich solid foods from the diet, and the high calcium content inhibits iron absorption.
* Viva Question: How long should you continue oral iron therapy for a child with IDA?
* Answer: For at least 3 months after the haemoglobin level has returned to normal, in order to replenish the body's iron stores.
* Trap: Prescribing oral iron to any Hong Kong child with a microcytic anaemia without checking iron studies.
* Pearl: Thalassaemia trait is highly prevalent in Hong Kong. You must check Ferritin and Hb pattern to differentiate IDA from Thalassaemia before starting iron therapy, to avoid iron overload.
* Pitfall: Assuming a normal Ferritin rules out IDA in a sick child. Ferritin is an acute-phase reactant and can be falsely normal or elevated during concurrent infections. Look at Transferrin saturation and TIBC for clarification.
* Pitfall: Rushing to blood transfuse a toddler with a Hb of 40 g/L who is running around the ward. Chronic IDA leads to physiological adaptation. Transfusion is rarely necessary and carries risks; oral iron is the definitive treatment.
