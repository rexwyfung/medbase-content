# Iron Deficiency Anaemia (IDA) (缺鐵性貧血)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A microcytic, hypochromic anaemia caused by inadequate iron stores to support normal red blood cell production. It is the most common nutritional deficiency worldwide.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Very common in Hong Kong toddlers (1-3 years old) and adolescent females.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Pathophysiology**: Iron is a crucial component of haemoglobin. When iron stores are depleted, the bone marrow produces smaller (microcytic) and paler (hypochromic) red blood cells with reduced oxygen-carrying capacity.
- **Causes by Age Group**:
  - *Infants (6-24 months)*: **Excessive cow's milk intake** (>600ml/day). Cow's milk is very poor in iron, and its high calcium content inhibits the absorption of iron from other foods. It can also cause microscopic GI bleeding. Delayed introduction of iron-rich solid foods (weaning) is another major cause.
  - *Adolescent Females*: **Menorrhagia** (heavy menstrual bleeding) combined with poor dietary intake (e.g., vegetarian diets without iron supplementation).
  - *Any age*: Chronic blood loss (e.g., Meckel's diverticulum, inflammatory bowel disease, hookworm in developing countries) or malabsorption (e.g., Coeliac disease).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Often asymptomatic and picked up on routine blood tests.
- If symptomatic, presents with insidious onset of lethargy, pallor, and poor appetite.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Symptoms**: Fatigue, weakness, irritability, poor concentration, pica (craving non-food items like ice, dirt, or paper).
- **Signs**:
  - Pallor (best seen in the conjunctivae, palmar creases, and nail beds).
  - Tachycardia, flow murmur (if anaemia is severe).
  - *Rare/Chronic signs*: Koilonychia (spoon-shaped nails), angular stomatitis, glossitis.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Thalassaemia Trait**: Extremely common in Hong Kong. Both cause microcytic, hypochromic anaemia.
  - *IDA*: Low RBC count, high RDW (Red Cell Distribution Width - cells are of varying sizes), low Ferritin. Mentzer Index (MCV/RBC) > 13.
  - *Thalassaemia Trait*: Normal/high RBC count, normal RDW (cells are uniformly small), normal/high Ferritin. Mentzer Index < 13.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Thalassaemia trait (Alpha or Beta).
- Anaemia of chronic disease (e.g., JIA, IBD).
- Lead poisoning.
- Sideroblastic anaemia.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Complete Blood Picture (CBP)**:
  - Low Haemoglobin (Hb).
  - Low Mean Corpuscular Volume (MCV) - Microcytic.
  - Low Mean Corpuscular Haemoglobin (MCH) - Hypochromic.
  - High Red Cell Distribution Width (RDW).
- **Blood Smear**: Microcytic, hypochromic red blood cells, pencil cells.
- **Iron Studies (The Definitive Test)**:
  - **Ferritin**: **LOW**. (This is the most sensitive and specific marker of total body iron stores). *Note: Ferritin is an acute-phase reactant, so it can be falsely normal/elevated during an infection.*
  - Serum Iron: Low.
  - Total Iron Binding Capacity (TIBC) / Transferrin: High (the body produces more transferrin to try and capture any available iron).
  - Transferrin Saturation: Low (<15%).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Dietary Modification (Crucial)**:
  - *Toddlers*: **Restrict cow's milk intake to < 500ml per day**. Encourage iron-rich solid foods (red meat, dark green leafy vegetables, fortified cereals). Advise giving Vitamin C (e.g., orange juice) with meals to enhance iron absorption.
- **Oral Iron Supplementation**:
  - **Ferrous Sulphate or Ferrous Fumarate** drops/syrup.
  - *Dose*: 3-6 mg/kg/day of elemental iron.
  - *Duration*: Continue for at least **3 months AFTER the haemoglobin normalizes** to fully replenish the bone marrow iron stores.
  - *Side effects*: Constipation, dark/black stools, abdominal pain, staining of teeth (advise using a straw or brushing teeth after).
- **Investigate Underlying Cause**: If the diet is adequate, investigate for chronic blood loss (e.g., Meckel's scan, endoscopy) or malabsorption (Coeliac serology).

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Dietitian**: For detailed dietary assessment and advice, especially for vegetarian/vegan families or picky eaters.
- **MCHC**: Routine screening and dietary advice during early childhood visits.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Severe, untreated iron deficiency anaemia in a toddler despite repeated medical advice may raise concerns about dietary neglect.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Neurodevelopmental**: Chronic iron deficiency in infancy is associated with long-term, irreversible deficits in cognitive and motor development, and behavioral problems.
- **Prognosis**: Excellent with appropriate dietary changes and iron supplementation.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "A 2-year-old boy presents with a Hb of 8.5 g/dL and an MCV of 65 fL. His mother says he is a picky eater and drinks 4 bottles of cow's milk a day. What is the most likely diagnosis, and what is the mechanism?" (Iron Deficiency Anaemia. Excessive cow's milk intake displaces iron-rich solid foods from the diet, and the high calcium content inhibits iron absorption).
- *Viva*: "How long should you continue oral iron therapy for a child with IDA?" (For at least 3 months *after* the haemoglobin level has returned to normal, in order to replenish the body's iron stores).
- *Pitfall*: Assuming a microcytic anaemia in a Hong Kong child is IDA without checking iron studies or a haemoglobin pattern, as Thalassaemia trait is highly prevalent.
