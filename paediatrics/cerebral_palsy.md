# Cerebral Palsy (CP) (腦性麻痺)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A group of permanent, non-progressive disorders of the development of movement and posture, causing activity limitation. It is attributed to non-progressive disturbances (injury or malformation) that occurred in the developing fetal or infant brain.
- **Local Epidemiology**: The most common cause of severe physical disability in childhood. Incidence is ~2 per 1000 live births globally and in Hong Kong. The risk is exponentially higher in extremely premature infants.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Pathophysiology**: The brain lesion is static (non-progressive), but the clinical manifestations (e.g., spasticity, contractures) evolve as the child grows.
- **Aetiology (Timing of Injury)**:
  - **Antenatal (80%)**: The most common cause. Includes congenital brain malformations, intrauterine infections (TORCH), vascular events (fetal stroke), and multiple gestations.
  - **Perinatal (10%)**: Hypoxic-Ischaemic Encephalopathy (HIE) at birth, severe neonatal jaundice (Kernicterus).
  - **Postnatal (10%)**: Occurring before age 2. Meningitis/encephalitis, traumatic brain injury, near-drowning.
- **Prematurity**: The single greatest risk factor. Premature brains are highly susceptible to Periventricular Leukomalacia (PVL) - ischaemic damage to the white matter tracts descending from the motor cortex, classically causing spastic diplegia.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Rarely diagnosed at birth. Usually presents in the first 1-2 years of life with delayed motor milestones (e.g., not sitting by 8 months, not walking by 18 months).
- Parents may notice abnormal muscle tone (the baby feels "stiff" or "floppy") or early hand preference (handedness before 12 months is a red flag for hemiplegia).

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs (Classification by Motor Type)
- **Spastic CP (70-80%)**: Damage to the motor cortex or pyramidal tracts. Characterized by increased muscle tone (spasticity), hyperreflexia, clonus, and positive Babinski sign.
  - *Spastic Diplegia*: Legs are more affected than arms. Classic "scissoring" gait. Strongly associated with prematurity and PVL.
  - *Spastic Hemiplegia*: One side of the body is affected (arm usually worse than leg). Often due to a perinatal stroke.
  - *Spastic Quadriplegia*: All four limbs, trunk, and bulbar muscles affected. Often due to severe HIE. High rate of severe intellectual disability and epilepsy.
- **Dyskinetic CP (10-15%)**: Damage to the basal ganglia (e.g., from Kernicterus or severe HIE). Characterized by involuntary movements: chorea (rapid, jerky), athetosis (slow, writhing), and dystonia (abnormal postures). Tone fluctuates.
- **Ataxic CP (<5%)**: Damage to the cerebellum. Characterized by poor balance, wide-based gait, intention tremor, and dysmetria.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Progressive Neurodegenerative Disorders**: e.g., Leukodystrophies, Spinal Muscular Atrophy. In CP, the brain lesion is static, and the child generally slowly gains milestones (or plateaus). If a child is *losing* previously acquired milestones (regression), it is NOT Cerebral Palsy; it is a progressive disorder requiring urgent investigation.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Progressive neurodegenerative disorders.
- Spinal cord lesions (e.g., tethered cord, tumour).
- Inborn errors of metabolism.
- Genetic syndromes presenting with hypotonia/spasticity.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Diagnosis is primarily clinical**, based on history and serial neurological examinations.
- **Neuroimaging (MRI Brain)**: Recommended for all children with suspected CP to identify the underlying structural abnormality (e.g., PVL, stroke, malformation) and rule out treatable/progressive conditions.
- **Genetic/Metabolic Testing**: Considered if the MRI is normal or if there are atypical features (e.g., regression, strong family history).
- **Screening for Comorbidities**: Formal audiology, ophthalmology, and EEG (if seizures occur).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **There is no cure.** Management is multidisciplinary, aiming to maximize function, independence, and quality of life.
- **Spasticity Management**:
  - *Physical Therapy*: The cornerstone. Stretching to prevent contractures.
  - *Oral Medications*: Baclofen, Diazepam (limited efficacy, systemic side effects).
  - *Botulinum Toxin (Botox) Injections*: Targeted injections into spastic muscles (e.g., calf muscles) to temporarily relieve spasticity and delay the need for surgery.
  - *Intrathecal Baclofen Pump*: For severe, generalized spasticity.
  - *Selective Dorsal Rhizotomy (SDR)*: Neurosurgical division of sensory nerve rootlets in the spinal cord to permanently reduce spasticity in selected patients (usually spastic diplegia).
- **Orthopaedic Surgery**: Tendon lengthening (e.g., Achilles), osteotomies to correct hip subluxation/dislocation (very common in spastic quadriplegia), and spinal fusion for severe scoliosis.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **The MDT is massive**: Paediatrician, Neurologist, Orthopaedic Surgeon, Physiotherapist, Occupational Therapist, Speech Therapist (for swallowing and communication), Dietitian, Medical Social Worker.
- **Child Assessment Centre (CAC)**: Referral is essential for formal assessment and placement in special schools or early education centres in Hong Kong.
- **Comorbidities**: Management of associated epilepsy (up to 50%), intellectual disability, visual/hearing impairments, and severe feeding difficulties (often requiring gastrostomy/PEG tube feeding).

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Children with severe disabilities are highly vulnerable to abuse and neglect. Caregiver burnout is a massive risk factor. Respite care and psychosocial support for the family are essential.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Complications**: Joint contractures, hip dislocation, severe scoliosis, recurrent aspiration pneumonia (due to bulbar palsy), malnutrition, osteopenia/fractures.
- **Prognosis**: Depends entirely on the severity. Mild hemiplegia patients may lead independent lives. Severe quadriplegia patients have a significantly reduced life expectancy, usually dying from respiratory complications (aspiration pneumonia).

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "What is the classic MRI finding in a child born at 26 weeks gestation who develops spastic diplegic cerebral palsy?" (Periventricular Leukomalacia - PVL. Ischaemic damage to the white matter tracts adjacent to the lateral ventricles).
- *Viva*: "A mother brings her 18-month-old child to the clinic. He was previously walking holding on to furniture, but over the last 3 months, his legs have become increasingly stiff, and he can no longer stand. Is this Cerebral Palsy?" (No. Cerebral Palsy is a non-progressive disorder. Loss of milestones indicates a progressive neurodegenerative or spinal cord disorder, requiring urgent investigation).
- *Pitfall*: Focusing only on the motor deficits and forgetting to screen for and manage the massive burden of comorbidities (epilepsy, feeding difficulties, visual impairment).
