# Posterior Urethral Valves (PUV) (後尿道瓣膜)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A congenital urological anomaly occurring exclusively in MALE infants where abnormal membranous folds in the posterior urethra (prostatic portion) obstruct urinary outflow.
- **Local Epidemiology**: The most common cause of severe obstructive uropathy in male children (incidence ~1 in 5000 to 8000 male births).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Obstruction**: The mucosal valves act like a sail, catching urine during voiding and puffing out, blocking the urethra.
- **Back-pressure (The cascade)**: 
  1. Bladder wall hypertrophies immensely, becoming trabeculated and thick.
  2. The ureters balloon outward (hydroureter) and the kidneys swell with extreme pressure (severe bilateral hydronephrosis).
  3. The extreme pressure destroys developing renal parenchyma (Renal Dysplasia/Failure).
- **Oligohydramnios**: The fetus cannot pee into the amniotic sac. Amniotic fluid (mostly fetal urine in mid-late pregnancy) drops safely to near zero.
- **Potter Sequence**: Without amniotic fluid to breathe in/allow movement, the fetus develops **Pulmonary Hypoplasia** (fatal lung underdevelopment), flattened facies, and limb contractures.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Usually detected on the 20-week antenatal anomaly ultrasound.
- Postnatal presentation (if missed antenatally): A newborn male who hasn't passed urine in 48 hours, has a visibly distended abdomen, and is in respiratory distress.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Antenatal**: Bilateral hydronephrosis, dilated tortuous ureters, thick-walled distended bladder, "keyhole" sign (dilated posterior urethra above the valves), and oligohydramnios.
- **Postnatal**: 
  - Poor, dribbling urinary stream.
  - Palpable, hard, distended bladder in the pelvis.
  - Respiratory distress shortly after birth (due to pulmonary hypoplasia).
  - Rapid onset of uremia/renal failure.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from Pelviureteric Junction (PUJ) obstruction: PUJ obstruction is usually unilateral, higher up (at the kidney pelvis), and the bladder is totally normal. PUV affects everything upstream of the urethra (bladder, both ureters, both kidneys).

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Bilateral Vesicoureteral Reflux (VUR)
- Urethral atresia (fatal)
- Prune Belly Syndrome (absent abdominal muscles, cryptorchidism, urinary tract anomalies)

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**: Attempt gently passing a transurethral or suprapubic feeding tube to drain the bladder immediately upon delivery.
- **Bloods**: Renal function panel (Creatinine, Urea, Potassium) - will be markedly deranged.
- **Ultrasound**: Confirm postnatal hydronephrosis and thick bladder wall.
- **Gold Standard**: **Micturating Cystourethrogram (MCUG)**. Dye is injected into the bladder. During voiding, X-rays will clearly show the dilated posterior urethra abruptly narrowing at the valve, creating the "spinning top" appearance.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute (Neonatal)**: 
  - ABCs (Intubate for pulmonary hypoplasia if needed).
  - IMMEDIATELY decompress the bladder. Pass a tiny 6Fr urethral catheter. If it won't pass through the valves, insert a suprapubic catheter. Let the urine drain slowly to prevent massive post-obstructive diuresis shock.
- **Definitive Treatment**: Cystoscopic **Valve ablation** (using a hot wire hook or laser to destroy the membrane).
- **If urethra is too small**: A temporary vesicostomy (bringing the bladder directly out to the lower abdominal skin) is done until the child is big enough for ablation.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Continuous pediatric nephrology follow-up. Up to 30% of these boys will inevitably progress to End-Stage Renal Failure (ESRF) by puberty despite perfect treatment, because the fetal pressure already permanently ruined their kidney tissue.

- N/A.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Pulmonary hypoplasia (the leading cause of neonatal death).
- End-Stage Renal Failure requiring dialysis/transplant.
- "Valve Bladder Syndrome" (a permanently stiff, dysfunctional bladder causing lifelong incontinence).

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Assuming a successful valve ablation operation means the child is "cured" of kidney disease. You MUST monitor their creatinine for life, as the prenatal renal dysplasia will often slowly progress to kidney failure perfectly as the child hits their adolescent growth spurt.
- **Viva Q**: "An antenatal ultrasound reveals severe oligohydramnios and a 'keyhole' bladder in a male fetus. When this baby is born, what will be the most life-threatening immediate complication?" Answer: Respiratory failure due to pulmonary hypoplasia (Potter sequence), because amniotic fluid is required for lung development.
