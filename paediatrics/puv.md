# Posterior Urethral Valves (PUV) (後尿道瓣膜)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Posterior Urethral Valves
* **Definition**: Congenital urological anomaly occurring exclusively in **MALE** infants
* **Anatomy**: Abnormal membranous folds in the **posterior urethra** (prostatic portion) → obstruct urinary outflow
* **Consequence**: Profound upstream back-pressure → **obstructive uropathy (阻塞性尿路病)** & **renal dysplasia (腎發育不良)**

### Local Epidemiology of Posterior Urethral Valves
* **Incidence**: ~1 in 5000 to 8000 male births
* **Significance**: Most common cause of severe obstructive uropathy in male children
* **HK Context**: Typically detected via HA universal 20-week maternal anomaly ultrasound

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics

### Mechanism of Obstruction in Posterior Urethral Valves
* **Valve Action**: Mucosal folds act like a sail → catch urine during voiding → puff out & occlude urethra
* **Urine Trapping**: Prevents normal bladder emptying → constant ↑ intravesical pressure

### Cascade of Back-Pressure & Renal Dysplasia
* **Bladder**: Immense smooth muscle hypertrophy → severe wall thickening & **trabeculation (小樑形成)**
* **Ureters**: Bilateral retrograde high pressure → massive dilatation → **hydroureter (輸尿管積水)**
* **Kidneys**: Extreme pressure transmission → **severe bilateral hydronephrosis (雙側腎積水)**
* **Renal Parenchyma**: High pressure destroys developing nephrons → cyst formation & permanent **renal dysplasia / ESRF (末期腎衰竭)**

### Potter Sequence Pathophysiology
* **Fetal Anuria**: Obstructed outflow → fetus cannot excrete urine into amniotic sac
* **Oligohydramnios (羊水過少)**: Amniotic fluid strictly depends on fetal urine in mid-late pregnancy → levels drop to near zero
* **Potter Sequence (波特症候群)** Cascade:
  * ↓ Amniotic fluid → no fluid to inhale → **Pulmonary Hypoplasia (肺發育不全)** (fatal)
  * Loss of fluid cushioning → uterine wall compresses fetus → flattened facies & severe limb contractures (talipes)

## 3. Clinical Presentation

### Antenatal Presentation of Posterior Urethral Valves
* **Timing**: Usually detected on 20-week routine structural ultrasound scan
* **Findings**: Male fetus with progressively distended bladder, absent amniotic fluid, and bilaterally swollen kidneys

### Postnatal Presentation of Posterior Urethral Valves
* **If missed antenatally**:
  * Newborn male failing to pass urine in first 48 hours
  * Visibly distended, rigid abdomen
  * Immediate severe respiratory distress (due to hypoplastic lungs)
  * Rapid onset uremia & acute kidney injury (AKI)

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Antenatal Signs on Ultrasound
* **Bladder**: Thick-walled, grossly distended
* **Urethra**: Dilated posterior urethra above valves → **"Keyhole" sign (鑰匙孔徵)**
* **Kidneys/Ureters**: Severe **bilateral hydronephrosis** & dilated tortuous ureters
* **Amniotic Fluid**: **Oligohydramnios**

### Postnatal Symptoms & Signs
* **Urination**: Poor, weak, dribbling urinary stream
* **Palpation**: Hard, massively distended bladder palpable above pubic symphysis
* **Respiratory**: Grunting, severe tachypnoea, cyanosis (from **Pulmonary Hypoplasia**)
* **General**: Potter facies (flattened nose, low-set ears), limb contractures

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)

### Differentiating PUV from Pelviureteric Junction (PUJ) Obstruction
* **PUJ Obstruction**: Usually unilateral → obstruction at kidney pelvis → normal ureters, completely **normal bladder**, normal amniotic fluid
* **PUV**: Strictly bilateral → affects entirely upstream → distended thick bladder, bilateral hydroureter, bilateral hydronephrosis, severe oligohydramnios

## 6. Differential Diagnosis

### Differential Diagnosis of Severe Neonatal Obstructive Uropathy

| Condition | Key Differentiating Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Posterior Urethral Valves (PUV)** | Males only. "Keyhole" sign. Thickened bladder. Bilateral hydronephrosis. |
| **Bilateral Vesicoureteral Reflux (VUR)** | Can affect both sexes. Usually normal bladder wall thickness. Diagnosed via MCUG. |
| **Prune Belly Syndrome (梅乾腹症候群)** | Absent abdominal wall muscles. Bilateral cryptorchidism. Urinary tract anomalies. |
| **Urethral Atresia** | Complete blockage. Usually fatal in utero unless patent urachus exists. |
| **Bilateral PUJ Obstruction** | Extremely rare to be severe bilaterally. Normal bladder. Normal ureters. |

## 7. Tiered Investigations

### Bedside Investigations for Suspected PUV
* **Catheterization**: Attempt gentle passage of 6Fr transurethral or suprapubic catheter → drains bladder & immediately relieves back-pressure

### Blood Investigations for Suspected PUV
* **Renal Function Test (RFT)**: ↑ Creatinine, ↑ Urea, severe electrolyte derangements (↑ K+, ↓ Na+)
* **Venous Blood Gas (VBG)**: Metabolic acidosis (from acute renal failure)

### Imaging Investigations for Suspected PUV
* **Postnatal Ultrasound (USG)**: 
  * Confirm bilateral hydronephrosis
  * Measure bladder wall thickness
  * Detect renal parenchymal thinning & cystic dysplasia

### Gold Standard Special Investigation for PUV
* **Micturating Cystourethrogram (MCUG) (排尿性膀胱尿道造影)**:
  * **Timing**: Done urgently postnatally
  * **Method**: Contrast dye injected via catheter into bladder; serial X-rays taken during voiding
  * **Pathognomonic Finding**: Dilated posterior urethra abruptly narrowing at valve → **"Spinning top" (陀螺) appearance**
  * **Secondary Finding**: Massive **Vesicoureteral Reflux (VUR)** due to high pressure

## 8. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)

### Acute Neonatal Management of Posterior Urethral Valves
* **Resuscitation**: ABCs → immediately intubate & mechanically ventilate if respiratory failure from pulmonary hypoplasia
* **Bladder Decompression (CRITICAL)**: 
  * Pass 6Fr transurethral catheter immediately
  * If fails → insert suprapubic catheter
  * **Precautions**: Drain urine slowly to prevent massive **post-obstructive diuresis** & hypovolemic shock
* **Medical**: Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics (e.g., Ampicillin + Gentamicin) as prophylaxis against UTI/urosepsis

### Definitive Surgical Management of Posterior Urethral Valves
* **Primary Approach**: **Cystoscopic Valve Ablation (膀胱鏡瓣膜切除術)**
  * Insert cystoscope → use hot wire hook, diathermy, or laser to incise/destroy the membranous valves
* **Secondary Approach (If urethra too small for cystoscope)**: 
  * **Vesicostomy (膀胱造口術)**: Temporary surgical diversion bringing bladder directly to lower abdominal skin
  * Definitive ablation delayed until infant grows larger

### Chronic Management & HA Follow-up
* **Prophylaxis**: Long-term oral antibiotics (e.g., Trimethoprim) to prevent pyelonephritis
* **Monitoring**: Serial USG to monitor hydronephrosis resolution; strict monitoring of serum creatinine & growth parameters

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care

### Multidisciplinary Team Involvement for PUV
* **Paediatric Nephrology/Urology**: Lifelong monitoring. Up to 30% inevitably progress to **End-Stage Renal Failure (ESRF)** by puberty despite perfect postnatal ablation
* **Medical Social Worker (MSW)**: Financial assistance for potential future dialysis/transplant costs
* **Clinical Psychologist**: Support for parents coping with child's chronic renal failure burden

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags

### Safeguarding Considerations in Congenital Uropathy
* Strictly congenital condition. 
* NAI (Non-Accidental Injury) is **N/A** for the primary pathology. 
* Monitor for medical neglect if parents repeatedly default on nephrology follow-up or prophylactic antibiotic administration.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis

### Life-Threatening Neonatal Complications
* **Pulmonary Hypoplasia**: Leading cause of early neonatal mortality
* **Urosepsis**: High risk due to stagnant urine

### Long-Term Renal & Urological Prognosis
* **End-Stage Renal Failure (ESRF)**: Requires peritoneal dialysis, haemodialysis, or renal transplant
* **Valve Bladder Syndrome (瓣膜膀胱症候群)**: Permanently hypertrophied, stiff, poorly compliant bladder → lifelong incontinence, poor emptying, and continuous upper tract damage
* **Growth Faltering**: Secondary to chronic kidney disease

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: An antenatal USG reveals severe oligohydramnios and a 'keyhole' bladder in a male fetus. When this baby is born, what will be the most life-threatening immediate complication?
* Answer: Respiratory failure due to pulmonary hypoplasia (Potter sequence), because amniotic fluid is required for normal lung development.
* Trap: Assuming a successful valve ablation operation means the child is "cured" of kidney disease.
* Pearl: You MUST monitor their creatinine for life, as the prenatal renal dysplasia will often slowly progress to kidney failure exactly as the child hits their adolescent growth spurt.
* Pitfall: Vigorously draining a massively distended PUV bladder all at once. This triggers massive post-obstructive diuresis, leading to profound hypovolemic shock and electrolyte collapse.
* Viva Question: What is the gold standard imaging modality to formally diagnose Posterior Urethral Valves postnatally?
* Answer: Micturating Cystourethrogram (MCUG) demonstrating the classic "spinning top" dilated posterior urethra.
