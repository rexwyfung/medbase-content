
# Childhood Asthma (兒童哮喘)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A chronic inflammatory disorder of the airways characterised by airway hyper-responsiveness and variable, reversible airflow obstruction.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Extremely common in HK (~10% of children). Exacerbations peak during seasonal changes (Spring/Autumn) and periods of poor air quality (high API).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Mechanism**: Chronic eosinophilic (Type 2) inflammation of the bronchial mucosa.
- **Triggers**: Viral URTI (most common trigger for acute exacerbations), aeroallergens (house dust mite is the most common in HK, cockroach, pet dander), exercise, cold air, pollution, passive smoking.
- **Atopic March**: Strong association with eczema and allergic rhinitis. Often a strong family history of atopy.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- See below.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Chronic/Interval Symptoms**:
  - Recurrent episodes of wheezing, breathlessness, chest tightness, and cough.
  - **Cough**: Often dry, worse at night or early morning, or triggered by exercise/laughing.
- **Acute Exacerbation**:
  - Tachypnoea, tachycardia, prolonged expiratory phase.
  - **Wheeze**: Widespread, polyphonic, expiratory wheeze.
  - **Work of Breathing**: Subcostal/intercostal recessions, tracheal tug, nasal flaring.
## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Viral Wheeze (Preschool)**: Very common in children < 5 years. Wheeze only occurs during viral colds, with no interval symptoms. Many outgrow this and do not develop true atopic asthma.
- **Foreign Body Aspiration**: Sudden onset, unilateral wheeze, focal decreased air entry.
- **Pertussis**: Paroxysmal cough with an inspiratory whoop, post-tussive vomiting, no wheeze.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Most Likely**:
  - Asthma (if > 5 years, atopic background, interval symptoms).
  - Viral-induced wheeze (if < 5 years, only wheezes with colds).
- **Must-Not-Miss**:
  - **Life-Threatening Asthma**: Silent chest (no wheeze because airflow is so poor), cyanosis, exhaustion, confusion, SpO2 < 92%.
  - Anaphylaxis (can present with severe wheeze).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**:
  - **Clinical Diagnosis**: Asthma is primarily a clinical diagnosis based on history and response to bronchodilators.
  - **Peak Expiratory Flow Rate (PEFR)**: Useful in older children (> 6 years) to assess severity and monitor response to treatment.
  - **Pulse Oximetry**: Essential in acute exacerbations.
- **Gold Standard**:
  - **Spirometry**: Shows obstructive pattern (FEV1/FVC < 80%) with **reversibility** (FEV1 increases by >12% after Salbutamol). Difficult to perform reliably in children < 6 years.
  - **Allergy Testing**: Skin prick testing or specific IgE (RAST) to identify triggers (e.g., House Dust Mite).
- **Monitoring**:
  - Asthma Control Test (ACT) questionnaire at clinic visits.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**:
  - Acute Exacerbation
  - **Oxygen**: Target SpO2 94-98%.
  - **Bronchodilators**: Inhaled Salbutamol (via spacer or nebuliser). Ipratropium Bromide added for severe cases.
  - **Systemic Corticosteroids**: Oral Prednisolone (3-5 days) or IV Hydrocortisone (if unable to tolerate oral). Give early to reduce inflammation and prevent admission.
  - **IV Magnesium Sulphate**: For severe, refractory exacerbations.
  - **IV Salbutamol / Aminophylline**: ICU level care.
- **Chronic/Definitive**:
  - **Stepwise approach (GINA Guidelines)**:
    - *Step 1*: SABA (Salbutamol) PRN.
    - *Step 2*: Add low-dose Inhaled Corticosteroid (ICS) (e.g., Fluticasone, Budesonide) as a daily preventer.
    - *Step 3*: Increase ICS dose OR add Long-Acting Beta-Agonist (LABA) (e.g., Seretide/Symbicort combination inhalers).
    - *Step 4/5*: High-dose ICS + LABA + consider Leukotriene Receptor Antagonist (Montelukast) or Biologics (e.g., Omalizumab/Anti-IgE for severe allergic asthma).
## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Asthma Nurse Specialist**: Crucial for inhaler technique education and providing a written Asthma Action Plan.
## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- **Safety Netting**: Educate parents on the difference between "reliever" (blue) and "preventer" (brown/purple) inhalers. Emphasise that preventers must be taken daily even when well.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Most children with mild/moderate asthma have a good prognosis and may outgrow their symptoms in adolescence.
- **Complications**: Severe acute asthma (status asthmaticus) can be fatal. Chronic poor control leads to airway remodelling and fixed airflow obstruction.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Prescribing an oral beta-agonist syrup. *Pearl*: Oral bronchodilators are obsolete due to systemic side effects (tremor, tachycardia) and poor efficacy compared to inhaled therapy. Always use an inhaler with a spacer.
- **Viva Question**: "What is a 'silent chest'?" *Answer*: In a severe asthma attack, the airways are so constricted that there is insufficient airflow to generate a wheeze. It is a sign of imminent respiratory arrest.
