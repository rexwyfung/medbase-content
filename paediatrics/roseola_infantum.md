# Roseola Infantum / HHV-6 (玫瑰疹 / 第六型人類疱疹病毒)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition of Roseola Infantum
* Acute benign viral exanthem of early childhood
* Characterized by 3-5 days of high fever (高熱)
* Sudden defervescence followed immediately by maculopapular rash (斑丘疹)

### Hong Kong Epidemiology of HHV-6
* Peak age of infection: 6-15 months
* >90% seroprevalence by age 2 in Hong Kong
* Transmission: Horizontal via respiratory droplets / saliva from asymptomatic shedding contacts
* Seasonal trend in HK: Year-round, slight ↑ in Spring/Autumn
* Maternal IgG protects infants <6 months

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Virology of HHV-6
* Virus: Human Herpesvirus 6 (HHV-6), primarily variant 6B (HHV-6B)
* Family: *Betaherpesvirinae*
* Genome: Enveloped, double-stranded DNA (dsDNA)

### Pathogenesis of HHV-6 Infection
* Primary entry: Oropharyngeal mucosa
* Viral replication: Salivary glands → viremia
* Cellular tropism: Infects mature CD4+ T lymphocytes
* Latency: Establishes lifelong latency in monocytes/macrophages and salivary glands
* Reactivation: Possible in immunocompromised states (e.g., post-bone marrow transplant)

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Prodromal & Febrile Phase Symptoms
* Sudden onset high fever (>39.5°C) lasting 3-5 days
* General appearance: Well, alert, active (paradoxical to ↑↑ temp)
* Mild upper respiratory symptoms
* Mild tympanic membrane injection
* Suboccipital / cervical lymphadenopathy (淋巴結腫大)

### Dermatological Signs (Eruptive Phase)
* Blanching maculopapular rash (斑丘疹)
* Rash onset: Strictly coincides with defervescence (rapid drop in temp)
* Distribution pattern: Starts on trunk/neck → spreads to extremities / face
* Duration of rash: 1-2 days (often fades rapidly)
* Non-pruritic (無瘙癢)
* Nagayama spots (永山斑): Erythematous papules on soft palate/uvula (seen in Asian populations)

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Fever-Rash Timing Relationship
* **Roseola:** Rash appears *after* fever breaks
* **Measles (麻疹):** Rash appears *during* peak fever
* **Rubella (風疹):** Rash appears *concurrently* with low-grade fever

### Clinical Appearance during Fever
* **Roseola:** Playful, normal appetite, non-toxic appearance despite ↑↑ temperature
* **Bacterial Sepsis / Meningitis:** Lethargic, toxic appearance, poor feeding, irritable

## 5. Differential Diagnosis
### Viral Exanthems Differential Diagnosis Table

| Condition | Key Clinical Distinguishers |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Measles (麻疹)** | Cough, coryza, conjunctivitis (3Cs), Koplik spots, toxic appearance, rash with fever |
| **Rubella (風疹)** | Low-grade fever, prominent postauricular lymphadenopathy, concurrent rash |
| **Erythema Infectiosum (傳染性紅斑)** | Parvovirus B19, "slapped cheek" rash, reticular lacelike rash on limbs, lack of high fever |
| **Enterovirus (腸病毒)** | HFMD lesions (vesicles on hands/feet/mouth), summer peak in HK |

### Non-Viral Differential Diagnosis Table

| Condition | Key Clinical Distinguishers |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Drug Eruption (藥疹)** | History of recent antibiotics (e.g., Amoxicillin), intensely pruritic, eosinophilia |
| **Kawasaki Disease (川崎病)** | Fever ≥5 days, bilateral non-exudative conjunctivitis, mucosal changes, cervical lymphadenopathy, polymorphous rash |
| **Urinary Tract Infection (尿道炎)** | Fever without focus, +ve urine culture, abnormal urinalysis |

## 6. Tiered Investigations
### 1st Line Bedside & Laboratory Investigations
* Diagnosis is primarily **clinical**
* Urinalysis / Urine Culture:
  * To rule out UTI in infants with fever without focus
* Complete Blood Picture (CBP):
  * Initial 1-2 days: Leukocytosis
  * Day 3-5: Leukopenia (↓ WBC)
  * Relative lymphocytosis

### Specific Virological Investigations (HA Protocols)
* Not required for uncomplicated cases
* HHV-6 PCR (Blood / CSF):
  * Indicated ONLY if complicated febrile seizure, suspected encephalitis, or immunocompromised patient
* Lumbar Puncture (LP):
  * Indicated if meningeal signs present or prolonged focal seizure to rule out bacterial meningitis

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Acute Supportive Management
* Reassurance of parents (benign, self-limiting nature)
* Maintain adequate oral hydration
* Antipyretics for comfort:
  * Paracetamol (撲熱息痛) 10-15 mg/kg/dose
  * Ibuprofen (布洛芬) 5-10 mg/kg/dose (if >6 months)

### HA Protocol for Febrile Seizures (熱性痙攣)
* Seizure <5 mins:
  * Recovery position
  * Airway protection
  * No acute anti-epileptic required
* Prolonged Seizure (>5 mins):
  * Abort seizure via HA algorithm:
  * Buccal Midazolam (0.5 mg/kg) OR
  * Per-rectal (PR) Diazepam (0.5 mg/kg)
* Admission criteria: Complex febrile seizure, age <18 months, or first episode for observation

### Antiviral Therapy Indications
* Uncomplicated Roseola: Nil
* Severe HHV-6 Encephalitis / Immunocompromised host (e.g., post-BMT):
  * IV Ganciclovir (更昔洛韋)
  * IV Foscarnet (膦甲酸)

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Parental Reassurance and Education
* Educate on typical timeline (fever breaks → rash appears → full recovery)
* Febrile seizure first-aid education
* Written fever management advice (DH pamphlets)

### Infection Control Guidelines
* Standard and contact precautions
* Exclude from HK daycare/kindergarten until:
  * Rash completely resolves
  * Child is afebrile and clinically well

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Medical Abuse and Neglect Flags
* Significant delay in seeking medical attention for prolonged/focal seizures
* Intentional medication overdose (e.g., Paracetamol toxicity from parental panic)

### Inconsistent Clinical Presentation
* Non-blanching petechiae/purpura mistaken by parents as "viral rash" (Must rule out trauma/NAI or meningococcemia)
* Bruising in non-mobile infants

## 10. Complications & Prognosis
### Neurological Complications
* Febrile Seizures (熱性痙攣):
  * Occurs in 10-15% of cases
  * Usually simple, generalized tonic-clonic
  * HHV-6 is the most common viral cause of febrile seizures in infancy
* Encephalitis / Meningitis (腦炎/腦膜炎):
  * Extremely rare in immunocompetent
  * Can cause status epilepticus

### Long-Term Prognosis
* Excellent for immunocompetent children
* Complete resolution without scarring
* Lifelong latency established
* Reactivation risk reserved for severe immunosuppression

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Viva Question: What is the classic timeline of rash and fever in Roseola Infantum? Answer: The blanching maculopapular rash appears exactly as the high fever abruptly resolves (defervescence), typically on day 3-5.
* Viva Question: Which virus is the most common cause of febrile seizures in children under 2 years old? Answer: Human Herpesvirus 6 (HHV-6).
* Trap: Prescribing antibiotics for the high fever and mild tympanic membrane injection before the rash appears. Pearl: HHV-6 often causes mild TM erythema without bulging; rely on clinical appearance and wait for defervescence if the child is well-appearing to avoid unnecessary antibiotics.
* Pitfall: Confusing the Roseola rash with an Amoxicillin-induced drug rash. If the child was given Amoxicillin for "fever" and develops a rash on day 4 when the fever drops, it is almost certainly the natural course of Roseola, not a true Penicillin allergy.
