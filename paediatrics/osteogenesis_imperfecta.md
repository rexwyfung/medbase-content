# Osteogenesis Imperfecta (成骨不全症 / 玻璃骨)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A genetic disorder causing extreme fragility of the bones. 
- **Local Epidemiology**: Approx 1 in 15,000 to 20,000 births.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Most commonly autosomal dominant mutations in **COL1A1 or COL1A2** genes.
- Creates a quantitative (Type I, mild) or qualitative (Types II-IV, severe) defect in **Type I collagen**. Type 1 collagen forms the basic scaffold for bone, sclera, and dentin.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Varies wildly depending on type.
- **Type I (Mild)**: Presents in childhood with fractures from trivial falls (e.g. stepping off a curb).
- **Type II (Perinatal lethal)**: Fetuses present with fractured limbs on anomaly scan or are born with crushed ribcages.
- Many are mistakenly investigated for NAI (child abuse) initially.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Skeletal**: Recurrent low-velocity fractures, progressive bowing of long bones, short stature, severe scoliosis.
- **Extraskeletal**: 
  - **Blue sclerae** (thin sclera reveals the underlying blue choroidal veins).
  - **Dentinogenesis imperfecta** (amber/translucent, easily crumbling teeth).
  - Progressive conductive/sensorineural hearing loss (due to fractured ossicles).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Must be definitively distinguished from NAI. 
- Blue sclerae, wormian bones on skull X-ray, and DI are virtually pathognomonic for OI over abuse.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Non-Accidental Injury (NAI / Child abuse)
- Rickets / Osteomalacia
- Juvenile Osteoporosis
- Hypophosphatasia

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bloods**: Normal calcium, phosphate and PTH (this contrasts sharply with rickets).
- **Imaging**: Skeletal survey. Often shows osteopenia, old uniting fractures with massive hyperplastic callus formation, wormian bones in skull, and "codfish" vertebrae (compression fractures).
- **Special**: DNA sequencing of COL1A1/COL1A2 for unequivocal diagnosis.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Pharmacological**: Cyclic IV **Bisphosphonates** (e.g., Pamidronate or Zoledronic acid) under HA protocol wildly reduce fracture rates and bone pain by suppressing osteoclasts.
- **Orthopaedic**: "Roding" operations (Fassier-Duval telescoping rods) placed down the medullary canal of long bones to prevent bowing and provide internal scaffolding.
- **Dental & ENT**: Close monitoring for crumbling teeth and hearing deficits.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Deep MSW involvement. Diagnosis shields the family from continuous SWD investigations for child abuse.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- **MANDATORY**: Any child presenting with unexplained fractures requires a full safeguarding screen. OI must be formally excluded, but true abuse is mathematically far more common than OI. If OI testing is pending, standard HA child protection protocols (SWD notification, keeping child safely in hospital) must proceed unaltered.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Unrelenting chronic pain, immobility, wheel-chair reliance.
- Cardiopulmonary failure due to severe kyphoscoliosis destroying chest capacity.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Assuming blue sclera completely confirms OI. Sometimes totally normal neonates have slightly blue sclera because it is naturally thin in infancy. It must persist.
- **Viva Q**: "How do the lab results differentiate OI from Rickets?" Answer: OI features a genetic structural defect, so Calcium, Phosphate, and Alk Phos are completely normal. Rickets (nutritional) shows low Phos, high Alk Phos, and sometimes low Calcium.
