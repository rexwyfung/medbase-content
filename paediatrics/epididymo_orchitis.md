# Epididymo-orchitis (附睾睾丸炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Acute inflammation of the epididymis, often spreading down to involve the testis itself (orchitis).
- **Local Epidemiology**: Bimodal presentation. Occurs in infants/toddlers (usually secondary to congenital urinary tract anomalies) and in sexually active adolescents (usually STIs).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Infants/Prepubertal Boys**: Often caused by ascending bacterial infection from the urinary tract (E. coli). Strongly associated with underlying anatomical structural abnormalities (e.g., ectopic ureter inserting into the vas deferens, PUV, or neurogenic bladder).
- **Postpubertal/Adolescents**: Exclusively driven by typical sexually transmitted infections, almost entirely **Chlamydia trachomatis** or **Neisseria gonorrhoeae**.
- **Viral Orchitis**: The classic cause is **Mumps** (Paramyxovirus), though much rarer now due to the MMR vaccine.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- A 16-year-old sexually active male presenting with a 3-day history of steadily worsening, severely swollen, fiery red, painful right testicle, accompanied by burning upon urination (dysuria).

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- Gradual onset of unilateral scrotal pain, swelling, and marked erythema.
- Fever, dysuria, and occasionally urethral discharge.
- **Prehn's Sign**: The pain is *partially relieved* by manually elevating the affected testicle to relieve weight/traction on the inflamed epididymis. (In torsion, elevating it does nothing or worsens pain).
- Posterior palpation localized to the epididymis is exquisitely tender initially before involving the whole testis.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from Torsion: Gradual onset (days vs minutes), clear infectious symptoms (fever, dysuria, discharge), and Doppler ultrasound conclusively shows massive hyperemia (extra blood flow) rather than ischemia.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Testicular Torsion
- Torsion of the Appendix Testis
- Incarcerated Inguinal Hernia
- Scrotal abscess

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Urine**: Urinalysis (leukocyte esterase + nitrites). Urine culture to identify UTIs.
- **STI Screen**: First-void urine for Nucleic Acid Amplification Test (NAAT / PCR) for Chlamydia and Gonorrhoea.
- **Bloods**: Elevated WBC and CRP/ESR.
- **Ultrasound Doppler**: The epididymis and testis are enlarged and show **strikingly increased vascularity/blood flow**.
- **Special for Prepubertal kids**: An ultrasound of the kidneys and bladder is MANDATORY to rule out congenital structural anomalies driving the UTI.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Prepubertal (Suspected Enteric UTI)**: Typical broad-spectrum IV/Oral antibiotics like Co-amoxiclav or Cefuroxime targeting E. coli.
- **Postpubertal (Suspected STI)**: Empiric treatment strictly per STI guidelines. Typically a single IM injection of Ceftriaxone (covers Gonorrhoea), plus 14 days of oral Doxycycline (covers Chlamydia).
- **Supportive**: Scrotal elevation (tight briefs), NSAIDs, ice packs.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Mandatory confidential partner notification and STI counseling for adolescents.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Detecting an STI (Chlamydia/Gonorrhea) in a prepubertal child is an absolute Red Flag for sexual abuse and requires an immediate child protection (CPPU) intervention.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Progression to a frank scrotal abscess (requiring surgical drainage).
- Chronic epididymal scarring occasionally leading to infertility.
- Mumps orchitis has a notoriously high rate of causing bilateral testicular atrophy and sterility if caught post-puberty.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Treating an 18-year-old for epididymo-orchitis with Ciprofloxacin without running an STI screen. Gonorrhea is highly resistant to Cipro globally; you must cover using Ceftriaxone.
- **Viva Q**: "A 6-year-old boy develops true E. coli epididymitis. What follow-up imaging is absolutely mandatory once the infection clears?" Answer: A renal and bladder ultrasound (and potentially an MCUG) because prepubertal epididymitis is very frequently caused by a major underlying anatomical abnormality, like an ectopic ureter.
