# Impetigo (膿皰瘡)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A highly contagious, superficial bacterial infection of the epidermis.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Extremely common in toddlers and school-age children in Hong Kong, especially during the hot, humid summer months and among children with underlying eczema.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Typically caused by **Staphylococcus aureus** or **Streptococcus pyogenes** (Group A Strep - GAS).
- Bacteria exploit micro-abrasions in the skin (e.g., from scratching eczema or insect bites) to invade the stratum corneum.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Starts as tiny red macules or vesicles, usually around the nose and mouth.
- Rapidly ruptures to form thick, sticky fluid that dries.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Non-bullous (70%)**: The classic **"honey-coloured crusts"** on an erythematous base. Often clustered. Asymptomatic but itchy.
- **Bullous (30%)**: Almost exclusively *Staph aureus* (it produces exfoliative toxins A and B which split the epidermis). Forms large, flaccid, clear blisters that easily burst, leaving raw, shiny, varnish-like zones.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from Herpes Simplex Virus (Cold Sores): HSV usually forms tight clusters of tiny uniform vesicles on the vermillion border and is intensely painful. Impetigo crusts are larger, honey-colored, and spread rapidly via autoinoculation (scratching).

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV 1)
- Chickenpox (Varicella)
- Eczema herpeticum
- Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Clinical diagnosis predominates**.
- **Swab**: Skin swab for bacterial culture and sensitivities (C&S) is useful if the infection is recurrent, fails to clear, or MRSA is suspected.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Hygiene**: Strictly separate towels and flannels. Cut child's fingernails short to prevent scratching and autoinoculation. Wash crusts gently with warm water and soap.
- **Localized/Mild**: Topical antibiotic ointment (Mupirocin or Fusidic Acid) applied 3 times a day for 5-7 days.
- **Extensive/Bullous/Systemic symptoms**: Oral antibiotics to cover Staph and Strep. E.g., Flucloxacillin or Cephalexin (Use Clindamycin if penicillin-allergic).

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **School Exclusion**: This is a major public health point. The child MUST be excluded from school/daycare until the lesions are completely healed and crusted over, OR until 48 hours after starting appropriate systemic antibiotics.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Severe, untreated, extremely widespread impetigo can be a sign of medical/hygiene neglect.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Post-Streptococcal Glomerulonephritis (PSGN)**: Can occur 3-6 weeks after Strep impetigo. Presenting with hematuria, edema, and hypertension. (Note: Treating impetigo with antibiotics does *not* reliably prevent PSGN).
- Acute Rheumatic Fever: Can follow strep *throat*, but virtually NEVER follows strep *skin* infections.
- Staphylococcal Scalded Skin Syndrome (SSSS): Systemic spread of the exfoliative toxins causing widespread skin peeling.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Sending a child with active, oozing impetigo back to school. They will infect the entire classroom. 
- **Viva Q**: "A child has Strep throat and another has Strep impetigo. Which one is at risk for Acute Rheumatic fever?" Answer: Only the child with Strep throat. Strep skin infections (impetigo) do NOT cause Acute Rheumatic Fever. However, BOTH throat and skin strep infections can cause Post-Streptococcal Glomerulonephritis.
