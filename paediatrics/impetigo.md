# Impetigo (膿皰瘡)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition
* **Definition:** Highly contagious, superficial bacterial skin infection restricted to the epidermis (stratum corneum).
* **Primary Targets:** Infants, toddlers, and school-aged children.

### Local Epidemiology (Hong Kong)
* **Prevalence:** Extremely common in HK due to the hot, humid summer climate.
* **Risk Factors:** ↑ risk in children with underlying Atopic Eczema (濕疹), poor hygiene, or crowded daycare/school settings.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Pathogenesis
* **Causative Pathogens:** *Staphylococcus aureus* (most common) or *Streptococcus pyogenes* (Group A Strep - GAS).
* **Mechanism:** Bacteria invade the stratum corneum via micro-abrasions (e.g., scratching insect bites or pre-existing eczema).
* **Autoinoculation:** Exudate from ruptured lesions contains high bacterial loads → scratching → rapidly spreads infection to adjacent skin.

### Bullous vs Non-Bullous Pathophysiology
* **Non-Bullous (70%):** Host immune response to *S. aureus* or GAS forms pustules → rupture into classic crusts.
* **Bullous (30%):** Almost exclusively *S. aureus*. Pathogen produces exfoliative toxins A/B → cleaves desmoglein-1 in the epidermis → large flaccid blisters without thick crusting.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Non-Bullous Impetigo Symptoms & Signs
* **Progression:** Erythematous macules (紅斑) → vesicular/pustular lesions → rapid rupture.
* **Classic Sign:** Confluent **"honey-coloured crusts" (蜜黃色結痂)** on an erythematous base.
* **Distribution:** Commonly clustered on face (perioral/perinasal), neck, and hands.
* **Symptoms:** Usually asymptomatic but highly pruritic (痕癢).

### Bullous Impetigo (大皰性膿皰瘡) Symptoms & Signs
* **Presentation:** Large, flaccid, clear/purulent blisters (bullae).
* **Progression:** Easily rupture → leaves raw, shiny, varnish-like eroded zones.
* **Demographics:** Most frequently seen in neonates and young infants.

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Impetigo vs Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV)
| Feature | Impetigo (膿皰瘡) | Herpes Simplex Virus (單純皰疹) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Lesion Type** | Honey-coloured crusts, large flaccid bullae | Tight clusters of uniform tiny vesicles |
| **Location** | Spreads rapidly anywhere (face/neck/hands) | Usually localized strictly to the vermillion border |
| **Pain Profile** | Pruritic, mildly tender | Intensely painful, burning sensation |

## 5. Differential Diagnosis
### Common Differentials
* **Viral:** Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV-1), Varicella Zoster Virus (Chickenpox), Hand Foot and Mouth Disease (手足口病).
* **Secondary Infections:** Eczema Herpeticum (皰疹性濕疹) - *life-threatening, check for punched-out erosions*.
* **Fungal:** Candidiasis (especially in the diaper region).

## 6. Tiered Investigations
### Bedside & Blood Investigations
* **Clinical Diagnosis:** Predominates. Typically requires NO routine lab investigations.

### Special Swab Investigations
* **Skin Swab for Culture & Sensitivities (C&S):** Indicated ONLY IF:
  * Treatment failure after 48-72h.
  * Recurrent outbreaks.
  * Suspicion of Methicillin-Resistant *S. aureus* (MRSA).
  * Post-Streptococcal Glomerulonephritis (PSGN) is suspected.

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Non-Pharmacological Management
* **Hygiene:** Strict separation of towels/flannels. Wash crusts gently with warm soap/water.
* **Autoinoculation Prevention:** Keep the child's fingernails short.

### 1st Line Management (Localized/Mild)
* **Topical Antibiotics:** **Mupirocin** (莫匹羅星) or **Fusidic Acid** (夫西地酸) ointment.
* **Regimen:** Apply 3x/day for 5-7 days.

### 2nd Line Management (Extensive/Bullous/Systemic)
* **Indications:** Widespread lesions, bullous form, systemic upset, or topical failure.
* **Oral Antibiotics (Targeting Staph/Strep):** **Flucloxacillin** (氟氯西林) or **Cephalexin** (頭孢氨苄).
* **HK HA Prescribing Pearl:** Co-amoxiclav or Cephalexin are often preferred in pediatrics due to simpler dosing regimens and better palatability → ↑ adherence.
* **Penicillin Allergy:** Use **Clindamycin** or **Erythromycin**.
* **Neonatal Bullous Impetigo:** Often requires hospital admission & IV systemic antibiotics (e.g., IV Flucloxacillin) to prevent progression.

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Public Health & School Exclusion
* **School Exclusion Policy:** CRITICAL. The child MUST be excluded from school/daycare until:
  * Lesions are completely dry and crusted over, OR
  * 48 hours post-initiation of appropriate systemic antibiotics.
* **MCHC & Public Health:** Education of parents and school nurses on the contagious nature of the condition to prevent local community outbreaks.

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Recognizing Neglect
* **Medical/Hygiene Neglect:** Severe, untreated, extremely widespread impetigo in an unkempt child.
* **Recurrent Infections:** Suggests profoundly poor home hygiene or an underlying severe untreated skin condition (e.g., severe scabies or neglected eczema).

## 10. Complications & Prognosis
### Renal Complications
* **Post-Streptococcal Glomerulonephritis (PSGN):** Occurs 3-6 weeks post-Strep impetigo. 
* **PSGN Triad:** Hematuria (血尿), Edema (水腫), Hypertension (高血壓). 
* *Note:* Antibiotic treatment of impetigo does ↓ spread but does **NOT** reliably prevent PSGN.

### Systemic Complications
* **Staphylococcal Scalded Skin Syndrome (SSSS):** Systemic hematogenous spread of *S. aureus* exfoliative toxins A/B → widespread blistering & epidermal sloughing. 
* **Cellulitis/Septicemia:** Deep tissue invasion, especially in immunocompromised children or neonates.

## 11. Examiner's Pearls
* Trap: Believing antibiotic treatment for Strep impetigo prevents Acute Rheumatic Fever (ARF). Pearl: Strep skin infections (impetigo) do NOT cause ARF (only Strep pharyngitis does). However, BOTH throat and skin Strep infections can cause PSGN.
* Viva Question: How long must a child with impetigo be excluded from school? Answer: Until all lesions are completely dry and crusted, or 48 hours after starting appropriate systemic antibiotics.
* Pitfall: Assuming a localized blister in a neonate is harmless. Bullous impetigo in neonates is a red flag for potential rapid progression to Staphylococcal Scalded Skin Syndrome (SSSS) and requires prompt systemic antibiotics.
* Trap: Using topical antibiotics for widespread or bullous impetigo. Pearl: Extensive, bullous, or systemically symptomatic impetigo mandates ORAL broad-spectrum antibiotics like Cephalexin or Flucloxacillin.
