# Down Syndrome (Trisomy 21) (唐氏綜合症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: The most common chromosomal abnormality causing intellectual disability, resulting from the presence of all or part of a third copy of chromosome 21.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Incidence in Hong Kong is approximately 1 in 700 to 1,000 live births. The risk is strongly and exponentially correlated with advancing maternal age (e.g., risk is ~1/100 at age 40).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Nondisjunction (95%)**: Failure of chromosome 21 to separate properly during meiosis (usually maternal meiosis I). Results in 47, XX,+21 or 47, XY,+21. Recurrence risk is ~1%.
- **Translocation (4%)**: An unbalanced Robertsonian translocation, usually involving chromosome 14 and 21 [e.g., t(14;21)]. The child has 46 chromosomes but 3 copies of the long arm of chr 21. *Crucial: If a parent is a balanced translocation carrier, the recurrence risk for future pregnancies is very high (up to 10-15% if mother is carrier, 100% if parent has a 21;21 translocation).*
- **Mosaicism (1%)**: Post-zygotic mitotic error. Some cells have 46 chromosomes, others have 47. Tends to have a milder phenotype.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Usually diagnosed prenatally via screening (Non-Invasive Prenatal Testing - NIPT, nuchal translucency) and confirmed by amniocentesis/CVS.
- Postnatally, recognized immediately in the delivery room due to characteristic dysmorphic facial features and profound generalized hypotonia ("floppy baby").

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Craniofacial**: Upslanting palpebral fissures, prominent epicanthic folds, flat facial profile, flat nasal bridge, small low-set ears, protruding tongue (due to macroglossia and a small oral cavity), Brushfield spots (white/grey spots on the periphery of the iris).
- **Musculoskeletal**: Generalized hypotonia, single transverse palmar crease (Simian crease), clinodactyly (incurved 5th finger), wide "sandal gap" between the 1st and 2nd toes, joint hypermobility.
- **Neurological**: Global developmental delay, mild to moderate intellectual disability (IQ usually 35-70).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Edwards Syndrome (Trisomy 18)**: Severe intellectual disability, prominent occiput, micrognathia, clenched hands with overlapping fingers, rocker-bottom feet. Usually fatal in the first year.
- **Patau Syndrome (Trisomy 13)**: Severe midline defects: cleft lip/palate, microphthalmia, holoprosencephaly, polydactyly. Usually fatal in the first year.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Congenital Hypothyroidism (can present with hypotonia, macroglossia, and developmental delay - must be ruled out, though it frequently co-exists with Down syndrome).
- Zellweger syndrome (severe hypotonia and dysmorphism).
- Other chromosomal microdeletions/duplications.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Diagnostic**: **Karyotype or Chromosomal Microarray (CMA)**. Mandatory to confirm the diagnosis and, crucially, to differentiate nondisjunction from translocation for genetic counseling.
- **Screening for Complications (HA Guidelines)**:
  - **Echocardiogram**: Mandatory for ALL newborns with Down syndrome. 50% have Congenital Heart Disease.
  - **Thyroid Function Tests (TFTs)**: At birth, 6 months, 12 months, and then annually (high risk of autoimmune hypothyroidism).
  - **Complete Blood Count (CBP)**: At birth (to check for Transient Abnormal Myelopoiesis) and annually (to screen for leukaemia).
  - **Audiology & Ophthalmology**: Annual screening for hearing loss (sensorineural and conductive) and cataracts/refractive errors.
  - **C-Spine X-ray**: Screen for atlantoaxial instability (usually done around age 3-5, or before participating in contact sports/Special Olympics).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- There is no medical cure. Management is anticipatory and multidisciplinary.
- **Early Intervention Programmes (EIP)**: The most critical aspect of management. Referral to Child Assessment Centre (CAC) for placement in special child care centres.
  - *Physiotherapy*: To improve motor milestones delayed by hypotonia.
  - *Speech Therapy*: Crucial due to anatomical challenges (macroglossia) and hearing deficits. Sign language (Makaton) is often used early on.
  - *Occupational Therapy*: For fine motor skills and activities of daily living.
- **Surgical**: Correction of cardiac defects (e.g., AVSD repair) or GI anomalies (e.g., duodenal atresia) in infancy.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Medical Social Worker (MSW)**: Essential for assisting with Disability Allowance applications and navigating the special education system in HK.
- **Parental Support**: Connect parents with the Hong Kong Down Syndrome Association. The initial diagnosis is a period of profound grief and adjustment.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Children with intellectual disabilities are statistically at a significantly higher risk of physical, emotional, and sexual abuse. Clinicians must maintain a high index of suspicion.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Cardiac (50%)**: **Atrioventricular Septal Defect (AVSD)** / Endocardial cushion defect is the most classic. Also VSD, ASD, Tetralogy of Fallot.
- **Gastrointestinal (5%)**: Duodenal atresia ("double bubble" sign on AXR), Hirschsprung disease, imperforate anus, Coeliac disease.
- **Haematology/Oncology**: 10-20x increased risk of Leukaemia. Acute Megakaryoblastic Leukaemia (AMKL) in early childhood (<3 years), Acute Lymphoblastic Leukaemia (ALL) in older children.
- **Neurology**: Early-onset Alzheimer's disease (often in their 40s) because the Amyloid Precursor Protein (APP) gene is located on chromosome 21.
- **Prognosis**: Life expectancy has improved dramatically (now 50-60+ years) due to advances in paediatric cardiac surgery and proactive medical care.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "What is the most common congenital heart defect in Down syndrome?" (Atrioventricular Septal Defect - AVSD).
- *Viva*: "Why is it absolutely necessary to perform a karyotype if the clinical diagnosis of Down syndrome is obvious?" (To determine if it is caused by nondisjunction or a translocation. If it is a translocation, the parents must be tested, as the recurrence risk for future pregnancies can be extremely high).
- *Pitfall*: Forgetting to screen for hypothyroidism annually, attributing the child's increasing lethargy and weight gain simply to "having Down syndrome."
