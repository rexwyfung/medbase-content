# X-linked Agammaglobulinaemia (Bruton) (X連鎖無丙種球蛋白血症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A primary humoral (B-cell) immunodeficiency characterized by a near-total absence of B-cells and severe hypogammaglobulinemia.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- X-linked recessive mutation in the **BTK** (Bruton Tyrosine Kinase) gene.
- BTK is essential for B-cell development. Without it, maturation completely arrests at the pre-B cell stage in the bone marrow.
- Result: No mature B-cells in the blood = No plasma cells = No antibodies (IgG, IgA, IgM, IgE are all near zero).
- Because T-cells are perfectly normal, cellular immunity against most viruses and fungi is intact.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- A 9-month-old boy who was completely healthy for the first 6 months of life, but has now been admitted to the PICU three times for severe *Streptococcus pneumoniae* pneumonia and H. influenzae sepsis.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Timing**: Onset precisely after 6 months of age. (Before 6 months, the baby is protected by transplacental maternal IgG).
- **Infections**: Recurrent, severe, pyogenic (pus-forming) encapsulated bacterial infections of the respiratory, GI, and urinary tracts (Otitis media, sinusitis, pneumonia).
- **Physical Exam Hallmark**: **Absent or remarkably tiny tonsils and lymph nodes**. (Since B-cells normally make up the bulk of germinal centers in lymph nodes, their absence makes the nodes disappear).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from SCID: XLA patients easily fight off Thrush, PCP, and CMV because their T-cells function perfectly. Their problem is purely bacterial (lack of antibodies).

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Common Variable Immunodeficiency (CVID - usually much later onset, and has normal B-cell numbers).
- Transient Hypogammaglobulinemia of Infancy (THI - a temporary delay in baby's own IgG production; they have normal B-cells and recover by age 2-3).
- Selective IgA Deficiency.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Immunoglobulins**: Pan-hypogammaglobulinemia (IgG, IgA, IgM all profoundly low).
- **Vaccine Response**: Total failure to mount antibody titers to the Tetanus or Pneumococcal vaccines.
- **Definitive**: Flow cytometry showing **CD19+ / CD20+ B-cells at near 0%** (<1%). Normal T-cell (CD3+) count. Genetic testing for BTK mutation.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Replacement Therapy**: Lifelong prophylactic **Intravenous Immunoglobulin (IVIG)** or Subcutaneous IG (SCIG) every 3-4 weeks. This completely revolutionizes their life expectancy.
- **Infections**: Aggressive and early use of broad-spectrum antibiotics for any febrile illness.
- **Contraindications**: Avoid live oral polio vaccine (OPV) as it can cause vaccine-associated paralytic poliomyelitis (they cannot clear gut enteroviruses well).

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Coping with the burden of lifetime IVIG infusions.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- N/A.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Chronic lung disease / Bronchiectasis due to repeated pneumonias.
- Very susceptible to chronic severe Enterovirus infections (meningoencephalitis), as clearing enteroviruses heavily relies on antibodies.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Diagnosing XLA in a female. It is strictly X-linked. If a female infant has zero B-cells and no IgG, it is Autosomal Recessive Agammaglobulinemia (mutation in $\mu$-heavy chain), NOT Bruton's.
- **Viva Q**: "Why are the tonsils completely invisible in a boy with XLA?" Answer: Tonsils are secondary lymphoid organs where B-cells normally proliferate in germinal centers. Since XLA patients have a genetic arrest in B-cell development, no B-cells populate the tonsils, leaving them completely hypoplastic.
