# Transposition of the Great Arteries (大動脈轉位)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Definition of Transposition of the Great Arteries (TGA)
* **Definition**: Cyanotic congenital heart defect (CHD) characterized by ventriculoarterial discordance.
* **Anatomy**: Aorta (主動脈) arises from Right Ventricle (RV); Pulmonary Artery (肺動脈) arises from Left Ventricle (LV). 

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
* **Incidence**: Most common cause of cyanotic CHD presenting in the neonatal period in HK.
* **Detection**: >70% of severe lesions like TGA now detected on routine antenatal ultrasound screening at 18-20 weeks.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics

### Pathophysiology of Parallel Circulations
* **Systemic Circuit**: Deoxygenated blood returns to RA → RV → Aorta → Body.
* **Pulmonary Circuit**: Oxygenated blood returns to LA → LV → Pulmonary Artery → Lungs.
* **Mixing Dependency**: Survival is impossible without mixing between circuits. 
* **Mixing Sites**: Atrial Septal Defect (ASD), Ventricular Septal Defect (VSD), or Patent Ductus Arteriosus (PDA).

### Risk Factors and Genetics
* **Maternal Factors**: Maternal diabetes mellitus (糖尿病) ↑ risk.
* **Genetic Syndromes**: Less commonly associated with major chromosomal syndromes compared to other CHDs, but polygenic inheritance is likely.

## 3. Clinical Presentation

### Neonatal Presentation
* **Onset**: Profound cyanosis (發紺) within first few hours to days of life.
* **Trigger**: Rapid clinical deterioration coincides with physiological closure of the ductus arteriosus (動脈導管) at day 1-2 of life, leading to ↓ mixing.
* **Oxygen Response**: Cyanosis is unresponsive to supplemental O2 (Failed Hyperoxia Test).

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Key Clinical Symptoms
* **Cyanosis**: Central cyanosis (slate blue tongue/lips), often severe and life-threatening.
* **Respiration**: Tachypnoea (氣促) without significant respiratory distress ("comfortable tachypnoea").

### Key Clinical Signs
* **Heart Sounds**: Single loud S2 (aorta is anterior to pulmonary artery).
* **Murmur**: 
  * Often NO murmur if ventricular septum is intact (simple TGA).
  * Pansystolic murmur (全收縮期雜音) may be present if associated with a VSD.
  * Systolic ejection murmur possible from ↑ flow or outflow tract stenosis.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)

### Distinguishing TGA from Other Neonatal Cyanosis
| Condition | Onset | Murmur | Respiratory Distress | Hyperoxia Test Response |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **TGA (大動脈轉位)** | Day 1-2 | Usually absent (unless VSD) | Mild/Absent | -ve (Fails to ↑ PaO2) |
| **Tetralogy of Fallot (法洛氏四聯症)** | Weeks-Months | Harsh ejection systolic | Absent | -ve (Fails to ↑ PaO2) |
| **Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS)** | Immediate | Absent | Severe (grunting/retractions)| +ve (PaO2 ↑) |

## 6. Differential Diagnosis

### Differential Diagnosis for Neonatal Cyanosis
* **Cyanotic CHDs**: Tetralogy of Fallot (TOF), Tricuspid Atresia, Truncus Arteriosus, Total Anomalous Pulmonary Venous Return (TAPVR).
* **Pulmonary**: Persistent Pulmonary Hypertension of the Newborn (PPHN), Severe Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS), Meconium Aspiration Syndrome.
* **Infectious**: Severe neonatal sepsis (Group B Streptococcus).

## 7. Tiered Investigations

### Bedside Investigations
* **Hyperoxia Test**: PaO2 remains <15 kPa (113 mmHg) on 100% O2.
* **Pre- and Post-ductal SpO2**: May show reverse differential cyanosis (lower limbs pinker than upper limbs) if PDA is present and pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR) is high.
* **ECG**: Usually normal in the neonatal period; may show right ventricular hypertrophy later.

### Imaging & Gold Standard Investigations
* **CXR**: Classic "Egg on a string" appearance (narrow upper mediastinum due to anteroposterior relationship of great vessels; globular heart). ↑ pulmonary vascular markings.
* **Echocardiogram (Gold Standard)**: Confirms discordant ventriculoarterial connection, evaluates mixing sites (ASD/VSD/PDA), and assesses coronary artery anatomy.

## 8. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)

### Acute Medical Resuscitation (NICU)
* **Prostaglandin E1 (PGE1) Infusion**: (Alprostadil) **LIFESAVING**. Initiated at 5 ng/kg/min to keep ductus arteriosus patent for mixing. 
* **Monitoring**: Watch for PGE1 side effects → apnoea (窒息), hypotension, jitteriness.
* **Airway**: Intubation and mechanical ventilation frequently required due to PGE1-induced apnoea or severe hypoxia.

### Acute Interventional Management
* **Balloon Atrial Septostomy (Rashkind Procedure)**: 
  * **Indication**: Inadequate mixing/profound hypoxia despite PGE1. Required in ~20% of TGA cases.
  * **Procedure**: Performed urgently at bedside under echo guidance or in cath lab. Balloon catheter tears atrial septum to render foramen ovale incompetent → ↑ mixing.

### Definitive Surgical Management (Hong Kong Children's Hospital Protocol)
* **Arterial Switch Operation (Jatene Procedure)**:
  * **Timing**: Standard of care; performed within first 1-2 weeks of life before LV muscle mass ↓.
  * **Method**: Great arteries transected and switched; coronary arteries reimplanted into neo-aorta.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care

### Psychosocial Support
* **Medical Social Worker (MSW)**: Financial assistance and emotional support for parents of critically ill neonate in NICU.
* **Long-term Follow-up**: Multidisciplinary care via HKCH Paediatric Cardiology for neurodevelopmental monitoring, growth tracking, and ongoing cardiac surveillance.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags

### Safeguarding Considerations in CHD
* **Not directly applicable**: TGA is a congenital anatomical defect. However, delayed presentation due to medical neglect should prompt standard HA safeguarding protocols.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis

### Prognosis and Survival Rates
* **Without Surgery**: >90% mortality in the 1st year of life.
* **Post-Arterial Switch**: Excellent long-term survival rates (>90% reaching adulthood).

### Long-term Surgical Complications
* **Coronary Artery Stenosis**: Ischaemia/infarction due to reimplantation site scarring.
* **Vascular Complications**: Neo-aortic root dilation and regurgitation.
* **Outflow Tracts**: Right ventricular outflow tract (RVOT) obstruction (often at the pulmonary anastomosis site).

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Trap: Giving 100% oxygen and expecting the cyanosis to improve. 
* Pearl: TGA is a mixing lesion; oxygen will not significantly improve systemic PaO2 and may actually promote physiological ductal closure, rapidly worsening the condition and leading to collapse.
* Viva Question: What is the most common and dangerous side effect of Prostaglandin E1 infusion? 
* Answer: Apnoea. Always secure IV access, closely monitor respiratory effort, and have intubation equipment ready before starting PGE1.
* Viva Question: What is the classic chest X-ray finding in Transposition of the Great Arteries?
* Answer: "Egg on a string" appearance, caused by a narrow vascular pedicle (aorta and pulmonary artery lying anteroposteriorly) and a globular right ventricle.
* Pitfall: Assuming a loud murmur is required to diagnose severe congenital heart disease. The most severe cases of simple TGA often present with NO murmur because the ventricular septum is intact and the only mixing is via a PDA or PFO.
