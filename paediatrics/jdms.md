# Juvenile Dermatomyositis (幼年型皮肌炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition
- A rare, systemic autoimmune **vasculopathy/angiopathy** primarily resulting in inflammation, atrophy, and severe **symmetrical proximal muscle weakness (myopathy) (近端肌肉無力)** and distinctive skin rashes.
### Local Epidemiology
- **Most common inflammatory myopathy** in childhood.
- **Typical Onset**: 5–10 years of age.
- **Incidence**: ~3 in 1,000,000 children.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Autoimmune Angiopathy
- An autoimmune attack is directed against the **endothelium** (lining) of the tiny capillaries supplying muscle and skin tissues.
- Creates **ischemic injury** and **inflammation (microangiopathy)**.
### Histopathology
- **Muscle Biopsy** characteristically shows an **inflammatory cell infiltrate** and **muscle atrophy**.
### Calcinosis Pathogenesis
- The hallmark pediatric complication, **Calcinosis (鈣化)**, occurs when the ischemic, damaged muscle/skin tissue pathologically undergoes **dystrophic calcification** (turns to stone).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
### Classic Presentation
- An 8-year-old boy presents with a 3-month history of becoming **increasingly clumsy**, frequently falling, and now **unable to climb stairs** or **comb his hair** (indicating profound proximal weakness).
- He has a strange **purplish rash** over his eyes.
### Disease Onset
- Can present acutely, but is typically **insidious** with **fever**, general **misery**, and progressive weakness.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Muscle Symptoms
- **Symmetrical Proximal Muscle Weakness**: Profound weakness of the shoulder and hip girdles.
- **+ve Gower's Sign (高爾氏徵)**: The child has to use their hands to walk up their own legs to stand from the floor.
- **Pharyngeal Muscle Involvement**: Weakness affecting swallowing, leading to **dysphagia**.
- **Myalgia**: Muscle pain is a common presenting feature.
- **Arthritis**: Joint inflammation often accompanies the myopathy.
### Pathognomonic Skin Signs
- **Heliotrope Rash (向日葵疹)**: A **violaceous (pink-purple) discoloration** over the upper eyelids, often with intense **periorbital edema**.
- **Gottron Papules (高特隆氏丘疹)**: Symmetrical, scaly, erythematous plaques rigidly localized directly over the **extensor surfaces** of the **MCP and PIP joints** of the fingers. Can also affect extensor surfaces of elbows and knees.
- **Nailfold Capillary Changes**: Tiny tortuous, dilated blood vessels seen at the base of the fingernails (**periungual erythema**).
- **Calcinosis Cutis (皮膚鈣化)**: Hard, stone-like subcutaneous calcium nodules that can ulcerate and constantly leak a white, chalky fluid.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Differentiating Pediatric Myopathies

| Feature | Juvenile Dermatomyositis (JDMS) | Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy (DMD) | Benign Acute Post-Viral Myositis |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Aetiology** | Autoimmune angiopathy | Genetic (X-linked recessive) | Post-viral (e.g., Influenza B) |
| **Typical Onset** | 5–10 years (Both sexes) | 3–5 years (Males only) | Any age |
| **Primary Weakness** | Symmetrical proximal | Symmetrical proximal | Localized to calves |
| **Skin Manifestations**| Heliotrope rash, Gottron papules | None | None |
| **Clinical Course** | Insidious, progressive | Chronic, progressive | Acute, rapidly transient |

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
### Key Differentials
- **Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)**: Often has joint pain and a malar rash, but does not present with primary profound muscle weakness.
- **Muscular Dystrophies** (e.g., Duchenne, Becker).
- **Post-viral Myositis**.

## 7. Tiered Investigations
### Bedside & Blood Investigations
- **Muscle Enzymes**: **↑ Creatine Kinase (CK)**, **↑ Aldolase**, **↑ AST**, and **↑ LDH** are markedly elevated due to active muscle destruction.
- **Inflammatory Markers**: **↑ CRP** and **↑ ESR** are typically raised.
- **Autoantibodies**: **+ve ANA**, and **Myositis-specific antibodies** (e.g., **Anti-TIF1-gamma**, **Anti-NXP2** - which is closely linked to calcinosis risk).
### Imaging Studies
- **MRI Proximal Thighs (STIR sequence)**: Brilliantly shows diffuse **edema/inflammation** in actively dying muscles.
### Special Investigations
- **Muscle Biopsy**: Shows **inflammatory cell infiltrate** and **atrophy**.
- **EMG**: Rarely needed if typical rashes + MRI edema are clear.

## 8. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Acute Immunosuppression
- **High-Dose Corticosteroids (高劑量類固醇)**: The absolute mainstay to rapidly halt the destructive vasculopathy. Often started as **IV pulses**.
### Chronic Steroid-Sparing Agents
- **Methotrexate (甲氨蝶呤)** or **Cyclosporin (環孢素)**: Universally started early to allow steroid weaning and maintain long-term remission.
### Supportive Management
- **Sun Protection**: UV exposure triggers JDMS flares. Strict **SPF** and clothing protection are mandated.
- **Intensive Physiotherapy**: Essential to prevent rapidly forming **joint contractures**.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Multidisciplinary Team Involvement
- **Physiotherapist (PT)**: Critical for maintaining range of motion and preventing contractures.
- **Occupational Therapist (OT)**: Assists with adaptations for severe proximal weakness in ADLs.
- **Medical Social Worker (MSW)**: Support for long-term chronic disease burden and financial assistance.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
### Disease Complications
- **Calcinosis (鈣化)**: The most dreaded pediatric complication (occurs in up to 30%). It is incredibly painful, disables joint movement, and is notoriously difficult to reverse.
- **GI Vasculitis**: Severe ischemia can lead to fatal **bowel perforation**.
- **Joint Contractures**: Consequence of chronic inflammation and immobility.
### Prognosis
- **Mortality**: **5%–10%** in severe, refractory, or untreated cases.
- **Malignancy Risk**: Unlike adult Dermatomyositis, JDMS is **very rarely** associated with occult malignancy.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Trap: Confusing psoriasis on the hands for Gottron papules. Pearl: Psoriasis typically occurs between the joints on the skin of the fingers. Gottron papules are rigidly centered directly over the MCP and PIP joints.
* Viva Question: An 8-year-old girl is diagnosed with JDMS. Her mother nervously asks if she needs to be screened for cancer, as the mother’s adult friend with dermatomyositis had lung cancer. What do you advise? Answer: Reassure the mother. Adult Dermatomyositis carries a high paraneoplastic risk for occult malignancy, but Juvenile Dermatomyositis does NOT carry this risk.
* Pitfall: Missing pharyngeal muscle involvement in JDMS. Always ask about swallowing difficulties, as bulbar weakness can severely affect swallowing and lead to aspiration pneumonia.
