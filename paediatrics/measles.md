# Measles (Rubeola) (麻疹)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A highly contagious, acute viral respiratory illness caused by the Measles virus (a paramyxovirus). It is characterized by a prodrome of fever and the "3 Cs," followed by a classic maculopapular rash.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Endemic transmission was eliminated in Hong Kong due to high vaccination coverage (MMR vaccine in the HKCIP). However, imported cases and small outbreaks still occur, particularly among unvaccinated individuals or those with waning immunity.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Transmission**: Airborne spread via respiratory droplets. It is one of the most contagious infectious diseases known (R0 of 12-18). The virus can remain suspended in the air for up to 2 hours after an infected person has left the room.
- **Pathogenesis**: The virus infects the respiratory epithelium, spreads to regional lymph nodes, and causes a primary viraemia. A secondary viraemia disseminates the virus to the skin, conjunctivae, respiratory tract, and other organs. The rash is actually an immune response (T-cell mediated) to virus-infected endothelial cells in the skin.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- **Incubation Period**: 10-14 days.
- **Prodrome (The "3 Cs")**: Lasts 2-4 days. The child is miserable, with a high fever (up to 40°C), **C**ough, **C**oryza (runny nose), and **C**onjunctivitis (red, watery eyes).
- **Koplik Spots**: Pathognomonic enanthem that appears 1-2 days *before* the rash and fades as the rash appears.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Koplik Spots**: Tiny white/blue-white spots on an erythematous base on the buccal mucosa (inside the cheeks, opposite the molar teeth). Often described as looking like "grains of salt on a wet red background."
- **The Rash (Exanthem)**:
  - Appears 3-4 days after the onset of fever.
  - **Erythematous, maculopapular rash**.
  - *Classic progression*: Starts on the face (behind the ears, along the hairline), then spreads downwards to the trunk and finally the limbs (cephalocaudal spread).
  - The lesions often become confluent (merge together), especially on the face and trunk.
  - The rash fades in the same order it appeared, often leaving a brownish discoloration and fine desquamation (peeling).
- **Other Signs**: Generalized lymphadenopathy.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Rubella (German Measles)**: The rash is similar but the child is usually much less unwell, the fever is lower, and it is classically associated with prominent post-auricular and suboccipital lymphadenopathy. Koplik spots are absent.
- **Roseola Infantum (HHV-6)**: High fever for 3-4 days, and the rash appears *only after* the fever abruptly breaks. In measles, the fever peaks *with* the rash.
- **Kawasaki Disease**: Presents with fever, rash, and conjunctivitis, but the conjunctivitis is non-exudative (no pus/watery discharge), and it lacks the severe cough and coryza of measles.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Rubella.
- Roseola Infantum.
- Parvovirus B19 (Erythema Infectiosum / Slapped Cheek).
- Kawasaki Disease.
- Drug eruption.
- Scarlet Fever (Group A Strep).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Diagnosis is primarily clinical**, but laboratory confirmation is mandatory for public health tracking.
- **Serology**: Measles-specific IgM antibodies in the blood (may be negative in the first few days of the rash).
- **PCR**: Measles virus RNA PCR from a throat swab, nasopharyngeal aspirate (NPA), or urine. This is the most sensitive and specific test.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Notification**: Measles is a statutory notifiable disease in Hong Kong. The Centre for Health Protection (CHP) must be notified immediately.
- **Infection Control**: Strict airborne isolation (negative pressure room) if admitted to the hospital.
- **Supportive Care**: The mainstay of treatment. Antipyretics, hydration, and treatment of secondary bacterial infections (e.g., antibiotics for otitis media or pneumonia).
- **Vitamin A**: The WHO recommends high-dose Vitamin A supplementation for all children with severe measles. It significantly reduces morbidity and mortality (especially from blindness and pneumonia), as measles rapidly depletes Vitamin A stores.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Public Health (CHP)**: Contact tracing is crucial. Susceptible contacts may be offered the MMR vaccine (within 72 hours of exposure) or Human Normal Immunoglobulin (HNIG) (within 6 days) to prevent or attenuate the disease.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Measles causes a profound, transient immunosuppression that lasts for weeks to months, making the child highly susceptible to secondary infections.
- **Common Complications**: Otitis media (most common), secondary bacterial pneumonia (the most common cause of death).
- **Neurological Complications**:
  - *Acute Disseminated Encephalomyelitis (ADEM)*: An autoimmune demyelinating disease occurring 1-2 weeks after the rash.
  - *Subacute Sclerosing Panencephalitis (SSPE)*: A rare, fatal, progressive degenerative neurological disease that occurs 7-10 years *after* the initial measles infection, caused by a persistent mutant measles virus in the brain.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "Describe the classic progression of the measles rash." (It is an erythematous maculopapular rash that starts on the face, behind the ears, and along the hairline, then spreads cephalocaudally down the trunk to the limbs, often becoming confluent).
- *Viva*: "What are Koplik spots, and where do you look for them?" (Pathognomonic tiny white spots on a red base found on the buccal mucosa opposite the molar teeth. They appear 1-2 days before the rash).
- *Viva*: "What vitamin supplementation is recommended by the WHO for children with severe measles to reduce mortality?" (Vitamin A).
