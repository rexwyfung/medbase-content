# Measles (Rubeola) (麻疹)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition
* **Acute viral respiratory illness** caused by **Measles virus** (paramyxovirus)
* Characterized by:
  * **Prodrome**: Fever + "3 Cs"
  * **Pathognomonic enanthem**: **Koplik spots** (柯氏斑)
  * **Classic exanthem**: **Maculopapular rash** (斑丘疹)

### Local Epidemiology (Hong Kong Context)
* Endemic transmission eliminated in HK via **HKCIP** (**MMR vaccine** coverage)
* Outbreaks/imported cases occur in:
  * Unvaccinated individuals
  * Waning immunity cohorts
* Major cause of childhood mortality globally (especially in low-income regions with **Vitamin A deficiency**)

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics

### Transmission Dynamics
* **Route**: Airborne spread via respiratory droplets
* **Infectivity**: Extremely contagious (**R0 = 12–18**)
* **Environmental survival**: Suspended in air up to **2 hours** post-exposure
* **Infectious period**: From 4 days before rash → 4 days after rash onset

### Pathogenesis
* Virus infects respiratory epithelium → regional lymph nodes
* **Primary viraemia** → systemic spread
* **Secondary viraemia** → disseminates to skin, conjunctivae, respiratory tract
* **Rash mechanism**: T-cell mediated immune response to virus-infected endothelial cells in skin
* **Immunosuppression**: Causes profound, transient ↓ cellular immunity (weeks to months) → ↑ susceptibility to secondary bacterial infections

## 3. Clinical Presentation

### Incubation & Prodrome Phase
* **Incubation period**: **10–14 days**
* **Prodrome duration**: **2–4 days**
* **Presentation**: 
  * Severe malaise
  * High fever (↑ 40°C)
  * **Cough** (咳嗽)
  * **Coryza** (鼻水)
  * **Conjunctivitis** (結膜炎)

### Enanthem (Koplik Spots)
* Appear 1–2 days *before* exanthem
* Fade as skin rash appears
* **Location**: Buccal mucosa (opposite molar teeth)
* **Appearance**: Tiny white/blue-white spots on erythematous base ("grains of salt on wet red background")

### Exanthem (Maculopapular Rash)
* Appears **3–4 days** post-fever onset
* **Progression**:
  * Starts on face (behind ears, hairline)
  * Spreads cephalocaudally (downwards) → trunk → limbs
* **Morphology**:
  * Initially discrete erythematous maculopapules
  * Becomes blotchy & confluent (especially face/trunk)
* **Resolution**:
  * Fades in order of appearance
  * Leaves brownish discoloration
  * Fine desquamation (peeling) occurs in 2nd week

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Characteristic Symptoms
* ↑ Fever
* Barking cough
* Rhinorrhea (coryza)
* Photophobia
* Marked irritability & lethargy

### Cutaneous & Mucosal Signs
* **Koplik spots** (柯氏斑)
* Cephalocaudal **maculopapular rash** (斑丘疹)
* Red, watery eyes (exudative conjunctivitis)
* Generalized lymphadenopathy

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)

### Differentiating Measles from Rubella (German Measles) (德國麻疹)
* **Toxicity**: Measles patient is severely unwell; Rubella is mild
* **Fever**: Measles ↑ 40°C; Rubella low-grade
* **Lymphadenopathy**: Rubella has prominent post-auricular/suboccipital nodes
* **Mucosa**: Rubella lacks Koplik spots

### Differentiating Measles from Roseola Infantum (HHV-6) (玫瑰疹)
* **Fever pattern**: Roseola has ↑ fever for 3-4 days → abruptly breaks → *then* rash appears
* **Measles pattern**: Fever peaks concurrently *with* rash

### Differentiating Measles from Kawasaki Disease (川崎病)
* **Conjunctivitis**: Kawasaki is non-exudative (limbal sparing, no watery discharge)
* **Respiratory**: Kawasaki lacks severe cough/coryza
* **Mucosa**: Kawasaki has strawberry tongue & cracked lips; Measles has Koplik spots

## 6. Differential Diagnosis

### Differential Diagnosis Table
| Condition | Pathogen / Cause | Key Differentiating Features |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Measles** | Paramyxovirus | Koplik spots, 3 Cs, severe prodrome, confluent rash |
| **Rubella** | Togavirus | Mild illness, suboccipital nodes, discrete pink rash |
| **Roseola Infantum** | HHV-6 / HHV-7 | High fever abruptly resolves → sudden rash appearance |
| **Erythema Infectiosum** | Parvovirus B19 | "Slapped cheek" rash, reticular lacelike limb rash |
| **Scarlet Fever** | Group A *Strep* | Sandpaper rash, strawberry tongue, circumoral pallor |
| **Kawasaki Disease** | Idiopathic vasculitis | ≥5 days fever, extremity peeling, non-purulent conjunctivitis |
| **Drug Eruption** | Hypersensitivity | Temporal link to new medication, eosinophilia |

## 7. Tiered Investigations

### Bedside & Public Health Investigations
* **Clinical diagnosis**: Primarily recognized via classic Hx + exam
* **Notification**: Statutory notifiable disease to **Centre for Health Protection (CHP)** immediately

### Laboratory Serology & PCR
* **Gold Standard**: **Measles virus RNA PCR**
  * **Specimen**: Throat swab, nasopharyngeal aspirate (NPA), saliva, or urine
  * Highly sensitive & specific for confirmation
* **Serology**: 
  * **Measles IgM** (+ve in acute phase, but may be -ve in first few days of rash)
  * **Measles IgG** (4-fold rise in paired acute/convalescent sera)

## 8. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)

### Public Health & Infection Control
* **Isolation**: Strict **airborne precautions** (negative pressure room) for hospitalized cases
* **CHP Notification**: Mandatory public health reporting in HK to facilitate tracking

### Acute Supportive Management
* **Mainstay of treatment**: 
  * Antipyretics (Paracetamol / Ibuprofen)
  * Hydration (IV fluids if oral intake poor)
* **Secondary Infection Treatment**: 
  * Prompt antibiotics if suspected otitis media or pneumonia (most common causes of mortality/morbidity)

### Disease-Modifying Therapy
* **Vitamin A Supplementation**: 
  * Recommended by WHO for severe cases
  * Measles rapidly depletes Vit A stores
  * ↓ Morbidity and mortality (prevents blindness & pneumonia)
* **Antivirals**: 
  * **Ribavirin** (利巴韋林) considered ONLY for severely immunocompromised patients (e.g., severe pneumonitis)

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care

### Contact Tracing & Prophylaxis
* **CHP Role**: Active contact tracing of exposed individuals in schools/community
* **Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP)**:
  * **MMR Vaccine**: Give within **72 hours** of exposure to susceptible contacts
  * **Human Normal Immunoglobulin (HNIG)**: Give within **6 days** of exposure for high-risk individuals (pregnant, immunocompromised, infants <6 months)

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags

### Safeguarding Considerations
* Generally N/A for direct non-accidental injury (NAI)
* **Medical neglect**: Assess for neglect if there is a severely delayed presentation with life-threatening complications (e.g., profound respiratory distress)
* **Vaccine refusal**: Document parental refusal of mandatory HKCIP vaccines (MMR) and provide targeted education

## 11. Complications & Prognosis

### Respiratory Complications
* **Otitis Media**: Most common complication
* **Pneumonia**: 
  * Primary viral (giant-cell pneumonitis) OR secondary bacterial
  * **Most common cause of mortality**
* **Tracheitis / Croup**: Due to airway inflammation

### Neurological Complications
* **Febrile Seizures**: Common during acute febrile phase
* **Acute Disseminated Encephalomyelitis (ADEM)**:
  * Autoimmune demyelinating disease
  * Occurs **1–2 weeks** post-rash
  * Presentation: Headache, lethargy, seizures, coma (15% mortality)
* **Subacute Sclerosing Panencephalitis (SSPE)** (亞急性硬化性全腦炎):
  * Rare (1:100,000), fatal, progressive degenerative neurological disease
  * Occurs **7–10 years** post-infection
  * Caused by persistent mutant virus in CNS

### Other Systemic Complications & High-Risk Groups
* **GI**: Diarrhoea, appendicitis, hepatitis
* **Ocular**: Corneal ulceration (especially with Vit A deficiency)
* **Immunocompromised host**: High risk of fatal dissemination, modified/absent rash, severe giant-cell pneumonia

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Viva Question: Describe the classic progression of the measles rash? Answer: An erythematous maculopapular rash starting on the face, behind the ears, and hairline, spreading cephalocaudally down the trunk to the limbs, often becoming confluent.
* Viva Question: What are Koplik spots, and where do you look for them? Answer: Pathognomonic tiny white/blue-white spots on a red base found on the buccal mucosa opposite the molar teeth, appearing 1-2 days before the rash.
* Viva Question: What vitamin supplementation is recommended for children with severe measles to reduce mortality? Answer: Vitamin A.
* Viva Question: What is the most feared late neurological complication of measles occurring years after the initial infection? Answer: Subacute sclerosing panencephalitis (SSPE).
* Trap: Confusing the fever pattern of Roseola Infantum with Measles. Pearl: In Roseola, the fever abruptly drops BEFORE the rash appears. In Measles, the fever remains high and peaks concurrently WITH the rash.
* Pitfall: Reassuring a patient whose fever settles but returns a few days later during a measles infection. This secondary fever spike strongly indicates a secondary bacterial infection (e.g., pneumonia or otitis media) requiring prompt antibiotics and clinical reassessment.
