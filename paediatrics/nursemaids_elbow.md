# Nursemaid's Elbow (牽拉肘)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition
* **Radial head subluxation** (橈骨小頭半脫位)
* Extremely common pediatric orthopedic presentation involving displacement of the **annular ligament** (環狀韌帶)

### Local Epidemiology (Hong Kong)
* Peak incidence: 1-4 years old
* Gender: F > M
* Laterality: Left arm > Right arm (often due to right-handed caregivers holding the child's left hand)

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics

### Mechanism of Injury
* Sudden axial traction (軸向牽引) applied to an extended, pronated forearm

### Anatomical Pathophysiology
* **Annular ligament** (環狀韌帶) slips proximally over the radial head
* Ligament becomes entrapped within the **radiocapitellar joint** (橈肱關節)
* → Immediate mechanical block and pain

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Typical Clinical History
* Child pulled abruptly by the hand or wrist
* Sudden swinging or lifting by the arms

### Key Symptoms
* Immediate sharp pain
* Sudden onset of crying
* Refusal to use the affected arm → **Pseudoparalysis** (假性癱瘓)

### Classic Physical Signs
* Arm held close to torso
* Elbow held in slight flexion (屈曲)
* Forearm held in pronation (旋前)
* Mild point tenderness localized strictly over the **radial head** (橈骨小頭)
* Pain/resistance upon attempted supination (旋後)
* ABSENCE of swelling, erythema, warmth, or ecchymosis

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)

### Distinguishing from Fractures
* **Mechanism**: Traction (pulling) vs. blunt force/fall (fracture)
* **Visual Inspection**: Normal joint contour vs. visible deformity/swelling (fracture)
* **Palpation**: Point tenderness strictly at radial head vs. diffuse or supracondylar tenderness (fracture)

## 5. Differential Diagnosis

### Key Differential Diagnoses Table
| Condition | Mechanism | Key Discriminators |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Nursemaid's Elbow** (牽拉肘) | Axial traction | No swelling; flexed/pronated posture; rapid recovery post-reduction |
| **Supracondylar Fracture** (髁上骨折) | Fall on outstretched hand (FOOSH) | Significant swelling; +ve focal bony tenderness; visible deformity |
| **Clavicle Fracture** (鎖骨骨折) | Fall / direct trauma | Point tenderness over clavicle; asymmetric Moro reflex |
| **Septic Arthritis** (化膿性關節炎) | Hematogenous spread | Fever; joint effusion; severe pain on *any* micromovement |
| **Osteomyelitis** (骨髓炎) | Hematogenous spread | Fever; focal bony tenderness; localized warmth |

## 6. Tiered Investigations

### Gold Standard Clinical Diagnosis
* Strictly a **clinical diagnosis** (臨床診斷) if history and physical presentation are classic
* Imaging delays definitive treatment and causes unnecessary radiation

### Indications for Radiography (X光檢查指徵)
* Atypical mechanism (e.g., fall, direct trauma)
* Presence of focal swelling, ecchymosis, or obvious deformity
* Failure of clinical reduction after 2-3 appropriate attempts
* Suspicion of non-accidental injury (NAI)

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)

### 1st Line Management: Hyperpronation Method (過度旋前法)
* Higher first-attempt success rate & statistically less painful than supination method
* Support child's elbow with one hand, placing thumb firmly over the **radial head**
* Grasp child's hand/wrist with your other hand
* Firmly and swiftly hyperpronate the forearm
* Feel for a palpable "click" (彈響) under the thumb

### 2nd Line Management: Supination-Flexion Method (旋後屈曲法)
* Support elbow with thumb over **radial head**
* Apply gentle traction and firmly supinate (旋後) the forearm
* Fully flex (屈曲) the elbow in one continuous motion
* Feel for palpable "click"

### Post-Reduction Assessment
* Leave child alone for 10-15 minutes
* Reassess for spontaneous use of arm (e.g., reaching for a toy/sticker)
* If successful → discharge with verbal/written advice
* Immobilization/casting is NOT required

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care

### Parental Education & Reassurance
* Reassure caregivers that no permanent structural damage occurred
* Educate on proper lifting techniques: lift child by the axillae (腋窩) or torso, never by the distal hands/wrists
* Warn about the risk of recurrence

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags

### Non-Accidental Injury (NAI) Screening
* Mechanism thoroughly inconsistent with developmental age (e.g., occurs in a non-mobile infant)
* Delayed presentation > 24 hours without logical explanation
* Unexplained bruising or associated injuries
* Changing or inconsistent stories from caregivers

## 10. Complications & Prognosis

### Clinical Prognosis
* Excellent long-term prognosis
* Full function returns immediately or within 30 minutes after successful reduction

### Recurrence Risk
* ↑ Recurrence rate (~20-30%) until age 5
* After age 5, the **annular ligament** thickens and attaches more firmly, virtually eliminating the condition

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: What is the most successful reduction technique for Nursemaid's elbow? Answer: The hyperpronation method, which has a higher first-attempt success rate and is less painful than the traditional supination-flexion method.
* Trap: Ordering an X-ray for every pediatric patient presenting with elbow pain and pseudoparalysis. Pearl: If the history and physical exam are completely classic for Nursemaid's elbow, proceed directly to reduction. X-rays are only indicated if there is swelling, deformity, atypical mechanism, or failed reduction.
* Pitfall: Attempting closed reduction on an elbow with obvious swelling or ecchymosis. This may disastrously exacerbate an underlying supracondylar fracture or radial neck fracture.
