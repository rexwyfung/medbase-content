# Retinoblastoma (視網膜母細胞瘤)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: The most common primary malignant intraocular tumor of childhood, arising from the immature retina.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Driven by loss-of-function mutations (the "two-hit hypothesis") in the **RB1 tumor suppressor gene** on Chromosome 13q14.
- **Sporadic (Non-Hereditary) (60%)**: A child is born with two normal RB1 alleles. A single retinal cell happens to acquire mutations in *both* alleles over time. Result: Unilateral tumor, no family history, no increased risk of other cancers.
- **Hereditary (40%)**: A child inherits one mutated RB1 allele in every cell in their body (Germline mutation). A second mutation in any retinal cell triggers cancer. Result: Often Bilateral, family history present, and massive lifelong risk of secondary cancers.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- A 1-year-old child's parents notice that their eye appears to have a "cat's eye" reflection in dim light and in flash photography.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Leukocoria (White Pupil)**: The absolute classic and most common presentation (>60%). The tumor replaces the normal red retina, replacing the red reflex with a white/yellow reflection.
- **Strabismus (Squint)**: The second most common sign (20%). Occurs because the tumor destroys macular vision, causing the eye to wander.
- **Vision Loss**: Often goes unnoticed in infants.
- **Advanced Disease**: Orbital inflammation, proptosis, or glaucoma (eye bulging).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from congenital cataracts: Cataracts also cause leukocoria, but are usually present immediately at birth and the lens itself is physically opaque upon close inspection. Retinoblastoma sits behind the lens.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Congenital Cataract
- Coats Disease (Severe retinal vascular disease causing exudants that mimic leukocoria).
- ROP (Retinopathy of Prematurity) - can cause scarring/detachment.
- Toxocariasis (Parasitic eye infection causing a white granuloma).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**: Red Reflex test using an ophthalmoscope in a darkened room (Must be done at all routine well-child checks!).
- **Gold Standard**: Examination Under Anesthesia (EUA) by a pediatric ophthalmologist.
- **Imaging**: Dedicated MRI Orbit/Brain to assess extent, optic nerve invasion, and check for "Trilateral Retinoblastoma" (a concurrent tumor in the pineal gland in the brain).
- **Genetic Testing**: CRITICAL. Blood drawn to check for germline RB1 mutations to determine if the child and relatives have the hereditary form.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Goal**: 1. Save the life. 2. Save the eye. 3. Save the vision.
- **Small/Early Tumors**: Local ablative therapies (Laser photocoagulation or Cryotherapy).
- **Moderate/Large Tumors**: Systemic chemotherapy or **Intra-arterial Chemotherapy** (threading a micro-catheter directly into the ophthalmic artery to deliver concentrated chemo directly into the eye, avoiding systemic toxicity).
- **Massive/Advanced Tumors**: Enucleation (surgical removal of the entire eye and the optic nerve). Mandatory if there is no hope of useful vision or if the tumor spreads into the optic nerve.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Genetic counseling is totally mandatory for the parents to predict the risk for future pregnancies.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- N/A.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Excellent survival (>95%) if contained within the eye. Fatal if it tracks down the optic nerve into the brain.
- **Secondary Cancers**: Children with the Hereditary form (germline RB1 mutation) have a severely high lifetime risk of developing **Osteosarcoma**, soft tissue sarcomas, and melanomas in their teens/20s.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Diagnosing a strabismus (squint) in a toddler, referring them for eye patching, and failing to perform a direct fundoscopy/red-reflex. Strabismus is the 2nd most common presentation of retinoblastoma.
- **Viva Q**: "A 16-year-old who lost his right eye to cancer as a baby now presents with a destructive bone tumor in his distal femur. What is the genetic link?" Answer: Germline mutation in the RB1 tumor suppressor gene, which causes hereditary retinoblastoma in infancy and classically dramatically increases the risk of osteosarcoma in adolescence.
