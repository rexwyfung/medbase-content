# Spina Bifida (Neural Tube Defects) (脊柱裂)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A spectrum of congenital neural tube defects (NTDs) caused by the failure of the embryonic neural tube to close completely during the first month of pregnancy (days 21-28).
- **Local Epidemiology**: Incidence has decreased significantly globally and in Hong Kong due to routine maternal folic acid supplementation and antenatal ultrasound screening (allowing for termination of pregnancy).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Pathophysiology**: The neural tube forms the brain and spinal cord. Failure of the caudal end to close leaves the spinal cord and meninges exposed. The exposed neural tissue is progressively damaged by the amniotic fluid during pregnancy, leading to neurological deficits below the level of the lesion.
- **Aetiology**: Multifactorial.
  - *Nutritional*: **Folic acid (Vitamin B9) deficiency** is the most significant preventable risk factor.
  - *Medications*: Maternal use of antiepileptics (e.g., Sodium Valproate, Carbamazepine) during the first trimester.
  - *Maternal conditions*: Pre-gestational diabetes, obesity.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Usually diagnosed antenatally via routine anomaly ultrasound scan at 18-20 weeks gestation, or by elevated maternal serum alpha-fetoprotein (AFP).
- If undiagnosed, presents at birth with a visible lesion on the back.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs (Types of Spina Bifida)
1. **Spina Bifida Occulta (Mildest)**:
   - Failure of the vertebral arches to fuse, but the spinal cord and meninges are normal.
   - Usually asymptomatic and an incidental finding on X-ray.
   - *Cutaneous markers*: May have a tuft of hair, a dimple, a lipoma, or a birthmark over the lower spine.
2. **Meningocele**:
   - A sac of meninges and cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) protrudes through the vertebral defect.
   - The spinal cord remains in its normal position within the canal.
   - Neurological deficits are usually minimal or absent.
3. **Myelomeningocele (Most severe and most common clinical form)**:
   - The spinal cord and nerve roots are herniated into the protruding sac, exposing the raw neural placode.
   - **Neurological Deficits**: Flaccid paralysis, muscle atrophy, and absent reflexes below the level of the lesion (usually lumbosacral).
   - **Sensory Loss**: Complete loss of sensation below the lesion.
   - **Sphincter Dysfunction**: Neurogenic bladder (incontinence, urinary retention) and bowel incontinence are almost universal.
   - **Orthopaedic Deformities**: Clubfoot (talipes equinovarus), hip dislocation, scoliosis (due to muscle imbalance).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Sacrococcygeal Teratoma**: A large tumour at the base of the spine, but it does not typically cause the profound lower limb paralysis and neurogenic bladder seen in myelomeningocele.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Sacrococcygeal teratoma.
- Spinal muscular atrophy (causes paralysis, but no back lesion).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Antenatal**: Maternal serum AFP (elevated), detailed fetal ultrasound, amniocentesis.
- **Postnatal**:
  - **MRI Spine and Brain**: To define the exact anatomy of the spinal lesion and to detect associated brain anomalies (e.g., Chiari II malformation, hydrocephalus).
  - **Renal Ultrasound**: Essential to assess for hydronephrosis caused by the neurogenic bladder.
  - **Urodynamic Studies**: To assess bladder function and pressures.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute Neonatal Management**:
  - The exposed placode must be covered with sterile, saline-soaked non-adherent dressings to prevent infection (meningitis) and drying.
  - The infant must be nursed prone.
  - **Surgical Closure**: Urgent neurosurgical closure of the defect within 24-48 hours of birth.
- **Chronic Management (The MDT Approach)**:
  - **Neurosurgery**: Management of hydrocephalus (VP shunt).
  - **Urology**: Crucial to preserve renal function. Clean Intermittent Catheterization (CIC) is often required to empty the neurogenic bladder and prevent reflux/UTIs. Prophylactic antibiotics.
  - **Orthopaedics**: Correction of clubfoot, hip dislocations, and scoliosis to maximize mobility.
  - **Physiotherapy**: Wheelchair provision, standing frames, gait training.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Prevention**: The most important aspect. All women planning pregnancy should take **400 mcg of Folic Acid daily** starting at least 1 month before conception. Women at high risk (e.g., previous child with NTD, taking valproate) require a high dose (**5 mg daily**).

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- N/A for this condition.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Hydrocephalus**: Develops in >80% of children with myelomeningocele due to the associated **Chiari II Malformation** (downward herniation of the cerebellar vermis and brainstem through the foramen magnum, blocking CSF flow). Requires a Ventriculoperitoneal (VP) shunt.
- **Latex Allergy**: Extremely common in these children due to frequent exposure during surgeries and catheterizations. Must use latex-free equipment.
- **Renal Failure**: The leading cause of death in adulthood if the neurogenic bladder is not managed properly.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "What is the most important preventable risk factor for neural tube defects, and what is the recommended prophylactic dose for a low-risk woman?" (Folic acid deficiency. The dose is 400 micrograms daily, starting before conception).
- *Viva*: "A newborn with a myelomeningocele has a rapidly increasing head circumference. What is the most likely cause, and what is the underlying anatomical defect?" (Hydrocephalus. It is caused by the associated Chiari II malformation blocking the flow of CSF).
- *Pitfall*: Forgetting to ask about maternal anti-epileptic drug use (especially Sodium Valproate) when taking a history for a child with a neural tube defect.
