# Childhood Obesity (兒童肥胖症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Excess body fat that negatively impacts a child's health. Defined objectively using Body Mass Index (BMI) plotted on age- and sex-specific growth charts.
  - *Overweight*: BMI > 85th centile.
  - *Obese*: BMI > 95th centile.
  - *Severe Obesity*: BMI > 99th centile.
- **Local Epidemiology**: A major public health crisis in Hong Kong. Approximately 1 in 5 primary school children in HK are overweight or obese. Driven by a highly sedentary lifestyle (heavy academic burden, screen time) and easy access to calorie-dense, nutrient-poor food.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Exogenous (Primary) Obesity**: Accounts for >95% of cases. Caused by a chronic imbalance between energy intake (excessive calories) and energy expenditure (sedentary lifestyle).
- **Endogenous (Secondary) Obesity**: Rare (<5%). Caused by underlying endocrine or genetic disorders.
- **Genetics**: Polygenic inheritance plays a strong role in determining susceptibility to an obesogenic environment. If both parents are obese, the child has an 80% chance of being obese.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Often identified during routine school health checks (Student Health Service) or when parents seek help for the child's weight, snoring, or joint pain.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Physical Examination**:
  - **Height**: Children with exogenous (nutritional) obesity are typically **TALL** for their age (accelerated bone maturation). *Crucial point: A child who is obese and SHORT must be investigated for an endocrine/genetic cause.*
  - **Acanthosis Nigricans**: Dark, velvety hyperpigmentation in the skin folds (neck, axillae). This is a clinical marker of **Insulin Resistance**.
  - **Striae**: Pink or purple stretch marks on the abdomen/thighs.
  - **Adipomastia**: Excess fat in the pectoral region (often mistaken for true gynaecomastia in boys).
  - **Blood Pressure**: Must be measured using an appropriately sized cuff, as hypertension is a common comorbidity.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Exogenous vs. Endogenous Obesity**:
  - *Exogenous (95%)*: Tall stature, normal intelligence, normal physical exam (other than fat mass).
  - *Endogenous (5%)*: Short stature, dysmorphic features, developmental delay, hypogonadism.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis (Causes of Endogenous Obesity)
- **Endocrine**: Hypothyroidism (causes weight gain, but rarely massive obesity), Cushing's Syndrome (central obesity, moon face, buffalo hump, growth arrest).
- **Genetic Syndromes**:
  - **Prader-Willi Syndrome**: Severe neonatal hypotonia, followed by insatiable appetite (hyperphagia) in early childhood, leading to massive obesity. Short stature, hypogonadism, intellectual disability.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **No investigations are needed to diagnose obesity (BMI is sufficient).**
- Investigations are aimed at detecting **Comorbidities (Metabolic Syndrome)**:
  - Fasting Lipid Profile (Dyslipidaemia).
  - Fasting Glucose and HbA1c (Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus).
  - Liver Function Tests (ALT is often elevated due to Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease - NAFLD).
- **Investigations for Endogenous Causes (Only if indicated by short stature or dysmorphism)**:
  - Thyroid Function Tests, overnight Dexamethasone suppression test (for Cushing's), genetic testing (for Prader-Willi).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Family-Based Lifestyle Modification (The Cornerstone)**:
  - Weight loss is rarely the goal in growing children; the goal is **weight maintenance** while the child grows taller, thereby reducing their BMI centile.
  - *Diet*: Eliminate sugary drinks (soda, juice), reduce fast food, increase vegetables and water intake. Portion control.
  - *Activity*: Minimum of 60 minutes of moderate-to-vigorous physical activity daily.
  - *Screen Time*: Strictly limit recreational screen time to < 2 hours per day.
  - The entire family must adopt these changes; it will fail if only the child is targeted.
- **Pharmacotherapy**: Rarely used in paediatrics. Orlistat or GLP-1 agonists (e.g., Liraglutide) are only considered in older adolescents with severe comorbidities who have failed lifestyle interventions.
- **Bariatric Surgery**: Reserved for extreme, life-threatening obesity in post-pubertal adolescents who have failed all other measures.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Dietitian**: Essential for practical, culturally appropriate dietary advice.
- **Psychology**: Obese children suffer from severe bullying, low self-esteem, and depression. Psychological support is crucial for successful lifestyle modification.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Extreme, life-threatening obesity where parents consistently refuse to engage with medical advice or dietitians can, in rare cases, be considered medical neglect.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Metabolic**: Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus (increasingly common in HK children), Dyslipidaemia, Hypertension, NAFLD.
- **Mechanical**: Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA - snoring, daytime somnolence), Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE), Blount disease (bowing of the tibia).
- **Psychosocial**: Depression, eating disorders, social isolation.
- **Prognosis**: Childhood obesity strongly tracks into adulthood, bringing a high risk of premature cardiovascular disease and mortality.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "You are examining an obese 8-year-old boy. You notice dark, velvety thickening of the skin on the back of his neck. What is this sign called, and what underlying metabolic abnormality does it indicate?" (Acanthosis Nigricans. It is a clinical marker of severe insulin resistance).
- *Viva*: "What is the most important physical measurement to help differentiate between simple exogenous obesity and a pathological endocrine cause like Cushing's syndrome?" (Height. Children with simple exogenous obesity are usually tall for their age. Children with endocrine causes of obesity are characteristically short or have arrested growth).
- *Pitfall*: Focusing solely on the child's diet and ignoring the family environment. Interventions that do not involve the parents changing the household food environment and their own habits are doomed to fail.
