
# Neurofibromatosis Type 1 (第一型神經纖維瘤病)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A multisystem neurocutaneous disorder (phakomatosis) characterised by changes in skin pigmentation and the growth of tumours along nerves.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Incidence is ~1 in 3,000. Followed up in specialised neurogenetics or general paediatric clinics in HA hospitals.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Genetics**: Autosomal Dominant. Mutation in the *NF1* gene on Chromosome 17.
- **Mechanism**: The *NF1* gene encodes neurofibromin, a tumour suppressor protein that negatively regulates the RAS pathway. Loss of function leads to uncontrolled cell proliferation (tumour formation).
- ~50% are *de novo* (new) mutations.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- See below.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Diagnostic Criteria (NIH)**: Requires 2 or more of the following:
  1. **Café-au-lait macules**: ≥6 spots (>5mm in prepubertal, >15mm in postpubertal).
  2. **Neurofibromas**: ≥2 of any type, or 1 plexiform neurofibroma (feels like a "bag of worms").
  3. **Axillary or inguinal freckling** (Crowe sign).
  4. **Optic pathway glioma** (OPG).
  5. **Lisch nodules**: ≥2 (iris hamartomas seen on slit-lamp exam).
  6. **Distinctive osseous lesion**: Sphenoid dysplasia or tibial pseudoarthrosis (anterolateral bowing of tibia).
  7. **First-degree relative** with NF1.
## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Legius Syndrome**: Has café-au-lait spots and freckling, but NO neurofibromas, Lisch nodules, or CNS tumours (SPRED1 gene mutation).
- **McCune-Albright Syndrome**: Large, irregular café-au-lait spots ("coast of Maine"), polyostotic fibrous dysplasia, precocious puberty.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Most Likely**:
  - NF1 (if criteria met).
- **Must-Not-Miss**:
  - Optic pathway glioma causing visual loss.
  - Malignant Peripheral Nerve Sheath Tumour (MPNST) arising from a plexiform neurofibroma.
  - Phaeochromocytoma (causing severe hypertension).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**:
  - **Clinical Diagnosis**: Based on NIH criteria.
  - **Ophthalmology review**: Slit-lamp for Lisch nodules, visual field/acuity testing for OPG.
  - **BP measurement**: Annually to screen for renal artery stenosis or phaeochromocytoma.
- **Gold Standard**:
  - **Genetic Testing**: *NF1* gene sequencing confirms the diagnosis, useful for genetic counselling.
  - **MRI Brain/Orbits**: Indicated if there are visual symptoms, precocious puberty, or neurological signs to look for Optic Pathway Glioma or other CNS tumours.
- **Monitoring**:
  - Annual multidisciplinary review (growth, BP, vision, development, spine for scoliosis).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**:
  - Not typically required unless presenting with acute complication (e.g., hypertensive crisis from phaeochromocytoma).
- **Chronic/Definitive**:
  - **No cure**. Management is surveillance and symptomatic.
  - **Surgery**: Excision of symptomatic or cosmetically unacceptable neurofibromas. Orthopaedic surgery for severe scoliosis or tibial pseudoarthrosis.
  - **MEK Inhibitors (e.g., Selumetinib)**: New targeted therapy showing promise for shrinking inoperable plexiform neurofibromas (availability in HK HA system may be subject to specific funding/named patient basis).
## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- High incidence of learning disabilities (up to 50%) and ADHD. Early educational assessment and support are crucial.
## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Genetic counselling for parents. Explain the 50% risk of transmission to offspring.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Neurological**: Epilepsy, learning disabilities.
- **Oncological**: Increased risk of MPNST, leukaemia, breast cancer.
- **Orthopaedic**: Scoliosis, pseudoarthrosis.
- **Endocrine**: Precocious puberty (often secondary to OPG involving the chiasm/hypothalamus).

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Assuming all café-au-lait spots are NF1. *Pearl*: 1-2 spots are common in the general population. You need 6 or more of the correct size.
- **OSCE Pearl**: If asked to examine a child with NF1, explicitly state you would check their blood pressure (for renal artery stenosis/phaeochromocytoma) and examine their spine (for scoliosis).
