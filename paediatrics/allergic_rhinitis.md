# Allergic Rhinitis & Conjunctivitis (過敏性鼻炎及結膜炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: IgE-mediated inflammation of the nasal and conjunctival mucosa triggered by aeroallergens.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Up to 40% of children in Hong Kong suffer from this, often heavily triggered by house dust mites (HDM) and the intense humidity/pollution. A key component of the "Atopic March".

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Inhaled allergens cross the nasal epithelium and bind to IgE on mast cells.
- Immediate phase (minutes): Histamine release causing sneezing, itching, and rhinorrhea.
- Late phase (hours): Eosinophil and T-cell infiltration causing chronic nasal congestion and mucosal hypertrophy.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- A school-aged child who constantly clears their throat, snores loudly, sleeps poorly, and rubs their nose upward repeatedly.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Nasal**: Paroxysmal sneezing, profuse clear rhinorrhea, severe nasal congestion/blockage.
- **Ocular**: Bilateral intensely itchy, red, watery eyes.
- **Classic Signs**: 
  - **"Allergic Salute"**: An upward rubbing of the nose leading to a transverse nasal crease.
  - **"Allergic Shiners"**: Dark venous pooling under the eyes due to chronic nasal congestion.
  - **Dennie-Morgan Lines**: Extra prominent skin folds under the lower eyelids.
  - **Pale, boggy (swollen) nasal turbinates** on otoscopy.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from Viral URI (common cold): Viral cold lasts 7-10 days and secretions turn purulent/thick. Allergic rhinitis lasts weeks/months and secretions remain clear and watery, with prominent itching.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Viral Upper Respiratory Tract Infection
- Adenoidal Hypertrophy (causes snoring/blockage but usually no itching/sneezing)
- Nasal Foreign Body (foul-smelling, UNILATERAL purulent discharge)
- Sinusitis

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- Usually a clinical diagnosis.
- Specific IgE (blood test) or Skin Prick Test to identify the specific aeroallergen (e.g., HDM, pet dander, pollen).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Environmental Control**: Allergen avoidance. Anti-mite mattress covers, frequent washing of bedding in hot water, HEPA filters. Keep humidity <50% to kill mites.
- **Medical Treatment - Stepwise**:
  - **Step 1 (Mild/Intermittent)**: 2nd generation oral antihistamines (e.g., Cetirizine, Loratadine) - Non-sedating.
  - **Step 2 (Moderate/Persistent)**: **Intra-nasal Corticosteroids (INCS)** (e.g., Fluticasone, Mometasone). **This is the gold standard and most effective treatment.**
  - **Ocular**: Topical olopatadine or ketotifen eye drops.
  - **Severe**: Allergen Immunotherapy (Sublingual or Subcutaneous) to induce tolerance.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Chronic sleep deprivation leads to irritability, ADHD-like symptoms, and significantly poor school performance. Reversing the blockage transforms the child's academic trajectory.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- N/A.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Chronic rhinosinusitis.
- Eustachian tube dysfunction leading to recurrent Otitis Media with Effusion (Glue Ear) and hearing loss.
- Exacerbation of concurrent asthma.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Giving a 1st generation antihistamine (like Chlorpheniramine) for school-going children. It crosses the blood-brain barrier, causes severe sedation, and destroys their ability to learn. Always use 2nd generation.
- **Viva Q**: "A child on an intra-nasal steroid spray complains of frequent epistaxis (nosebleeds). What is the likely cause?" Answer: Poor spray technique. Spraying directly onto the highly vascular nasal septum causes thinning and bleeding. They must angle the nozzle laterally (outward towards the ear) while spraying.
