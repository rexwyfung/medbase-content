# Chickenpox (Varicella) (水痘)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A highly contagious primary infection caused by the Varicella-Zoster Virus (VZV), a DNA herpesvirus.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Historically a universal childhood illness in Hong Kong. Incidence has decreased significantly since the introduction of the universal Varicella vaccine into the Hong Kong Childhood Immunisation Programme (HKCIP) in 2014.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Transmission**: Airborne spread (respiratory droplets) and direct contact with vesicular fluid. Extremely contagious (attack rate >90% in susceptible household contacts).
- **Incubation Period**: 10 to 21 days (usually 14-16 days).
- **Infectivity**: From 48 hours *before* the rash appears until *all* lesions have crusted over (usually 5-7 days after rash onset).
- **Pathogenesis**: Virus enters via respiratory mucosa, replicates in regional lymph nodes, causes a primary viraemia, then a secondary viraemia leading to the widespread cutaneous rash. The virus then establishes lifelong latency in the dorsal root ganglia (can reactivate later in life as Herpes Zoster / Shingles).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- **Prodrome**: Mild fever, malaise, anorexia, and headache for 1-2 days before the rash (more prominent in older children/adults).
- **The Rash**: The hallmark feature. It is intensely pruritic (itchy) and evolves rapidly.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Evolution of Lesions**: Macules -> Papules -> Vesicles ("dewdrop on a rose petal") -> Pustules -> Crusted Scabs.
- **Distribution**: Centripetal. Starts on the head/face and trunk, then spreads to the limbs. Lesions can also occur on mucous membranes (mouth, conjunctiva, vagina).
- **"Crops"**: The classic sign is finding lesions in **all stages of development simultaneously** in the same area of skin (i.e., new vesicles appearing next to old crusted scabs).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (HFMD)**: Lesions are primarily on the palms, soles, and oral mucosa. Chickenpox rarely affects palms/soles and is concentrated on the trunk.
- **Smallpox (Eradicated)**: Lesions are all at the *same* stage of development and are centrifugally distributed (worse on limbs/face).

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (Coxsackievirus A16, EV71).
- Impetigo (Staphylococcus aureus / Streptococcus pyogenes).
- Disseminated Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV).
- Insect bites (papular urticaria).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Diagnosis is almost entirely clinical**.
- **VZV PCR**: Can be performed on vesicular fluid if the diagnosis is in doubt, or in immunocompromised patients presenting with atypical rashes.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Healthy Children**:
  - **Supportive Care**: The mainstay. Calamine lotion, oral antihistamines (Chlorphenamine) for severe itch to prevent scratching and secondary infection. Paracetamol for fever.
  - *Crucial*: **AVOID NSAIDs (Ibuprofen)**. NSAID use during chickenpox is strongly associated with an increased risk of severe necrotizing fasciitis caused by Group A Streptococcus.
  - *Crucial*: **AVOID Aspirin**. Risk of Reye syndrome (acute encephalopathy and liver failure).
- **High-Risk Groups (Immunocompromised, Neonates, Pregnant Women, Adolescents/Adults)**:
  - **Antiviral Therapy**: Oral or IV **Aciclovir**. Must be started within 24-48 hours of rash onset to be effective.
- **Post-Exposure Prophylaxis**:
  - *Varicella Zoster Immunoglobulin (VZIG)*: Given to high-risk susceptible contacts (e.g., pregnant women, immunocompromised children, neonates whose mothers develop chickenpox 5 days before to 2 days after delivery) within 10 days of exposure.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Infection Control**: Strict airborne and contact isolation if admitted to the hospital.
- **School Exclusion**: The child must be excluded from school/nursery until **all lesions have crusted over** (usually about 1 week).

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- N/A for this condition.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Secondary Bacterial Infection**: The most common complication. Scratching leads to infection with Staph aureus or Group A Strep (impetigo, cellulitis, or life-threatening necrotizing fasciitis).
- **Neurological**: Cerebellar ataxia (usually benign and self-limiting within weeks), Encephalitis (rare but severe).
- **Respiratory**: Varicella pneumonia (rare in healthy children, but a major cause of mortality in adults and pregnant women).
- **Prognosis**: Excellent in healthy children.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "What is the classic description of the chickenpox rash that helps differentiate it from other viral exanthems?" (The presence of lesions in all stages of development simultaneously - macules, vesicles, and crusts - appearing in 'crops').
- *Viva*: "Why is Ibuprofen contraindicated for fever control in a child with chickenpox?" (It significantly increases the risk of severe secondary bacterial skin infections, particularly necrotizing fasciitis caused by Group A Streptococcus).
- *Pitfall*: Allowing a child to return to school when they feel well but still have fluid-filled vesicles. They remain highly infectious until *every single lesion* is crusted.
