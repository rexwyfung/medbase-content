# Chickenpox (Varicella) (水痘)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition
- **Highly contagious primary infection** caused by **Varicella-Zoster Virus (VZV) (水痘帶狀皰疹病毒)**, a DNA herpesvirus.
### Local Epidemiology
- Historically a universal childhood illness.
- Incidence ↓↓ significantly since integration of universal **Varicella vaccine (水痘疫苗)** into **HKCIP (香港兒童免疫接種計劃)** in 2014.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Transmission Dynamics
- **Route**: Airborne (respiratory droplets) & direct contact with vesicular fluid.
- **Infectivity Rate**: Extremely contagious (attack rate >90% in susceptible household contacts).
- **Infectivity Period**: From 48 hours prior to rash onset → until **all** lesions have crusted over (usually 5-7 days post-rash).
### Viral Pathogenesis
- **Incubation Period**: 10-21 days (average 14-16 days).
- **Pathogenesis**: Virus enters via respiratory mucosa → regional lymph node replication → 1° viraemia → reticuloendothelial system → 2° viraemia → widespread cutaneous rash.
- **Latency**: Virus establishes lifelong latency in **dorsal root ganglia (背根神經節)** → potential reactivation in later life as **Herpes Zoster / Shingles (帶狀皰疹/生蛇)**.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Prodromal Symptoms
- 1-2 days prior to rash (more prominent in older children/adults).
- Mild fever, malaise, anorexia, headache.
### Dermatological Signs
- **Intensely pruritic (痕癢)**, rapidly evolving rash.
- **Centripetal Distribution**: Starts on head/face & trunk → spreads to limbs. Can involve mucous membranes (mouth, conjunctiva, vagina).
- **Evolution of Lesions**: Macules → Papules → Vesicles (**"dewdrop on a rose petal"**) → Pustules → Crusted Scabs.
- **"Crops" Phenomenon**: Pathognomonic sign is finding lesions in **all stages of development simultaneously** in the same skin area.
- **Quantity**: Typically 50-500 lesions.

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Differentiating from Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease
- **HFMD (手足口病)**: Lesions primarily on palms, soles, oral mucosa. Chickenpox rarely affects palms/soles & concentrates on trunk/face.
### Differentiating from Smallpox
- **Smallpox (天花)** (Eradicated): Lesions are all at the *same* stage of development & distributed centrifugally (worse on limbs/face). VZV features *various* stages in centripetal distribution.

## 5. Differential Diagnosis
### Common Exanthems Differential Table
| Condition | Pathogen | Key Differentiating Features |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Hand, Foot, and Mouth Disease (手足口病)** | Coxsackievirus A16, EV71 | Acral distribution (palms/soles), painful oral ulcers. |
| **Impetigo (膿疱疹)** | *Staph. aureus* / *Strep. pyogenes* | Golden crusts, usually localized, lacking systemic prodrome. |
| **Eczema Herpeticum (皰疹性濕疹)** | HSV-1 | Grouped monomorphic vesicles on eczematous skin, clinically toxic. |
| **Papular Urticaria (丘疹性蕁麻疹)** | Insect bites | Pruritic papules without vesicular evolution, clustered on exposed skin. |

## 6. Tiered Investigations
### Bedside & Clinical Investigations
- **Clinical Diagnosis**: Almost entirely clinical based on exposure history & pathognomonic rash ("crops" of pleomorphic vesicles).
### Laboratory Investigations
- **VZV PCR**: Gold standard if doubt exists. Performed on vesicular fluid swab. Indicated for atypical presentations or immunocompromised patients.

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Acute Supportive Management (Healthy Children)
- **Symptom Control**: Calamine lotion, oral antihistamines (**Chlorphenamine**) for severe pruritus (prevents excoriation & 2° bacterial infection).
- **Antipyresis**: **Paracetamol (撲熱息痛)** for fever.
- **CRITICAL CONTRAINDICATIONS**:
  - **AVOID NSAIDs (Ibuprofen)**: Strongly associated with ↑ risk of severe 2° **necrotizing fasciitis (壞死性筋膜炎)** by Group A *Streptococcus*.
  - **AVOID Aspirin**: Risk of **Reye syndrome (雷氏綜合症)** (acute encephalopathy + hepatic failure).
### Antiviral Therapy (High-Risk Groups)
- **Indications**: Immunocompromised, neonates, pregnant women, severe disease.
- **Agent**: IV/Oral **Aciclovir (阿昔洛韋)**. Must initiate <24-48 hours of rash onset. (Oral aciclovir has poor bioavailability; routine use in healthy children is limited/not recommended).
### Post-Exposure Prophylaxis
- **Varicella Zoster Immunoglobulin (VZIG)**: Given <10 days of exposure to high-risk contacts (e.g., pregnant women, immunocompromised, neonates whose mothers develop varicella 5 days prior to 2 days post-delivery).

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Infection Control & Isolation
- **Hospital Admission**: Strict airborne & contact isolation. Avoid admitting near immunocompromised oncology/haematology patients.
- **School Exclusion**: Exclude from school/nursery until **ALL** lesions have crusted over (~1 week).

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Safeguarding Considerations
- N/A primarily for VZV. However, severe untreated skin infections/neglected hygiene might prompt social worker evaluation.

## 10. Complications & Prognosis
### Dermatological & Soft Tissue Complications
- **Secondary Bacterial Infection**: Most common complication. Excoriation → *Staph. aureus* or Group A *Strep* → impetigo, cellulitis, or life-threatening **necrotizing fasciitis (壞死性筋膜炎)** or **toxic shock syndrome (中毒性休克綜合症)**. Suspect if new/persistent high fever occurs after initial few days.
### Neurological Complications
- **Cerebellitis (小腦炎)**: Ataxia ~1 week post-rash onset. Usually benign, self-limiting within 1 month.
- **Generalized Encephalitis/Meningitis**: Rare, more severe but better prognosis than HSV encephalitis.
### Systemic Complications
- **Varicella Pneumonia (水痘肺炎)**: Rare in healthy children; major cause of morbidity/mortality in adults & pregnant women.
- **Purpura Fulminans**: Rare vasculitis causing necrotic skin lesions due to cross-reacting antibodies inactivating Protein C/S.
- **Progressive Disseminated Disease**: Occurs in immunocompromised patients (mortality up to 20%). Features haemorrhagic lesions, pneumonitis, DIC.
### Prognosis
- Excellent in healthy children.

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Viva Question: What is the classic description of the chickenpox rash that helps differentiate it from other viral exanthems? Answer: The presence of lesions in all stages of development simultaneously (macules, vesicles, and crusts) appearing in 'crops'.
* Viva Question: What complication should you suspect if a child with chickenpox develops a new high fever 4 days after the rash appears? Answer: Secondary bacterial infection (e.g., Staph. aureus or Group A Streptococcus), potentially leading to cellulitis or necrotizing fasciitis.
* Viva Question: Why is Ibuprofen contraindicated for fever control in a child with chickenpox? Answer: It significantly increases the risk of severe secondary bacterial skin infections, particularly necrotizing fasciitis caused by Group A Streptococcus.
* Trap: Assuming a prolonged febrile phase is just severe chickenpox. Pearl: A secondary fever spike or persistent fever beyond the first few days strongly suggests a secondary bacterial infection.
* Pitfall: Allowing a child to return to school when they feel well but still have fluid-filled vesicles. They remain highly infectious until every single lesion is fully crusted.
