

# Atrial Septal Defect (心房中隔缺損)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A congenital defect in the interatrial septum allowing left-to-right shunting of blood.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Accounts for ~10% of congenital heart disease in HK. Secundum ASD is the most common type. Often detected incidentally during school screenings (Student Health Service).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Types**: Ostium secundum (most common, central), Ostium primum (lower septum, associated with AVSD and Down syndrome), Sinus venosus (upper septum, associated with anomalous pulmonary venous drainage).
- **Shunt**: Left-to-right shunt due to higher left atrial compliance/pressure. Leads to right atrial and right ventricular volume overload, and increased pulmonary blood flow.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Often asymptomatic in childhood.
- May present with recurrent chest infections or mild exercise intolerance in older children.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Auscultation**:
  - **Fixed, widely split S2**: Hallmark sign. Due to delayed closure of the pulmonary valve (constant increased RV volume regardless of respiration).
  - **Ejection systolic murmur** at LUSB: Due to increased flow across the *normal* pulmonary valve (not across the ASD itself).
  - Mid-diastolic rumble at LLSB (if large shunt, due to increased flow across tricuspid valve).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **VSD**: Pansystolic murmur at LLSB, normal S2 splitting.
- **Pulmonary Stenosis**: Ejection systolic murmur, but S2 splitting varies with respiration, and may have an ejection click.
- **Innocent Pulmonary Flow Murmur**: Normal physiological splitting of S2.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Innocent pulmonary flow murmur.
- Pulmonary stenosis.
- Partial Anomalous Pulmonary Venous Return (PAPVR) - often coexists with sinus venosus ASD.
- Ostium primum ASD (risk of mitral regurgitation and heart block).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
**Bedside**: **ECG**: Right axis deviation, incomplete RBBB (rsR' in V1) is classic for secundum ASD. Left axis deviation suggests primum ASD.
- **Imaging**: **CXR**: Cardiomegaly (Right Atrial/Ventricular enlargement), prominent pulmonary artery, increased pulmonary vascular markings (plethora).
**Gold Standard**: **Echocardiogram**: Confirms defect size, location, direction of shunt, and assesses RV volume overload.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Observation**: Small defects (<5mm) may close spontaneously.
- **Intervention**: Indicated for significant left-to-right shunt (RV volume overload) to prevent long-term complications. Usually done at 3-5 years of age.
  - **Transcatheter Device Closure**: First-line for suitable secundum ASDs (performed at Hong Kong Children's Hospital - HKCH).
  - **Surgical Closure**: For primum, sinus venosus, or large secundum ASDs with inadequate rims.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Dental hygiene education (though endocarditis prophylaxis is generally NOT required for isolated, uncomplicated ASDs before closure).
- MSW referral if financial assistance is needed for specialized care or procedures.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Not directly applicable.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- If uncorrected: Atrial arrhythmias (AF/AFL) in adulthood, right heart failure, paradoxical emboli (stroke risk), Eisenmenger syndrome (rare, late).
- Excellent prognosis post-closure.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Thinking the murmur is from blood flowing through the ASD. *Pearl*: The pressure gradient across the atria is too low to cause a murmur. The murmur is from increased flow across the pulmonary valve.
- **OSCE Pearl**: Always specifically listen for and describe the S2 splitting. "Fixed and widely split" is the magic phrase.
