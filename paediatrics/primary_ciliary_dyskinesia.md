# Primary Ciliary Dyskinesia (PCD) / Kartagener Syndrome (原發性纖毛運動障礙)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A rare, genetically heterogeneous, autosomal recessive disorder characterized by structural and/or functional defects of motile cilia.
- **Kartagener Syndrome**: A specific subgroup of PCD comprising the triad of: Situs inversus totalis, chronic sinusitis, and bronchiectasis.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Rare, but likely underdiagnosed. Delayed diagnosis often leads to irreversible lung damage.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Mutations in genes coding for ciliary motor proteins (most famously the outer dynein arms, e.g., DNAH5).
- Lack of normal ciliary beating completely abolishes mucociliary clearance.
- **Situs Inversus**: Cilia in the embryonic node normally beat to generate a leftward fluid flow, which dictates normal organ laterality. When they fail, organs arrange randomly (50% chance of situs inversus).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- **Neonates**: Unexplained neonatal respiratory distress syndrome in a *term* baby requiring prolonged oxygen, paired with continuous rhinitis.
- **Childhood**: Chronic, daily, wet productive cough starting from early infancy. Recurrent severe otitis media with effusion (leading to hearing loss).

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- Continuous nasal discharge (chronic rhinitis) since birth.
- Chronic wet cough.
- Chronic serous otitis media / "glue ear" with conductive hearing loss.
- Auscultation: widespread crackles/wheeze.
- Dextrocardia on examination (apex beat on the right - present in 50%).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from Cystic Fibrosis (CF): PCD patients normally have perfectly normal growth, no pancreatic insufficiency, and totally normal bowel habits.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Cystic Fibrosis
- Immunodeficiency (e.g., common variable immunodeficiency)
- Severe asthma (though asthma is dry, PCD is definitively wet)
- Post-infectious bronchiectasis

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Screening (Bedside)**: Nasal Nitric Oxide (nNO) test. Levels are *extremely low* in PCD (unlike asthma where exhaled NO is high).
- **Imaging**: Chest X-ray/HRCT revealing dextrocardia/situs inversus and mid-lobe predominant bronchiectasis (CF often favours upper lobes).
- **Definitive/Special**: 
  - High-speed video microscopy of ciliary beat frequency/pattern from nasal brushings.
  - Electron Microscopy (EM) of cilia cross-sections (looking for missing dynein arms or spoke defects).
  - Genetic panel testing.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Airway Clearance**: Unrelenting daily chest physiotherapy is the cornerstone. Must physically move the mucus since the cilia cannot.
- **Infection check**: Aggressive antibiotic therapy for respiratory exacerbations based on sputum cultures (Haemophilus influenzae is very common early on).
- **ENT**: Regular audiology checks. Grommet insertion is controversial (they often drain permanently purulent fluid if inserted in PCD), so hearing aids are often preferred for conductive loss.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Requires a specialized respiratory and ENT team. 
- Infertility counseling later in life.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Neglect in maintaining daily chest physiotherapy routines can rapidly accelerate irreversible bronchiectasis.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Bronchiectasis and progressive decline in lung function.
- Conductive hearing loss (permanent if middle ear bones become damaged).
- **Infertility**: Almost all males are infertile (sperm tails are modified cilia and thus immotile). Females often have subfertility or ectopic pregnancies (fallopian tube cilia are immotile).

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Assuming situs inversus is harmless. While harmless structurally, its presence must instantly prompt the question: "Does this child have chronic cough or runny nose?" to rule out Kartagener's.
- **Viva Q**: "Why do 50% of PCD patients have situs inversus, and the other 50% have normal situs?" Answer: Because without the embryonic nodal cilia to direct leftward lateralization, internal organ placement defaults to pure random chance—a flip of a coin (50/50).
