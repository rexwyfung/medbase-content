# Seborrheic Dermatitis (脂溢性皮炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition of Seborrheic Dermatitis
* Self-limiting, inflammatory papulosquamous skin condition.
* Localizes to areas with ↑ sebaceous gland density (scalp, face, intertriginous zones).

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
* Peak incidence: Neonates/infants <3 months.
* Spontaneous resolution usually by 6-12 months.
* ↑ Prevalence and severity in HK due to hot, humid climate (濕熱氣候).
* 2nd peak occurs during puberty/adolescence.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Sebaceous Gland Hyperactivity
* Transplacental maternal androgens → ↑ infant sebaceous gland size & activity.
* → ↑ Sebum production in rich zones.

### Fungal Colonization
* Overgrowth of lipophilic commensal yeast ***Malassezia* spp. (皮屑芽孢菌)**.
* Fungal lipases degrade sebum → pro-inflammatory free fatty acids.
* → Epidermal hyperproliferation & erythema.

### Immune Response
* Altered innate immunity & alternative complement pathway activation.
* **-ve** Atopic association.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Scalp Presentation
* **Cradle cap (頭泥)**: Thick, yellow, greasy, adherent scaling crusts.
* Primarily affects vertex and anterior fontanelle.
* **-ve** Alopecia (hair loss rare).

### Face and Flexural Presentation
* Erythematous, salmon-pink macules/patches with yellow greasy scaling.
* Face: Forehead, eyebrows, nasolabial folds, postauricular folds.
* Intertriginous zones: Axillae, neck creases, umbilicus.
* **Nappy area (尿布區)**: Involvement is common and occurs early.

### General Clinical Status
* **-ve Pruritus (無痕癢)** (Infant appears unbothered).
* **+ve** Normal feeding and sleeping patterns.
* **-ve** Failure to thrive.

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Seborrheic vs. Atopic Dermatitis
* Onset: <2 months (Seborrheic) vs. >2 months (Atopic).
* Itch: **-ve** Pruritus (Seborrheic) vs. **+ve** Severe Pruritus (Atopic).
* Nappy area: **+ve** Involved (Seborrheic) vs. **-ve** Spared (Atopic).
* Scales: Greasy/yellow (Seborrheic) vs. Dry/white (Atopic).
* Exudate: **-ve** Weeping (Seborrheic) vs. **+ve** Weeping/crusting (Atopic).

## 5. Differential Diagnosis
### Differential Diagnosis Table for Infant Skin Rashes
| Condition | Key Differentiating Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Atopic Dermatitis (濕疹)** | Highly pruritic, spares nappy area, dry skin, +ve family history. |
| **Diaper Candidiasis (念珠菌尿布疹)** | Beefy red erythema, +ve satellite lesions, primarily nappy area. |
| **Psoriasis (牛皮癬)** | Well-demarcated erythematous plaques, silvery scales, +ve Auspitz sign. |
| **Langerhans Cell Histiocytosis (LCH)** | Refractory rash, hemorrhagic/petechial lesions, +ve systemic signs. |
| **Tinea Capitis (頭癬)** | +ve Alopecia, broken hairs, +ve cervical lymphadenopathy. Rare in infants. |

## 6. Tiered Investigations
### First-Line Diagnostic Approach
* Clinical diagnosis based on history and typical distribution.
* **-ve** Routine laboratory tests required.

### Second-Line Investigations (Atypical/Refractory Cases)
* Skin swabs: For Microscopy & Culture (M&C) if **+ve** purulent discharge/crusting (exclude 2° bacterial/fungal infection).
* Skin biopsy: Indicated ONLY if severe, refractory, or high suspicion of LCH/Psoriasis.

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### General Conservative Management
* Reassurance: Benign condition, self-resolving by 6-12 months.
* Daily bathing with mild, soap-free liquid emollient washes.

### Scalp Management (Cradle Cap Protocol)
* 1st Line: Apply **Emollients (潤膚膏)** (olive oil, baby oil, white soft paraffin) overnight → soften crusts.
* Morning routine: Gentle brushing with soft toothbrush/comb → wash with mild baby shampoo.
* 2nd Line (HA Protocol): **Ketoconazole 2% shampoo** (leave on 3-5 mins before rinsing, 2x/week for resistant cases).

### Face and Body Management
* 1st Line: Frequent regular **Emollients**.
* 2nd Line (Mild/Mod inflammation): Topical imidazole cream (e.g., **Clotrimazole 1%** or **Miconazole 2%**).
* 3rd Line (Severe inflammation): Ultra-mild topical steroid (e.g., **Hydrocortisone 1% cream**) applied strictly for 3-5 days ONLY.

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Primary Care and Parental Support
* Maternal and Child Health Centre (MCHC) monitoring to track disease resolution.
* Parental education: Address aesthetic anxieties; explicitly advise against aggressive mechanical scraping of crusts (→ risk of bleeding and infection).

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Clinical Red Flags for Further Investigation
* Severe, diffuse erythroderma + **Failure to Thrive (FTT)** + chronic diarrhea → strictly exclude severe primary immunodeficiency (e.g., SCID, HIV, Omenn syndrome).
* Hemorrhagic/petechial rash mimicking seborrheic distribution → strictly exclude **Langerhans Cell Histiocytosis (LCH)**.

### Safeguarding Context
* Severe, thick, malodorous cradle cap may reflect parental coping deficits or poor hygiene.
* Alone, it is **-ve** for Non-Accidental Injury (NAI), but mandates psychosocial exploration and support via MCHC/social workers.

## 10. Complications & Prognosis
### Common Complications
* Secondary bacterial infection (impeditiginization via *Staphylococcus aureus*).
* Secondary fungal superinfection (*Candida albicans*), especially in nappy area.
* Post-inflammatory hypopigmentation (spontaneously resolves over months).

### Clinical Prognosis
* Excellent.
* Universally self-resolves by 6-12 months of age concurrently with waning maternal androgens.

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Viva Question: How do you clinically differentiate seborrheic dermatitis from atopic dermatitis in a 2-month-old?
  Answer: Seborrheic dermatitis is non-pruritic, affects the nappy area, has greasy yellow scales, and onsets <2 months. Atopic eczema is highly pruritic, spares the nappy area, has dry scales, and typically onsets >2 months.
* Trap: Prescribing potent topical steroids to quickly clear infantile seborrheic dermatitis.
  Pearl: Never use potent steroids on infants due to systemic absorption and skin atrophy risks. Strictly adhere to emollients, antifungals (Ketoconazole), or maximum 1% Hydrocortisone for very brief periods.
* Pitfall: Aggressively picking off "cradle cap" scales. Explain to parents that manual peeling damages the epidermis, causing excoriation and increasing the risk of secondary *Staph. aureus* infections; instead, soften overnight with oil and gently wash.
* Trap: Ignoring a severe, refractory, petechial rash in a seborrheic distribution.
  Pearl: Always consider Langerhans Cell Histiocytosis (LCH) in an infant presenting with severe, hemorrhagic seborrheic-like dermatitis that fails standard topical therapy.
