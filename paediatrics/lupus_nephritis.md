# Lupus Nephritis (狼瘡性腎炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition of Lupus Nephritis
* **Definition**: Renal involvement in **Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) (系統性紅斑狼瘡)**, a chronic autoimmune disease.
* **Incidence**: Occurs in up to **50-60%** of children with SLE.
* **Impact**: A major determinant of long-term morbidity and mortality.

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
* **Prevalence**: SLE is more prevalent and often more severe in **Asian populations** (including HK) compared to Caucasians.
* **Demographics**: Predominantly affects **adolescent females** (Female:Male ratio dramatically ↑ post-puberty).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Autoantibody Production & Immune Complexes
* **Mechanism**: Loss of self-tolerance → production of autoantibodies, particularly **Anti-dsDNA (抗雙鏈DNA抗體)**.
* **Complex Formation**: Autoantibodies bind to nuclear antigens → circulating immune complexes.

### Glomerular Deposition & Inflammation
* **Deposition**: Immune complexes deposit within glomeruli (subendothelial, subepithelial, or mesangial spaces).
* **Complement Activation**: Classical complement pathway is activated → **consumption of C3 and C4 (↓ C3, ↓ C4)**.
* **Tissue Damage**: Intense inflammation + influx of inflammatory cells → irreversible glomerular damage and fibrosis if untreated.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Renal Presentation
* **Asymptomatic**: Often detected on routine urinalysis (isolated proteinuria/haematuria).
* **Nephrotic Syndrome (腎病綜合症)**: Heavy proteinuria, severe oedema, hypoalbuminaemia, hyperlipidaemia.
* **Nephritic Syndrome (腎炎綜合症)**: Haematuria (often macroscopic), hypertension, oliguria.
* **Rapidly Progressive Glomerulonephritis (RPGN)**: Acute renal failure with active urinary sediment.

### Systemic SLE Features
* **Constitutional**: Unexplained fever, fatigue, significant weight loss.
* **Mucocutaneous**: **Malar (butterfly) rash (蝴蝶斑)**, photosensitivity, discoid rash, oral/nasal ulcers, non-scarring alopecia.
* **Musculoskeletal**: Arthralgia, non-erosive arthritis.
* **Haematological**: Cytopenias (autoimmune haemolytic anaemia, thrombocytopenia, leukopenia).

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Primary Nephrotic Syndrome (Minimal Change Disease) vs Lupus Nephritis
* **Age**: MCD typically affects younger children (2-6 yrs); SLE primarily affects adolescents.
* **Systemic Features**: MCD has NO systemic features (no malar rash, no joint pain).
* **Complement Levels**: Normal in MCD; **↓ C3/C4** in active SLE.

### IgA Nephropathy vs Lupus Nephritis
* **Presentation**: IgA nephropathy presents with synpharyngitic macroscopic haematuria.
* **Complements**: Normal in IgA nephropathy; **↓ C3/C4** in SLE.
* **Serology**: ANA and Anti-dsDNA are negative in IgA nephropathy.

## 5. Differential Diagnosis
### Common Paediatric Glomerulonephritides
| Differential Diagnosis | Key Differentiating Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Post-Streptococcal Glomerulonephritis (PSGN)** | Follows Group A Strep infection by 1-3 wks. +ve ASOT. ↓ C3 but **normal C4**. |
| **IgA Nephropathy** | Concurrent URTI + haematuria. Normal complements. -ve ANA. |
| **Henoch-Schönlein Purpura (HSP) Nephritis** | Palpable purpura (lower limbs/buttocks), abdo pain, arthritis. Normal complements. |

### Must-Not-Miss Complications in SLE
* **Class IV Lupus Nephritis (Diffuse Proliferative)**: The most severe/aggressive form → rapid progression to end-stage renal failure if untreated.
* **Macrophage Activation Syndrome (MAS)**: Life-threatening complication of active SLE → unremitting fever, ↓↓ cytopenias, ↑↑ ferritin, liver dysfunction.

## 6. Tiered Investigations
### Bedside Investigations
* **Urinalysis**: Proteinuria, haematuria, cellular casts (red cell casts = active glomerulonephritis).
* **Blood Pressure**: Often ↑ (hypertensive urgency/emergency requires immediate management).

### Gold Standard Serological Tests
* **ANA (Anti-Nuclear Antibody)**: Highly sensitive (screening test, +ve in >95%), but not specific.
* **Anti-dsDNA**: Highly specific for SLE; directly correlates with renal disease activity.
* **Anti-Sm (Smith)**: Highly specific for SLE, but lacks sensitivity.
* **Complements (C3, C4)**: **↓ C3 & ↓ C4** during active disease due to immune complex consumption.

### Diagnostic Imaging & Biopsy
* **Renal Biopsy**: MANDATORY for any SLE patient with clinical evidence of nephritis to guide treatment.
* **Immunofluorescence**: Pathognomonic **"full house" pattern** (IgG, IgA, IgM, C3, and C1q deposition).

### ISN/RPS Histological Classification of Lupus Nephritis
| Class | Description | Clinical Implication |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Class I** | Minimal mesangial | Normal renal function, asymptomatic. |
| **Class II** | Mesangial proliferative | Microscopic haematuria/proteinuria. |
| **Class III** | Focal (<50% glomeruli) | Active inflammation. Requires immunosuppression. |
| **Class IV** | Diffuse Proliferative (>50%)| Most severe/aggressive. Mandatory high-dose immunosuppression. |
| **Class V** | Membranous | Heavy proteinuria → nephrotic syndrome. |
| **Class VI** | Advanced Sclerosing | >90% sclerosed glomeruli. Irreversible ESRF. |

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Acute Management of Renal Flare
* **Hypertensive Emergencies**: IV antihypertensives (e.g., Labetalol) for acute nephritic syndrome.
* **Acute Pulmonary Oedema**: Strict fluid restriction, IV Furosemide, O2 support.

### Induction Therapy (Severe Class III / IV)
* **Goal**: Rapidly halt acute inflammation and prevent permanent nephron loss.
* **Corticosteroids**: IV **Methylprednisolone (甲潑尼龍)** pulses (3 days) → high-dose oral Prednisolone.
* **Immunosuppressant (1st Line HA Protocol)**: **Mycophenolate Mofetil (MMF) (霉酚酸酯)**. Preferred in HK due to comparable efficacy and less gonadal toxicity vs Cyclophosphamide.
* **Alternative Immunosuppressant**: IV **Cyclophosphamide (環磷酰胺)** (reserved for very severe or MMF-refractory cases).

### Maintenance Therapy
* **Goal**: Maintain remission and prevent relapse.
* **Corticosteroids**: Low-dose oral Prednisolone (weaned to lowest effective dose).
* **Immunosuppressant**: MMF or Azathioprine.

### Adjunctive & Renoprotective Therapy
* **Hydroxychloroquine (羥氯喹)**: Recommended for ALL patients with SLE → ↓ flares & ↑ overall survival.
* **ACEi / ARB**: Mandatory for renoprotection (↓ proteinuria) and blood pressure control.

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Multidisciplinary Team Coordination
* **Specialist Care**: Joint management by **Paediatric Rheumatology** and **Paediatric Nephrology**.
* **Psychosocial Support**: Clinical Psychology/MSW input for chronic illness burden, school absenteeism, and body image issues (Cushingoid appearance, alopecia).

### Adolescent-Specific Counselling
* **Teratogenicity**: MMF and Cyclophosphamide are highly teratogenic.
* **Contraception**: Effective, non-oestrogen-based contraception is mandatory for sexually active adolescent females.
* **Compliance**: High risk of medication non-adherence in adolescents; requires constant monitoring and open communication.

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Environmental & Lifestyle Triggers
* **Sun Protection**: UV light is a major trigger for systemic flares → strict daily sunscreen use and sun avoidance required.
* **Infection Prevention**: High-dose immunosuppression ↑ risk of severe sepsis. Avoid all live vaccines while actively immunosuppressed.

## 10. Complications & Prognosis
### Disease-Related Complications
* **Renal**: End-Stage Renal Failure (ESRF) requiring dialysis or transplantation.
* **Cardiovascular**: Premature atherosclerosis → major cause of late-stage mortality in SLE patients.

### Treatment-Related Complications
* **Immunosuppressants**: ↑ susceptibility to severe/opportunistic infections.
* **Chronic Corticosteroids**: Cushingoid features, severe osteoporosis, avascular necrosis (AVN) of the hip, growth restriction, cataracts.
* **Cyclophosphamide**: Premature ovarian failure/infertility (gonadal toxicity), haemorrhagic cystitis, ↑ risk of secondary malignancies.

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Trap: Relying only on ANA to diagnose Lupus Nephritis. Pearl: ANA is sensitive but not specific. You need Anti-dsDNA and low Complements (C3/C4) to confirm active SLE, and a renal biopsy to stage the nephritis.
* Viva Question: What is the 'full house' pattern on immunofluorescence in a renal biopsy? Answer: It refers to the characteristic finding in Lupus Nephritis where there is positive staining for all major immunoglobulins (IgG, IgA, IgM) and complement components (C3, C1q).
* Pitfall: Prescribing standard induction immunosuppressants without discussing reproductive health. MMF and Cyclophosphamide are highly teratogenic; strict contraceptive counselling must occur before starting treatment in adolescent females.
* Viva Question: Which histological class of Lupus Nephritis requires the most aggressive induction therapy? Answer: Class IV (Diffuse Proliferative), as it carries the highest risk of rapid progression to End-Stage Renal Failure.
* Trap: Automatically using IV Cyclophosphamide as 1st-line induction for all severe lupus nephritis. Pearl: MMF is increasingly preferred as 1st-line induction in Hong Kong HA protocols because it offers comparable efficacy with a much safer side-effect profile, specifically avoiding the severe gonadal toxicity associated with Cyclophosphamide.
