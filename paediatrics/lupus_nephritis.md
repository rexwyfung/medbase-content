
# Lupus Nephritis (狼瘡性腎炎)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Renal involvement in Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE), an autoimmune disease.
- **Local Epidemiology**: SLE is more prevalent and often more severe in Asian populations (including HK) compared to Caucasians. It predominantly affects adolescent females. Lupus nephritis occurs in up to 50-60% of children with SLE and is a major determinant of morbidity and mortality.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Mechanism**: Loss of self-tolerance leads to the production of autoantibodies (especially Anti-dsDNA). These antibodies form immune complexes with nuclear antigens.
- The immune complexes deposit in the glomeruli (subendothelial, subepithelial, or mesangial), activating the classical complement pathway.
- This causes intense inflammation, influx of inflammatory cells, and glomerular damage.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- See below.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Renal Signs**: Can be completely asymptomatic (picked up on routine urinalysis) or present with:
  - Nephrotic syndrome (heavy proteinuria, severe oedema, hypoalbuminaemia).
  - Nephritic syndrome (haematuria, hypertension, oliguria).
  - Rapidly Progressive Glomerulonephritis (RPGN) with acute renal failure.
- **Systemic SLE Features**:
  - *Constitutional*: Fever, fatigue, weight loss.
  - *Mucocutaneous*: Malar (butterfly) rash, photosensitivity, discoid rash, oral ulcers, non-scarring alopecia.
  - *Musculoskeletal*: Arthralgia, non-erosive arthritis.
  - *Haematological*: Cytopenias (autoimmune haemolytic anaemia, thrombocytopenia, leukopenia).
## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Primary Nephrotic Syndrome (Minimal Change Disease)**: Usually affects younger children (2-6 years), has NO systemic features (no rash, no joint pain), and normal complement levels.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Most Likely**:
  - Lupus Nephritis (in an adolescent female with a rash, joint pain, and abnormal urinalysis).
- **Must-Not-Miss**:
  - **Class IV Lupus Nephritis (Diffuse Proliferative)**: The most severe and aggressive form, rapidly leading to end-stage renal failure if not treated aggressively.
  - **Macrophage Activation Syndrome (MAS)**: A life-threatening complication of active SLE.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**:
  - **Urinalysis**: Proteinuria, haematuria, cellular casts.
  - **Blood Pressure**: Often elevated.
- **Gold Standard**:
  - **Immunology**:
    - **ANA (Anti-Nuclear Antibody)**: Highly sensitive (screening test), but not specific.
    - **Anti-dsDNA**: Highly specific for SLE and correlates with renal disease activity.
    - **Anti-Sm (Smith)**: Highly specific for SLE.
    - **Complements (C3, C4)**: **Low** during active disease (due to consumption by immune complexes).
    - **Renal Biopsy**: **MANDATORY** for any SLE patient with clinical evidence of nephritis.
    - Guides treatment based on the ISN/RPS Classification (Class I to VI).
    - *Class III (Focal) and Class IV (Diffuse Proliferative)* require aggressive immunosuppression.
    - *Class V (Membranous)* presents with nephrotic syndrome.
    - *Immunofluorescence*: Shows "full house" pattern (deposition of IgG, IgA, IgM, C3, and C1q).
- **Monitoring**:
  - Monitor Anti-dsDNA titres and C3/C4 levels to track disease activity and response to treatment.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**:
  - Treat hypertensive emergencies or acute pulmonary oedema if presenting with acute nephritic syndrome.
- **Chronic/Definitive**:
  - **Induction Therapy (for severe Class III/IV)**: Goal is to rapidly halt inflammation.
  - **High-dose Corticosteroids**: IV Methylprednisolone pulses followed by high-dose oral Prednisolone.
  - PLUS an immunosuppressant: **Mycophenolate Mofetil (MMF)** or **IV Cyclophosphamide**. (MMF is often preferred in HK due to less gonadal toxicity compared to Cyclophosphamide).
  - **Maintenance Therapy**: Goal is to prevent relapse.
    - Low-dose oral Prednisolone.
    - PLUS **MMF** or **Azathioprine**.
  - **Adjunctive Therapy**:
    - **Hydroxychloroquine**: Recommended for ALL patients with SLE. Reduces flares and improves survival.
    - **ACEi/ARB**: For renoprotection and blood pressure control.
## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Rheumatology and Nephrology joint care.
- Counselling regarding the teratogenic effects of MMF and Cyclophosphamide, and the need for effective contraception in adolescent females.
## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- **Sun protection**: Crucial, as UV light can trigger systemic flares.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Disease Complications**: End-stage renal failure, cardiovascular disease (premature atherosclerosis is a major cause of late mortality in SLE).
- **Treatment Complications**: High risk of severe infections (due to immunosuppression), Cushingoid features, osteoporosis, avascular necrosis of the hip (from chronic steroids), infertility (from Cyclophosphamide).

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Relying only on ANA to diagnose Lupus Nephritis. *Pearl*: ANA is sensitive but not specific. You need Anti-dsDNA and low Complements (C3/C4) to confirm active SLE, and a renal biopsy to stage the nephritis.
- **Viva Question**: "What is the 'full house' pattern on immunofluorescence in a renal biopsy?" *Answer*: It refers to the characteristic finding in Lupus Nephritis where there is positive staining for all major immunoglobulins (IgG, IgA, IgM) and complement components (C3, C1q).
