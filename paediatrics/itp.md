
# Immune Thrombocytopenic Purpura (ITP) (免疫性血小板減少性紫癜)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: An acquired, immune-mediated disorder characterised by isolated thrombocytopenia (platelet count < 100 x 10^9/L) in the absence of other causes.
- **Local Epidemiology**: The most common cause of acute onset thrombocytopenia in otherwise well children in HK. Peak age is 2-5 years.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Autoantibodies (usually IgG) are formed against platelet surface antigens (e.g., GPIIb/IIIa).
- Antibody-coated platelets are prematurely destroyed by macrophages in the reticuloendothelial system, primarily the spleen.
- Often triggered by a preceding viral infection (e.g., URTI, Varicella) or, rarely, vaccination (e.g., MMR) 1-4 weeks prior.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- See below.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Sudden onset** of bruising, petechiae, and purpura in a previously healthy, active child.
- Mucosal bleeding: Epistaxis, bleeding gums, haematuria, menorrhagia (in adolescent girls).
- **CRITICAL NEGATIVE SIGNS**: The child is otherwise completely well. There is **NO** significant hepatosplenomegaly, **NO** significant lymphadenopathy, and **NO** bone pain.
## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Acute Lymphoblastic Leukaemia (ALL)**: Presents with bruising, but the child is usually unwell (fever, lethargy, bone pain) and has hepatosplenomegaly or lymphadenopathy.
- **Henoch-Schönlein Purpura (HSP)**: Rash is typically palpable, concentrated on the buttocks/legs, and platelet count is NORMAL or high.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Most Likely**:
  - Acute ITP.
- **Must-Not-Miss**:
  - **Leukaemia (ALL/AML)**: Must be ruled out by examining the blood film.
  - **Aplastic Anaemia**: Will have pancytopenia, not isolated thrombocytopenia.
  - **Haemolytic Uraemic Syndrome (HUS)**: Will have microangiopathic haemolytic anaemia (schistocytes) and acute kidney injury.
  - **Non-Accidental Injury (NAI)**: Bruising in unusual locations (e.g., ears, neck, inner thighs) in a child with a normal platelet count.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**:
  - **Complete Blood Count (CBC)**: **Isolated** thrombocytopenia. Haemoglobin and White Cell Count MUST be normal (mild anaemia is acceptable if there has been significant epistaxis).
  - **Peripheral Blood Smear**: Crucial to confirm true thrombocytopenia (rule out platelet clumping) and to **rule out blasts** (leukaemia) or schistocytes (HUS). Platelets may appear large (megathrombocytes).
- **Gold Standard**:
  - ITP is a diagnosis of exclusion.
  - **Bone Marrow Aspiration**: NOT routinely required for typical acute ITP.
  - *Indications for Bone Marrow*: Atypical features (abnormal WBC/Hb, hepatosplenomegaly, bone pain), failure to respond to IVIG/Steroids, or before starting corticosteroids (to ensure you aren't partially treating and masking leukaemia).
- **Monitoring**:
  - Serial CBCs to monitor platelet count.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**:
  - **Intracranial Haemorrhage (ICH)**: The most feared, but rare (<1%) complication. If suspected (headache, vomiting, altered consciousness), requires immediate CT brain, IVIG, IV Methylprednisolone, and potentially platelet transfusion (platelets are rapidly destroyed but may provide transient haemostasis).
- **Chronic/Definitive**:
  - **Observation**: First-line for the vast majority of children with mild/moderate skin bleeding only, regardless of the platelet count. Spontaneous recovery occurs in 80% within 6 months.
  - **Medical Intervention**: Indicated for significant mucosal bleeding (e.g., prolonged epistaxis) or life-threatening bleeding.
  - **IV Immunoglobulin (IVIG)**: Rapidly increases platelet count (blocks Fc receptors in the spleen). Expensive, requires admission.
  - **Corticosteroids (Oral Prednisolone)**: Cheaper, but requires a bone marrow aspirate first in many HA protocols to rule out leukaemia.
  - **Anti-D Immunoglobulin**: Option for Rh-positive patients, but carries a risk of severe haemolysis.
  - **Chronic ITP (>12 months)**: May require second-line therapies (Rituximab, Thrombopoietin receptor agonists like Eltrombopag/Romiplostim) or, rarely, Splenectomy.
## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Not generally required for acute self-limiting cases.
## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- **Safety Netting**: Advise parents to avoid contact sports, NSAIDs/Aspirin (use Paracetamol for pain/fever), and to seek immediate medical attention for head trauma or prolonged bleeding.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Excellent prognosis. ~80% resolve spontaneously within 6 months (Acute ITP).
- ~20% progress to Chronic ITP (>12 months), more common in older children/adolescents.
- Severe bleeding (ICH) is very rare (<1%).

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Transfusing platelets for a child with ITP and a platelet count of 5. *Pearl*: Platelet transfusions are generally CONTRAINDICATED in ITP because the autoantibodies will immediately destroy the transfused platelets. They are only used in life-threatening emergencies (e.g., ICH) alongside IVIG/steroids.
- **Viva Question**: "A 3-year-old has petechiae and a platelet count of 10. Hb and WBC are normal. The blood film shows no blasts. He is playing happily. What is the treatment?" *Answer*: Observation and safety netting. Treatment is based on bleeding symptoms, not the absolute platelet count.
