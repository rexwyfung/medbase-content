# Peptic Ulcer Disease (H. pylori) (胃潰瘍 / 幽門螺旋菌)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A defect in the gastric or duodenal mucosa extending through the muscularis mucosae.
- **Local Epidemiology**: H. pylori prevalence in HK children is heavily tied to familial carriage. Decreasing incidence with better hygiene, but still a key cause of child morbidity.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **H. Pylori**: A gram-negative, spiral flagellated bacterium. Produces urease (converting urea to ammonia) to neutralize stomach acid locally, allowing it to survive and damage the protective mucous layer, exposing the epithelium to acid.
- **NSAIDs**: Inhibit COX-1 dependent prostaglandins which are vital for mucus/bicarbonate secretion.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Can present insidiously with chronic abdominal pain or acutely with a catastrophic upper GI bleed.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- Chronic dull, aching or burning epigastric pain.
- **Duodenal ulcers**: Pain improves with feeding (acid is buffered by food), worsens hours later.
- **Gastric ulcers**: Pain worsens with feeding (acid secretion increases).
- Iron deficiency anemia, melena (black, tarry stools), hematemesis ("coffee ground" vomit).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from functional abdominal pain which is periumbilical, never wakes the child at night, and has no systemic signs (no anemia, normal growth).
- Peptic ulcer pain *classically wakes the child from sleep*.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Functional Dyspepsia
- Coeliac Disease
- Crohn's disease (upper GI involvement)
- Eosinophilic Oesophagitis

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Non-invasive**: Urea Breath Test (13C-UBT) or Stool H. pylori antigen. (Serology is useless as it doesn't distinguish past from current infection).
- **Invasive/Definitive**: Esophagogastroduodenoscopy (OGD) with biopsies. 
- **Rapid Urease Test (CLO test)**: Done directly on the biopsy sample during endoscopy.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute GI Bleed**: Resuscitation, IV PPI infusion, endoscopic clipping/injection of adrenaline.
- **H. pylori Eradication (HA standard)**: "Triple Therapy" for 10-14 days.
  - PPI (e.g., Omeprazole) + Clarithromycin + Amoxicillin (or Metronidazole if allergic).
- **Post-treatment**: MUST confirm eradication $\ge$ 4 weeks after stopping treatment using a Urea Breath Test or stool antigen.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Family screening is often discussed as parents/siblings may be asymptomatic carriers frequently re-infecting the child.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Ensure no accidental ingestion of adult NSAIDs/Aspirin is occurring at home.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Perforation (presenting as rigid, broad-like abdomen and air under diaphragm on erect CXR).
- Severe hemorrhage leading to hypovolemic shock.
- Gastric outlet obstruction due to acute edema or chronic scarring.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Giving a child with abdominal pain an NSAID or Aspirin. Always avoid entirely if ulcer features exist.
- **Viva Q**: "Why must the child stop PPIs 2 weeks before a urea breath test?" Answer: PPIs suppress the H. pylori, causing a false-negative breath test result. They must be off PPIs for 2 weeks, and off antibiotics for 4 weeks.
