
# Duodenal Atresia (十二指腸閉鎖)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Congenital complete obliteration of the duodenal lumen.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Incidence ~1 in 5,000 to 10,000 live births. Strong association with **Down Syndrome (Trisomy 21)**; ~30% of infants with duodenal atresia have Down syndrome.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Mechanism**: Failure of recanalisation of the embryonic duodenum around the 8th-10th week of gestation.
- The atresia is almost always located *distal* to the ampulla of Vater (where the bile duct enters).
- Associated anomalies (VACTERL association) are common: Vertebral, Anorectal, Cardiac, Tracheo-oesophageal, Renal, Limb.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- See below.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Antenatal**: Polyhydramnios (due to inability of the fetus to swallow and absorb amniotic fluid). Often detected on antenatal morphology ultrasound.
- **Postnatal**:
  - **Bilious Vomiting**: Occurs very early, typically within the first 24-48 hours of life, often after the first feed. (Vomiting is bilious because the obstruction is distal to the bile duct).
  - **Upper Abdominal Distension**: The stomach and proximal duodenum are distended, but the lower abdomen is scaphoid (empty).
  - Failure to pass meconium (though some may pass a small amount initially).
## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Pyloric Stenosis**: Vomiting is *non-bilious* and presents later (2-8 weeks).
- **Malrotation with Volvulus**: Also presents with bilious vomiting in a neonate. This is the critical differential to rule out immediately, as volvulus causes bowel necrosis.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Most Likely**:
  - Duodenal Atresia (especially if antenatal polyhydramnios or Down syndrome features).
- **Must-Not-Miss**:
  - **Malrotation with Midgut Volvulus**: A surgical emergency requiring immediate laparotomy to save the bowel.
  - Jejunal/Ileal Atresia.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**:
  - **Abdominal X-ray (Erect/Decubitus)**: The diagnostic test of choice.
  - **"Double Bubble" Sign**: Pathognomonic. One large air bubble in the stomach (left) and a smaller air bubble in the distended proximal duodenum (right), with an absolute lack of gas in the distal bowel.
- **Gold Standard**:
  - **Echocardiogram**: Mandatory to screen for associated congenital heart defects (especially AVSD, common in Down syndrome) before surgery.
  - **Karyotype/Microarray**: To confirm Down syndrome if suspected.
- **Monitoring**:
  - U&E and VBG to monitor hydration and acid-base status.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**:
  - **NBM (Nil By Mouth)**.
  - **Gastric Decompression**: Insert a large-bore orogastric (OG) tube on free drainage to decompress the stomach and prevent aspiration.
  - **IV Fluids**: Resuscitate and provide maintenance fluids + replacement of OG tube losses (usually with 0.9% Normal Saline + KCl).
- **Chronic/Definitive**:
  - **Surgery (Duodenoduodenostomy)**: The definitive treatment. The distended proximal duodenum is anastomosed to the collapsed distal duodenum, bypassing the atretic segment.
  - *Timing*: It is an urgent, but not emergent, surgery. The baby must be fully resuscitated, and cardiac anomalies must be assessed first.
## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- If Down syndrome is diagnosed, refer to the Down Syndrome Association and arrange multidisciplinary follow-up (developmental, audiology, ophthalmology).
## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Explain the surgical procedure and the expected post-operative course (prolonged need for TPN while waiting for the anastomosis to function).

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Post-operative**: Prolonged ileus/delayed gastric emptying is very common. The dilated proximal duodenum takes time to regain normal motility. TPN is often required for weeks.
- **Long-term**: Excellent survival rate (>90%). Prognosis is largely determined by associated anomalies (e.g., severe cardiac defects). Risk of late complications like megaduodenum or blind loop syndrome.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Assuming a "double bubble" on AXR is always duodenal atresia. *Pearl*: Malrotation with volvulus or a duodenal web can also cause a double bubble. If there is *any* distal gas, you must urgently rule out malrotation (e.g., with an upper GI contrast study).
- **Viva Question**: "A neonate with Down syndrome vomits green fluid on day 1 of life. What is the diagnosis until proven otherwise, and what will the X-ray show?" *Answer*: Duodenal atresia. The X-ray will show the classic 'double bubble' sign with no distal bowel gas.
