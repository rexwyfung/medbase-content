# Duodenal Atresia (十二指腸閉鎖)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Definition of Duodenal Atresia
**Congenital complete obliteration** of the duodenal lumen.
### Local Epidemiology & Demographics
- **Incidence:** ~1 in 5,000–10,000 live births.
- **Genetic Association:** ~30% of affected infants have **Down Syndrome** (Trisomy 21) (唐氏綜合症).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Embryological Pathophysiology
- **Mechanism:** Failure of vacuolization/recanalisation of the embryonic duodenum around the 8th–10th week of gestation.
- **Anatomical Location:** Atresia is almost always **distal to the ampulla of Vater** → results in **bilious vomiting** (膽汁性嘔吐).
### Associated Congenital Anomalies (VACTERL)
Frequently associated with the **VACTERL** association:
- **V**ertebral defects
- **A**norectal malformations (e.g., imperforate anus)
- **C**ardiac defects (esp. AVSD, VSD)
- **T**racheo-oesophageal fistula (TEF)
- **R**enal anomalies
- **L**imb defects

## 3. Clinical Presentation
### Antenatal Presentation
- **Polyhydramnios** (羊水過多): Often detected on antenatal US (fetus cannot swallow/absorb amniotic fluid).
- **Antenatal US Scan:** Fetal morphology scan may show a fluid-filled stomach and dilated proximal duodenum.
### Postnatal Presentation
- **Early Bilious Vomiting:** Typically presents on Day 1–2 of life.
- **Abdominal Contour:** Upper abdominal distension with a flat/scaphoid lower abdomen.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Essential Postnatal Signs
- **Vomiting:** Copious, green/yellow **bilious output** (膽汁性嘔吐) within the first 24–48 hrs, typically after the 1st feed.
- **Distension:** Epigastric fullness/distension.
- **Bowel Habit:** Failure to pass meconium (though a small initial amount of pale meconium may pass).
- **Syndromic Signs:** Look for dysmorphic features of Down syndrome (e.g., upslanting palpebral fissures, single palmar crease, hypotonia).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Discriminatory Features vs Other GI Obstructions
| Feature | Duodenal Atresia (十二指腸閉鎖) | Pyloric Stenosis (幽門狹窄) | Malrotation w/ Volvulus (腸旋轉不良伴腸扭轉) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Age of Onset** | Day 1–2 | 2–8 weeks | Day 1–3 (or anytime) |
| **Vomitus** | **Bilious** (Green) | **Non-bilious** (White/Clear) | **Bilious** (Green) |
| **Key Sign** | Double bubble on AXR | Palpable "olive" mass, visible peristalsis | Sudden shock, PR bleeding, surgical emergency |

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
### High-Yield Differential Diagnoses
- **Most Likely:** Duodenal Atresia (esp. if Trisomy 21 + polyhydramnios).
- **Must-Not-Miss:** **Malrotation with Midgut Volvulus** (Surgical emergency → high risk of total bowel necrosis).
- **Other Atresias:** Jejunal/Ileal atresia (AXR shows multiple air-fluid levels).
- **Lower GI Causes:** Meconium Ileus (胎糞性腸梗阻) (rule out Cystic Fibrosis), Hirschsprung Disease (先天性巨結腸).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
### Bedside & Blood Investigations
- **VBG/ABG & U&E:** Monitor for dehydration and electrolyte shifts. Look for hypochloraemic hypokalaemic metabolic alkalosis (if vomiting is prolonged, though early presentation is often just isotonic dehydration).
### Imaging Investigations
- **Gold Standard Bedside Imaging:** **Abdominal X-ray (Erect/Decubitus)**.
  - **Pathognomonic Sign:** **"Double Bubble" Sign** (雙氣泡徵) → 1 large air bubble (stomach) + 1 smaller bubble (proximal duodenum) + **Absolute lack of distal bowel gas**.
- **Upper GI Contrast Study:** ONLY performed if any distal gas is present, to urgently rule out malrotation/volvulus.
### Special Investigations
- **Echocardiogram:** Mandatory pre-op to screen for congenital heart defects (esp. AVSD).
- **Genetics:** Karyotype/Microarray to confirm Trisomy 21.

## 8. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Acute Resuscitation & Optimization
- **Fasting:** Strict **NBM** (Nil By Mouth) (禁食).
- **Gastric Decompression:** Insert large-bore **Orogastric (OG) / Nasogastric (NG) tube** on free drainage with intermittent aspiration → prevents aspiration pneumonia.
- **IV Fluids:** Aggressive resuscitation if shocked. Commence maintenance fluids + 1:1 replacement of OG tube losses (usually 0.9% NaCl + KCl) to maintain electrolyte balance.
### Definitive Surgical Management
- **Procedure:** **Duodenoduodenostomy** (十二指腸吻合術) (Diamond-shaped anastomosis bypassing the atretic segment).
- **Timing:** This is an urgent, but **NOT** emergent surgery. The neonate must be fully resuscitated, electrolytes corrected, and cardiac status cleared (Echo) prior to theatre.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Multidisciplinary Support Systems
- **MDT Involvement:** Paediatric Surgeon, Neonatologist, Paediatric Cardiologist (if CHD present), Dietitian.
- **Psychosocial (MSW/SWD):** Referral to Medical Social Worker. If Down syndrome diagnosed, refer to HK Down Syndrome Association for parental support and early intervention.
- **MCHC Follow-up:** Long-term developmental, audiology, and ophthalmology screening for Trisomy 21.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Parental Counselling & Vulnerability
- **Counselling:** Parents must be prepared for a prolonged NICU stay and delayed enteral feeding post-op.
- **Safeguarding:** While a structural anomaly carries no direct NAI risk, the combination of a prolonged ICU admission, difficult feeding, and a new syndromic diagnosis significantly ↑ risk of parental bonding failure or neglect. MSW monitoring is essential.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
### Post-Operative & Long-Term Complications
- **Short-Term (Post-op):** Prolonged ileus and delayed gastric emptying are extremely common (the chronically dilated proximal duodenum takes weeks to regain normal motility) → necessitates prolonged **Total Parenteral Nutrition (TPN)**.
- **Long-Term:** Blind loop syndrome, megaduodenum, peptic ulcer disease, GERD.
- **Prognosis:** Excellent survival rate (>90%). Overall morbidity/mortality is largely dictated by the severity of associated anomalies (e.g., severe congenital heart defects).

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Trap: Assuming a "double bubble" on AXR without assessing distal gas is definitively isolated duodenal atresia. Pearl: Malrotation with volvulus or a duodenal web can also cause a double bubble. If ANY distal gas is seen, you MUST urgently rule out malrotation with an upper GI contrast study.
* Viva Question: A neonate with Down syndrome vomits green fluid on day 1 of life. What is the diagnosis until proven otherwise, and what will the X-ray show? Answer: Duodenal atresia. The X-ray will show the classic 'double bubble' sign with a complete absence of distal bowel gas.
* Pitfall: Rushing a stable neonate with isolated duodenal atresia to theatre immediately. Ensure adequate fluid resuscitation, electrolyte correction, and a mandatory Echocardiogram to rule out major structural heart defects (e.g., AVSD) prior to induction of anaesthesia.
