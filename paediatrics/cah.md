# Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia (先天性腎上腺增生症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition of Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia
* Autosomal recessive (AR) disorder of adrenal steroidogenesis
* Characterized by specific enzyme deficiency → ↓Cortisol synthesis → Loss of negative feedback → ↑ACTH secretion
* Result: Adrenal cortical hyperplasia + overproduction of intermediate steroid precursors (shunted to active androgens)

### Hong Kong Epidemiology of Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia
* Overall incidence in HK: ~1 in 10,000 to 15,000 live births
* Carrier frequency: ~1 in 60
* 21-hydroxylase deficiency (21-OHD) accounts for >90% of cases locally
* Phenotypes:
    * Classical Salt-Wasting (SW): 75% of classical cases
    * Classical Simple Virilizing (SV): 25% of classical cases
    * Non-Classical (NC-CAH): Late-onset, more common but milder

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Genetic Mutation in Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia
* Defect in *CYP21A2* gene 
* Located on Chromosome 6p21.3
* Causes 21-hydroxylase deficiency (21-羥化酶缺乏症)

### Adrenal Steroidogenesis Pathway Alterations
* ↓21-hydroxylase → Blockade of 17-hydroxyprogesterone (17-OHP) conversion to 11-deoxycortisol
* Blockade of Progesterone conversion to 11-deoxycorticosterone
* Pathophysiological consequences:
    * ↓Cortisol → Impaired stress response + ↑ACTH
    * ↓Aldosterone → Mineralocorticoid deficiency → Salt wasting (Na+ loss, K+ retention)
    * ↑ACTH → Drives precursor buildup (↑17-OHP) → Shunted to androgen synthesis pathway → ↑Testosterone → Virilization

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Neonatal Presentation of Salt-Wasting CAH
* Typically presents at days 7-14 of life
* Poor feeding
* Recurrent vomiting
* Lethargy
* Weight loss (failure to regain birth weight)
* Severe dehydration
* Hemodynamic instability (Shock)
* Arrhythmia (due to ↑K+)

### Clinical Signs of Virilization in Females (46,XX)
* Ambiguous genitalia (性別不明生殖器) at birth
* Clitoromegaly
* Labial fusion
* Single urogenital sinus
* Hyperpigmented labia majora (ACTH cross-reactivity with MSH receptors)

### Clinical Signs in Males (46,XY)
* Normal male genitalia at birth
* Macrogenitosomia (enlarged penis)
* Hyperpigmented scrotum
* Bilateral palpable testes (important differentiator from virilized females)

### Childhood Presentation of Simple Virilizing / Non-Classical CAH
* Precocious pseudopuberty (Premature pubarche)
* Rapid somatic growth 
* Accelerated bone age
* Early epiphyseal fusion → Adult short stature
* Severe acne
* Hirsutism (in females)
* Oligomenorrhea or primary amenorrhea

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Differentiating CAH from Pyloric Stenosis in a Vomiting Neonate
* Pyloric Stenosis profile:
    * Hypochloremic metabolic alkalosis
    * ↓Na+, ↓K+, ↓Cl-, ↑pH
    * Palpable "olive" mass
    * Visible peristalsis
* CAH (Adrenal Crisis) profile:
    * Metabolic acidosis
    * ↓Na+, ↑K+, ↓pH
    * Ambiguous genitalia or hyperpigmentation

### Differentiating CAH from Complete Androgen Insensitivity Syndrome (CAIS)
* CAH (46,XX):
    * Uterus present
    * Internal ovaries
    * Virilized external genitalia
* CAIS (46,XY):
    * Uterus absent
    * Internal testes
    * Normal female external genitalia

## 5. Differential Diagnosis
### Differential Diagnosis of Neonatal Virilization and Vomiting
| Condition | Pathophysiology | Key Distinctive Features |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **21-Hydroxylase CAH** | ↓Cortisol, ↓Aldosterone, ↑Androgens | ↑↑17-OHP, ↑K+, ↓Na+, ambiguous genitalia (XX) |
| **11β-Hydroxylase CAH** | ↓Cortisol, ↑DOC (mineralocorticoid), ↑Androgens | ↑Blood pressure, ↓K+, ↑Androgens |
| **Pyloric Stenosis** | Gastric outlet obstruction | Hypochloremic alkalosis, "olive" mass, normal genitalia |
| **Gastroenteritis** | Infectious diarrhea/vomiting | Normal K+, normal genitalia, infectious contacts |

### Prader Staging System for Ambiguous Genitalia
| Stage | Clinical Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Prader I** | Clitoromegaly only, normal female labia |
| **Prader II** | Clitoromegaly, distinct urethral & vaginal openings, partial labial fusion |
| **Prader III** | Clitoromegaly, single urogenital sinus, complete labial fusion |
| **Prader IV** | Phallic structure, urogenital sinus at base of phallus, rugated fused labia |
| **Prader V** | Apparent male penis with penile urethra, empty scrotum (non-palpable testes) |

## 6. Tiered Investigations
### First-Line Biochemical Investigations for CAH
* Serum 17-OHP (17-hydroxyprogesterone) - Markedly ↑ (>300 nmol/L is diagnostic)
* Venous blood gas (VBG) - Metabolic acidosis
* Serum electrolytes - Hyponatremia (低血鈉), Hyperkalemia (高血鉀)
* Random blood glucose - Hypoglycemia

### Gold Standard Diagnostic Test for CAH
* Short Synacthen Test (SST) / ACTH Stimulation Test
* Measures baseline and 60-minute post-ACTH levels of 17-OHP and Cortisol
* Confirms diagnosis in equivocal cases or Non-Classical CAH

### Imaging and Genetic Investigations for CAH
* Pelvic Ultrasound (USG)
    * Identifies presence of uterus and ovaries in virilized neonates
    * Confirms 46,XX karyotype internal structures
* Karyotyping (Chromosomal analysis)
    * Confirms genetic sex (46,XX vs 46,XY)
* *CYP21A2* molecular genetic testing
    * Identifies specific mutations for definitive diagnosis and genetic counseling

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Acute Management of Adrenal Crisis (腎上腺危機)
* Assess ABCDE (Airway, Breathing, Circulation, Disability, Exposure)
* Establish large-bore IV or intraosseous (IO) access
* Fluid resuscitation:
    * 0.9% NaCl (Normal Saline) bolus 20 mL/kg IV STAT
    * Repeat until hemodynamically stable
* Glucocorticoid replacement:
    * IV Hydrocortisone (氫化可的松) STAT
    * Dose: 25 mg (infants), 50 mg (toddlers/children), 100 mg (adolescents/adults)
* Correction of Hypoglycemia:
    * 10% Dextrose 2 mL/kg IV bolus
* Hyperkalemia management (if ECG changes or K+ >6.5 mmol/L):
    * IV Calcium Gluconate (cardioprotection)
    * Salbutamol nebulizer
    * IV Insulin + Dextrose infusion

### Chronic Pharmacological Management of CAH
| Medication Category | Drug Name | Dosage/Protocol | Target Goal |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Glucocorticoid** | Oral Hydrocortisone | 10-15 mg/m²/day (divided into 3 doses) | Suppress ACTH, prevent virilization, allow normal growth |
| **Mineralocorticoid** | Oral Fludrocortisone (氟氫可的松) | 0.05-0.2 mg/day (once or twice daily) | Maintain Na+/K+ balance, normalize plasma renin |
| **Salt Supplement** | Oral NaCl | 1-2 g/day (17-34 mmol/day) | Essential for infants <1 year on breastmilk/formula |

### Sick Day Rules and Stress Dosing for CAH
* Mild-to-Moderate Illness (Fever >38°C, infection):
    * Double the oral Hydrocortisone dose
    * Fludrocortisone dose remains unchanged
* Severe Illness (Vomiting, diarrhea, surgery, major trauma):
    * Switch to IM/IV Hydrocortisone immediately
    * Proceed to Emergency Department (A&E)

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Multidisciplinary Team Roles in CAH
* Pediatric Endocrinologist
* Pediatric Urologist / Gynecologist (for genital reconstructive surgery decisions)
* Clinical Geneticist
* Clinical Psychologist
* Specialized Endocrine Nurse

### Psychosocial Care and Counseling
* Gender assignment counseling for severely virilized 46,XX females
* Delayed surgical intervention consensus (allowing patient autonomy when older vs early functional reconstruction)
* Psychological support for gender identity and psychosexual development
* Genetic counseling for parents (1 in 4 recurrence risk for subsequent pregnancies)

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Safeguarding Concerns in CAH Management
* Non-compliance with medication
* Repeated admissions for avoidable adrenal crises
* Failure to thrive
* Loss to follow-up
* Medical neglect requiring social services (MSW) intervention

## 10. Complications & Prognosis
### Long-Term Complications of CAH
* Short final adult height (premature epiphyseal fusion)
* Testicular Adrenal Rest Tumors (TARTs) in males (can cause infertility)
* Polycystic Ovarian Syndrome (PCOS) and reduced fertility in females
* Obesity and Metabolic Syndrome (due to chronic glucocorticoid therapy)
* Osteopenia/Osteoporosis
* Recurrent life-threatening Adrenal Crises

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Viva Question: What is the classic electrolyte triad in a CAH salt-wasting crisis? Answer: Hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, and metabolic acidosis.
* Trap: Assuming a virilized neonate with bilateral palpable gonads has CAH. Pearl: Palpable gonads in a virilized neonate indicate male pseudohermaphroditism or normal male anatomy, as CAH females (46,XX) have internal ovaries (which are non-palpable).
* Pitfall: Delaying IV hydrocortisone in suspected adrenal crisis pending laboratory results. Give steroids immediately to prevent cardiovascular collapse; perform diagnostic blood tests later.
* Viva Question: Why do infants with salt-wasting CAH require oral sodium chloride supplements in addition to Fludrocortisone? Answer: Because infant formula and breastmilk are low in sodium, and immature infant kidneys are relatively resistant to mineralocorticoids.
