# Febrile Convulsion (熱性痙攣)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A seizure occurring in childhood after 1 month of age, associated with a febrile illness not caused by an infection of the CNS, without previous neonatal seizures or a previous unprovoked seizure.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Extremely common in Hong Kong. Affects 3-4% of healthy children. Peak age of onset is between 6 months and 5 years (most common at 18 months).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Pathophysiology**: The exact mechanism is unknown, but it is related to the vulnerability of the developing, immature brain to rapid temperature changes and circulating inflammatory cytokines (e.g., IL-1beta) during a fever.
- **Genetics**: Strong genetic predisposition. Autosomal dominant inheritance with incomplete penetrance. Associated with mutations in sodium channel genes (e.g., *SCN1A*, *SCN1B*). A positive family history in a first-degree relative significantly increases the risk.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Typically occurs on the first day of a febrile illness. The seizure is often triggered by the *rapid rise* in temperature rather than the absolute peak temperature.
- Common triggers in HK: Viral Upper Respiratory Tract Infections (URTI), Roseola Infantum (HHV-6), Influenza, Adenovirus, and post-vaccination (e.g., MMR).

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Simple Febrile Convulsion (80%)**:
  - Generalized (tonic-clonic: stiffening followed by rhythmic jerking).
  - Duration < 15 minutes.
  - Does NOT recur within a 24-hour period.
  - Resolves spontaneously with a brief post-ictal drowsy phase.
- **Complex Febrile Convulsion (20%)**:
  - Focal features (e.g., jerking of one limb or one side of the face).
  - Duration > 15 minutes.
  - Multiple seizures within a 24-hour period.
  - Incomplete recovery or post-ictal Todd's paresis.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Febrile Convulsion vs. Rigors**: Rigors (severe shivering) do not involve loss of consciousness, abnormal eye movements (uprolling), or post-ictal drowsiness. Holding the limb stops the shaking in rigors, but not in a clonic seizure.
- **Febrile Convulsion vs. CNS Infection**: Meningitis/Encephalitis presents with prolonged altered mental status, bulging fontanelle, nuchal rigidity, or petechial rash.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Meningitis / Encephalitis (CRITICAL to rule out).
- Epilepsy triggered by fever (e.g., Dravet syndrome).
- Rigors / Chills.
- Breath-holding spells (usually triggered by crying/frustration, not fever).
- Hypoglycaemia or electrolyte imbalance.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Simple Febrile Convulsion**: Routine blood tests, neuroimaging (CT/MRI), and EEG are **NOT** indicated. Diagnosis is clinical.
- **Bloods**: Only if clinically indicated to find the source of fever (e.g., CBP, CRP, Urine culture if no obvious focus).
- **Lumbar Puncture (LP)**: Strongly consider if:
  - Signs of meningeal irritation (neck stiffness, Kernig/Brudzinski signs).
  - Child is < 6-12 months old and unimmunized or incompletely immunized for *Haemophilus influenzae* type b (Hib) or *Streptococcus pneumoniae*.
  - The child has been pre-treated with antibiotics (which can mask meningitis signs).
  - Complex febrile convulsion features.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute Management (A&E)**:
  - ABCDE approach. Place in the recovery position.
  - Most stop spontaneously. If seizure lasts > 5 minutes (Status Epilepticus protocol): Give IV Lorazepam or PR Diazepam / Buccal Midazolam.
  - Identify and treat the source of the fever.
- **Chronic Management**:
  - **Reassurance**: This is the most important step. Explain that it is benign and does not cause brain damage.
  - **Antipyretics**: Paracetamol or Ibuprofen are used for comfort, but studies definitively show they **DO NOT** prevent febrile convulsions.
  - **Prophylaxis**: Long-term prophylactic anticonvulsants (e.g., Sodium Valproate) are NOT recommended due to side effects outweighing the benefits.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Parental Education**: High parental anxiety is universal (parents often think the child is dying). Teach first aid: stay calm, note the time, place on side, do not put anything in the mouth.
- **MCHC**: Routine follow-up to ensure normal developmental milestones.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Seizures without a documented fever in the A&E or ward.
- Retinal haemorrhages or subdural haematoma (signs of Abusive Head Trauma / Shaken Baby Syndrome).
- Inconsistent history of the event from caregivers.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Prognosis**: Excellent. No impact on intelligence, development, or behavior.
- **Recurrence Risk**: ~30% overall. Higher risk if: age < 1 year at first onset, family history of FC, short duration of fever before seizure, or relatively low fever at onset.
- **Risk of Epilepsy**: Slightly increased compared to the general population (1%). Risk is ~1-2% for simple FC, but increases to 5-10% if there are complex features, neurodevelopmental abnormalities, or a family history of epilepsy.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Pitfall*: Ordering an EEG for a simple febrile convulsion. (It has no predictive value for recurrence or epilepsy risk).
- *Viva*: "A mother asks if giving regular Paracetamol will prevent the next seizure. What do you say?" (No, antipyretics do not prevent febrile seizures, they only relieve discomfort).
- *Viva*: "What are the indications for a Lumbar Puncture in a child with a febrile seizure?" (Meningeal signs, complex features, incomplete Hib/Pneumococcal vaccination in infants).
