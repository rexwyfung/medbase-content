# Febrile Convulsion (熱性痙攣)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition of Febrile Convulsion
* **Definition**: Seizure in childhood (after 1 month of age), associated with febrile illness not caused by CNS infection, without previous neonatal/unprovoked seizures.
### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
* **Prevalence**: Extremely common in HK → Affects **3-4%** of healthy children.
* **Peak Age**: 6 months to 5 years (Peak incidence @ **18 months**).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Pathophysiology of Febrile Seizures
* **Mechanism**: Unknown exact mechanism → immature, developing brain highly vulnerable to rapid temperature ↑ and circulating inflammatory cytokines (e.g., **IL-1β**) during fever.
* **Trigger**: Seizure threshold ↓ by rapid rate of temperature rise rather than absolute peak temperature.
### Genetic Predisposition
* **Inheritance**: Autosomal dominant with incomplete penetrance.
* **Mutations**: Associated with sodium channel genes (e.g., **SCN1A**, **SCN1B**).
* **Family History**: +ve FHx in 1st-degree relative significantly ↑ risk (~10% risk).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
### Typical Clinical Timing
* **Onset**: Typically occurs on **Day 1** of febrile illness.
### Common Viral Triggers in Hong Kong
* **Viral URTI (上呼吸道感染)**
* **Roseola Infantum (玫瑰疹)** (HHV-6)
* **Influenza (流感)**
* **Adenovirus (腺病毒)**
* **Post-vaccination** (e.g., **MMR**)

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Classification: Simple vs. Complex Febrile Convulsion
| Feature | Simple Febrile Convulsion (單純性熱性痙攣) | Complex Febrile Convulsion (複雜性熱性痙攣) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Frequency** | 80% of cases | 20% of cases |
| **Type** | Generalized (tonic-clonic: stiffening → rhythmic jerking) | Focal features (e.g., 1 limb or 1 side of face jerking) |
| **Duration** | < 15 minutes | > 15 minutes |
| **Recurrence** | Does NOT recur within 24 hours | Multiple seizures within 24 hours |
| **Recovery** | Spontaneous resolution + brief post-ictal drowsiness | Incomplete recovery or post-ictal **Todd's paresis (托德麻痺)** |

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Differentiating Febrile Convulsion from Rigors
* **Rigors (寒戰)**: 
    * Severe shivering without loss of consciousness (LOC)
    * Normal eye movements
    * No post-ictal state
    * Holding limb → stops shaking
* **Febrile Convulsion (熱性痙攣)**: 
    * +ve LOC
    * Abnormal eye movements (uprolling)
    * +ve post-ictal drowsiness
    * Holding limb → shaking continues (clonic jerks)
### Differentiating Febrile Convulsion from CNS Infection
* **CNS Infection**: 
    * Prolonged altered mental status
    * Bulging fontanelle
    * Nuchal rigidity / meningism
    * Petechial rash / photophobia

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
### Critical Differential Diagnoses
* **Meningitis (腦膜炎) / Encephalitis (腦炎)** (CRITICAL to rule out)
* **Epilepsy (腦癇症)** triggered by fever (e.g., **Dravet syndrome (德拉韋綜合症)**)
* **Rigors / Chills (寒戰)**
* **Breath-holding spells (屏氣發作)**: Usually triggered by crying/frustration, NOT fever.
* **Hypoglycaemia (低血糖)** or electrolyte imbalance

## 7. Tiered Investigations
### Routine Bedside & Blood Investigations
* **Simple Febrile Convulsion**: Routine bloods, neuroimaging (CT/MRI), and EEG are **NOT** indicated (Clinical diagnosis).
* **Bloods**: Only if clinically indicated to identify fever source:
    * **CBP (全血細胞計數)**
    * **CRP (C反應蛋白)**
    * **Urine culture (尿液培養)** if no obvious focus
### Indications for Lumbar Puncture (LP)
* Strongly consider if:
    * +ve signs of meningeal irritation (neck stiffness, Kernig/Brudzinski signs).
    * Child < 6-12 months old AND unimmunized/incompletely immunized for **Hib** or **Streptococcus pneumoniae (肺炎鏈球菌)**.
    * Pre-treated with antibiotics (can mask meningitis signs).
    * +ve Complex febrile convulsion features.

## 8. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Acute A&E Management Protocol
* **Resuscitation**: **ABCDE** approach → Place in recovery position.
* **Observation**: Most stop spontaneously.
* **Status Epilepticus Protocol** (If seizure > 5 mins):
    * 1st Line: Give IV **Lorazepam (勞拉西泮)** OR PR **Diazepam (地西泮)** / Buccal **Midazolam (咪達唑侖)**.
* **Fever Control**: Identify and treat underlying fever source.
### Chronic Management & Prophylaxis
* **Reassurance**: Crucial step. Explain benign nature → does NOT cause brain damage.
* **Antipyretics**: **Paracetamol (撲熱息痛)** / **Ibuprofen (布洛芬)** for comfort. Studies definitively show they do **NOT** prevent febrile convulsions.
* **Prophylaxis**: Long-term prophylactic anticonvulsants (e.g., **Sodium Valproate (丙戊酸鈉)**) **NOT** recommended (Side effects > benefits).

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Parental Education & First Aid
* **High Parental Anxiety**: Universal (parents often fear child is dying).
* **First Aid Education**: 
    * Stay calm
    * Note time
    * Place on side (recovery position)
    * Do NOT insert objects into mouth
### Community Follow-up
* **MCHC (母嬰健康院)**: Routine follow-up to monitor normal developmental milestones.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Non-Accidental Injury (NAI) Indicators
* Seizures WITHOUT documented fever in A&E/ward.
* +ve **Retinal haemorrhages (視網膜出血)** or **Subdural haematoma (硬腦膜下血腫)** → signs of **Abusive Head Trauma (虐待性頭部創傷)** / **Shaken Baby Syndrome (搖嬰綜合症)**.
* Inconsistent/changing history from caregivers.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
### General Prognosis & Recurrence Risk
* **Prognosis**: Excellent. Normal intelligence, development, and behaviour.
* **Recurrence Risk**: ~**30%** overall.
    * ↑ Risk if: Age < 1 year at onset
    * +ve FHx of FC
    * Short fever duration before seizure
    * Low peak temperature at onset
### Risk of Developing Epilepsy
* **General Population Risk**: ~1%
* **Simple FC Risk**: ~1-2% (Slightly ↑)
* **Complex FC Risk**: 5-10% (Significantly ↑ if complex features, neurodevelopmental abnormalities, or +ve FHx of epilepsy).

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Pitfall: Ordering an EEG for a simple febrile convulsion. It has no predictive value for recurrence or epilepsy risk.
* Viva Question: A mother asks if giving regular Paracetamol will prevent the next seizure. What do you say? Answer: No, antipyretics do not prevent febrile seizures; they only relieve discomfort.
* Viva Question: What are the indications for a Lumbar Puncture in a child with a febrile seizure? Answer: Meningeal signs, complex features, or incomplete Hib/Pneumococcal vaccination in infants under 12 months.
* Trap: Assuming shivering with uprolling eyes is a rigor. Pearl: Rigors do not cause abnormal eye movements or post-ictal drowsiness; shaking that continues when the limb is held is clonic jerking, indicating a seizure.
