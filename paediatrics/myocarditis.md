# Myocarditis & Pericarditis (心肌炎及心包炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Myocarditis**: Inflammation of the cardiac muscle (myocardium) resulting in myocyte necrosis and cardiac dysfunction.
- **Pericarditis**: Inflammation of the fibroserous sac surrounding the heart.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Typically triggered by seasonal respiratory or gastrointestinal viruses (Enteroviruses, Parvovirus, Adenovirus). Historically noted as a rare side-effect of mRNA COVID-19 vaccines in adolescent males.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Usually viral. Direct viral damage to the myocytes is followed by a T-cell mediated autoimmune attack acting against the heart tissue, leading to edema, necrosis, and reduced contractility (myocarditis).
- In pericarditis, fibrinous exudate roughs up the smooth pericardial layers, causing pain when they rub together. Fluid can build up (pericardial effusion).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Frequently follows a 1-2 week prodrome of a simple "common cold" or "stomach bug".
- **Myocarditis**: Exertional dyspnea, fatigue, palpitations, and new-onset heart failure symptoms.
- **Pericarditis**: Acute, sharp, pleuritic chest pain that worsens when lying flat or breathing deeply, and significantly *improves* when sitting up and leaning forward.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Myocarditis**: Inappropriately fast heart rate (tachycardia independent of fever), gallop rhythm (S3), muffled heart sounds, hepatomegaly.
- **Pericarditis**: A harsh, scratching **pericardial friction rub** heard best at the left lower sternal border.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from musculoskeletal chest pain: Costochondritis hurts when you physically press on the chest wall (reproducible point tenderness); pericarditis does not.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Asthma/Pneumonia (for the breathlessness)
- Costochondritis (for chest pain)
- Cardiac Arrhythmias (SVT)
- PIMS-TS / Kawasaki Disease

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **ECG**: 
  - *Myocarditis*: Non-specific T wave changes, ventricular ectopics, or heart blocks.
  - *Pericarditis*: Widespread, diffuse saddle-shaped ST elevations and PR segment depressions.
- **Bloods**: Extremely elevated Troponin I or T, elevated BNP (marking stretch/failure). PCR for viruses.
- **Imaging**: Echocardiogram (checks for reduced Ejection Fraction in myocarditis, or a rim of fluid/effusion in pericarditis).
- **Gold Standard**: Cardiac MRI (shows patchy late gadolinium enhancement).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Myocarditis**: Purely supportive. Strict bed rest (to reduce myocardial oxygen demand). Inotropes (Milrinone) and diuretics if in heart failure. IVIG is sometimes used if autoimmune mediated. If fulminant, may require VA-ECMO (Extracorporeal Membrane Oxygenation) as a bridge to recovery or transplant.
- **Pericarditis**: High-dose NSAIDs (Ibuprofen) + Colchicine for 2-4 weeks. 

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Absolute restriction from competitive sports for 3 to 6 months post-recovery to prevent sudden death during exercise.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Fulminant myocarditis $\rightarrow$ fatal cardiogenic shock.
- Pericarditis $\rightarrow$ Pericardial Tamponade (effusion physically crushes the right heart, preventing filling $\rightarrow$ Beck's Triad: hypotension, muffled heart sounds, raised JVP). Requires emergency pericardiocentesis.
- Dilated cardiomyopathy later in life.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Giving beta-blockers for the "tachycardia" in acute myocarditis. Beta-blockers are negative inotropes; if the heart is already failing, you will throw them into instant cardiogenic shock.
- **Viva Q**: "Why does leaning forward relieve the pain of pericarditis?" Answer: Leaning forward pulls the inflamed anterior visceral and parietal pericardium away from the diaphragmatic pleura, reducing the painful friction generated by breathing and gravity.
