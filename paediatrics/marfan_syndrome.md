# Marfan Syndrome (馬凡氏綜合症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: An autosomal dominant connective tissue disorder affecting the skeletal, cardiovascular, and ocular systems.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Incidence ~1 in 5,000. Often underdiagnosed until catastrophic cardiovascular events.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Mutation in the **FBN1** gene (chromosome 15q21) which encodes **fibrillin-1**, a glycoprotein essential for the formation of elastic fibers in connective tissue.
- Structurally weak connective tissue directly leads to aortic dilation/rupture and extreme joint laxity. Furthermore, mutated fibrillin fails to bind TGF-beta properly, leading to excess TGF-beta signaling which causes tissue overgrowth.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- May present in childhood with unusually tall stature, scoliosis, or a subluxated lens noticed by optometry.
- A sudden family history of early sudden death (aortic dissection) is a massive red flag.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Skeletal**: Tall stature with long limbs (arm span > height), arachnodactyly (spider fingers - positive thumb and wrist signs), pectus excavatum or carinatum, severe scoliosis, high arched palate.
- **Ocular**: Ectopia lentis (upward, superior dislocation of the lens), severe myopia.
- **Cardiovascular**: Aortic root dilation, mitral valve prolapse (late systolic murmur with mid-systolic click).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Homocystinuria also causes marfanoid habitus, but has DOWNWARD lens dislocation, intellectual disability, and thrombotic risks.
- Loeys-Dietz syndrome has similar aortic issues but typically has a bifid uvula / cleft palate and generalized arterial tortuosity.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Homocystinuria
- Loeys-Dietz Syndrome
- Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome (has hypermobility but lacks the tall stature/arachnodactyly).
- Klinefelter Syndrome (XXY) - tall, but distinct hypogonadism.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside/Clinical**: Modified Ghent criteria assessment.
- **Imaging**: Echocardiogram (mandatory, serial measurements of the aortic root diameter/Z-score).
- **Special**: Slit-lamp exam by Ophthalmology. FBN1 genetic testing (definitive).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Cardiovascular**: Medical treatment to slow aortic root dilation using Beta-blockers (Atenolol/Propranolol) or Angiotensin II receptor blockers (Losartan, which also antagonizes TGF-beta signaling).
- **Surgical**: Prophylactic aortic root replacement when the root diameter exceeds specific thresholds (usually >5cm or rapid growth).
- **Lifestyle**: Absolute prohibition of isometric exercises (weightlifting) and contact sports to prevent aortic dissection.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Cardiology, Orthopaedics (for progressive scoliosis), Ophthalmology. Genetic counseling for family members is critical.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Aortic rupture risk.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Aortic root dissection or rupture (primary cause of sudden death).
- Without surgery, life expectancy is reduced. With rigorous monitoring and modern surgery, life expectancy approaches normal.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Forgetting to differentiate from Homocystinuria.
- **Viva Q**: "What is the Steinberg wrist sign and Walker-Murdoch thumb sign?" Answer: Wrist sign = thumb and little finger overlap when wrapped around the opposite wrist. Thumb sign = entire distal phalanx of thumb extends beyond the ulnar border of the clenched fist.
