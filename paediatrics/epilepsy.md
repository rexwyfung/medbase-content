# Epilepsy (腦癇症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A neurological disorder characterized by an enduring predisposition to generate unprovoked epileptic seizures. Clinically defined as having >= 2 unprovoked seizures occurring > 24 hours apart, or one unprovoked seizure with a high probability of further seizures.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Affects approximately 0.5-1% of the paediatric population in Hong Kong. It is one of the most common chronic neurological conditions managed in HA paediatric clinics.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Pathophysiology**: A sudden, abnormal, hypersynchronous electrical discharge of cortical neurons. It results from an imbalance between excitatory (Glutamate) and inhibitory (GABA) neurotransmission.
- **Aetiology (ILAE Classification)**:
  - *Structural*: Cortical dysplasia, brain tumours, hypoxic-ischaemic injury, stroke.
  - *Genetic*: e.g., Dravet syndrome (*SCN1A* mutation), Childhood Absence Epilepsy.
  - *Infectious*: Post-meningitis or encephalitis.
  - *Metabolic*: Inborn errors of metabolism.
  - *Immune*: Autoimmune encephalitis (e.g., Anti-NMDA receptor encephalitis).
  - *Unknown*.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
Presentation depends entirely on the seizure type and epilepsy syndrome:
- **Generalized Seizures** (involve both hemispheres from onset):
  - *Absence*: Brief staring spells (<15 seconds), immediate recovery, no post-ictal confusion. Often misdiagnosed as "daydreaming" at school.
  - *Tonic-Clonic (GTCS)*: Sudden loss of consciousness, stiffening (tonic), followed by rhythmic jerking (clonic), cyanosis, and a prolonged post-ictal sleep/confusion phase.
  - *Myoclonic*: Sudden, brief, shock-like muscle jerks (e.g., Juvenile Myoclonic Epilepsy).
- **Focal Seizures** (originate in one hemisphere):
  - Can be with or without impairment of awareness.
  - Symptoms depend on the lobe involved (e.g., motor jerking of one arm, sensory tingling, visual hallucinations, or automatisms like lip-smacking/fumbling).
- **Infantile Spasms (West Syndrome)**: Occurs at 4-8 months. Clusters of sudden flexion/extension of the neck, trunk, and limbs, usually upon waking.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **History is paramount**: Obtain a detailed eyewitness account or smartphone video.
- Look for: Aura (warning sign indicating focal onset), lateralizing signs (head turning to one side), automatisms, cyanosis, tongue biting, urinary incontinence.
- **Post-ictal state**: Todd's paresis (temporary focal weakness after a seizure) strongly suggests a focal structural lesion.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Syncope (Fainting)**: Usually has a clear trigger (standing, pain, heat), a prodrome (dizziness, tunnel vision, pallor, sweating), and rapid recovery upon lying flat without prolonged confusion.
- **Non-Epileptic Attack Disorder (Pseudoseizures)**: Often features asynchronous movements, pelvic thrusting, side-to-side head shaking, eyes tightly closed (resisting opening), and a normal EEG during the event.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Syncope (Vasovagal or Cardiac - e.g., Long QT syndrome).
- Breath-holding spells (in toddlers, triggered by crying/frustration).
- Tics / Tourette syndrome.
- Sleep disorders (Night terrors, Narcolepsy).
- Migraine variants.
- Non-epileptic seizures (psychogenic).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Electroencephalogram (EEG)**: Essential to classify the seizure type and syndrome. 
  - *Classic findings*: 3 Hz generalized spike-and-wave in Childhood Absence Epilepsy; Hypsarrhythmia in West Syndrome.
  - *Note: A normal interictal EEG does NOT rule out epilepsy.*
- **Neuroimaging (MRI Brain)**: The modality of choice to look for structural causes. Indicated for all focal seizures, onset < 2 years of age, abnormal neurological exam, or refractory epilepsy.
- **Bloods/Genetics**: Metabolic screen, chromosomal microarray, or epilepsy gene panels if specific syndromes are suspected.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute (Status Epilepticus > 5 mins)**:
  - ABCDE approach.
  - 1st line: IV Lorazepam or Buccal Midazolam / PR Diazepam.
  - 2nd line: IV Levetiracetam, Phenytoin, or Sodium Valproate.
  - 3rd line: Rapid Sequence Induction (RSI) and PICU admission for Midazolam or Thiopentone infusion.
- **Chronic (Anti-Seizure Medications - ASMs)**: Usually started after the 2nd unprovoked seizure.
  - *Focal*: Carbamazepine, Levetiracetam.
  - *Generalized*: Sodium Valproate (AVOID in females of childbearing age due to severe teratogenicity and PCOS risk), Levetiracetam, Lamotrigine.
  - *Absence*: Ethosuximide.
  - *Infantile Spasms*: Vigabatrin, ACTH/Oral Steroids.
- **Refractory Epilepsy**: Ketogenic diet, Vagal Nerve Stimulation (VNS), or Epilepsy Surgery (e.g., lesionectomy, corpus callosotomy).

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **School Care Plan**: Teachers must be trained to administer rescue Buccal Midazolam.
- **Safety Advice**: Avoid unsupervised swimming, bathing (use showers), and climbing heights.
- **Psychology**: High rates of comorbid ADHD, anxiety, and learning difficulties.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Seizures resulting from occult head trauma (Abusive Head Trauma / Shaken Baby Syndrome).
- Fabricated or Induced Illness (Munchausen by proxy) - a caregiver reporting fake seizures to obtain medication or medical attention.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Complications**: Status epilepticus, Sudden Unexpected Death in Epilepsy (SUDEP), physical injuries during seizures.
- **Prognosis**: Highly dependent on the syndrome. 
  - *Good*: Childhood Absence Epilepsy and Benign Rolandic Epilepsy are often outgrown by adolescence.
  - *Poor*: Epileptic encephalopathies (e.g., West Syndrome, Lennox-Gastaut Syndrome) have a poor prognosis for seizure control and severe cognitive impairment.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "What is the classic EEG finding in Childhood Absence Epilepsy, and how can you provoke it in the clinic?" (3 Hz generalized spike-and-wave discharge; provoked by 3 minutes of hyperventilation).
- *Viva*: "Why is Sodium Valproate contraindicated as first-line therapy in teenage girls?" (It is highly teratogenic, causing neural tube defects, and is associated with Polycystic Ovary Syndrome).
- *Pitfall*: Diagnosing epilepsy after a single unprovoked seizure (unless EEG/MRI show a very high risk of recurrence).
