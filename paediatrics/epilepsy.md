# Epilepsy (腦癇症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Epilepsy
* **Epilepsy (腦癇症)**: Neurological disorder → enduring predisposition to unprovoked seizures.
* **Diagnostic Criteria**:
  * $\ge$ 2 unprovoked seizures occurring > 24 hrs apart OR
  * 1 unprovoked seizure + high probability of further seizures.

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
* Affects ~0.5-1% of paediatric pop in HK.
* Most common chronic neurological condition managed in HA paediatric clinics.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics

### Core Pathophysiology of Epilepsy
* Sudden, abnormal, hypersynchronous electrical discharge of cortical neurons.
* Imbalance: **↑ Excitatory** (Glutamate) vs **↓ Inhibitory** (GABA) neurotransmission.

### ILAE Aetiological Classification
* **Structural**: Cortical dysplasia, brain tumours, Hypoxic-Ischaemic Encephalopathy (HIE), stroke.
* **Genetic**: e.g., Dravet syndrome (**SCN1A mutation**), Childhood Absence Epilepsy.
* **Infectious**: Post-meningitis, post-encephalitis.
* **Metabolic**: Inborn errors of metabolism.
* **Immune**: Autoimmune encephalitis (e.g., Anti-NMDA receptor encephalitis).
* **Unknown**.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Generalized Seizures (兩側大腦半球發作)
* Involve both hemispheres from onset.
* **Absence (失神發作)**: Brief staring spells (<15s) → immediate recovery, no post-ictal confusion. Often misdiagnosed as "daydreaming".
* **Tonic-Clonic (強直陣攣發作/GTCS)**: Sudden LOC → stiffening (tonic) → rhythmic jerking (clonic) → cyanosis → prolonged post-ictal sleep/confusion.
* **Myoclonic (肌陣攣發作)**: Sudden, brief, shock-like muscle jerks (e.g., Juvenile Myoclonic Epilepsy).

### Focal Seizures (局部性腦癇發作)
* Originate in one hemisphere.
* With or without awareness impairment.
* Symptoms depend on lobe (e.g., motor jerking of one arm, sensory tingling, visual hallucinations, automatisms like lip-smacking/fumbling).

### Infantile Spasms (嬰兒點頭痙攣 / West Syndrome)
* Onset: 4-8 months.
* Clusters of sudden flexion/extension of neck, trunk, limbs.
* Usually occurs upon waking.

### Clinical Signs & History Taking in Epilepsy
* **History**: Eyewitness account or smartphone video is paramount.
* **Key Features**:
  * **Aura**: Warning sign indicating focal onset.
  * **Lateralizing signs**: Head turning to one side.
  * Automatisms, cyanosis, tongue biting, urinary incontinence.
* **Post-ictal state**: **Todd's paresis** (temporary focal weakness) → strongly suggests focal structural lesion.

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)

### Differentiating Syncope from Epilepsy
* **Trigger**: Clear (standing, pain, heat).
* **Prodrome**: Dizziness, tunnel vision, pallor, sweating.
* **Recovery**: Rapid upon lying flat without prolonged post-ictal confusion.

### Differentiating NEAD from Epilepsy
* **NEAD (Non-Epileptic Attack Disorder / 假性發作)** features:
  * Asynchronous movements, pelvic thrusting, side-to-side head shaking.
  * Eyes tightly closed (resisting opening).
  * Normal EEG during the event.

## 5. Differential Diagnosis

### Common Differentials for Paediatric Seizures
| Condition | Key Differentiating Feature |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Syncope (暈厥)** | Prodrome +ve, rapid recovery flat. Cardiac (Long QT) → exertional/sudden. |
| **Breath-holding spells** | Toddlers, triggered by crying/frustration → cyanosis/pallor → brief LOC. |
| **Tics / Tourette syndrome** | Stereotypic, suppressible, no LOC, consciousness retained. |
| **Sleep disorders** | Night terrors (no recall, deep sleep), Narcolepsy. |
| **Migraine variants** | Gradual onset aura, severe headache follows. |
| **Psychogenic (NEAD)** | Eyes closed, asynchronous movements, normal interictal/ictal EEG. |

## 6. Tiered Investigations

### Bedside & Blood Investigations for Seizures
* **Bedside**: Smartphone video review.
* **Bloods**: Metabolic screen (glucose, electrolytes, Ca/Mg), toxicology.

### EEG Findings in Paediatric Epilepsy
* **Electroencephalogram (EEG)**: Essential to classify seizure type/syndrome.
* **Classic Findings**:
  * **Childhood Absence Epilepsy**: 3 Hz generalized spike-and-wave.
  * **West Syndrome**: Hypsarrhythmia (chaotic background, multifocal spikes/sharp waves).
* *Note*: Normal interictal EEG does NOT rule out epilepsy.

### Neuroimaging (MRI Brain) Indications for Seizures
* Modality of choice for structural causes.
* **Indications**: Focal seizures, onset < 2 yrs, abnormal neuro exam, refractory epilepsy, developmental regression.

### Special/Genetic Investigations for Seizures
* **Genetics**: Chromosomal microarray, targeted epilepsy gene panels (for specific epileptic encephalopathies).

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)

### Acute Management: Status Epilepticus (癲癇持續狀態)
* Definition: Seizure > 5 mins. Apply ABCDE approach.
* **1st Line (0-10 mins)**: **IV Lorazepam** OR **Buccal Midazolam** / PR Diazepam.
* **2nd Line (10-30 mins)**: **IV Levetiracetam**, **IV Phenytoin**, or **IV Sodium Valproate**.
* **3rd Line (>30 mins)**: Rapid Sequence Induction (RSI) → PICU admission for **Midazolam** or **Thiopentone** infusion.

### Chronic Management: Anti-Seizure Medications (ASMs)
* Usually started after 2nd unprovoked seizure.

| Seizure Type | 1st Line ASMs | Key Notes / HA Context |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Focal** | **Carbamazepine**, **Levetiracetam** | Carbamazepine → watch for HLA-B*1502 (Stevens-Johnson risk in Han Chinese). |
| **Generalized** | **Sodium Valproate**, **Levetiracetam**, **Lamotrigine** | Valproate → **AVOID in females of childbearing age** (highly teratogenic, PCOS). |
| **Absence** | **Ethosuximide** | Valproate is alternative if GTCS also present. |
| **Infantile Spasms** | **Vigabatrin**, **ACTH / Oral Steroids** | Vigabatrin → irreversible visual field constriction risk. |

### Management of Refractory Epilepsy
* **Ketogenic Diet**: High fat, low carb → alters brain metabolism to raise seizure threshold.
* **Vagal Nerve Stimulation (VNS)**: Implanted pacemaker for vagus nerve.
* **Epilepsy Surgery**: Lesionectomy, corpus callosotomy, hemispherotomy.

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care

### School & Community Care Plan for Epilepsy
* **Teachers**: Must be trained to administer rescue **Buccal Midazolam**.
* **Safety Advice**: Avoid unsupervised swimming, bathing (use showers), and climbing heights.

### Psychological Support in Childhood Epilepsy
* High rates of comorbid **ADHD**, anxiety, and learning difficulties.
* Require Medical Social Worker (MSW) and Clinical Psychologist assessment for educational support.

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags

### Abusive Head Trauma (AHT) presenting as Seizures
* Seizures resulting from occult head trauma (Shaken Baby Syndrome).
* Look for retinal haemorrhages, subdural haematomas, disproportionate/changing caregiver history.

### Munchausen Syndrome by Proxy (FII) presenting as Seizures
* Fabricated or Induced Illness (FII).
* Caregiver reporting fake seizures (or inducing them via suffocation/poisoning) to obtain medication or medical attention.
* Clue: Seizures only happen when caregiver is present, normal EEG/video telemetry.

## 10. Complications & Prognosis

### Complications of Epilepsy
* **Status Epilepticus**: Brain damage, hypoxia, death.
* **SUDEP**: Sudden Unexpected Death in Epilepsy (↑ risk in uncontrolled GTCS, nocturnal seizures).
* Physical injuries during seizures (burns, fractures, drowning).

### Prognosis of Specific Paediatric Epilepsy Syndromes
* **Good Prognosis**: **Childhood Absence Epilepsy** & **Benign Rolandic Epilepsy** → often outgrown by adolescence.
* **Poor Prognosis**: **Epileptic Encephalopathies** (e.g., West Syndrome, Lennox-Gastaut Syndrome) → severe cognitive impairment, refractory to ASMs.

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Viva Question: What is the classic EEG finding in Childhood Absence Epilepsy, and how can you provoke it in the clinic? Answer: 3 Hz generalized spike-and-wave discharge; provoked by 3 minutes of hyperventilation.
* Viva Question: Why is Sodium Valproate contraindicated as first-line therapy in teenage girls? Answer: It is highly teratogenic (causing neural tube defects) and is associated with Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS).
* Pitfall: Diagnosing epilepsy after a single unprovoked seizure. Only diagnose if EEG/MRI show a very high risk of recurrence; otherwise, watchful waiting is standard practice.
* Trap: Assuming a normal interictal EEG rules out epilepsy. Pearl: A normal interictal EEG does NOT rule out epilepsy; the diagnosis is primarily clinical based on history and video evidence.
