# Oculocutaneous Albinism (OCA) (眼皮膚白化症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Heterogeneous group of autosomal recessive (AR) disorders of **melanin biosynthesis** (黑色素生合成) leading to generalized hypopigmentation of hair, skin, and eyes.
- **Local Epidemiology (Hong Kong)**:
  - Rare in HK.
  - **OCA1** (tyrosinase-related) & **OCA2** (P-protein related) most common subtypes globally; local subtype prevalence data limited.
  - Requires lifelong management within Hong Kong Hospital Authority (HA) (醫院管理局) Dermatology & Ophthalmology clinics.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Genetics**: Strictly **Autosomal Recessive (AR)** (體數色體隱性遺傳).
- **Basic Mechanism**: Defect in melanin production pathway within melanocytes.
- **Subtypes & Defects**:
  - **OCA1**: Mutation in *TYR* gene → defect in **Tyrosinase** (酪氨酸酶) enzyme.
    - **OCA1a**: Complete absence of tyrosinase activity → **No melanin** produced ever.
    - **OCA1b**: Reduced tyrosinase activity → Minimal melanin produced over time.
  - **OCA2**: Mutation in *OCA2* gene → defect in P-protein (melanosomal transmembrane protein) → affects melanosomal pH & tyrosinase processing.
  - **OCA3**: Mutation in *TYRP1* gene (rare).
  - **OCA4**: Mutation in *SLC45A2* gene (rare).
- **Developmental Impact**:
  - Lack of melanin in embryogenesis → aberrant optic nerve fibre decussation at **chiasm** (視交叉) (excessive contralateral projection) & **foveal hypoplasia** (黃斑中心凹發育不良).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Present at birth with striking hypopigmentation (depending on subtype).
- Nystagmus and poor visual acuity often the presenting complaints in infancy if hypopigmentation is subtle (e.g., OCA1b/OCA2).

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Cutaneous (Skin/Hair)**:
  - Hair: Snow-white (OCA1a) to pale blonde/golden/light brown (OCA1b/OCA2).
  - Skin: Milky white (OCA1a) to creamy white/light beige (OCA1b/OCA2).
  - Inability to tan; extreme **photosensitivity** (光敏感).
- **Ocular (Mandatory for Diagnosis)**:
  - **Congenital Nystagmus** (先天性眼球震顫): Involuntary, horizontal, typically sensory defect nystagmus.
  - **Iris Transillumination** (虹膜透照): Due to lack of iris pigment; red reflex visible through the iris tissue.
  - Irises: Blue/pink (OCA1a) to hazel/light brown.
  - **Profound Photophobia** (畏光).
  - **Foveal Hypoplasia** (黃斑中心凹發育不良): Visible on ophthalmoscopy (loss of foveal reflex).
  - Significantly reduced visual acuity (typically 6/60 or worse).
  - Strabismus (斜視) & reduced stereopsis.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Must distinguish isolated OCA from **Syndromic Albinism** (requires urgent referral due to systemic implications):
| Feature | Isolated OCA | Hermansky-Pudlak Syndrome (HPS) | Chediak-Higashi Syndrome (CHS) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Pigmentation** | Generalized ↓ | Generalized ↓ | Generalized ↓ (often silvery hair) |
| **Hematology** | Normal | **Platelet storage pool deficiency** → Bleeding diathesis (易出血 tendancy) | **Giant lysosomal granules** in WBCs; Neutropenia |
| **Immunology** | Normal | Normal | **Severe Immunodeficiency** → Recurrent infections |
| **Other** | - | **Pulmonary fibrosis** (肺纖維化), Granulomatous colitis | Progressive neurologic degeneration; Hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis (HLH) |

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Ocular Albinism (OA)** (眼白化症): X-linked recessive; effects limited to eyes; skin/hair normal or slightly lighter than relatives.
- **Piebaldism** (斑駁病): Autosomal dominant; stable patches of leukoderma (depigmented skin) and a white forelock; **no ocular involvement**.
- **Vitiligo** (白癜風): Acquired autoimmune destruction of melanocytes; progressive, patchy depigmentation; onset usually post-natal; **no congenital ocular involvement**.
- **Waardenburg Syndrome**: Sensory hearing loss, dystopia canthorum, heterochromia iridis, white forelock; localized hypopigmentation.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Clinical Diagnosis**: Based on characteristic ocular signs + generalized hypopigmentation.
- **Dermatology/Ophthalmology Exam**: Slit-lamp exam for iris transillumination, fundoscopy for foveal hypoplasia.
- **Specialized Ocular Tests**:
  - **Visual Evoked Potentials (VEP)** (視覺誘發電位): Shows asymmetric cortical responses indicating **misrouting of optic pathways** at chiasm (hallmark pathognomonic finding, though rarely needed clinically).
  - Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT): Confirms foveal hypoplasia.
- **Genetic Testing**: Molecular genetic testing (gene panels) to confirm specific subtype and for genetic counselling.
- **Screening for Syndromes** (if clinically suspected):
  - Blood smear for giant granules (CHS).
  - Platelet aggregation studies (HPS).

## 8. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **No cure** exists for the underlying defect. Management is supportive and preventive.
- **Dermatological Care (HA Dermatology Clinic)**:
  - **Strict Sun Protection** (防曬): Lifetime adherence mandatory to prevent malignancy.
  - Broad-spectrum, high-factor sunscreen (**SPF 50+**, PA+++) applied daily.
  - Sun-protective clothing (UPF 50+), wide-brimmed hats, seeking shade.
  - Avoid mid-day sun (10 AM - 4 PM).
  - Regular (6-12 monthly) total body skin checks by dermatologist for pre-malignant lesions (actinic keratoses) or skin cancer.
- **Ophthalmological Care (HA Ophthalmology Clinic)**:
  - Refractive error correction (glasses/contacts) to optimize remaining vision.
  - Tinted glasses or photochromic lenses (變色鏡片) for **photophobia**.
  - No effective treatment for nystagmus; occasionally eye muscle surgery (Kestenbaum procedure) to shift null point if significant head turn is present.
  - Low vision aids (magnifiers, electronic aids) prescribed by low vision specialists.
- **Other**: **Genetic Counselling** (遺傳諮詢) for parents (recurrence risk for subsequent pregnancies is 1 in 4).

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **MDT approach**: Pediatrician, Dermatologist, Ophthalmologist, Geneticist, Low Vision Specialist.
- **Maternal & Child Health Centres (MCHC)** (母嬰健康院): Initial detection and referral during routine child health surveillance.
- **Medical Social Worker (MSW)** (醫務社會工作者): Assess need for Social Welfare Department (SWD) (社會福利署) support, disability allowance (due to visual impairment).
- **Educational Support**: Coordinate with school via Special Educational Needs (SEN) coordinators.
  - Use of high-contrast materials, large print textbooks, preferential seating (front of class), and assistive technology.
- **Psychosocial Support**: Address issues of visible difference, teasing/bullying, and social stigma. Referral to clinical psychology if needed. Connection with support groups (e.g., Hong Kong Albinism Association).

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Failure of parents to adhere to strict sun protection protocols constitutes **medical neglect** (醫療忽視).
- Child presenting with severe, recurrent sunburns should raise safeguarding concerns.
- Risk of skin cancer at extremely young ages (<20 years) if neglected.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Dermatological**:
  - Premature skin aging (pachyderma, solar elastosis).
  - **Actinic Keratoses** (光化性角化症) (pre-malignant).
  - High risk of early-onset **Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC)** (鱗狀細胞癌) and **Basal Cell Carcinoma (BCC)** (基底細胞癌). Melanoma is less common but can occur (often amelanotic).
- **Ocular**:
  - Permanent **visual impairment** (視力障礙); usually legally blind. Stereopsis is poor.
  - Nystagmus and visual acuity generally stable throughout life (do not deteriorate).
- **Prognosis**: Normal life expectancy *unless* fatal syndromic forms (CHS/HPS) or advanced skin cancer develops.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **"Why is vision poor in albinism?"**: Foveal hypoplasia (lack of detail vision) + optic pathway misrouting (impaired binocular vision/stereopsis). Melanin is essential for normal embryological development of these structures.
- **"How can you differentiate OCA1a from other types?"**: OCA1a has **zero** pigment production; hair remains white and skin white throughout life. Other types (OCA1b, OCA2) develop some pigment (yellow/brown hair, pigmented nevi, ability to tan slightly) with age.
- **Critical association**: If an "albino" child presents with unusual bleeding/bruising, suspect **Hermansky-Pudlak Syndrome**; if they have recurrent severe infections, suspect **Chediak-Higashi Syndrome**.
- **Local Pitfall**: Assuming HK sun exposure is negligible; UV index in HK is frequently high/extreme, requiring rigorous protection year-round.
- **Definition Check**: Oculocutaneous Albinism affects **Skin, Hair, AND Eyes**. If only eyes are affected, it is Ocular Albinism.
