# Undescended Testis (Cryptorchidism) (隱睪症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: The failure of one or both testes to descend completely into the scrotum. The testis may be located in the abdomen, inguinal canal, or just outside the external ring.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Very common. Affects ~3-5% of full-term male infants and up to 30% of premature male infants. The majority will descend spontaneously within the first 3-6 months of life.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Embryology**: The testes develop in the abdomen (near the kidneys). During the 3rd trimester, guided by the gubernaculum and driven by androgens, they descend through the inguinal canal into the scrotum.
- **Pathophysiology**: Failure of this process results in cryptorchidism. The higher temperature in the abdomen/inguinal canal impairs spermatogenesis (leading to infertility) and increases the risk of malignant transformation.
- **Associated Anomalies**: Almost all true undescended testes are associated with a patent processus vaginalis (an indirect inguinal hernia).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Usually an asymptomatic finding during the routine newborn physical examination or at MCHC checks.
- The scrotum appears empty, hypoplastic, or asymmetrical.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Palpation**:
  - *Palpable*: The testis can be felt in the inguinal canal or groin.
  - *Impalpable*: The testis cannot be felt (it may be intra-abdominal, absent/agenesis, or atrophic).
- **Retractile Testis (Crucial to distinguish)**: The testis is pulled high into the groin by a hyperactive cremasteric reflex. *Key sign*: It can be manually manipulated down into the base of the scrotum and will **stay there temporarily** without tension. This is a normal variant and does not require surgery.
- **True Undescended Testis**: Cannot be manipulated into the scrotum, or immediately springs back up under tension when released.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Retractile Testis**: Can be brought into the scrotum and stays there. True undescended testis cannot.
- **Ectopic Testis**: The testis has descended through the external ring but then deviated from the normal path (e.g., to the perineum, femoral triangle, or superficial inguinal pouch).

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Retractile testis.
- Ectopic testis.
- Absent testis (anorchia) - e.g., due to in utero torsion.
- Disorders of Sex Development (DSD) - especially if bilateral impalpable testes or associated with hypospadias.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Unilateral Palpable Testis**: NO imaging is required. Diagnosis is clinical. Ultrasound is not recommended as it does not change management.
- **Bilateral Impalpable Testes**:
  - *Urgent investigation required* to rule out a Disorder of Sex Development (e.g., Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia in a virilized female).
  - **Karyotype**, 17-OH Progesterone, electrolytes.
  - **Diagnostic Laparoscopy**: The gold standard to locate intra-abdominal testes or confirm anorchia.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Observation**: For the first 6 months of life, as spontaneous descent can still occur (especially in premature infants).
- **Surgical Management (Orchidopexy)**:
  - **Timing**: Recommended between **6 and 12 months of age**.
  - *Why early?* To maximize fertility potential (histological degradation of the testis begins as early as 6-12 months) and to allow for self-examination for testicular cancer later in life.
  - **Procedure**: The testis is mobilized, the associated hernia sac (patent processus vaginalis) is ligated, and the testis is fixed in a sub-dartos pouch in the scrotum.
- **Intra-abdominal Testis**: Requires a laparoscopic orchidopexy (often a 2-stage Fowler-Stephens procedure).

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **MCHC**: Routine screening at newborn, 6-week, and 6-month checks is vital for timely referral to Paediatric Surgery.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- N/A for this condition.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Infertility**: Risk is increased, especially with bilateral undescended testes, even after surgery.
- **Testicular Cancer**: The risk of malignancy (seminoma) is increased 3-5 fold. Orchidopexy does *not* eliminate this risk, but it brings the testis into the scrotum so that any lump can be easily palpated and detected early.
- **Testicular Torsion**: Increased risk if left undescended.
- **Inguinal Hernia**: Associated with a patent processus vaginalis.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "How do you clinically differentiate a retractile testis from a true undescended testis?" (A retractile testis can be gently manipulated into the base of the scrotum and will remain there temporarily without tension when released. A true undescended testis cannot be brought down, or immediately springs back up).
- *Viva*: "At what age should an orchidopexy be performed for an undescended testis, and what are the two main reasons for doing the surgery?" (Between 6 and 12 months of age. The reasons are to optimize future fertility and to allow for easy palpation of the testis to detect testicular cancer early).
- *Pitfall*: Ordering an ultrasound for a unilateral palpable undescended testis. It is unnecessary, often inaccurate, and delays surgical referral.
