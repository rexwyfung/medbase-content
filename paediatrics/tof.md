# Tetralogy of Fallot (TOF) (法洛四聯症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: The most common cyanotic congenital heart disease presenting beyond the neonatal period. 
- **Local Epidemiology**: Accounts for approximately 7-10% of all congenital heart defects.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **The Four Classic Features**: All stem from a single embryological error: the anterior and cephalad deviation of the infundibular septum.
  1. **Ventricular Septal Defect (VSD)**: Usually large and non-restrictive.
  2. **Overriding Aorta**: The aorta is shifted to the right and sits directly over the VSD, receiving blood from both the left and right ventricles.
  3. **Right Ventricular Outflow Tract Obstruction (RVOTO)**: Pulmonary stenosis, which can be infundibular (subvalvular muscle spasm), valvular, or supravalvular.
  4. **Right Ventricular Hypertrophy (RVH)**: Secondary to pumping against the RVOTO.
- **Haemodynamics**: The degree of cyanosis depends *entirely* on the severity of the RVOTO. Blood takes the path of least resistance. If the pulmonary stenosis is severe, deoxygenated blood from the RV shunts Right-to-Left across the VSD into the overriding aorta, causing systemic cyanosis.
- **Genetics**: Strongly associated with **DiGeorge Syndrome (22q11.2 microdeletion)** in about 15% of cases.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
Presentation varies widely based on the degree of pulmonary stenosis:
- **Severe RVOTO**: Profound cyanosis immediately at birth. The pulmonary circulation is duct-dependent (relies on the PDA).
- **Mild RVOTO ("Pink Tet")**: The infant may not be cyanotic initially and presents only with a murmur. As the child grows, the infundibular muscle hypertrophies, worsening the obstruction and leading to progressive cyanosis.
- **Hypercyanotic Spells ("Tet Spells")**: A classic and life-threatening presentation. Sudden episodes of profound cyanosis, hyperpnoea (deep, rapid breathing), irritability, and potentially syncope or seizures. 
  - *Triggers*: Crying, feeding, defecation, or waking up. These events either lower systemic vascular resistance (SVR) or increase infundibular spasm, worsening the Right-to-Left shunt.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Cyanosis**: Central cyanosis (lips, tongue).
- **Clubbing**: Seen in older, uncorrected children (rare nowadays due to early surgery).
- **Murmur**: A harsh, **ejection systolic murmur** at the upper left sternal border.
  - *Crucial Concept*: The murmur is caused by blood forced through the *pulmonary stenosis*, NOT the VSD (the VSD is large and silent).
- **Heart Sounds**: Single S2 (the pulmonary component, P2, is very soft or absent due to the abnormal valve).
- **During a Tet Spell**: The murmur paradoxically becomes **softer or disappears completely**. This is because the infundibular spasm shuts down the RV outflow tract; with no blood flowing through the stenosis, there is no murmur. All blood shunts silently through the VSD.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Transposition of the Great Arteries (TGA)**: The most common cyanotic CHD presenting *in the first week of life*. Presents with profound cyanosis on day 1, usually without a significant murmur (unless a VSD is present). TOF typically presents slightly later or with a loud murmur.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Other cyanotic CHDs: Transposition of the Great Arteries (TGA), Tricuspid Atresia, Truncus Arteriosus, Total Anomalous Pulmonary Venous Return (TAPVR).
- Severe isolated Pulmonary Stenosis.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Echocardiogram**: The definitive diagnostic test. Visualizes the overriding aorta, VSD, and RVOTO anatomy.
- **Chest X-ray (CXR)**: 
  - Classic **"Boot-shaped heart"** (Coeur en sabot). The apex is upturned due to RVH, and the main pulmonary artery segment is concave (missing).
  - **Oligemic lung fields**: The lungs look abnormally dark due to decreased pulmonary blood flow.
- **ECG**: Right Ventricular Hypertrophy (RVH) and Right Axis Deviation.
- **Genetics**: Chromosomal microarray to check for 22q11.2 deletion.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute Management of a "Tet Spell" (Medical Emergency)**:
  1. **Knee-to-Chest Position**: (Or squatting in an older child). This kinks the femoral arteries, increasing Systemic Vascular Resistance (SVR), which forces blood back into the pulmonary circulation.
  2. **High-flow Oxygen**: (Though it has limited effect if the shunt is massive).
  3. **Morphine (IV/IM/SC)**: Calms the child, stops crying, and reduces hyperpnoea.
  4. **IV Fluids**: Volume expansion increases RV preload.
  5. **IV Propranolol or Esmolol**: A beta-blocker that relaxes the infundibular muscle spasm, opening the RV outflow tract.
- **Surgical Management**:
  - *Palliative Shunt*: A **Blalock-Taussig (BT) shunt** (connecting the subclavian artery to the pulmonary artery) is performed if the infant is too small, premature, or has unfavourable anatomy for full repair. It artificially increases pulmonary blood flow.
  - *Definitive Complete Repair*: Usually performed electively at **6 months of age**. Involves patch closure of the VSD and surgical relief of the RVOTO (often requiring a transannular patch across the pulmonary valve).

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Paediatric Cardiology & Cardiothoracic Surgery**.
- **Genetic Counseling**: Important if 22q11.2 deletion is identified (implications for immune function, calcium levels, and future pregnancies).

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- N/A for this condition.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Pre-operative**: Polycythaemia (body's response to chronic hypoxia, increasing the risk of stroke/thrombosis), brain abscess, infective endocarditis.
- **Post-operative (Long-term)**: The surgical repair often leaves the patient with severe **Pulmonary Regurgitation**. Over decades, this causes RV dilation and failure, arrhythmias, and often requires pulmonary valve replacement in early adulthood.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "Why does a toddler with Tetralogy of Fallot instinctively squat when they feel breathless?" (Squatting kinks the femoral arteries, which significantly increases Systemic Vascular Resistance (SVR). This higher systemic pressure decreases the Right-to-Left shunt across the VSD and forces more blood through the narrowed pulmonary artery, improving oxygenation).
- *Viva*: "What happens to the intensity of the murmur during a severe hypercyanotic Tet spell, and why?" (The murmur gets softer or disappears entirely. The infundibular muscle spasms shut, stopping flow across the pulmonary outflow tract, which is the source of the murmur. All blood shunts silently through the VSD).
