# Langerhans Cell Histiocytosis (LCH) (朗格漢斯細胞組織細胞增生症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition of LCH
- Rare, clonal, neoplastic proliferation of abnormal **Langerhans cells** (dendritic cells/macrophages).
- **Disease Spectrum**: 
  - Single/Benign: Solitary bone lesion (historically **Eosinophilic Granuloma**).
  - Disseminated/Malignant: Rapidly fatal, multi-system disease (historically **Letterer-Siwe disease**).
  - Triad: **Hand-Schüller-Christian disease** (lytic skull lesions + exophthalmos + diabetes insipidus).

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
- Rare; incidence ~4-5 per million children/year.
- HA centralized care: Paediatric Oncology is managed at the **Hong Kong Children's Hospital (HKCH)**.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Cellular Pathophysiology of LCH
- Abnormal dendritic cells cluster → form granulomatous tumors → local tissue destruction + intense cytokine storm.
- **Histological Hallmark**: Presence of **Birbeck granules** (伯貝克顆粒) inside the cells (distinctive "tennis-racket" appearance on electron microscopy).

### Genetic Drivers in LCH
- Pathologically driven by somatic mutations in the MAPK pathway.
- **BRAF V600E** gene mutation present in ~50-60% of cases.
- **MAP2K1** mutations often found in BRAF -ve cases.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
### Single-System Presentation
- Typically older children (e.g., 6yo).
- Presents as a painful, rapidly growing lump on the skull or long bone.

### Multi-System Presentation
- Typically toddlers/infants (e.g., <2yo).
- Presents with severe, bleeding scalp rash (mimicking horrific cradle cap), chronic ear discharge (otorrhoea), and massive thirst/urination.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Skeletal System Signs (80%)
- Lytic, painful **"punched-out" lesions** (skull, long bones, spine).
- Can present acutely with pathological fractures.

### Dermatological Signs (33%)
- Severe, recalcitrant **seborrheic dermatitis-like rash** (greasy, scaly).
- Classically affects scalp, ear canals, and flexural/groin folds.
- High-yield sign: Rash is often **erosive and bleeds** (unlike standard eczema).

### Endocrine System Signs (25%)
- High affinity for infiltrating the posterior pituitary stalk.
- Destruction of ADH-producing cells → **Central Diabetes Insipidus (DI)** (中樞性尿崩症).
- Presentation: Massive polyuria and polydipsia.

### Other Organ System Signs
- **Reticuloendothelial**: Hepatosplenomegaly (肝脾腫大), lymphadenopathy.
- **Ophthalmic**: **Exophthalmos** (突眼) (bulging eyes 2° to orbital bone retrobulbar lesions).
- **Haematological**: Bone marrow failure → pancytopenia (anaemia, bruising, recurrent infections).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Differentiating LCH Rash from Seborrheic Dermatitis
- **Typical Cradle Cap**: Clears rapidly with emollients/mild topical steroids; does NOT bleed.
- **LCH Rash**: Erosive, petechial/bleeding, completely refractory to standard topical eczema therapies.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
### Table of Differential Diagnoses for LCH
| Category | Differential Diagnosis | Distinguishing Clinical Features |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Dermatological** | Seborrheic Dermatitis | Non-bleeding, resolves with mild topical steroids/oils |
| | Severe Atopic Eczema | Highly pruritic, +ve personal/family Hx of atopy |
| **Malignant** | Leukaemia / Lymphoma | Differentiated via bone marrow biopsy and flow cytometry |
| | Neuroblastoma | Abdominal mass, ↑ urine VMA/HVA |
| **Infectious** | Osteomyelitis | Acute fever, ↑ CRP/ESR, +ve blood cultures |
| | Chronic Mastoiditis | Usually responds to IV antibiotics; no "punched-out" lytic skull lesions |

## 7. Tiered Investigations
### Bedside & Laboratory Investigations
- **Bedside**: Urine osmolality & specific gravity (↓ in DI).
- **Bloods**: CBP (rule out cytopenia), LFT (assess liver infiltration), Coagulation profile, U&E (Na+ ↑ in dehydration from DI).

### Radiological Investigations
- **Skeletal Survey**: Gold standard initial imaging → sharply defined **"punched-out" lytic lesions** in skull/long bones with NO surrounding sclerosis.
- **MRI Brain/Pituitary**: Thickened pituitary stalk, absence of the normal "bright spot" of the posterior pituitary (indicates DI).
- **USG Abdomen**: Assess for hepatosplenomegaly.

### Definitive Diagnostic Investigations
- **Tissue Biopsy**: Required from skin rash or bone lesion.
- **Immunohistochemistry**: +ve for **CD1a**, **S-100**, and **Langerin (CD207)**.
- **Electron Microscopy**: Identifies pathognomonic **Birbeck Granules** ("tennis-rackets").
- **Genetics**: Molecular testing for **BRAF V600E** mutation.

## 8. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Management of Single-System LCH
- Often requires minimal intervention.
- HA Protocols: Observation, surgical curettage, or intralesional corticosteroid injection. 
- Prognosis: Excellent.

### Management of Multi-System LCH
- Treated as a malignancy under HKCH Oncology protocols.
- **Systemic Chemotherapy** (1-2 years): Standard backbone is **Cytarabine**, **Vinblastine**, and **Prednisolone**.

### Targeted Therapy for Refractory LCH
- Reserved for severe disease or failure of 1st-line chemo.
- **BRAF inhibitors** (e.g., **Vemurafenib**): Highly effective for disease carrying the BRAF V600E mutation.

### Endocrine Management (Central DI)
- Requires lifelong synthetic ADH replacement → **Desmopressin (DDAVP)** (去氨加壓素) via oral or intranasal route.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### MDT Involvement in LCH
- **Paediatric Oncologist**: Coordinates chemotherapy at HKCH.
- **Paediatric Endocrinologist**: Continuous long-term monitoring for anterior/posterior pituitary deficits.
- **Orthopaedic Surgeon**: Management of pathological fractures / bone lesions.
- **MSW / CP**: Psychosocial support for families coping with a cancer-like diagnosis and lifelong chronic endocrine deficits.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Safeguarding Considerations in LCH
- Pathological fractures in LCH can occasionally mimic NAI. 
- Must meticulously document lytic bone lesions on skeletal survey to differentiate LCH from inflicted trauma.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
### Prognosis & Mortality Risk
- **Single-system disease**: Excellent survival (>99%).
- **Multi-system disease**: High mortality if the disease infiltrates **"risk organs"** (liver, spleen, or bone marrow) and fails to respond to initial chemotherapy induction.

### Long-Term Complications
- **Endocrine**: Permanent Central DI; high risk of anterior pituitary deficits (e.g., Growth Hormone deficiency → short stature).
- **Neurological**: LCH-associated neurodegeneration (late-onset ataxia, cognitive decline).
- **Musculoskeletal**: Permanent orthopaedic deformities.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Trap: Ignoring a toddler who constantly drinks massive amounts of water and wets the bed. Pearl: If this classic sign of Diabetes Insipidus is combined with a scalp rash or chronic "ear infections" (actually LCH in the petrous bone), the diagnosis is LCH until proven otherwise.
* Viva Question: If you examine a biopsy of LCH under an electron microscope, what classic shape will the organelles form? Answer: Birbeck granules, which have a characteristic 'tennis racket' appearance.
* Pitfall: Mistaking LCH ear discharge for simple otitis media. Unresponsive, chronic otorrhoea with aural polyps or mastoid involvement must prompt imaging and biopsy to rule out LCH.
* Viva Question: Which immunohistochemical markers are classically positive in an LCH biopsy? Answer: CD1a, S-100, and Langerin (CD207).
* Trap: Assuming Central Diabetes Insipidus will resolve after successful chemotherapy for LCH. Pearl: Pituitary damage is usually permanent and requires lifelong synthetic ADH (DDAVP) replacement.
* Viva Question: What is the classic triad of Hand-Schüller-Christian disease? Answer: Lytic skull lesions, exophthalmos, and diabetes insipidus.
