# Meningitis (腦膜炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Acute inflammation of the meninges (the arachnoid and pia mater) surrounding the brain and spinal cord. It is a life-threatening medical emergency.
- **Local Epidemiology**: In Hong Kong, the incidence of bacterial meningitis (specifically *Haemophilus influenzae* type b and *Streptococcus pneumoniae*) has plummeted dramatically due to the high uptake of the Department of Health's Childhood Immunisation Programme. Viral meningitis (Enterovirus) is now more common.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Pathogenesis**: Pathogens typically colonize the nasopharynx, invade the bloodstream (bacteraemia), and cross the blood-brain barrier to enter the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF).
- **Inflammation**: Multiplication of bacteria in the CSF triggers an intense inflammatory cascade. This leads to increased permeability of the blood-brain barrier, cerebral oedema, increased Intracranial Pressure (ICP), decreased cerebral perfusion, and neuronal ischaemia/damage.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
The presentation is highly age-dependent:
- **Neonates (< 1 month)**: Very non-specific. Temperature instability (fever or hypothermia), poor feeding, irritability, lethargy, jaundice, apnoea, or seizures. *Meningeal signs are often absent.*
- **Infants (1 month - 2 years)**: Fever, vomiting, high-pitched cry, irritability, lethargy, and a **bulging anterior fontanelle**.
- **Older Children & Adolescents**: The classic triad of **Fever, Headache, and Neck Stiffness**. Accompanied by photophobia, vomiting, and altered mental status.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Nuchal Rigidity**: Resistance to passive neck flexion.
- **Kernig's Sign**: Inability to fully extend the knee when the hip is flexed at 90 degrees due to pain.
- **Brudzinski's Sign**: Passive flexion of the neck causes involuntary flexion of the hips and knees.
- **Rash**: A **non-blanching petechial or purpuric rash** is the hallmark of Meningococcal septicaemia (*Neisseria meningitidis*). This is a "do not miss" sign.
- **Signs of Raised ICP**: Bradycardia, hypertension, irregular respirations (Cushing's triad - a late sign), papilloedema, focal neurological deficits, or decreasing GCS.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Meningitis vs. Encephalitis**: Meningitis primarily affects the meninges (headache, neck stiffness). Encephalitis involves the brain parenchyma itself, presenting early with profound altered mental status, bizarre behavior, seizures, and focal neurological signs. (Note: Meningoencephalitis is a combination of both).
- **Meningitis vs. Febrile Convulsion**: A child with a simple febrile convulsion recovers rapidly and has a normal neurological exam. A child with meningitis remains lethargic, toxic, and has meningeal signs.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Viral Encephalitis (e.g., HSV, Enterovirus).
- Sepsis / Septicaemia.
- Brain abscess or subdural empyema.
- Subarachnoid haemorrhage.
- Severe systemic infections (e.g., Pyelonephritis, Pneumonia) causing "meningism" (meningeal irritation without actual CNS infection).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Lumbar Puncture (LP)**: The gold standard for diagnosis. CSF is sent for cell count, protein, glucose, Gram stain, culture, and multiplex PCR (BioFire).
  - *Bacterial CSF*: High opening pressure, turbid appearance, **high polymorphs (neutrophils)**, **high protein**, **low glucose** (< 50% of serum glucose).
  - *Viral CSF*: Clear appearance, **high lymphocytes**, normal/mildly elevated protein, **normal glucose**.
- **CONTRAINDICATIONS TO LP**: Signs of raised ICP (focal neuro signs, papilloedema, GCS < 8, abnormal posturing), cardiovascular compromise/shock, severe coagulopathy, or infection at the LP site.
- **Bloods**: CBP, CRP, **Blood cultures** (crucial to draw *before* antibiotics if possible, but do not delay treatment), Serum glucose (to compare with CSF glucose), Coagulation profile.
- **Imaging**: CT Brain is NOT routinely required before LP unless there are contraindications to LP (e.g., focal signs, GCS < 8) to rule out a mass lesion or severe cerebral oedema.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **CRITICAL RULE: DO NOT DELAY ANTIBIOTICS.** If an LP is contraindicated or delayed, draw blood cultures and give IV antibiotics immediately.
- **Empiric Antibiotic Therapy (HA Guidelines)**:
  - *Neonates (< 1 month)*: **IV Ampicillin + Cefotaxime** (Covers Group B Strep, *E. coli*, and *Listeria monocytogenes*).
  - *Children (> 1 month)*: **IV Ceftriaxone or Cefotaxime** (Covers *N. meningitidis*, *S. pneumoniae*, and *H. influenzae* type b). Add IV Vancomycin if highly penicillin-resistant pneumococcus is suspected.
- **Corticosteroids**: **IV Dexamethasone** should be given *before or with* the first dose of antibiotics. It reduces the inflammatory response to bacterial lysis and significantly reduces the risk of sensorineural hearing loss (especially in Hib meningitis).
- **Antiviral**: Add IV Acyclovir if HSV encephalitis is suspected.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Public Health**: Meningococcal and Hib infections are statutory notifiable diseases in HK. The Department of Health (DH) must be notified.
- **Contact Tracing**: Close contacts of Meningococcal or Hib cases require chemoprophylaxis (e.g., oral Rifampicin or Ciprofloxacin) to eradicate nasopharyngeal carriage.
- **Audiology**: Mandatory formal hearing assessment for all survivors before discharge or shortly after.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- An unimmunized child presenting with a vaccine-preventable disease (e.g., Hib, Pneumococcus) raises concerns about medical neglect.
- Abusive Head Trauma (Shaken Baby Syndrome) can present with lethargy, vomiting, and seizures, mimicking meningitis. Always check for retinal haemorrhages.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Neurological Sequelae**: Sensorineural hearing loss (most common, ~10-20%), seizures/epilepsy, hydrocephalus, cerebral palsy, cognitive impairment, and learning difficulties.
- **Systemic**: Septic shock, Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC), Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome (adrenal haemorrhage in meningococcaemia), limb amputation due to ischaemia.
- **Prognosis**: Mortality is ~5-10% for bacterial meningitis in developed countries, with significant morbidity in survivors.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "What are the absolute contraindications to performing a Lumbar Puncture?" (Signs of raised ICP like focal neuro deficits or GCS < 8, haemodynamic instability, coagulopathy).
- *Viva*: "Why do we give Dexamethasone, and when must it be given?" (To reduce meningeal inflammation and the risk of hearing loss. It MUST be given before or concurrently with the first dose of antibiotics; it is ineffective if given later).
- *Pitfall*: Delaying life-saving antibiotics to get a CT scan or perform an LP.
