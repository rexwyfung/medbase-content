
# Childhood Tuberculosis (結核病)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: An infectious disease caused by *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*.
- **Local Epidemiology**: HK is an area of intermediate TB burden. The Department of Health (DH) provides free BCG vaccination at birth. Childhood TB is often a sentinel event, indicating recent transmission from an infectious adult contact in the community (often a family member or domestic helper).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Transmission**: Inhalation of aerosolised droplet nuclei from an adult with cavitary pulmonary TB. Children rarely transmit TB to others because their lesions contain few bacilli and they lack the force to cough up sputum.
- **Primary Infection**: Bacilli lodge in the alveoli, forming a Ghon focus. They spread to regional hilar lymph nodes (Ghon complex).
- **Progression**: In most children, the immune system contains the infection (Latent TB). However, young children (< 5 years) and immunocompromised infants have a high risk of progression to active disease (Primary Pulmonary TB) or severe disseminated disease (Miliary TB, TB Meningitis).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- See below.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Latent TB**: Asymptomatic. Normal physical exam and CXR. Positive TST/IGRA.
- **Pulmonary TB**:
  - Persistent, non-remitting cough (> 2-3 weeks).
  - Unexplained fever, night sweats.
  - Faltering growth / weight loss.
  - *Signs*: Often normal, or focal crackles/wheeze (if enlarged hilar nodes compress a bronchus).
- **Extrapulmonary TB**:
  - *TB Lymphadenitis (Scrofula)*: Painless, matted, firm cervical lymph nodes. May caseate and form a discharging sinus.
  - *TB Meningitis*: Insidious onset of lethargy, irritability, cranial nerve palsies (especially CN VI), progressing to coma.
  - *Miliary TB*: Disseminated disease. Hepatosplenomegaly, fever.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Atypical Mycobacteria (NTM)**: Can also cause cervical lymphadenitis, but the child is usually completely well with no systemic symptoms, and CXR is normal.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Most Likely**:
  - Pulmonary TB (if prolonged cough and positive contact history).
- **Must-Not-Miss**:
  - **TB Meningitis**: High mortality and severe neurological morbidity if diagnosis is delayed.
  - **Miliary TB**.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**:
  - **Chest X-ray (CXR)**: The most important initial test.
  - *Classic finding in children*: **Hilar or mediastinal lymphadenopathy** (unlike adults who typically have apical cavitation).
  - *Miliary*: Diffuse, tiny (1-2mm) nodular opacities.
  - **Tuberculin Skin Test (TST / Mantoux)**: Injected intradermally. Read at 48-72 hours. Measures delayed hypersensitivity. Can be falsely positive due to prior BCG vaccination (common in HK).
- **Gold Standard**:
  - **Interferon-Gamma Release Assay (IGRA - e.g., QuantiFERON-TB Gold)**: Blood test. More specific than TST as it does not cross-react with the BCG vaccine. Preferred in HK for children > 5 years.
  - **Microbiology (The Gold Standard)**:
    - **Gastric Lavage / Aspirate**: Children swallow their sputum. Early morning gastric aspirates (x3 days) are sent for Acid-Fast Bacilli (AFB) smear, PCR (GeneXpert), and culture.
    - *Note*: Smear is often negative in children (paucibacillary disease), but PCR is highly sensitive and rapid. Culture takes weeks but is essential for drug susceptibility testing.
- **Monitoring**:
  - LFTs before and during treatment (anti-TB drugs are hepatotoxic).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**:
  - Not applicable unless presenting with severe TB meningitis or miliary TB.
- **Chronic/Definitive**:
  - **Latent TB Infection (LTBI)**: Treated to prevent progression to active disease. Usually Isoniazid (INH) for 6-9 months, or Rifampicin for 4 months.
  - **Active Pulmonary TB**:
    - *Intensive Phase (2 months)*: Isoniazid (INH), Rifampicin (RIF), Pyrazinamide (PZA), and Ethambutol (EMB).
    - *Continuation Phase (4 months)*: INH and RIF.
    - *Total duration*: 6 months.
    - **TB Meningitis / Miliary TB**: Requires longer treatment (9-12 months) and the addition of **Corticosteroids** (Dexamethasone/Prednisolone) to reduce inflammation and prevent neurological sequelae.
## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Directly Observed Treatment (DOT)**: Mandatory in HK. Administered via DH Chest Clinics to ensure compliance and prevent multi-drug resistant TB (MDR-TB).
## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- **Contact Tracing is CRITICAL**: Every child with TB represents a failure of public health. DH must be notified to screen all household and close contacts to find the index case.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Excellent prognosis for pulmonary TB if treated early and compliance is good.
- **Complications**: Bronchiectasis (from endobronchial TB), severe neurological deficits (from TB meningitis), drug-induced hepatitis.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Relying on a positive Mantoux test to diagnose active TB in HK. *Pearl*: Almost all children in HK receive the BCG vaccine at birth, which can cause a false-positive Mantoux. IGRA is preferred for specificity, and CXR/microbiology are needed to confirm *active* disease.
- **Viva Question**: "Why do we do gastric aspirates instead of sputum cultures in a 3-year-old with suspected TB?" *Answer*: Young children cannot expectorate sputum; they swallow it. Early morning gastric aspirates collect the swallowed respiratory secretions that accumulated overnight in the stomach.
