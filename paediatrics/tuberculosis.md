# Childhood Tuberculosis (兒童結核病)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition of Tuberculosis
* **Definition**: Chronic, infectious disease caused by the obligate aerobe, intracellular acid-fast bacillus (AFB) ***Mycobacterium tuberculosis* (結核分枝桿菌)** (MTB).
* **Childhood Sentinel Event**: Childhood TB serves as an epidemiological sentinel event indicating recent, ongoing community transmission from an infectious adult contact.

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
* **Burden**: HK is an area of intermediate TB burden (incidence ~50/100,000).
* **Age Distribution**: Bimodal age distribution in children (peaks at 5 years & 14 years).
* **Vaccination**: HK **Department of Health (DH) (衞生署)** provides universal free **BCG vaccination (卡介苗)** at birth to all neonates.
* **Contact Source**: Often a family member or domestic helper. Contact tracing by DH is paramount.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Transmission Dynamics
* **Route**: Inhalation of aerosolised droplet nuclei (1-5 μm) from an adult/adolescent with cavitary pulmonary TB.
* **Infectivity**: Pre-pubertal children rarely transmit TB (paucibacillary lesions, weak cough reflex, lack of cavitation force).

### Pathophysiology of Primary Infection
* **Ghon Focus**: Inhaled bacilli lodge in terminal alveoli → engulfed by alveolar macrophages → survive & replicate intracellularly → form a localized caseating granuloma (**Ghon focus**).
* **Ghon Complex**: Bacilli drain via lymphatics to regional hilar/paratracheal lymph nodes. Ghon focus + hilar lymphadenopathy = **Ghon complex**.
* **Cell-Mediated Immunity (CMI)**: At 2-8 weeks, T-cell mediated delayed hypersensitivity (Type IV) develops, walling off infection.

### Disease Progression & Dissemination
* **Latent TB Infection (LTBI) (潛伏結核感染)**: Immune system contains infection. Patient is asymptomatic, non-infectious, with +ve TST/IGRA.
* **Active Primary TB**: Failure of immune containment (high risk if age < 5 years, HIV, immunosuppressed) → progressive primary pulmonary TB.
* **Haematogenous Spread**: Occurs before CMI develops → seeding to meninges, bones, kidneys. Risk of **TB Meningitis (結核性腦膜炎)** and **Miliary TB (粟粒性結核)** is highest in infants < 1 year.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Latent TB Symptoms & Signs
* **Symptoms**: Completely asymptomatic.
* **Signs**: Normal physical examination. Normal CXR.

### Pulmonary TB Symptoms & Signs
* **Key Symptoms**:
    * Persistent, non-remitting chronic cough (> 2-3 weeks).
    * Unexplained persistent fever, nocturnal diaphoresis (night sweats).
    * Anorexia, faltering growth / weight loss.
    * Lethargy, reduced playfulness.
* **Signs**:
    * Often unremarkable early on.
    * Focal coarse crackles or localized monophonic wheeze (due to enlarged hilar nodes compressing a bronchus).
    * Dullness to percussion (if pleural effusion present in older children).

### Extrapulmonary TB Symptoms & Signs
* **TB Lymphadenitis (Scrofula) (結核性淋巴結炎)**:
    * Painless, firm, matted unilateral cervical/supraclavicular lymphadenopathy.
    * May progress to overlying skin erythema, caseation, & a discharging sinus (cold abscess).
* **TB Meningitis (TBM) (結核性腦膜炎)**:
    * Insidious onset (weeks).
    * Phase 1: Lethargy, irritability, vomiting, fever.
    * Phase 2: Cranial nerve palsies (esp. CN VI → strabismus), meningismus, altered sensorium.
    * Phase 3: Coma, decerebrate posturing, ↑ ICP, hemiplegia.
* **Miliary TB (粟粒性結核)**:
    * Disseminated haematogenous disease.
    * High swinging fever, hepatosplenomegaly, generalized lymphadenopathy.

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Differentiating TB Lymphadenitis from NTM Lymphadenitis
* **Non-Tuberculous Mycobacteria (NTM)** (e.g., *M. avium* complex):
    * Usually occurs in children < 5 years who are otherwise completely well.
    * No systemic symptoms (no fever, no weight loss).
    * CXR is completely normal.
    * History lacks TB contact.

### Differentiating Pulmonary TB from Atypical Pneumonia
* **Mycoplasma pneumoniae**:
    * Acute/subacute onset, prominent extra-pulmonary features (erythema multiforme, haemolysis).
    * Responds rapidly to Macrolides. TB cough persists despite standard antibiotics.

## 5. Differential Diagnosis
### Most Likely Differentials for Chronic Cough & Weight Loss
* **Prolonged Bacterial Bronchitis (PBB)**: Wet cough, responds to 2 weeks of Co-amoxiclav.
* **Asthma (哮喘)**: Variable wheeze, atopic history, dry nocturnal cough, responds to bronchodilators/ICS.
* **Bronchiectasis (支氣管擴張)**: Purulent sputum, clubbing, often secondary to prior severe infection.

### Must-Not-Miss Differentials
* **Malignancy (Lymphoma/Leukaemia) (惡性腫瘤)**: Presents with prolonged fever, night sweats, weight loss, & mediastinal mass (lymphoma).
* **HIV/AIDS (愛滋病)**: Presents with opportunistic infections & failure to thrive. (Always consider HIV testing in TB patients).

## 6. Tiered Investigations
### Bedside Investigations
* **Tuberculin Skin Test (TST / Mantoux) (結核菌素試驗)**:
    * Intradermal injection of PPD. Read induration at 48-72 hours.
    * Measures delayed hypersensitivity.
    * False +ve: Prior BCG vaccination (universal in HK) or NTM.
    * False -ve: Severe TB (miliary/TBM), immunosuppression, malnutrition, recent live viral vaccine (MMR).

### Blood & Serological Investigations
* **Interferon-Gamma Release Assay (IGRA) (干擾素釋放試驗)** (e.g., QuantiFERON-TB Gold):
    * Gold Standard for LTBI screening in HK for children > 5 years.
    * Measures IFN-γ release by T-cells in response to MTB-specific antigens (ESAT-6, CFP-10).
    * **High Specificity**: Does NOT cross-react with BCG vaccine or most NTM.
* **Baseline Bloods**:
    * FBC (anaemia of chronic disease, leukocytosis/leukopenia).
    * ESR/CRP (usually ↑).
    * LFTs (Baseline crucial before starting hepatotoxic anti-TB drugs).
    * HIV serology (Mandatory).

### Imaging Investigations
* **Chest X-Ray (CXR) (胸部X光)**:
    * Most important initial diagnostic test.
    * **Classic paediatric findings**: Unilateral hilar/paratracheal lymphadenopathy, Ghon focus (peripheral opacity).
    * Adolescents/Adults: Apical infiltrates/cavitation.
    * **Miliary TB**: Diffuse, bilateral 1-2mm uniform nodular opacities ("millet seeds").
* **CT Thorax / Brain**:
    * CT Thorax: Better defines hilar adenopathy & airway compression.
    * CT/MRI Brain (if TBM suspected): Basal meningeal enhancement, hydrocephalus, tuberculomas, infarcts in basal ganglia.

### Microbiological Investigations (The Gold Standard)
* **Gastric Aspirates/Lavage (胃液抽取)**:
    * Young children swallow sputum. Requires early morning fasting gastric aspirates via NGT x 3 consecutive days.
* **Induced Sputum / Bronchoscopy**:
    * In older, cooperative children (induced with hypertonic saline).
* **Processing of Samples**:
    * **Smear**: Acid-Fast Bacilli (AFB) stain (Ziehl-Neelsen or Auramine). Often -ve in children (paucibacillary).
    * **Molecular**: PCR / Xpert MTB/RIF assay. Rapid (hours), detects MTB DNA & Rifampicin resistance.
    * **Culture**: Liquid media (MGIT) or solid (Löwenstein-Jensen). Takes 2-6 weeks. Gold standard for definitive diagnosis & comprehensive Drug Susceptibility Testing (DST).
* **Lumbar Puncture (CSF)**:
    * Crucial if TBM is suspected (after ruling out ↑ ICP).
    * Findings: ‘Spider web’ clot, WBC ↑ (lymphocytic predominance), Protein ↑↑, Glucose ↓ (< 50% of serum). Send for AFB, TB PCR, & culture.

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Acute Management of TB Emergencies
* **TB Meningitis / Raised ICP**: Neuroprotection, immediate empirical anti-TB therapy, adjunctive systemic corticosteroids to ↓ inflammation, neurosurgical consult for hydrocephalus (EVD/VP shunt).
* **Airway Compromise**: From severe hilar adenopathy compressing the trachea/bronchi. May require short-course corticosteroids.

### Definitive Medical Management of Active Pulmonary TB
* **Standard Regimen (6 Months)**:
    * **Intensive Phase (2 Months)**: Isoniazid (INH), Rifampicin (RIF), Pyrazinamide (PZA), Ethambutol (EMB) [HRZE].
        * *(Note: EMB is used cautiously in young children due to optic neuritis risk, but is standard in HA protocols until drug susceptibilities are known).*
    * **Continuation Phase (4 Months)**: Isoniazid (INH) & Rifampicin (RIF) [HR].
* **Monitoring**: Regular clinical review, monthly weight checks (dose adjustments needed as child grows), monitor LFTs for drug-induced hepatitis, visual acuity testing if on EMB.

### Management of Extrapulmonary & Severe TB
* **TB Meningitis / Miliary / Osteoarticular TB**:
    * Extended duration: 9-12 months total (2 months HRZE + 7-10 months HR).
    * **Adjunctive Corticosteroids**: Dexamethasone or Prednisolone tapered over 4-8 weeks is mandatory in TBM & TB pericarditis to prevent strictures & neurological sequelae.

### Management of Latent TB Infection (LTBI)
* **Regimens**:
    * Isoniazid (INH) monotherapy for 6-9 months (often with Pyridoxine/Vit B6).
    * *Alternative*: Rifampicin (RIF) for 4 months, or INH+RIF for 3 months.

### Key Anti-TB Medications & Side Effects Table
| Medication (藥物) | Symbol | Key Side Effects (副作用) | Supplemental Therapy |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Isoniazid (異煙肼)** | H | Hepatotoxicity, Peripheral neuropathy | **Pyridoxine (Vit B6)** given to prevent neuropathy |
| **Rifampicin (利福平)** | R | Hepatotoxicity, Orange-red body fluids (tears/urine), CYP450 inducer | Reassure parents regarding orange urine |
| **Pyrazinamide (吡嗪酰胺)** | Z | Hepatotoxicity (most severe), Hyperuricaemia, Arthralgia | N/A |
| **Ethambutol (乙胺丁醇)** | E | Optic neuritis (↓ visual acuity, red-green colour blindness) | Avoid/use with extreme caution in pre-verbal children |

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Directly Observed Treatment (DOT)
* **HK Department of Health Protocol**: DOT is mandatory for all active cases in HK to ensure compliance, monitor side effects, & prevent Multi-Drug Resistant TB (**MDR-TB**).
* Administered via local DH Chest Clinics (胸肺科診所). Medications are taken under the direct supervision of a healthcare worker.

### Multi-Disciplinary Team Involvement
* **Medical Social Worker (MSW)**: Financial assistance, school reintegration.
* **Infection Control Nurse**: Notification to the Centre for Health Protection (CHP).
* **Public Health Team**: Vital for community outbreak prevention.

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Public Health Safeguarding & Contact Tracing
* **Index Case Identification**: A child with TB is considered a "sentinel event". Children usually do not transmit TB; they acquired it from an infectious adult.
* **Action**: Mandatory statutory notification to DH. Active **reverse contact tracing** is CRITICAL to screen all household members, domestic helpers, & close contacts via CXR & symptom screening to locate the infectious source & prevent further spread.

## 10. Complications & Prognosis
### Disease Complications
* **Pulmonary**: Bronchiectasis, endobronchial stenosis, lobar collapse, progressive lung destruction.
* **Neurological (Post-TBM)**: High risk of severe permanent neurological morbidity (hydrocephalus, deafness, intellectual disability, hemiplegia, seizures).
* **Orthopaedic (Pott's Disease)**: Vertebral collapse, kyphosis, spinal cord compression.

### Iatrogenic Complications
* **Drug-Induced Liver Injury (DILI)**: Due to INH, RIF, & PZA. Requires immediate cessation of offending drugs & careful reintroduction once LFTs normalize.

### Prognosis
* Uncomplicated pulmonary TB treated early has an excellent prognosis with near 100% cure rate.
* TB meningitis has a guarded prognosis, with high mortality & severe morbidity if treatment is delayed.

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Trap: Relying on a positive Mantoux test to diagnose active TB in HK. Pearl: Almost all children in HK receive the BCG vaccine at birth, causing a false-positive Mantoux. IGRA is preferred for specificity, and CXR/Microbiology are required to confirm active disease.
* Viva Question: Why do we do gastric aspirates instead of sputum cultures in a 3-year-old with suspected TB? Answer: Young children cannot expectorate sputum; they swallow it. Early morning fasting gastric aspirates collect the swallowed respiratory secretions that accumulated overnight in the stomach.
* Pitfall: Prescribing Ethambutol without caution in a 2-year-old. Ethambutol causes dose-dependent optic neuritis, which is nearly impossible to monitor in pre-verbal children who cannot perform visual acuity or colour vision testing.
* Viva Question: What is the most important public health step when you diagnose a 2-year-old with pulmonary TB? Answer: Statutory notification to the Department of Health and initiating reverse contact tracing of all household members (including domestic helpers) to find the infectious adult source.
* Trap: Stopping anti-TB medications immediately when the child starts vomiting without checking bloods. Pearl: Nausea and vomiting can be early signs of hepatotoxicity from INH/RIF/PZA. Check LFTs immediately before deciding to alter the regimen.
* Viva Question: Why is Pyridoxine (Vitamin B6) co-prescribed with Isoniazid (INH)? Answer: To prevent INH-induced peripheral neuropathy, particularly in vulnerable populations like exclusively breastfed infants, malnourished children, and adolescents.
