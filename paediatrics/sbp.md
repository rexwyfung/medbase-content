# Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis (自發性腹膜炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of SBP
* Acute bacterial infection of **Ascites (腹水)** fluid.
* No identifiable, surgically treatable intra-abdominal source (e.g., no bowel perforation/abscess).

### Hong Kong Local Epidemiology
* Severe, life-threatening complication in two main pediatric groups:
    * **Decompensated Liver Cirrhosis (肝硬化):** Notably post-Kasai **Biliary Atresia (膽道閉鎖)** (common in HK/Asia).
    * **Nephrotic Syndrome (腎病綜合症):** Usually during relapse/steroid-resistant phases.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics

### Pathophysiology in Liver Cirrhosis
* **Portal Hypertension (門靜脈高壓) →** Bowel wall edema + **Ascites (腹水)**.
* Cirrhosis → Massively impaired reticuloendothelial system (RES).
* ↓ Complement levels in ascitic fluid → ↓ opsonic activity.
* **Bacterial Translocation (細菌易位):** Gut flora transmigrates across intact but hyper-permeable intestinal mucosa into peritoneal fluid.

### Pathophysiology in Nephrotic Syndrome
* Massive proteinuria → Urinary loss of **IgG** and alternative complement pathway factors (Factor B).
* Defective opsonization + edematous bowel wall → High susceptibility to encapsulated organisms.

### Key Causative Organisms
* **Cirrhotic SBP:** Gram -ve enterics (** *E. coli* **, *Klebsiella*), Gram +ve (** *Strep/Enterococcus* **).
* **Nephrotic SBP:** Encapsulated organisms, classically ** *Streptococcus pneumoniae* ** (肺炎鏈球菌).

## 3. Clinical Presentation

### Typical Clinical Presentation
* Can be highly subtle/atypical in young children.
* Sudden clinical deterioration or unexplained fever in a patient with known chronic ascites.
* Often presents as a worsening of baseline liver failure rather than classic acute abdomen.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Key Symptoms of SBP
* High fever, chills/rigors.
* Diffuse abdominal pain.
* Worsening abdominal distension (rapid ↑ in abdominal girth).
* Vomiting / feed intolerance.

### Key Clinical Signs of SBP
* Abdominal tenderness (diffuse).
* Rebound tenderness/guarding (often less pronounced than in surgical peritonitis due to fluid buffering).
* Worsening **Hepatic Encephalopathy (肝性腦病)** (confusion, altered sleep-wake cycle, flapping tremor/asterixis).
* Rapid ↓ in urine output / ↓ renal function (impending hepatorenal syndrome).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)

### Distinguishing SBP vs Secondary Bacterial Peritonitis
| Feature | Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis (SBP) | Secondary Bacterial Peritonitis |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Pathology** | Bacterial translocation | Bowel perforation / internal abscess |
| **Microbiology** | **Monomicrobial** (1 organism) | **Polymicrobial** (mixed flora, anaerobes) |
| **Ascitic Fluid Total Protein**| Usually < 10 g/L | Usually > 10 g/L |
| **Ascitic Fluid Glucose** | > 2.7 mmol/L (similar to serum) | < 2.7 mmol/L (consumed by massive bacteria) |
| **Ascitic Fluid LDH** | < Upper limit of normal for serum | > Upper limit of normal for serum |
| **Imaging (AXR/CT)** | Massive ascites, no free air | Pneumoperitoneum (free air under diaphragm) |

## 6. Differential Diagnosis

### Differential Diagnosis for Fever and Ascites
| Diagnosis | Differentiating Clinical Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Secondary Peritonitis** | Sudden agonizing pain, rigid board-like abdomen, free gas on AXR. |
| **Ascending Cholangitis (膽管炎)**| Extremely common post-Kasai. Jaundice flare, pale stools, normal ascitic PMN count. |
| **Viral Gastroenteritis** | Prominent diarrhea, contact history, normal ascitic PMN count. |
| **Urinary Tract Infection** | Dysuria, cloudy urine, +ve urine culture, normal ascitic PMN count. |

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)

### Gold Standard Diagnostic Investigation
* **Diagnostic Paracentesis (腹腔穿刺術):** MUST perform before empirical antibiotics.
* **Diagnostic Criterion:** Ascitic fluid Polymorphonuclear (PMN) cell count **≥ 250 cells/mm³**.
* **Microbiology Technique:** MUST heavily inoculate fluid (10mL if possible) *directly* into aerobic/anaerobic blood culture bottles at the bedside (↑ yield to >80%).
* **Biochemistry:** Send for protein, glucose, LDH, albumin (calculate Serum-Ascites Albumin Gradient [SAAG] > 11 g/L = portal HTN).

### Routine Blood Investigations
* **Blood Cultures:** Mandatory (frequently +ve bacteremia).
* **CBP & CRP/PCT:** ↑ WBC (neutrophilia), ↑ inflammatory markers.
* **Renal/Electrolytes (RFT):** **Mandatory** to monitor for Hepatorenal Syndrome (↑ Urea, ↑ Creatinine, ↓ Na).
* **Liver Function Tests (LFT):** Worsening bilirubin/transaminases, ↓ Albumin.
* **Coagulation Profile (PT/APTT/INR):** Check before paracentesis (often deranged in cirrhosis, but rarely requires correction unless clinically bleeding).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)

### Acute Antibiotic Management Protocol
* **Timing:** Administer immediately *after* paracentesis if PMN ≥ 250/mm³.
* **1st Line Empirical IV:** 3rd-generation cephalosporins.
    * **IV Cefotaxime (頭孢噻肟):** Preferred in neonates/infants (avoids biliary sludging).
    * **IV Ceftriaxone (頭孢曲松):** Alternative for older children/Nephrotic SBP.
* **Duration:** Typically 5–7 days depending on clinical response.

### Hepatorenal Syndrome (HRS) Prevention Protocol
* **Indication:** Mandatory in cirrhotic SBP (prevents functional renal failure driven by splanchnic vasodilation).
* **Intervention:** **IV Albumin (白蛋白)** infusion.
* **Dosing Schedule:**
    * **Day 1:** 1.5 g/kg IV (Max 100g).
    * **Day 3:** 1.0 g/kg IV.

### Long-Term Prophylaxis & Chronic Management
* **Indication:** Post-SBP recovery (secondary prophylaxis) or primary prophylaxis if ascitic protein is extremely low.
* **Regimen:** Long-term oral antibiotics.
    * **PO Ciprofloxacin (環丙沙星)** or **PO Norfloxacin**.
    * Alt (especially in Nephrotic Syndrome): **PO Co-trimoxazole**.
* **Duration:** Continued lifelong or until liver transplantation.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)

### Multi-Disciplinary Team Involvement
* **Hepatology/Surgical Team:** SBP indicates end-stage liver disease. Triggers urgent listing for **Liver Transplantation (肝臟移植)**.
* **Dietitian:** Manage malnutrition, optimize high-calorie/low-sodium feed formulations.
* **Medical Social Worker (MSW):** Support family with chronic illness burden, assist with transplant logistics/financial aid.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags

### Safeguarding Considerations
* Generally N/A for the pathology of SBP itself.
* Monitor for medical neglect (e.g., missing essential prophylactic antibiotic doses leading to recurrent severe infections).

## 11. Complications & Prognosis

### Major Complications of SBP
* **Hepatorenal Syndrome (肝腎綜合症):** Massive splanchnic vasodilation → profound renal vasoconstriction → anuric renal failure.
* **Septic Shock (敗血性休克):** Rapid progression to multi-organ failure.
* **Hepatic Encephalopathy (肝性腦病):** Infection acts as a major trigger for ammonia accumulation.

### Prognosis of SBP
* High short-term mortality without prompt antibiotics.
* **High Recurrence Rate:** ~70% at 1 year without prophylaxis.
* 1-year survival post-SBP is poor without liver transplantation.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Trap: Waiting for the ascitic fluid culture results before starting IV antibiotics. Pearl: Ascitic fluid PMN count ≥ 250 cells/mm³ is the absolute trigger for immediate empirical antibiotics to prevent mortality.
* Viva Question: Why is giving IV Albumin so critical in treating SBP in cirrhotic patients? Answer: SBP causes massive splanchnic vasodilation leading to functional hypovolemia and renal failure (Hepatorenal syndrome). High-dose IV Albumin acts as a potent volume expander to physically maintain renal perfusion.
* Pitfall: Sending ascitic fluid only in a sterile universal container for culture. Always inoculate ascitic fluid directly into aerobic and anaerobic blood culture bottles at the bedside to maximize the yield of detecting the causative organism.
* Viva Question: What is the most likely causative organism for SBP in a child with relapsing Nephrotic Syndrome? Answer: Streptococcus pneumoniae (肺炎鏈球菌) due to massive urinary loss of immunoglobulins and complement.
* Trap: Assuming multiple organisms grown from an ascitic fluid culture is severe SBP. Pearl: A polymicrobial culture result almost always indicates Secondary Bacterial Peritonitis (e.g., perforated bowel) and requires urgent surgical intervention, not just medical therapy.
