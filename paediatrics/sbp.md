# Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis (SBP) (自發性腹膜炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: An acute bacterial infection of ascitic fluid without any identifiable intra-abdominal source (e.g., no perforated bowel).
- **Local Epidemiology**: A severe, life-threatening complication in children with decompensated cirrhosis (e.g., Biliary Atresia) or Nephrotic Syndrome.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Liver Disease**: Portal hypertension leads to ascites. Cirrhosis massively impairs the reticuloendothelial system and reduced complement levels lower opsonic activity in the ascitic fluid, allowing gut bacteria to undergo "bacterial translocation" across the intestinal wall into the fluid.
- **Nephrotic Syndrome**: Massive loss of immunoglobulins (IgG) and complement factors in the urine makes the ascitic fluid highly susceptible to infection, classically by *Strep pneumoniae*.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Can be subtle in children. Unexplained fever and sudden clinical deterioration in a patient with known ascites.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- High fever, chills.
- Diffuse abdominal pain, marked tenderness, worsening abdominal distension, rebound tenderness (though less pronounced than surgical peritonitis).
- Worsening hepatic encephalopathy (confusion, lethargy) or sudden rapid drop in renal function.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Must distinguish from *Secondary* Bacterial Peritonitis (e.g., perforated appendix). SBP is monomicrobial (usually E. coli or pneumococcus). Secondary peritonitis is profoundly polymicrobial on culture.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Secondary Bacterial Peritonitis (perforation, appendicitis)
- Viral Gastroenteritis
- Urinary Tract Infection
- Cholangitis (very common in post-Kasai Biliary Atresia patients)

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Diagnostic Paracentesis (Before giving antibiotics)**: 
  - Send for cell count, Gram stain, and culture (must heavily inoculate fluid directly into blood culture bottles at the bedside to increase yield).
  - **Diagnostic Criterion**: Ascitic fluid Polymorphonuclear (PMN) cell count $\ge$ 250 cells/mm³.
- **Bloods**: Blood cultures (frequently positive). FBC, CRP, LFTs, Renal function (mandatory to check for Hepatorenal syndrome).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Antibiotics**: Empirical IV 3rd-generation cephalosporins (e.g., Cefotaxime or Ceftriaxone) to cover enteric gram-negatives and pneumococcus. Given immediately after the tap.
- **IV Albumin**: Mandatory in cirrhotic patients with SBP to prevent Hepatorenal Syndrome. Given on Day 1 (1.5g/kg) and Day 3 (1g/kg) via standard HA liver unit protocols.
- **Prophylaxis**: Long-term oral antibiotics (e.g., Norfloxacin/Ciprofloxacin) are given post-recovery to prevent recurrence while awaiting liver transplant.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Indicates end-stage liver failure. Requires urgent hepatology listing for liver transplantation.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- N/A.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Septic shock.
- Development of Hepatorenal Syndrome (extremely high mortality if it occurs).
- Recurrence rate is high.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Waiting for the ascitic fluid culture results before starting antibiotics. The PMN count >250 is the trigger for immediate antibiotics.
- **Viva Q**: "Why is giving IV Albumin so critical in treating SBP?" Answer: SBP causes massive splanchnic vasodilation leading to functional hypovolemia and renal failure (Hepatorenal syndrome). High-dose Albumin acts as a potent volume expander to physically maintain renal perfusion.
