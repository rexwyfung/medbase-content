# Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus & DKA (一型糖尿病及糖尿病酮症酸中毒)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Disease Definition
* **T1DM (一型糖尿病):** Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic β-cells → absolute insulin deficiency. Lifelong exogenous insulin required.
* **DKA (糖尿病酮症酸中毒):** Acute, life-threatening metabolic crisis of T1DM driven by complete insulin absence + stress hormone surge.

### Local Epidemiology & Demographics
* **HK Incidence:** ~2-3 per 100,000 (lower than Caucasian populations but ↑ rapidly). 
* **Bimodal Age Peak:** 4-6 yrs & early puberty (10-14 yrs).
* **Seasonal variation:** ↑ presentations in winter/autumn (linked to viral triggers).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Autoimmune Pathogenesis
* Environmental trigger (e.g., Coxsackie B virus) in genetically susceptible host → T-cell mediated destruction of islets.
* **Pre-clinical phase:** Progressive β-cell loss. Clinical symptoms only manifest when >80-90% of β-cells destroyed.

### DKA Ketogenesis Mechanism
* Absolute insulin ↓ + counter-regulatory hormones ↑ (Glucagon, Catecholamines, Cortisol, GH).
* **Hyperglycemia:** ↓ peripheral glucose uptake + ↑ hepatic gluconeogenesis/glycogenolysis.
* **Ketogenesis:** Profound cellular starvation → rapid lipolysis → massive free fatty acids (FFAs) → hepatic conversion to **Ketones** (β-hydroxybutyrate, acetoacetate) → high anion gap metabolic acidosis.

### Pubertal Insulin Resistance
* Pubertal surges in GH & sex steroids → physiological insulin resistance.
* Adolescents require significantly ↑ basal & bolus insulin doses/kg vs prepubertal children.

### Genetic Susceptibility
* **HLA Genotypes:** Strongly linked to **HLA-DR3** & **HLA-DR4** (chromosome 6).
* Polygenic inheritance, but 85% of newly diagnosed children have NO family history of T1DM.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Classic Chronic Presentation
* **The 4 Ts:** * **T**hirst (**Polydipsia** - 多飲)
  * **T**oilet (**Polyuria** - 多尿 / secondary enuresis)
  * **T**iredness (Lethargy)
  * **T**hinner (**Weight loss** - 體重下降 despite **Polyphagia** - 多食)
* **Infections:** Unrelenting candidal diaper rash (toddlers), oral thrush, recurrent skin infections.

### Acute DKA Crisis Signs
* **Respiratory:** **Kussmaul breathing** (深大呼吸) - deep, rapid, gasping to blow off CO2 (respiratory compensation for metabolic acidosis).
* **Breath:** Fruity/Acetone odor.
* **Fluid Status:** Severe dehydration (↓ skin turgor, sunken eyes, dry mucosa), tachycardia, delayed capillary refill, hypotension (late/pre-terminal sign).
* **Abdominal:** Acute severe abdominal pain + vomiting (mimicking acute abdomen, driven by ketosis/gastric stasis).

### Severe DKA Neurological Signs
* Altered GCS, confusion, lethargy → Coma.
* **Cerebral Edema (腦水腫) Warning:** Headache, irritability, ↓ HR (bradycardia), ↑ BP (hypertension), cranial nerve palsies.

## 4. Discriminatory Features
### T1DM vs T2DM Differential Table

| Feature | T1DM (一型糖尿病) | T2DM (二型糖尿病) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Onset** | Acute, rapid weight loss | Insidious, asymptomatic usually |
| **Body Habitus** | Normal / Underweight | Obese / Overweight |
| **Insulin Resistance Signs** | Absent | Present (**Acanthosis Nigricans**, PCOS) |
| **Autoantibodies** | +ve (GAD, IA2, ZnT8) | -ve |
| **C-Peptide** | Very Low / Undetectable | Normal / High |
| **DKA at Presentation** | Common (~30%) | Rare (but HHS possible) |

### DKA vs Acute Surgical Abdomen
* DKA causes severe abdominal pain mimicking appendicitis. 
* **Key discriminator:** DKA has profound polyuria despite severe clinical dehydration, +ve ketones, severe acidosis. *Rule:* ALWAYS check CBG/urine dipstick in any child with acute abdomen!

## 5. Differential Diagnosis
### Endocrine & Metabolic Differentials
* **Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus**
* **MODY** (Maturity-Onset Diabetes of the Young): Autosomal dominant, mild fasting hyperglycemia, non-insulin dependent initially.
* **Stress Hyperglycemia:** Transient ↑ glucose during severe sepsis, trauma, or febrile illness (no profound acidosis/ketosis).
* **Diabetes Insipidus:** Massive polyuria/polydipsia but NO hyperglycemia, NO glycosuria.

## 6. Tiered Investigations
### Immediate Bedside Tests
* **CBG (Capillary Blood Glucose):** Usually >11.1 mmol/L.
* **Blood Ketones:** Capillary β-hydroxybutyrate (**>3.0 mmol/L** = severe DKA).
* **Urine Dipstick:** Heavy glycosuria (+++) & ketonuria (+++).

### Urgent Bloods for DKA Protocol
* **VBG (Venous Blood Gas):** Key for DKA staging.
  * Mild DKA: pH <7.3, HCO3 <15
  * Mod DKA: pH <7.2, HCO3 <10
  * Severe DKA: pH <7.1, HCO3 <5
* **U&Es (Renal Panel):** Monitor Potassium (K+). Total body K+ is severely depleted, though serum K+ may initially appear normal/high due to extracellular shift from acidosis. 
* **Calculated Anion Gap:** Na - (Cl + HCO3). Normal 12±4. DKA = High AG.

### Confirmatory Immunological Bloods
* **Autoantibodies:** Anti-GAD, Anti-IA2, Islet Cell Antibodies (ICA), Zinc transporter 8 (ZnT8).
* **C-Peptide:** Extremely low, proving absent endogenous insulin.
* **HbA1c:** >6.5% (confirms chronicity of hyperglycemia).
* **Autoimmune Screen:** Thyroid TFTs (Hashimoto's) & Tissue Transglutaminase IgA (Coeliac disease) due to high co-occurrence.

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Acute DKA: Step 1 Fluid Resuscitation
* **CRITICAL RULE:** NEVER give an IV insulin bolus (triggers lethal cerebral edema).
* **Shock present:** 10-20ml/kg 0.9% NaCl bolus over 15-30 mins.
* **No shock/Post-resus:** Calculate fluid deficit (assume 5-10% dehydration) + maintenance. Replace slowly over **48 hours** using 0.9% NaCl to prevent rapid osmotic shifts.

### Acute DKA: Step 2 Insulin Infusion
* Start continuous IV Insulin infusion (**0.05 - 0.1 units/kg/hr**).
* **Timing:** Must start **1-2 hours AFTER** commencing IV fluids.
* When CBG drops < 14 mmol/L, add 5% Dextrose to IV fluids to prevent hypoglycemia while continuing insulin to clear ketones.

### Acute DKA: Step 3 Potassium Replacement
* Add K+ (usually 40 mmol/L) to IV maintenance fluids as soon as urine output is confirmed.
* Insulin dramatically drives K+ intracellularly → high risk of lethal hypokalemia.

### Acute DKA: Cerebral Edema Protocol
* Monitor strictly: hourly GCS, pupils, HR, BP.
* **Signs:** Cushing's triad (↓HR, ↑BP, irregular breathing), sudden headache.
* **Action:** IMMEDIATELY give **IV Hypertonic Saline (3%) 3-5ml/kg** OR **IV Mannitol 0.5-1g/kg**. Restrict IV fluids by 1/3. Intubate if airway compromised.

### Chronic Long-Term Management
* **MDI (Multiple Daily Injections):** Basal-Bolus regimen. Long-acting (Glargine/Detemir) OD + Rapid-acting (Novorapid/Lispro) before meals.
* **CSII (Insulin Pump):** Continuous basal micro-dosing + meal boluses. 
* **Monitoring:** CGM (Continuous Glucose Monitoring) targeting high "Time In Range" (TIR). Target HbA1c < 7.0%.
* **Carb Counting:** Adjusting rapid insulin dose based on specific carbohydrate grams ingested.

### Sick Day Rules
* **NEVER STOP BASAL INSULIN** even if anorexic/vomiting.
* Monitor CBG & Ketones every 2 hours.
* Intercurrent illness ↑ stress hormones → ↑ insulin requirement (despite poor oral intake) to prevent DKA.

### Hypoglycemia Management
* **"Rule of 15":** If CBG < 4.0 mmol/L → give 15g fast-acting carb (juice/glucose tabs) → wait 15 mins → recheck. 
* Once >4.0 mmol/L, give complex carb (bread/milk) to sustain.
* **Severe (Unconscious/Seizing):** IM Glucagon or IV 10% Dextrose (2ml/kg).

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Core MDT Members
* Paediatric Endocrinologist, DSN (Diabetes Specialist Nurse), Dietitian, Clinical Psychologist, MSW (Medical Social Worker).

### Psychosocial Challenges
* **Burnout:** High daily burden of carb-counting, injections, finger-pricks.
* **Adolescence:** Rebellion, peer pressure, skipping doses to "fit in".
* **Diabulimia:** Intentional insulin omission to induce glycosuria and massive weight loss (highly dangerous eating disorder variant).

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Medical Neglect Indicators
* Recurrent DKA admissions strongly indicate medical neglect, parental sabotage, or severe unmanaged psychiatric distress.
* Requires immediate MSW & SWD (Social Welfare Department) involvement in HK for care orders if child's life is at risk.

## 10. Complications & Prognosis
### Acute Complications
* Hypoglycemia (can lead to seizures/coma).
* **Cerebral Edema (腦水腫):** The #1 cause of mortality in paediatric DKA.
* Hypokalemia-induced arrhythmias.

### Chronic Microvascular Complications
* **Diabetic Retinopathy:** Annual retinal screening from age 11.
* **Diabetic Nephropathy:** Annual urine albumin:creatinine ratio (microalbuminuria). Treat with ACE inhibitors if +ve.
* **Diabetic Neuropathy:** Peripheral sensory loss (stocking-glove), autonomic neuropathy.

### Chronic Macrovascular Complications
* Accelerated atherosclerosis → premature IHD, stroke, peripheral vascular disease. Monitor lipids and BP annually.

## 11. Examiner's Pearls
* Trap: Giving an IV insulin bolus for paediatric DKA in A&E. Pearl: This is an automatic fail. It triggers rapid osmotic shifts and lethal cerebral edema. Fluid rehydration strictly comes first, and insulin is ALWAYS a slow continuous infusion.
* Viva: A teenager is brought to A&E unconscious. Which finding on standard baseline bloods would completely rule out DKA? Answer: A normal blood pH and normal bicarbonate on VBG. DKA explicitly requires metabolic acidosis by definition.
* Pitfall: Stopping insulin when a diabetic child is vomiting and not eating. Never stop basal insulin! Stress hormones during illness drive ketogenesis; they need MORE insulin, not less. Advise frequent CBG/ketone checks and sugar-containing fluids.
* Viva: Why do we proactively add potassium to IV fluids in DKA when their initial blood test shows hyperkalemia? Answer: Total body K+ is profoundly depleted via massive osmotic diuresis. The acidotic state shifted K+ out of cells, artificially elevating serum levels. Once insulin is started, K+ rapidly shifts back into cells, which can trigger a fatal hypokalemic arrhythmia if not supplemented.
