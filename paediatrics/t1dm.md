# Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus & DKA (一型糖尿病及糖尿病酮症酸中毒)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: An autoimmune condition where the immune system selectively destroys pancreatic beta cells, leading to absolute insulin deficiency. Patients require lifelong exogenous insulin. Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) is its acute, life-threatening crisis.
- **Local Epidemiology**: One of the most common chronic childhood and adolescent diseases globally. Incidence is rising. The bimodal peak of presentation occurs at 4-6 years of age and in early puberty (10-14 years).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Insulin Deficiency**: Without insulin, glucose cannot enter cells (muscle/adipose) for energy. The body enters a state of perceived starvation, despite profound hyperglycemia in the blood.
- **DKA Mechanism**: To survive the cellular starvation, the liver rapidly breaks down fat into free fatty acids and converts them into **Ketones** (beta-hydroxybutyrate, acetoacetate) for alternative brain fuel. Ketones are strong organic acids, dropping the blood pH and causing severe metabolic acidosis.
- **Pubertal Insulin Resistance**: Hormones like Growth Hormone (GH) and sex steroids surge during puberty, physiologically inducing insulin resistance. Teenagers require significantly higher basal and bolus insulin doses per kilogram than prepubertal children.
- **Genetics**: HLA-DR3 and HLA-DR4 are heavily associated. It is polygenic, triggered by environmental factors (e.g., enterovirus) in susceptible individuals.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- **New Onset T1DM**: A 10-year-old boy presents with a 4-week history of constantly drinking water, urinating frequently (multiple times a night), and losing 5kg of weight despite eating voraciously.
- **DKA**: A 13-year-old girl is rushed to A&E with vomiting, extreme abdominal pain, deep and rapid "gasping" breathing, and altered sensorium. Her breath smells "fruity."

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Chronic/New Presentation**:
  - The 4 Ts: **T**hirst (Polydipsia), **T**oilet (Polyuria/Nocturia/Enuresis in a previously dry child), **T**iredness, **T**hinner (Weight loss).
  - Polyphagia (extreme hunger).
  - Candidiasis (e.g., severe unrelenting diaper rash in a toddler, thrush in an older girl).
- **DKA (The Acute Crisis)**:
  - **Kussmaul Breathing**: Deep, rapid, gasping respirations as the lungs desperately attempt to blow off CO2 to compensate for the severe metabolic acidosis.
  - Fruity/Acetone odor on the breath.
  - Tachycardia, severe dehydration, delayed capillary refill.
  - **Acute Abdomen**: Severe abdominal pain and vomiting (secondary to ketosis and hypoperfusion) that can perfectly mimic appendicitis.
  - Altered Level of Consciousness / Coma.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from Type 2 Diabetes (T2DM): T2DM typically presents in obese adolescents with signs of insulin resistance (Acanthosis Nigricans), strong family history, and less pronounced acute weight loss or sudden DKA.
- Distinct from Acute Appendicitis: DKA can cause severe vomiting and an acute rigid abdomen. However, DKA will present with Polyuria, extreme hyperglycemia, and profound acidosis, whereas appendicitis typically will not. ALWAY do a blood strip/urine dipstick for a child with an acute abdomen!

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus
- MODY (Maturity-Onset Diabetes of the Young - autosomal dominant, non-insulin dependent)
- Stress Hyperglycemia (e.g., during severe sepsis or trauma)
- Diabetes Insipidus (causes polyuria/polydipsia, but absolutely NO hyperglycemia)

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside (CRITICAL)**: Capillary Blood Glucose (CBG), Blood Ketones (Beta-hydroxybutyrate > 3.0 mmol/L indicates severe DKA), Urine Dipstick (heavy glucose and ketones).
- **Bloods (DKA Protocol)**:
  - **VBG (Venous Blood Gas)**: Measures pH, pCO2, Base Excess, and Bicarbonate. (Defining DKA: pH < 7.3 or Bicarbonate < 15 mmol/L).
  - Renal Function (U&Es): Specifically noting **Potassium**. (Total body potassium is fiercely depleted, even if blood K+ is temporarily high moving to extracellular space).
  - HbA1c (confirms chronic hyperglycemia > 6.5%).
- **Special/Immune**:
  - Autoantibodies to confirm T1DM: Anti-GAD, Islet Cell Antibodies (ICA), Anti-IA2, Zinc transporter 8 (ZnT8).
  - C-Peptide (will be extremely low, proving the pancreas is not making endogenous insulin).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **ACUTE DKA MANAGEMENT (Crucial Paediatric Protocol)**:
  - **NEVER** give an immediate IV Insulin bolus. This is a fatal adult protocol mistake that causes rapid osmotic shifts and lethal cerebral edema in children.
  - **Step 1 (Fluids)**: Initial fluid resuscitation (10-20ml/kg 0.9% NaCl over 1 hour) ONLY for profound shock. Then, carefully calculate deficit over 48 hours to avoid rapid shifts.
  - **Step 2 (Insulin)**: Start a low-dose continuous IV Insulin infusion (usually 0.05 - 0.1 units/kg/hr) at least 1-2 hours *after* starting fluids.
  - **Step 3 (Potassium)**: Carefully add Potassium to the IV fluids as soon as urine output is confirmed, as insulin will furiously drive K+ back into the cells, risking lethal hypokalemia.
  - **Strict Neuro Obs**: Monitor religiously for fatal **Cerebral Edema** (headache, bradycardia, hypertension). Treat with IV Mannitol or 3% Hypertonic Saline immediately.
- **CHRONIC MANAGEMENT**:
  - **Basal-Bolus Regimen (MDI)**: Long-acting basal (Glargine) plus rapid-acting bolus (Novorapid) before meals.
  - **Insulin Pumps (CSII)**: Provides continuous basal pulses and mealtime boluses.
  - **Monitoring**: Continuous Glucose Monitoring (CGM e.g. Dexcom/Libre) monitoring Time In Range (TIR).
  - **Carb Counting**: Adjusting insulin based on specifically calculated food carbohydrates.
  - **Sick Day Rules**: *NEVER stop basal insulin* even if vomiting/not eating. Check glucose/ketones every 2 hours. The body needs *more* insulin during stressful sick periods to keep ketosis at bay.
  - **The "Rule of 15" for Hypoglycemia**: Blood glucose < 4.0 mmol/L. Take 15g fast-acting carbs (juice/sugar), wait 15 mins, re-check. Once >4.0, eat a complex carb (bread/biscuit).

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Essential MDT involvement: Paediatric Endocrinologist, Diabetic Specialist Nurse (DSN), Dietician, and Clinical Psychologist.
- High burden of burnout and psychosocial stress. Adolescents frequently battle compliance ("rebellion") and fear of social ostracization.
- **Diabulimia**: A dangerous eating disorder variant where teenagers intentionally omit insulin to induce severe glycosuria (peeing out calories) for extreme weight loss.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Recurring admission for severe DKA can be a direct marker of severe medical neglect, active parental sabotage, or severe psychological distress requiring immediate MSW intervention.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Acute**: Hypoglycemia, DKA, Cerebral Edema (the most common cause of mortality in paediatric DKA).
- **Chronic Vascular**:
  - Microvascular: Diabetic Retinopathy (blindness), Nephropathy (renal failure/microalbuminuria), Neuropathy.
  - Macrovascular: Early coronary artery disease and strokes.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Bolusing IV Insulin in paediatric DKA in the A&E. An examiner will automatically fail you if you do this. It leads directly to acute cerebral edema. Fluid rehydration strictly comes first, and insulin is a slow, continuous infusion.
- **Viva**: "A teenager is brought to A&E unconscious. Which finding on standard baseline bloods would completely rule out DKA?" Answer: A normal blood pH and bicarbonate on VBG. DKA requires acidosis by definition.
- **Viva**: "Why do we add potassium to IV fluids in DKA when their initial blood test shows hyperkalemia?" Answer: Because their *total body* potassium is profoundly depleted from massive osmotic diuresis. The acidotic blood shifted intracellular K+ outwards making it superficially high. The moment you give insulin, K+ rushes violently back into cells, completely collapsing the blood level and triggering fatal arrhythmias if not supplemented.
