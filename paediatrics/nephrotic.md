# Nephrotic Syndrome (腎病綜合症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A clinical syndrome defined by a classic triad:
  1. **Heavy Proteinuria**: > 40 mg/m2/hr, or Urine Protein:Creatinine Ratio (UPCR) > 200 mg/mmol, or +++ / ++++ on urine dipstick.
  2. **Hypoalbuminaemia**: Serum albumin < 25 g/L.
  3. **Generalized Oedema**.
  *(Hyperlipidaemia is also a hallmark feature).*
- **Local Epidemiology**: Incidence is significantly higher in Asian populations (including Hong Kong) compared to Caucasians. Peak age of onset is 2 to 6 years. More common in boys (2:1).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Glomerular Barrier Dysfunction**: The primary defect is damage to the podocytes of the glomerular filtration barrier. The loss of negative charge and structural integrity allows massive leakage of plasma proteins (primarily albumin) into the urine.
- **Oedema Formation**: Hypoalbuminaemia decreases plasma oncotic pressure, causing fluid to shift from the intravascular space into the interstitial space. This leads to intravascular volume depletion, which activates the Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System (RAAS), causing sodium and water retention, further exacerbating the oedema.
- **Hyperlipidaemia**: The liver compensates for the low oncotic pressure by upregulating the synthesis of lipoproteins (cholesterol and triglycerides).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- A previously healthy child presents with an insidious onset of **periorbital oedema**.
- *Classic Trap*: The periorbital swelling is often worse in the morning and is frequently misdiagnosed initially as allergic conjunctivitis.
- Over days to weeks, the swelling progresses to generalized oedema (anasarca), including ascites, scrotal/vulval oedema, and pitting oedema of the lower limbs.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Frothy Urine**: Due to the high protein content.
- **Pitting Oedema**: Sacral (if bedbound) or pedal.
- **Ascites**: Shifting dullness, distended abdomen.
- **Weight Gain**: Rapid increase in weight due to fluid retention.
- **Blood Pressure**: Usually NORMAL or even low (due to intravascular volume depletion). *Hypertension is a red flag for other glomerular diseases.*

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Nephrotic vs. Nephritic Syndrome (e.g., APSGN)**:
  - *Nephrotic*: Massive proteinuria, severe oedema, normal BP, normal renal function, no/minimal haematuria.
  - *Nephritic*: Macroscopic haematuria (cola-coloured urine), hypertension, oliguria, acute kidney injury, mild-to-moderate oedema.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Acute Post-Streptococcal Glomerulonephritis (APSGN).
- Protein-Losing Enteropathy.
- Liver Failure (decreased albumin synthesis).
- Heart Failure.
- Severe Allergic Reaction (Angioedema).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Urine**:
  - Dipstick: +++ or ++++ protein.
  - Early morning UPCR: > 200 mg/mmol.
  - Microscopy: Oval fat bodies, hyaline casts. Rule out significant haematuria.
- **Bloods**:
  - Low Serum Albumin (< 25 g/L).
  - High Cholesterol and Triglycerides.
  - U&E and Creatinine: Usually normal.
  - **Complement levels (C3, C4)**: MUST be checked. They are **NORMAL** in typical Minimal Change Disease. Low C3 suggests APSGN, Lupus Nephritis, or MPGN.
- **Renal Biopsy**: NOT routinely indicated for the typical childhood presentation (1-10 years old), as >90% have Minimal Change Disease (MCD) which responds to steroids.
  - *Indications for Biopsy*: Age < 1 year or > 10 years, macroscopic haematuria, hypertension, low C3, renal impairment, or steroid resistance.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Specific Therapy (The Mainstay)**:
  - **High-Dose Oral Prednisolone**: 2 mg/kg/day (max 60mg) for 4-6 weeks, followed by a tapering dose over another 4-6 weeks. ~90% of children will achieve remission (urine becomes protein-free).
- **Supportive Therapy**:
  - **Diet**: Strict fluid restriction and low-sodium diet during the oedematous phase.
  - **Diuretics**: Use with EXTREME CAUTION. Furosemide + Spironolactone are only used for severe, symptomatic oedema (e.g., respiratory compromise from ascites/pleural effusion) because the patient is already intravascularly depleted. Over-diuresis can precipitate hypovolaemic shock or thrombosis.
  - **Albumin Infusion**: 20% IV Albumin followed by IV Furosemide is reserved for severe hypovolaemia or refractory oedema.
- **Relapse Management**: Relapses are very common (triggered by viral URTIs). Treated with shorter courses of steroids. Frequent relapsers or steroid-dependent patients require steroid-sparing agents (e.g., Cyclophosphamide, Tacrolimus, Mycophenolate Mofetil).

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Education**: Parents MUST be taught how to use urine dipsticks at home to check the first morning urine daily. Early detection of a relapse (+++ protein for 3 consecutive days) allows early steroid initiation, preventing severe oedema.
- **Dietitian**: Guidance on low-salt and low-fat diets, and managing increased appetite from steroids.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Non-compliance with steroid therapy or failure to attend follow-ups leading to severe, life-threatening complications constitutes medical neglect.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Infection (Major cause of mortality)**: High risk of **Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis (SBP)** and sepsis, usually caused by *Streptococcus pneumoniae*. Due to loss of immunoglobulins and complement factors in the urine, and immunosuppression from steroids.
- **Thrombosis**: A hypercoagulable state due to loss of Antithrombin III in the urine, increased hepatic synthesis of procoagulant factors, and haemoconcentration. Risk of DVT, Renal Vein Thrombosis, and Sagittal Sinus Thrombosis.
- **Steroid Toxicity**: Cushingoid facies, obesity, growth suppression, osteoporosis, cataracts, hypertension.
- **Prognosis**: Excellent for Minimal Change Disease (most eventually outgrow it). Poor for Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (FSGS), which often progresses to End-Stage Renal Failure.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "Why are nephrotic patients at such high risk of thrombosis?" (Loss of Antithrombin III in the urine, increased liver production of fibrinogen, and intravascular volume depletion causing sluggish flow).
- *Viva*: "A 4-year-old with nephrotic syndrome presents with sudden onset severe abdominal pain and fever. What is the most likely diagnosis?" (Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis, most likely caused by *Strep pneumoniae*).
- *Pitfall*: Giving aggressive IV diuretics to a nephrotic child without assessing their intravascular volume status, precipitating shock or a thrombotic event.
