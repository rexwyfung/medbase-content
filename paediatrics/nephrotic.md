# Nephrotic Syndrome (腎病綜合症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition
- A clinical syndrome defined by a classic triad:
  - **Heavy Proteinuria (大量蛋白尿)**: > 40 mg/m2/hr, or Urine Protein:Creatinine Ratio (UPCR) > 200 mg/mmol, or +++ / ++++ on urine dipstick.
  - **Hypoalbuminaemia (低白蛋白血症)**: Serum albumin < 25 g/L.
  - **Generalized Oedema (全身水腫)**.
- Additional hallmark: **Hyperlipidaemia (高脂血症)**.

### Local Epidemiology & Demographics
- Incidence ↑ in Asian populations (incl. Hong Kong).
- Peak age of onset: 1 to 10 years (median 2 to 6 years).
- Sex: ♂ > ♀ (2:1 ratio).
- Frequently associated with **Atopy (特應性)**.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics

### Glomerular Barrier Dysfunction
- Primary defect: Damage to **podocytes (足細胞)** of the glomerular filtration barrier.
- Structural integrity & -ve charge lost → massive leakage of plasma proteins (primarily albumin) into urine.
- Light microscopy: normal. Electron microscopy: **podocyte fusion**.

### Oedema Formation Mechanism
- Massive proteinuria → severe **Hypoalbuminaemia**.
- ↓ Plasma oncotic pressure → fluid shifts from intravascular to interstitial space.
- Intravascular volume depletion → RAAS activation → Na+ & H2O retention → exacerbates oedema.

### Hyperlipidaemia Pathophysiology
- Liver compensates for ↓ oncotic pressure → ↑ synthesis of lipoproteins (cholesterol & triglycerides).

### Genetics & Steroid Resistance
- Up to 30% of children with **Steroid-Resistant Nephrotic Syndrome (SRNS)** have mutations in genes encoding podocyte/GBM proteins.
- Genetic testing recommended for all SRNS cases.

## 3. Clinical Presentation

### Initial Presentation
- Previously healthy child.
- Insidious onset.
- Often precipitated by a viral URTI.

### Progressive Swelling
- **Periorbital oedema (眼眶水腫)**: Classically worse in the morning. Often initially misdiagnosed as allergic conjunctivitis.
- Progresses over days/weeks to generalized **anasarca (全身水腫)**.
- Can include ascites, scrotal/vulval oedema, & pitting pedal oedema.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Cardinal Signs
- **Frothy Urine (泡沫尿)**: Due to high protein content.
- **Pitting Oedema (凹陷性水腫)**: Sacral (if bedbound), pedal, scrotal/vulval.
- **Ascites (腹水)**: Distended abdomen, shifting dullness.
- **Weight Gain**: Rapid ↑ due to total body fluid retention.
- **Pleural Effusions (胸腔積液)** → Breathlessness.

### Blood Pressure & Fluid Status
- BP: Usually NORMAL or ↓ (due to intravascular volume depletion).
- **Hypertension (高血壓)** is a red flag suggesting other glomerulonephritis.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)

### Nephrotic vs. Nephritic Syndrome
| Clinical Feature | Nephrotic Syndrome (腎病綜合症) | Nephritic Syndrome (e.g., APSGN) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Proteinuria** | Massive (+++ / ++++) | Mild to Moderate (+ / ++) |
| **Haematuria** | None / Microscopic | **Macroscopic (Cola-coloured urine)** |
| **Blood Pressure** | Normal or ↓ | **Hypertension (高血壓)** |
| **Oedema** | Severe (Anasarca) | Mild to Moderate |
| **Renal Function** | Normal | **Impaired (AKI, Oliguria)** |
| **Complement Levels** | Normal | Low C3 |

## 6. Differential Diagnosis

### Primary Causes
- **Minimal Change Disease (微小病變型腎病)** (>85% of childhood cases).
- Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (FSGS).
- Membranous Nephropathy.

### Secondary Causes
- Henoch–Schönlein purpura (HSP).
- Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) / Lupus Nephritis.
- Infections (Malaria, Hepatitis B/C).
- Allergens (Bee sting).

### Conditions Mimicking Oedema
- Acute Post-Streptococcal Glomerulonephritis (APSGN).
- Protein-Losing Enteropathy.
- Liver Failure (↓ albumin synthesis).
- Heart Failure.
- Severe Allergic Reaction (Angioedema).

## 7. Tiered Investigations

### Bedside Urinalysis
- **Urine Dipstick**: +++ or ++++ protein.
- **Early Morning UPCR**: > 200 mg/mmol.
- **Urine Microscopy**: Oval fat bodies, hyaline casts. MUST rule out macroscopic haematuria.

### Blood Investigations
- **Serum Albumin**: ↓ (< 25 g/L).
- **Lipid Profile**: ↑ Cholesterol & Triglycerides.
- **Renal Function (U&E, Cr)**: Usually normal. ↑ Urea/Cr suggests intravascular depletion or AKI.
- **Full Blood Count (FBC)**: ↑ Hb / Haematocrit indicates haemoconcentration (hypovolaemia).
- **Complement (C3, C4)**: MUST check. Normal in Minimal Change Disease. ↓ C3 suggests APSGN, SLE, or MPGN.
- **Infection Screen**: ASOT/Anti-DNAse B, Hep B/C, Malaria screen (if travel history).

### Special Fluid Status Tests
- **Urinary Sodium**: < 10 mmol/L strongly indicates severe intravascular depletion.

### Renal Biopsy Indications
- NOT routine for typical childhood presentation (1-10 years, normal BP, normal C3, no macroscopic haematuria).
- **Indications**: Age < 1 or > 10 years, macroscopic haematuria, HTN, ↓ C3, renal impairment, steroid resistance.

## 8. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)

### 1st Line Specific Therapy (Steroid-Sensitive)
- **High-Dose Oral Prednisolone (類固醇)**: 60 mg/m2/day for 4 weeks.
- **Tapering Phase**: 40 mg/m2 alternate days for a further 4 weeks, then weaned/stopped.
- ~90% achieve remission (protein-free urine); median time to remission is 11 days.

### Acute Supportive Therapy
- **Diet**: Strict fluid restriction & low-sodium diet during the active oedematous phase.
- **Hypovolaemia Management**: If abdo pain, faintness, ↓ BP, or ↑ Hb → Urgent IV 0.9% Saline or 4.5% Albumin.
- **Diuretics**: Use with EXTREME CAUTION. Only for severe, symptomatic oedema (e.g., respiratory compromise). IV 20% Albumin followed by IV Furosemide. Over-diuresis alone precipitates shock/thrombosis.

### Chronic & Relapse Management
- **Relapses**: Frequent (often triggered by viral URTIs). Treated with shorter steroid courses.
- **Steroid-Sparing Agents**: Indicated for frequent relapsers or steroid-dependent patients.
  - Levamisole, Mycophenolate Mofetil, Tacrolimus, Rituximab.
- **Steroid-Resistant (SRNS)**: Refer to Paediatric Nephrologist. Requires biopsy, ACE inhibitors, NSAIDs, and alternative immunosuppression.

### Infection Prophylaxis
- **Antibiotics**: Penicillin prophylaxis routinely used during relapse.
- **Vaccinations**: Pneumococcal & seasonal influenza vaccines strongly recommended.
- **Varicella Exposure**: Treat immediately with Aciclovir.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care

### Patient & Parent Education
- Home Urine Testing: Parents MUST dipstick first-morning urine daily.
- Early Relapse Detection: +++ protein for 3 consecutive days allows early steroid initiation, preventing anasarca and hospitalization.

### Dietitian Support
- Guidance on low-salt and low-fat diets.
- Strategies for managing hyperphagia (increased appetite) caused by prolonged steroid use.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags

### Medical Neglect Red Flags
- Non-compliance with daily dipstick monitoring, steroid therapy, or failure to attend follow-ups → leads to life-threatening complications (shock, sepsis, thrombosis) and constitutes medical neglect.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis

### Hypovolaemia & Shock
- Intravascular space is severely depleted despite total body fluid overload.
- Can lead to severe abdominal pain, faintness, peripheral vasoconstriction, and hypovolaemic shock.

### Thrombosis (Hypercoagulable State)
- ↑ Risk of DVT, Renal Vein Thrombosis, and Sagittal Sinus Thrombosis.
- Causes: Urinary loss of Antithrombin III, ↑ hepatic synthesis of clotting factors, thrombocytosis (steroid-exacerbated), and haemoconcentration.

### Infection Risk
- Major cause of mortality. High risk for capsulated bacteria (**Streptococcus pneumoniae**).
- Complications: **Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis (自發性細菌性腹膜炎)**, sepsis, cellulitis.
- Causes: Urinary loss of immunoglobulins (IgG) and complement, and steroid immunosuppression.

### Steroid Toxicity
- Chronic use → Cushingoid facies, obesity, growth suppression, osteoporosis, cataracts, hypertension.

### Prognosis
- **Steroid-Sensitive (MCD)**: Excellent. The vast majority outgrow the condition by adulthood without permanent renal damage.
- **Steroid-Resistant (FSGS)**: Poor. High risk of progression to End-Stage Renal Disease (ESRD) requiring dialysis or transplant.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Viva Question: What is the most common cause of primary nephrotic syndrome in childhood? Answer: Minimal Change Disease (微小病變型腎病), accounting for 85-90% of cases.
* Viva Question: Why are nephrotic patients at such high risk of thrombosis? Answer: Loss of Antithrombin III in urine, ↑ hepatic production of clotting factors, thrombocytosis, and haemoconcentration from intravascular volume depletion.
* Viva Question: A 4-year-old with nephrotic syndrome presents with sudden onset severe abdominal pain, fever, and rebound tenderness. What is the most likely diagnosis? Answer: Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis (SBP), most commonly caused by Streptococcus pneumoniae.
* Trap: Giving aggressive IV loop diuretics (e.g., Furosemide) alone to treat severe oedema. Pearl: The patient is already severely intravascularly depleted. Diuresis alone can precipitate hypovolaemic shock and massive thrombosis. You must use IV 20% Albumin followed by IV Furosemide.
* Pitfall: Misdiagnosing periorbital oedema in a young child as allergic conjunctivitis. Always perform a urine dipstick to rule out nephrotic syndrome in any child presenting with puffy eyes, especially in the morning.
* Pitfall: Performing a routine renal biopsy in a 5-year-old with classic nephrotic syndrome. Biopsy is NOT indicated unless there are atypical features (age <1 or >10, macroscopic haematuria, HTN, ↓ C3, or steroid resistance).
