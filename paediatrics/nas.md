# Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome (新生兒戒斷綜合症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition of Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome
- **Withdrawal syndrome** of neonates caused by the cessation of in utero exposure to **addictive drugs** (most commonly opioids e.g., methadone, heroin).
- Triggers massive CNS hyperirritability, autonomic dysfunction, and GI disturbances.

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
- Increasingly driven by **polydrug abuse (多重藥物濫用)** (Opioids + Benzodiazepines + Methamphetamine/Ketamine).
- Strong linkage to **Social Welfare Department (SWD) (社會福利署)** cases and maternal psychiatric comorbidities.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Pathophysiology of Drug Withdrawal
- Maternal drugs cross placenta → **Fetal dependence**.
- Abrupt separation from placenta at birth → ↓ dopamine/serotonin, ↑ noradrenaline/acetylcholine.
- Rebound release of excitatory neurotransmitters → **CNS & Autonomic hyperarousal**.

### Drug Half-Life & Onset Timing
| Drug Type | Typical Onset | Duration of Symptoms |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Heroin (海洛英)** | 24-48 hrs | 8-10 days |
| **Methadone (美沙酮)** | 48-72 hrs (up to 1 week) | Up to 30 days+ |
| **Methamphetamine/Cocaine** | 24-48 hrs | Often transient, behavioral |

## 3. Clinical Presentation
### Clinical Presentation Timing & Observation Protocols
- **Onset** varies based on drug half-life.
- **HA Protocol**: Mandatory prolonged inpatient observation.
- Heroin/short-acting: observe minimum **5 days**.
- Methadone/Buprenorphine: observe minimum **7 days**.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
### CNS Symptoms of NAS
- **High-pitched cry (高音哭聲)**.
- Excessive **jitteriness/tremors (震顫)** & irritability.
- ↑ muscle tone (hypertonia), hyperactive Moro reflex.
- Poor sleep patterns.
- **Seizures (抽搐)** (rare, 2-5%, usually methadone).

### Gastrointestinal Symptoms of NAS
- Frantic but **uncoordinated sucking**.
- Poor feeding & weight loss.
- **Vomiting (嘔吐)** & loose watery stools.

### Autonomic & Respiratory Symptoms of NAS
- Sweating, frequent yawning, sneezing.
- **Tachypnoea (呼吸急促)** (>60 breaths/min).
- Mild fever/temperature instability.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Distinguishing NAS from Metabolic & Infectious Mimics
- **Jitteriness vs. Seizures**: Jitteriness stops when limb is held/flexed; true seizures do not stop with passive flexion.
- **Rule out Hypoglycaemia**: Must check **CBG**; NAS jitteriness can directly mimic low glucose.
- **Rule out Sepsis**: Fever + tachypnoea in NAS overlaps with neonatal sepsis. If CRP/WBC ↑ or clinically unstable → treat as sepsis.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
### Common Differential Diagnoses for Neonatal Irritability
| Differential | Differentiating Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Hypoglycaemia (低血糖)** | CBG < 2.6 mmol/L; jitteriness resolves with feeding/IV dextrose. |
| **Hypocalcaemia (低血鈣)** | ↓ Ca2+ on blood gas/biochem; often presents day 2-3 of life. |
| **Neonatal Sepsis / Meningitis** | Lethargy, temp instability, ↑ CRP, +ve cultures. |
| **Hypoxic Ischaemic Encephalopathy (HIE)** | History of perinatal asphyxia, altered level of consciousness. |
| **Thyrotoxicosis (甲狀腺毒症)** | Maternal Graves' disease history, ↑ HR, goitre. |

## 7. Tiered Investigations
### Bedside Investigations for Suspected NAS
- **CBG**: Rule out hypoglycaemia & electrolyte derangement.
- **Finnegan Neonatal Abstinence Scoring System (FNASS)**: Performed every 3-4 hours (usually 1-2 hours post-feed).

### Laboratory & Toxicology Investigations
- **Maternal Urine Toxicology**: Screen for opioids, amphetamines, cocaine, benzos (requires maternal consent legally).
- **Neonatal Meconium/Urine Toxicology**: Meconium reflects 2nd/3rd-trimester exposure; urine reflects recent days.
- **Infectious Disease Screen**: MUST screen baby for Hepatitis B, C, HIV, and Syphilis (VDRL) given maternal high-risk behaviors.

## 8. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Non-Pharmacological Management of NAS
- **1st Line for all infants**:
  - Swaddling (包裹), minimal handling.
  - Dark, quiet environment.
  - Pacifier use for non-nutritive sucking.
  - Frequent, small, high-calorie feeds (hypercaloric formula if weight loss).

### Pharmacological Management of NAS (HA Protocols)
- **Indication**: Finnegan score persistently > 8 on three consecutive occasions, or > 12 on two occasions.
- **Oral Morphine Solution**: 1st line for opioid withdrawal. Dose titrated up until symptoms controlled, then gradually weaned over days/weeks.
- **Phenobarbitone (苯巴比妥)**: 1st line for non-opioid (sedative/hypnotic) withdrawal, polydrug abuse, or as adjunct for refractory opiate NAS.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Multidisciplinary Team & Social Welfare Involvement
- **Medical Social Worker (MSW) (醫務社工)**: CRITICAL role. Formal assessment of parenting capacity.
- **Care and Protection Order (CPO) (照顧或保護令)**: Often applied via SWD if home environment is unsafe or mother lacks capacity.
- **Maternal and Child Health Centre (MCHC) (母嬰健康院)**: For close post-discharge developmental and growth monitoring.

### Breastfeeding Guidelines in Maternal Drug Abuse
- **Contraindications**: Active illicit drug use, HIV positive status, ongoing polydrug abuse.
- **Supported**: Mother on stable methadone maintenance program, -ve for HIV/Hep C, strong psychosocial support.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Safeguarding Concerns & NAI Risks
- **Parental Red Flags**: Erratic behavior on the ward, requesting early discharge against medical advice (DAMA), failure to attend antenatal checks.
- **NAI Risk**: Irritable, poorly consolable infant + stressed/impaired mother = high risk for Shaken Baby Syndrome (Abusive Head Trauma).
- **Action**: Mandates urgent MSW review and holding the baby in the ward until safe discharge plan is formulated.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
### Short-term & Long-term Complications of NAS
- **Short-term**: Faltering growth, severe dehydration from diarrhea, skin excoriation (from excessive rubbing/hyperactivity).
- **Long-term**: Neurodevelopmental delay, ADHD, behavioral/attentive difficulties (though hard to distinguish organic drug effect from chaotic socioeconomic/environmental factors).

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Viva Question: Why is NAS often exceptionally difficult to manage in modern contexts? Answer: Due to polydrug use. Opiate replacement (morphine) only covers opiate withdrawal. Symptoms driven by simultaneous meth/benzo use will persist.
* Trap: Automatically recommending breastfeeding for all mothers with opiate use. Pearl: While stable methadone maintenance is compatible with breastfeeding, active illicit drug use or HIV positivity are strict contraindications.
* Pitfall: Attributing jitteriness solely to NAS without checking bloods. Always rule out hypoglycaemia and hypocalcaemia with a bedside CBG before assuming NAS is the sole cause.
* Viva Question: What is the significance of the modified Finnegan score? Answer: It dictates the initiation and weaning of pharmacological treatment; treatment is typically started if three consecutive scores are >8 or two are >12.
