# Malignant Bone Tumours (Osteosarcoma & Ewing Sarcoma) (惡性骨腫瘤 - 骨肉瘤及尤文氏肉瘤)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: The two major primary malignant bone tumors of childhood and adolescence.
- **Local Epidemiology**: A classic cause of severe, chronic limb pain in teenagers (during growth spurts).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Osteosarcoma**: Originates from primitive bone-forming mesenchymal cells that produce malignant osteoid. Tends to occur at the **metaphysis** (the growing ends) of long bones (Distal femur, proximal tibia, proximal humerus). Highly associated with prior radiation and Retinoblastoma (RB1 mutation).
- **Ewing Sarcoma**: Originates from neuroectodermal cells. Tends to occur in the **diaphysis** (shaft) of long bones, or flat bones (pelvis, ribs). Driven strictly by the **t(11;22)** chromosome translocation.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- A 14-year-old boy complaining of worsening dull, aching pain in his left knee for 2 months that now wakes him up at night. He has a subtle, firm, tender swelling just above the knee cap.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Pain**: Deep, localized bone pain. **Crucially, the pain is NOT relieved by rest and wakes them from sleep.**
- **Swelling**: A palpable, firm mass appearing weeks to months after the pain begins.
- **Fracture**: May present suddenly as a pathological fracture through weakened tumor bone.
- **Systemic Symptoms**: Fever and weight loss are somewhat common in Ewing Sarcoma (mimicking osteomyelitis), but rare in Osteosarcoma.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from "Growing Pains": Growing pains are bilateral, intermittent, usually in calves/thighs (not joints), and completely vanish by morning. Bone tumors are firmly unilateral, progressively worsening, and localized to a specific boney point.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Osteomyelitis (Bacterial bone infection)
- Osgood-Schlatter Disease (traction apophysitis of the tibial tuberosity - pain precisely strictly on the bump, mechanically induced).
- Trauma / Stress Fracture

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bloods**: Alkaline Phosphatase (ALP) or Lactate Dehydrogenase (LDH) may be markedly elevated.
- **X-Ray (Classic Findings)**:
  - **Osteosarcoma**: Metaphyseal destruction. **"Sunburst" pattern** (spicules of malignant bone shooting outward into soft tissue) and **Codman Triangle** (the tumor lifts the periosteum creating a triangular wedge of reactive bone).
  - **Ewing Sarcoma**: Diaphyseal destruction. **"Onion-skin"** (lamellated) periosteal reaction as the bone tries to furiously lay down new layers to contain the tumor.
- **Staging**: CT Thorax (Because BOTH tumors metastasize almost strictly to the lungs early on).
- **Biopsy**: Proves the diagnosis.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Osteosarcoma**: Neo-adjuvant chemotherapy $\rightarrow$ Complete surgical resection (often limb-salvage surgery removing the bone and replacing it with metal endoprosthesis, though amputation is sometimes required) $\rightarrow$ Adjuvant chemotherapy. *Important: Osteosarcoma is completely resistant to radiation.*
- **Ewing Sarcoma**: Highly sensitive to BOTH chemotherapy and radiation. Surgery is done if feasible without massive morbidity, supplemented with radiation.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Immense psychological impact on a teenager facing potential amputation and aggressive chemotherapy. Requires intense orthopedic rehab.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- N/A.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Lung metastases are the primary cause of death. Survival drops significantly if metastases are present at diagnosis.
- Endoprosthesis complications (loosening, severe joint infections).

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: X-raying the knee of a teenager reporting "knee pain" without examining the hip or the lower femur. Often, distal femur osteosarcomas present as referred "knee joint" pain.
- **Viva Q**: "A bone biopsy of a diaphyseal tumor shows sheets of small round blue cells and a t(11;22) translocation. Which X-ray finding likely matches this?" Answer: An "onion-skin" periosteal reaction (Ewing Sarcoma).
