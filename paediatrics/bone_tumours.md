# Malignant Bone Tumours (Osteosarcoma & Ewing Sarcoma) (惡性骨腫瘤 - 骨肉瘤及尤文氏肉瘤)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Childhood Bone Malignancies
* **Osteosarcoma (骨肉瘤)**: Primary malignant bone tumour derived from primitive bone-forming mesenchymal cells producing malignant osteoid.
* **Ewing Sarcoma (尤文氏肉瘤)**: Highly aggressive primary bone tumour derived from primitive neuroectodermal tissue (PNET).

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
* Peak incidence: Adolescence (during pubertal growth spurts).
* **Osteosarcoma**: Most common primary bone malignancy in youth.
* **Ewing Sarcoma**: Second most common; peak age 10-15 yrs.
* Classic presentation in local HK HA clinics: Teenager presenting with chronic, unexplained limb pain initially dismissed as sports injury or "growing pains".

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics

### Pathophysiology & Genetics of Osteosarcoma
* Origin: Primitive mesenchymal cells $\rightarrow$ produce malignant osteoid matrix.
* Location: **Metaphysis (幹骺端)** (growing ends) of long bones.
* Top sites: Distal femur > proximal tibia > proximal humerus (areas of max growth).
* Genetics/Associations: 
  * Strongly associated with **Retinoblastoma (視網膜母細胞瘤)** (RB1 mutation).
  * Li-Fraumeni syndrome (TP53 mutation).
  * Prior therapeutic radiation exposure.

### Pathophysiology & Genetics of Ewing Sarcoma
* Origin: Neuroectodermal cells.
* Location: **Diaphysis (骨幹)** (shaft) of long bones (femur, tibia) or flat bones (pelvis, ribs, scapula).
* Genetics: Strictly driven by **t(11;22)** chromosome translocation $\rightarrow$ EWS-FLI1 fusion protein.

## 3. Clinical Presentation

### Classic Clinical Presentation
* **Demographic**: 14-year-old boy.
* **Chief Complaint**: Progressive, dull, aching pain in knee/thigh for weeks/months.
* **Red Flag**: Pain wakes him up from sleep at night.
* **Progression**: Development of a subtle, firm, tender soft tissue swelling over the affected bone.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Key Symptoms
* **Bone Pain (骨痛)**: Deep, localized, progressively worsening. NOT relieved by rest. Night pain (+ve).
* **Mass/Swelling**: Palpable, firm, rapidly enlarging mass appearing weeks/months post-pain onset.
* **Pathological Fracture (病理性骨折)**: Sudden severe pain/deformity from minor trauma through weakened tumour bone.
* **Systemic Symptoms**: Fever, weight loss, fatigue (More common in Ewing Sarcoma; mimics osteomyelitis. Rare in Osteosarcoma).

### Key Signs on Physical Examination
* Localized boney tenderness upon palpation.
* Firm, fixed, ill-defined soft tissue mass attached to underlying bone.
* Overlying skin warmth or erythema (+ve in Ewing Sarcoma).
* $\downarrow$ Range of motion (ROM) in adjacent joint.
* Regional lymphadenopathy (rare but possible).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)

### Distinguishing Bone Tumours from "Growing Pains" (生長痛)
* **Growing Pains**: 
  * Bilateral & intermittent.
  * Involves calves/thigh muscles (not joints/bones).
  * Pain entirely vanishes by morning.
  * Normal physical exam.
* **Bone Tumours**: 
  * Strictly unilateral.
  * Progressively worsening & continuous.
  * Localized strictly to a boney point.
  * Palpable mass & abnormal X-ray.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis

### Differentials for Paediatric Bone Pain & Swelling
| Differential Diagnosis | Key Distinguishing Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Osteomyelitis (骨髓炎)** | Acute onset, high fever, exquisitely tender, $\uparrow\uparrow$ CRP/ESR. Difficult to distinguish from Ewing Sarcoma clinically. |
| **Osgood-Schlatter Disease (奧斯古-施拉特症)** | Traction apophysitis. Pain strictly at tibial tuberosity. Mechanically induced (jumping/sports). Bilateral in 30%. |
| **Trauma / Stress Fracture (應力性骨折)** | Clear history of overexertion/trauma. Rest relieves pain. Distinct fracture line on imaging. |
| **Benign Bone Cysts / Tumours** | e.g., Osteoid Osteoma (severe night pain dramatically relieved by NSAIDs). |

## 7. Tiered Investigations

### Bedside & Laboratory Investigations
* **Blood tests**:
  * **Alkaline Phosphatase (ALP) (鹼性磷酸酶)**: Often $\uparrow\uparrow$ in Osteosarcoma (correlates with osteoblast activity & tumour burden).
  * **Lactate Dehydrogenase (LDH) (乳酸脫氫酶)**: Often $\uparrow\uparrow$ in Ewing Sarcoma (poor prognostic marker).
  * ESR/CRP: Mildly $\uparrow$ (may be markedly $\uparrow$ in Ewing, mimicking infection).

### Gold Standard Imaging Studies
* **Plain X-Ray (X光)** (Initial test of choice):
  * **Osteosarcoma**: Metaphyseal destruction. **"Sunburst" pattern (放射狀)** (spicules of malignant bone shooting into soft tissue) + **Codman Triangle (科德曼氏三角)** (periosteal elevation).
  * **Ewing Sarcoma**: Diaphyseal permeative destruction. **"Onion-skin" (洋蔥皮樣)** lamellated periosteal reaction.
* **MRI of entire affected bone**: Gold standard for local staging, evaluating soft tissue extension, and detecting "skip" metastases within the same bone.

### Staging & Tissue Diagnosis
* **CT Thorax (胸部電腦掃描)**: Mandatory. Both tumours strictly metastasize to **Lungs (肺部)** early.
* **Isotope Bone Scan / PET-CT**: To detect distant boney metastases.
* **Bone Biopsy**: Essential for definitive histological diagnosis. Must be performed by the orthopaedic oncology surgeon planning the final resection to avoid seeding the definitive surgical tract.

## 8. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)

### Evidence-Based Management of Osteosarcoma
* **Radioresistance**: Osteosarcoma is highly resistant to radiotherapy.
* **HA Protocol Workflow**:
  1. **Neo-adjuvant Chemotherapy**: (e.g., Methotrexate, Doxorubicin, Cisplatin) to shrink tumour & treat micrometastases.
  2. **Definitive Surgical Resection**: Gold standard. Limb-salvage surgery (bone resection + metal endoprosthesis) preferred. Amputation reserved for neurovascular involvement or massive soft-tissue spread.
  3. **Adjuvant Chemotherapy**: Based on histological necrosis percentage (>90% necrosis = good response).

### Evidence-Based Management of Ewing Sarcoma
* **Radiosensitivity**: Ewing Sarcoma is highly sensitive to BOTH chemotherapy and radiotherapy.
* **HA Protocol Workflow**:
  1. **Systemic Neo-adjuvant Chemotherapy**: (e.g., VDC/IE - Vincristine, Doxorubicin, Cyclophosphamide / Ifosfamide, Etoposide).
  2. **Local Control**: Surgery if feasible without massive morbidity. **Radiotherapy (放射治療)** utilized if surgical margins are positive or surgery would cause unacceptable functional loss (e.g., pelvic tumours).
  3. **Adjuvant Chemotherapy**: To complete systemic eradication.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care

### MDT Involvement & Psychosocial Rehabilitation
* **Orthopaedic Oncology / Paediatric Oncology**: Core medical/surgical management.
* **Medical Social Worker (MSW) (醫務社工)**: Financial assistance for custom prosthetics or home modifications.
* **Clinical Psychologist (CP) (臨床心理學家)**: Intense psychological support for teenagers facing body image changes, amputation, or aggressive chemo side-effects.
* **Physiotherapist (PT) / Occupational Therapist (OT)**: Intensive post-op orthopaedic rehab and gait training.

## 10. Complications & Prognosis

### Common Complications
* **Metastatic Disease**: Lung metastases (肺轉移) are the primary cause of death.
* **Surgical/Endoprosthetic Complications**: Aseptic loosening, severe deep joint infections, limb-length discrepancies requiring revision surgeries.
* **Chemotherapy Toxicity**: Doxorubicin (Cardiotoxicity), Cisplatin (Ototoxicity/Nephrotoxicity), Secondary malignancies.

### Prognosis
* Survival drops significantly if lung or bone metastases are present at diagnosis.
* Tumour necrosis rate post-neo-adjuvant chemo is the strongest predictor of overall survival in Osteosarcoma.

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Trap: X-raying the knee of a teenager reporting "knee pain" without examining the hip or the entire femur. Pearl: Distal femur osteosarcomas often present as referred "knee joint" pain; always examine the joint above and below.
* Viva Question: A bone biopsy of a diaphyseal tumour shows sheets of small round blue cells and a t(11;22) translocation. Which X-ray finding likely matches this? Answer: An "onion-skin" periosteal reaction (Ewing Sarcoma).
* Pitfall: Rushing to biopsy a suspected malignant bone tumour in a peripheral hospital. The biopsy tract must be excised during definitive surgery; inappropriate biopsy placement by a non-specialist can make limb-salvage impossible, forcing amputation.
* Viva Question: What is the significance of the Codman triangle on plain X-ray? Answer: It indicates an aggressive boney process (like Osteosarcoma) lifting the periosteum faster than it can lay down new bone, creating a triangular wedge of reactive bone.
