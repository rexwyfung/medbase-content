# Small Bowel Obstruction (e.g., Jejunal/Ileal Atresia) (小腸阻塞)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Congenital complete or partial occlusion of the small bowel lumen.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Duodenal atresia is common and associated with Down syndrome. Jejunoileal atresia presents equally frequently but has different etiology.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Duodenal atresia**: Failure of recanalization of the embryonic bowel. High association with Trisomy 21.
- **Jejunoileal atresia**: Caused by an in-utero vascular accident (e.g., localized ischemia/volvulus/intussusception during embryonic life). Not typically associated with chromosomal anomalies.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Bilious (green) vomiting in the first 24-48 hours of life. 
- **Rule of thumb**: Bilious vomiting in a newborn is a SURGICAL EMERGENCY until proven otherwise.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- Abdominal distension (more pronounced in more distal obstructions like ileal atresia).
- Failure to pass meconium within 24 hours.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Level of obstruction dictates signs: 
  - Duodenal: Very early vomiting, minimal distension.
  - Ileal: Later vomiting, massive distension.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Malrotation with midgut volvulus (Must-Not-Miss)
- Meconium ileus (Cystic Fibrosis)
- Hirschsprung's disease

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Imaging (Gold Standard)**: AXR.
  - Duodenal: "Double bubble" sign (gas in stomach and proximal duodenum, airless distally).
  - Jejunoileal: Few dilated loops of bowel with air-fluid levels.
- **Special**: Contrast enema (to rule out Hirschsprung's, or to identify microcolon which strongly suggests complete unused distal bowel, typical in ileal atresia or meconium ileus).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute Resuscitation**: NBM, pass a large-bore NG tube for free drainage (stops vomiting/aspiration risk), IV fluid resuscitation and electrolyte correction (they lose chloride and potassium).
- **Definitive Treatment**: Laparotomy for resection of the atretic segment and primary anastomosis (or temporary stoma if bowel is too dilated/unhealthy).

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Enteral feeding adjustments are heavily supported by Pediatric Dietitians. Prolonged parenteral nutrition requires long-term central venous catheter management training for parents.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- No specific NAI associations, but vigilant post-op wound care requires reliable parental capability.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Short bowel syndrome (if large segments resected), dependence on TPN (liver failure risk), adhesive bowel obstruction in the future.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Assuming bilious vomiting is "just feeding intolerance". Never discharge a bilious vomiting neonate without a pediatric surgical review and contrast study to exclude volvulus.
- **Viva Q**: "Why does jejunal atresia happen vs duodenal atresia?" Answer: Jejunal is due to a late mesenteric vascular accident in-utero; Duodenal is an early embryological failure of vacuolization/recanalization.
