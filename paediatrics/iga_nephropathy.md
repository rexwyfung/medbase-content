
# IgA Nephropathy (IgA 腎病)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: The most common primary glomerulonephritis worldwide. Characterised by the predominant deposition of IgA immune complexes in the glomerular mesangium.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Highly prevalent in East Asia, including Hong Kong. A major cause of chronic kidney disease and end-stage renal failure (ESRF) in young adults in HK.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Mechanism**: A "multi-hit" hypothesis.
  1. Production of poorly O-glycosylated IgA1 (Galactose-deficient IgA1).
  2. Formation of autoantibodies (IgG) against this abnormal IgA1.
  3. Formation of circulating immune complexes.
  4. Deposition of these complexes in the renal mesangium, triggering inflammation, mesangial proliferation, and eventual glomerulosclerosis.
- Often triggered by mucosal infections (URTI or GI tract), as mucosal surfaces are the primary sites of IgA production.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- See below.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Classic Presentation**: **Synpharyngitic Macroscopic Haematuria**. Painless, visible (cola-coloured or red) haematuria occurring *concurrently* (within 1-2 days) with an upper respiratory tract infection.
- **Alternative Presentation**: Asymptomatic microscopic haematuria with or without mild proteinuria, often picked up incidentally on routine school health screening (Student Health Service in HK).
- **Rare Presentation**: Nephrotic syndrome or rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis (RPGN).
## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Post-Streptococcal Glomerulonephritis (APSGN)**: Macroscopic haematuria occurs 1-2 *weeks* AFTER a throat infection (post-infectious), whereas IgA nephropathy haematuria occurs *during* the infection (synpharyngitic). APSGN usually has low C3 complement levels; IgA nephropathy has normal complement levels.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Most Likely**:
  - IgA Nephropathy (especially in an Asian patient with synpharyngitic haematuria).
- **Must-Not-Miss**:
  - **Alport Syndrome**: X-linked progressive nephritis with sensorineural hearing loss and eye abnormalities.
  - **Lupus Nephritis**: Can mimic any glomerulonephritis. Check ANA/Anti-dsDNA.
  - **Rapidly Progressive Glomerulonephritis (RPGN)**: Crescentic IgA nephropathy causing rapid loss of renal function.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**:
  - **Urinalysis**: Haematuria (dysmorphic red cells, red cell casts indicate glomerular origin), variable proteinuria.
  - **Blood Pressure**: Hypertension is a poor prognostic sign.
- **Gold Standard**:
  - **Bloods**: U&E, Creatinine (eGFR), Serum IgA (elevated in ~50%, but not diagnostic), **Normal C3 and C4** (crucial to differentiate from APSGN or Lupus).
  - **Renal Biopsy**: The definitive diagnostic test.
  - *Light Microscopy*: Mesangial proliferation.
  - *Immunofluorescence*: **Granular deposition of IgA** (and often C3) in the mesangium.
  - *Oxford Classification (MEST-C score)*: Used by pathologists to grade severity and predict prognosis (Mesangial hypercellularity, Endocapillary proliferation, Segmental glomerulosclerosis, Tubular atrophy/interstitial fibrosis, Crescents).
- **Monitoring**:
  - Urine Protein-to-Creatinine Ratio (UPCR) to quantify proteinuria.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**:
  - Not usually applicable unless presenting with hypertensive emergency or RPGN.
- **Chronic/Definitive**:
  - **Supportive / Renoprotective**:
    - **ACE Inhibitors (Enalapril) or ARBs (Losartan)**: First-line therapy for ANY patient with proteinuria (>0.5g/day) or hypertension. Reduces intraglomerular pressure and proteinuria, slowing progression.
    - Strict blood pressure control.
    - **Immunosuppression**:
    - **Corticosteroids (Prednisolone)**: Considered for patients with persistent significant proteinuria despite maximal ACEi/ARB therapy, provided eGFR is >50 ml/min. (The STOP-IgAN and TESTING trials guide this, balancing benefit vs. infection risk).
    - *Other agents*: Mycophenolate Mofetil (MMF) or Cyclophosphamide may be used in severe/crescentic cases.
  - **Newer Therapies**: SGLT2 inhibitors (e.g., Dapagliflozin) are increasingly used for renoprotection in CKD, including IgA nephropathy.
## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Nephrology follow-up is lifelong.
## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Counsel that the disease is chronic. Macroscopic haematuria may recur with future colds, which is frightening but usually self-limiting.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Rule of Thirds**: ~1/3 have benign non-progressive disease, ~1/3 have slow progression, ~1/3 progress to End-Stage Renal Failure (ESRF) within 10-20 years.
- **Poor Prognostic Factors**: Hypertension, severe proteinuria, impaired GFR at presentation, and extensive fibrosis/crescents on biopsy.
- High recurrence rate in renal transplants.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Assuming low C3 in a patient with IgA nephropathy. *Pearl*: Complement levels (C3, C4) are typically NORMAL in IgA nephropathy. Low C3 suggests APSGN, Lupus, or C3 Glomerulopathy.
- **Viva Question**: "What is the classic timing of macroscopic haematuria in IgA nephropathy compared to post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis?" *Answer*: IgA nephropathy is 'synpharyngitic' (occurs at the same time as the URTI). APSGN is 'post-infectious' (occurs 1-2 weeks after the URTI).
