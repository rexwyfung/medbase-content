# Achondroplasia & Thanatophoric Dysplasia (軟骨發育不全與致死性骨發育不全)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Achondroplasia is the most common form of short-limbed (rhizomelic) dwarfism. Thanatophoric Dysplasia is a related but lethal, severe skeletal dysplasia.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Achondroplasia occurs in ~1 in 20,000-30,000 births globally including HK.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Genetics**: Both are caused by mutations in the **FGFR3** (Fibroblast Growth Factor Receptor 3) gene on chromosome 4.
- **Pathophysiology**: FGFR3 is a negative regulator of bone growth. The mutation causes an *overactive* receptor, prematurely severely limiting endochondral ossification (the process by which long bones grow). Membranous ossification (e.g. skull vault) is unaffected, leading to a large head relative to the body.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Unlikely to be missed standardly; shortened long bones identified by ultrasound in the 3rd trimester.
- Presents with extreme disproportionate short stature. Postnatal growth tracking on specialized Achondroplasia charts is mandatory.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Achondroplasia**: Rhizomelic (proximal) shortening of arms/legs, highly prominent forehead (frontal bossing), midface hypoplasia, "trident" hands.
- Normal intelligence.
- **Thanatophoric Dysplasia**: Extreme limb shortening, very narrow bell-shaped thorax, enormous head ("cloverleaf skull"). Always fatal shortly after birth due to pulmonary hypoplasia.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinguish closely from Hypochondroplasia (milder FGFR3 mutation, often not diagnosed until later childhood).

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Hypochondroplasia
- Osteogenesis Imperfecta
- Pseudoachondroplasia

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Imaging**: Skeletal survey. Shows narrowing of the interpedicular distance of the lower spine, squared iliac wings, and short thick tubular bones.
- **Special**: Cranial ultrasound / MRI Brain/Spine to screen for foramen magnum stenosis. Genetic confirmation (FGFR3 targeted test).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Neurological**: Urgent neurosurgical decompression if symptoms of foramen magnum compression (sleep apnoea, hyperreflexia, sudden death).
- **Orthopaedic**: Management of progressive thoracolumbar kyphosis and severe genu varum (bow legs) which may require osteotomies.
- **ENT**: Frequent middle ear infections and upper airway obstruction require tympanostomy tubes or adenotonsillectomy.
- **New Medical Therapies**: *Vosoritide* (CNP analog) is an emerging daily injection that bypasses the FGFR3 block, increasing height velocity in growing children.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Strong psychological support necessary for integration into regular schools due to physical differences. Environmental modifications (step stools, lower desks) needed.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Foramen magnum stenosis is the silent killer. Infants should NEVER be put in unsupportive carriers ("umbrella strollers") that allow the heavy head to quickly jerk backward, risking fatal cervical cord crushing.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Obstructive sleep apnoea, spinal stenosis, obesity (very high risk as height doesn't increase but caloric intake matches standard peers).
- Lifespan is generally normal aside from the early neurological risks.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Judging a larger appearing head as hydrocephalus without verification. Plagiocephaly and macrocephaly are normal here, but true hydrocephalus requires ultrasound/MRI.
- **Viva Q**: "Why is the maternal/paternal age relevant?" Answer: Around 80% are spontaneous new mutations, universally strongly correlated with advanced **paternal** age.
