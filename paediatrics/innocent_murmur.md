

# Innocent Murmur (無害性心雜音)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A heart murmur in the absence of any cardiac structural or physiological abnormality. Also known as functional or physiological murmurs.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Extremely common in Hong Kong paediatric practice. Over 50% of normal children will have an innocent murmur at some point, commonly picked up during Student Health Service (DH) screenings or incidental GP visits for upper respiratory tract infections (URTIs).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Generated by normal blood flow through normal heart structures.
- Exacerbated by high-output states: fever (e.g., during viral illness), anaemia, thyrotoxicosis, or anxiety.
- No underlying genetic or structural defect.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Usually asymptomatic. Discovered incidentally.
- Normal growth and development.
- Normal exercise tolerance.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **The "7 S's" of Innocent Murmurs**:
  1. **S**oft (Grade 1-2/6, never thrill)
  2. **S**hort (not holosystolic)
  3. **S**ystolic (except venous hum)
  4. **S**weet (not harsh, often vibratory/musical)
  5. **S**mall (localised, no radiation)
  6. **S**ingle (no clicks, normal split S2)
  7. **S**ensitive (changes with posture/respiration)
- **Common Types**:
  - **Still's Murmur**: Vibratory/musical, LLSB, loudest supine.
  - **Pulmonary Flow Murmur**: Blowing, LUSB.
  - **Venous Hum**: Continuous, infraclavicular, disappears when supine or turning neck.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Pathological Red Flags**: Diastolic murmurs, pansystolic murmurs, Grade ≥3/6, presence of a thrill, radiation, abnormal S2 (e.g., fixed split in ASD), clicks, cyanosis, failure to thrive.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Atrial Septal Defect (ASD) (pulmonary flow murmur mimic).
- Ventricular Septal Defect (VSD) (small VSD can be soft but is pansystolic).
- Pulmonary Stenosis (often has an ejection click).
- Patent Ductus Arteriosus (PDA) (continuous murmur mimic for venous hum).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
**Bedside**: Clinical examination is usually sufficient.
- **Imaging**: Echocardiogram is the gold standard but ONLY indicated if there are "red flag" features or parental anxiety cannot be reassured. Routine Echo for classic innocent murmurs is discouraged in HA to avoid over-medicalisation and resource wastage.
- **ECG/CXR**: Not routinely indicated unless pathology is suspected.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
**Acute**: None required.
- **Chronic**: Reassurance. No activity restrictions. No endocarditis prophylaxis.
- **HA Protocols**: Discharge from paediatric outpatient clinic if clinically confident.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **MCHC**: Reassurance to parents during routine developmental checks.
- Address parental anxiety ("cardiac neurosis") which is common in HK parents when told their child has a "heart problem".

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Not directly applicable, but ensure the child is receiving regular well-child care.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Excellent prognosis. Most resolve by adolescence.
- No long-term complications.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Ordering an Echo for a classic Still's murmur in an OSCE. *Pearl*: Confidently diagnose it clinically and explain to the "parent" why an Echo is unnecessary.
- **Viva**: "What makes a venous hum disappear?" *Answer*: Lying supine, turning the head, or gentle pressure over the jugular vein.
