# Oesophageal Atresia & Tracheo-Oesophageal Fistula (食道閉鎖及氣管食道瘻管)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of OA & TOF
* Congenital interruption of oesophagus **(OA)**.
* Usually accompanied by fistulous tract connecting to trachea **(TOF)**.

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
* Incidence: ~1 in 3000-4000 live births globally & locally.
* Handled exclusively by tertiary **Hospital Authority (HA)** paediatric surgical centres (e.g., HKCH, QEH, QMH).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics

### Embryological Pathophysiology
* Defective septation of primitive foregut into respiratory (anterior) & GI (posterior) tracts.
* Occurs during 4th-5th week of gestation.

### Anatomical Variants of OA/TOF
| Type (Gross Classification) | Anatomical Description | Frequency | Radiographic Hallmark |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Type C (Most Common)** | Proximal blind pouch + Distal TOF | >85% | Gas in stomach/bowel |
| **Type A (Pure OA)** | Proximal & Distal blind pouches (No TOF) | ~8% | Gasless abdomen |
| **Type E (H-type)** | Intact oesophagus + TOF (H-fistula) | ~4% | Recurrent aspiration pneumonias |
| **Type B & D** | Rare variants with proximal TOF ± distal TOF | <2% | Variable |

### Genetic and Syndromic Associations
* **VACTERL Association** (~50% of cases):
  * **V**ertebral anomalies
  * **A**norectal malformations (Imperforate anus)
  * **C**ardiac defects (VSD, Tetralogy of Fallot)
  * **T**racheo-**E**sophageal fistula
  * **R**enal anomalies (Agenesis, horseshoe kidney)
  * **L**imb defects (Radial hypoplasia)
* Chromosomal anomalies: **Trisomy 18 (Edwards)**, **Trisomy 21 (Down)**.

## 3. Clinical Presentation

### Antenatal Presentation
* **Polyhydramnios (羊水過多)**: Fetus cannot swallow & absorb amniotic fluid.
* Absent or strictly small stomach bubble on 2nd-trimester anomaly scan.

### Postnatal Presentation
* Onset immediately post-delivery or during 1st feed.
* **Cyanosis (發紺)**, choking, and coughing triggered by feeding.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Primary Respiratory Signs
* Continuous, excessive salivation ("frothing/bubbling" at the mouth).
* **Respiratory distress (呼吸窘迫)**.
* Apnoea episodes.

### Primary Gastrointestinal Signs
* Complete inability to swallow saliva or milk.
* Abdominal distension (Air crosses from trachea → distal fistula → stomach).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)

### Distinguishing OA/TOF from Duodenal Atresia
* **OA/TOF**: Immediate frothing, choking on 1st feed. NO bile.
* **Duodenal Atresia**: Presents later. Hallmark is **Bile-stained vomiting (膽汁性嘔吐)**.

### Distinguishing OA/TOF from Choanal Atresia
* **OA/TOF**: Unaffected by crying. Triggered by feeding.
* **Bilateral Choanal Atresia**: Cyanosis at rest, paradoxically relieved by crying (mouth breathing).

## 6. Differential Diagnosis

### Differential Diagnoses for Neonatal Respiratory Distress with Feeding Difficulties
| Differential Diagnosis | Key Differentiating Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS)** | Preterm infant, surfactant deficiency, diffuse ground-glass on CXR. |
| **Meconium Aspiration Syndrome (MAS)** | Term/post-term, meconium-stained liquor, patchy infiltrates on CXR. |
| **High Bowel Obstruction** | Prominent vomiting (often bilious), abdominal distension, delayed presentation. |
| **Transient Tachypnoea of Newborn (TTN)** | C-section delivery, fluid in fissures on CXR, resolves rapidly. |

## 7. Tiered Investigations

### Bedside Investigations
* Attempt passage of stiff, wide-bore **(10Fr) nasogastric (NG) tube**.
* +ve finding: Tube arrests rigidly at ~9-11 cm from lips.

### Imaging Investigations
* **Erect Chest & Abdominal X-Ray (CXR/AXR)**:
  * NG tube seen curled in proximal blind oesophageal pouch.
  * **Gas in bowel/stomach** → confirms presence of distal TOF.
  * **Gasless abdomen** → indicates Pure OA (Type A).
  * Check for vertebral/rib anomalies (VACTERL).

### Special Investigations (Pre-operative Mandatory Screen)
* **Echocardiogram (超聲心動圖)**: 
  1. Exclude major congenital heart defects.
  2. Map aortic arch laterality (critical to determine surgical approach: right thoracotomy if left arch).
* **Renal Ultrasound**: Screen for renal agenesis/dysplasia.

## 8. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)

### Acute Pre-operative Resuscitation
* **Nil By Mouth (NBM) (禁食)**.
* Strict upright/45° positioning → ↓ gastric acid reflux into lungs via distal TOF.
* Continuous low-suction of proximal pouch via **Replogle tube** → prevents saliva aspiration.
* IV fluids & IV broad-spectrum antibiotics (prevent/treat chemical pneumonitis).

### Surgical Management in HA Settings
* **Primary Extrapleural Repair**: Right thoracotomy → ligation of fistula + primary oesophageal anastomosis.
* **Long-gap OA Management**: 
  * Gap too wide for immediate joining.
  * Staged repair: Initial gastrostomy (for feeding) + continuous upper pouch suction → delayed primary anastomosis (after oesophageal growth).

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care

### Multidisciplinary Team (MDT) Rehabilitation
* **Paediatric Surgeon & Neonatologist**: Acute management.
* **Speech Therapist (ST) (言語治療師)**: Vital for post-op swallowing rehab & feeding aversion management.
* **Dietitian**: Ensure adequate caloric intake for catch-up growth.

### Long-term Community Care in HK
* Routine surveillance at **Maternal & Child Health Centre (MCHC) (母嬰健康院)**.
* Strict monitoring of growth charts for **Failure to Thrive (FTT)**.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags

### Safeguarding in Complex Congenital Anomalies
* High caregiver burden → ↑ risk of neglect or non-accidental injury (NAI).
* Assess coping mechanisms; early referral to **Medical Social Worker (MSW) (醫務社工)**.
* Parental hyper-vigilance required for sudden choking/cyanotic episodes at home.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis

### Early Post-operative Complications
* Anastomotic leak (presents with pneumothorax/pleural effusion).
* Oesophageal stricture (requires serial balloon dilatations).
* Recurrent fistula.

### Late / Chronic Complications
* **Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) (胃食道反流病)**: Almost universal due to altered motility. Often requires fundoplication.
* **Tracheomalacia (氣管軟化)**: Weak tracheal rings. Causes classic **"TOF cough"** (harsh, brassy, barking cough) and recurrent wheeze.

### Prognosis Indicators
* Overall survival >90%.
* Mortality primarily dictated by extreme prematurity (<1500g) and severe concurrent cardiac anomalies (Spitz classification).

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: What does the acronym VACTERL stand for?
  * Answer: Vertebral, Anorectal, Cardiac, Tracheal, Esophageal, Renal, Limb (radial hypoplasia) anomalies.
* Trap: Using oral contrast (barium swallow) studies to diagnose suspected OA.
  * Pearl: Avoid oral contrast due to massive risk of fatal aspiration pneumonitis. Passing a firm 10Fr NG tube and obtaining an X-ray is the safe, gold-standard diagnostic step.
* Pitfall: Assuming a gasless abdomen on X-ray is a normal variant in a frothing neonate.
  * Gasless abdomen in a neonate with a curled NG tube indicates Pure Oesophageal Atresia (Type A) without a distal fistula connecting to the respiratory tract.
* Viva Question: Why is an echocardiogram mandatory before repairing an OA/TOF?
  * Answer: To identify life-threatening congenital heart defects and, crucially, to determine the position of the aortic arch, as surgery is typically performed via a thoracotomy on the side opposite the arch.
