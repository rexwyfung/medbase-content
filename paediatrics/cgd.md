# Chronic Granulomatous Disease (CGD) (慢性肉芽腫病)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A primary immunodeficiency affecting phagocytes (neutrophils and macrophages), rendering them entirely incapable of killing certain types of ingested bacteria and fungi.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Usually X-linked recessive (affecting males), though autosomal recessive forms exist.
- Mutation in the **NADPH oxidase** enzyme complex.
- **Normal Physiology**: A neutrophil eats a bacteria, putting it in a phagosome. NADPH oxidase then pumps massive amounts of superoxide radicals (H2O2 / bleach) into the phagosome, causing a lethal "respiratory burst."
- **Pathology**: The neutrophil eats the bacteria fine, but the "bleach pump" is broken. The bacteria just lives happily inside the macrophage. The immune system tries to wall off these unkillable bacteria by building walls of macrophages around them (Granulomas).
- Catalase-negative bacteria generate their own small amounts of peroxide which the neutrophil steals to kill them. But **Catalase-Positive** organisms destroy their own peroxide, meaning the CGD neutrophil has absolutely zero weapons against them.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- A 3-year-old boy presenting with his third massive liver abscess and a history of chronic osteomyelitis.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Infections**: Deep, life-threatening abscesses in the liver, spleen, lungs, and lymph nodes.
- **Organisms**: Strict susceptibility to Catalase-Positive organisms (SPACE):
  - **S**taphylococcus aureus
  - **P**seudomonas
  - **A**spergillus species (Fungus - leading cause of mortality)
  - **C**andida / Nocardia / Corynebacterium
  - **E**nterobacteriaceae (Serratia, Burkholderia, Klebsiella)
- **Granulomas**: Obstruction of the GI tract or urinary tract purely due to huge granuloma masses.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from typical abscesses: Healthy kids get superficial skin abscesses. Deep visceral liver or spleen abscesses (especially with weird bugs like Serratia) always mandate testing for CGD.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Hyper-IgE Syndrome (also gets Staph abscesses, but usually "cold" abscesses lacking inflammation).
- Leukocyte Adhesion Deficiency (infections have no pus/granulomas).
- Cystic Fibrosis (both get Pseudomonas/Aspergillus lungs, but CF has high sweat chloride and digestive issues).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **NBT Test (Nitroblue Tetrazolium)**: Older classic test. Normal neutrophils turn the yellow dye blue (because they make superoxide). CGD neutrophils leave the dye yellow.
- **Gold Standard**: **Dihydrorhodamine 123 (DHR) flow cytometry assay**. Normal neutrophils fluoresce brightly; CGD neutrophils do not fluoresce at all.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Prophylaxis is mandatory and lifelong**:
  - Antibacterial: Daily Trimethoprim-Sulfamethoxazole (Co-trimoxazole).
  - Antifungal: Daily Itraconazole.
- **Acute Infections**: Require incredibly prolonged (months) of IV antibiotics/antifungals, often coupled with surgical drainage of abscesses.
- **Immunomodulation**: Interferon-gamma (IFN-$\gamma$) injections (helps super-activate macrophages, circumventing some of the defect).
- **Curative**: Hematopoietic Stem Cell Transplant (HSCT).

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Absolute restriction from gardening, mulching, or playing in damp hay/leaves. These activities release massive clouds of Aspergillus spores, which are rapidly fatal if inhaled by a CGD patient.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- N/A.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- High mortality from invasive pulmonary Aspergillosis or Burkholderia cepacia complex.
- Inflammatory bowel disease-like granulomatous colitis.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Forgetting that CGD patients have completely normal levels of White Blood Cells, B-cells, T-cells, and immunoglobulins. All standard immune workups look perfect. You must request the specific functional test (DHR).
- **Viva Q**: "Why do patients with CGD get sick from Staphylococcus but easily fight off Streptococcus?" Answer: Streptococcus is catalase-negative. It produces small amounts of its own hydrogen peroxide waste. Since the CGD neutrophil can't make its own peroxide, it just uses the Strep's own peroxide waste to kill it. Staphylococcus is catalase-positive, meaning it destroys its own peroxide waste, leaving the CGD neutrophil completely defenceless.
