# Coeliac Disease (乳糜瀉)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: An autoimmune, systemic enteropathy triggered by the ingestion of gluten in genetically susceptible individuals.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Historically considered rare in Asian populations, but incidence is rising in Hong Kong, partly due to increased awareness, better diagnostic tests, and the westernization of diets (increased wheat consumption).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **The Trigger**: Gluten, a protein complex found in **wheat, rye, and barley**. (Oats are generally safe but are often cross-contaminated).
- **Pathogenesis**: 
  - Incompletely digested gluten peptides (gliadin) cross the intestinal epithelium.
  - Tissue Transglutaminase (tTG) modifies gliadin, making it highly immunogenic.
  - In genetically susceptible individuals, this triggers a T-cell mediated autoimmune response against the intestinal mucosa.
  - This chronic inflammation leads to **villous atrophy, crypt hyperplasia, and intraepithelial lymphocytosis** in the small intestine (primarily the duodenum).
- **Consequence**: The loss of absorptive surface area (villi) leads to profound malabsorption of macro- and micronutrients (iron, calcium, folate, fat).
- **Genetics**: Almost 100% of patients carry the **HLA-DQ2 or HLA-DQ8** genes. (However, 30% of the general population also carries these genes, so their presence is necessary but not sufficient for the disease).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- **Classic Presentation (Infants/Toddlers)**: Presents between 6 and 24 months of age, shortly after the introduction of gluten-containing cereals into the diet (weaning).
- **Atypical/Silent Presentation (Older Children/Adults)**: May present with extra-intestinal symptoms (e.g., isolated iron deficiency anaemia, short stature, delayed puberty) or be asymptomatic and picked up on screening.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Gastrointestinal**: 
  - Chronic diarrhoea (often pale, bulky, foul-smelling, and difficult to flush - steatorrhoea).
  - Abdominal pain, bloating, excessive flatus.
  - Vomiting.
- **Malabsorption/Systemic**:
  - **Faltering Growth (Failure to Thrive)**: A classic sign. Weight drops off centiles, followed by height.
  - Muscle wasting (especially prominent buttocks).
  - Lethargy, irritability.
  - **Anaemia**: Usually Iron Deficiency (microcytic), but can be Folate/B12 deficiency (macrocytic).
- **Dermatological**: **Dermatitis Herpetiformis** (intensely itchy, blistering rash on extensor surfaces - knees, elbows, buttocks). Pathognomonic for Coeliac disease but rare in young children.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Cow's Milk Protein Allergy (CMPA)**: Presents earlier (often in the first few months of life) when exposed to formula, whereas Coeliac disease cannot present until gluten is introduced (usually >6 months). CMPA often has prominent skin (eczema) or respiratory symptoms.
- **Toddler's Diarrhoea (Functional Diarrhoea)**: The child has frequent loose stools (often containing undigested food like peas/carrots) but is **thriving and growing perfectly normally** along their centiles. Coeliac causes faltering growth.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Cow's Milk Protein Allergy (CMPA).
- Post-gastroenteritis syndrome (transient lactose intolerance).
- Inflammatory Bowel Disease (Crohn's disease).
- Cystic Fibrosis (causes malabsorption and steatorrhoea).
- Giardiasis (parasitic infection causing chronic diarrhoea).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **CRITICAL RULE**: All testing MUST be done while the child is on a **gluten-containing diet**. If they stop eating gluten before testing, the antibodies disappear and the gut heals, leading to a false negative.
- **First-line Serology**:
  - **Total IgA**: Must be checked first, as selective IgA deficiency is common in Coeliac patients.
  - **Anti-Tissue Transglutaminase (Anti-tTG) IgA**: The most sensitive and specific initial screening test.
  - *(If Total IgA is low, check Anti-tTG IgG or Anti-Deamidated Gliadin Peptide (DGP) IgG).*
- **Endoscopy and Duodenal Biopsy (The Gold Standard)**:
  - Required to confirm the diagnosis in most cases.
  - *Histology shows*: Villous atrophy, crypt hyperplasia, and increased intraepithelial lymphocytes (Marsh classification).
- **ESPGHAN Guidelines (Biopsy-avoidance)**: In specific circumstances, a biopsy can be avoided if the child has classic symptoms, Anti-tTG IgA is >10 times the upper limit of normal, and a second blood sample confirms positive Endomysial Antibodies (EMA IgA).
- **Other Bloods**: FBC (anaemia), Iron studies, Folate, B12, Calcium, Vitamin D, LFTs.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **The ONLY treatment is a strict, lifelong Gluten-Free Diet (GFD)**.
- This means absolute avoidance of all wheat, rye, and barley.
- Even trace amounts (cross-contamination from toasters, shared fryers) can trigger mucosal damage, even if the child is asymptomatic.
- **Nutritional Support**: Correct deficiencies (Iron, Calcium, Vitamin D supplements) initially.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Dietitian**: Absolutely essential. A GFD is incredibly difficult to maintain, expensive, and socially isolating. The dietitian educates the family on reading labels, finding hidden gluten (e.g., in soy sauce, sausages, play-dough), and ensuring a balanced diet.
- **Coeliac Support Groups**: Crucial for psychosocial support and practical tips.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- N/A for this condition.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Complications of untreated disease**: Osteoporosis, severe short stature, delayed puberty, infertility, and a significantly increased risk of **Enteropathy-associated T-cell Lymphoma (EATL)** and small bowel adenocarcinoma in adulthood.
- **Associated Autoimmune Conditions**: Patients have a higher risk of developing Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus and Autoimmune Thyroiditis.
- **Prognosis**: Excellent. With a strict GFD, the gut mucosa heals completely, symptoms resolve, and catch-up growth occurs.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "A 14-month-old presents with chronic diarrhoea and faltering growth. You suspect Coeliac disease. The mother tells you she has already started him on a gluten-free diet for the past month and he is improving. What must you advise her before ordering the diagnostic blood tests?" (She must reintroduce gluten into his diet for at least 6 weeks before testing. Testing while on a gluten-free diet will result in false-negative serology and biopsies).
- *Viva*: "What are the classic histological findings on a duodenal biopsy in a patient with Coeliac disease?" (Villous atrophy, crypt hyperplasia, and an increase in intraepithelial lymphocytes).
- *Pitfall*: Ordering Anti-tTG IgA without simultaneously checking Total IgA levels. If the child has selective IgA deficiency (which is more common in Coeliacs), the Anti-tTG IgA will be falsely negative.
