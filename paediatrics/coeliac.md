# Coeliac Disease (乳糜瀉)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition
* Autoimmune, systemic enteropathy (腸病) triggered by **Gluten** (麩質) ingestion in genetically susceptible individuals.
* Characterized by **Gluten**-dependent manifestations, specific autoantibodies, **HLA-DQ2/DQ8** haplotypes, & enteropathy.

### Local Epidemiology
* Prevalence: ~1 in 100 globally. 
* HK Context: Historically rare in Asians, incidence ↑ rapidly in HK due to ↑ awareness, better diagnostics & westernized diet (↑ wheat consumption).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Pathogenesis
* **Trigger**: **Gluten** (麩質) (protein in wheat, rye, barley). Oats generally safe but risk cross-contamination.
* **Mechanism**: Incompletely digested gluten peptides (**gliadin**) cross intestinal epithelium.
* **Tissue Transglutaminase (tTG)** (組織轉谷氨醯胺酶) modifies gliadin → highly immunogenic.
* Triggers T-cell mediated autoimmune response against intestinal mucosa.
* **Consequence**: Chronic inflammation → **Villous atrophy** (絨毛萎縮), **crypt hyperplasia**, & **intraepithelial lymphocytosis** (predominantly duodenum).
* **Result**: Loss of absorptive surface area → profound malabsorption of macro/micronutrients (iron, calcium, folate, fat).

### Genetics
* Almost 100% carry **HLA-DQ2** or **HLA-DQ8** genes.
* Present in 30% of general population (necessary but not sufficient for disease).
* Associated with Down syndrome, Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus, autoimmune thyroid disease.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
### Classical Presentation
* Onset: 8–24 months of age.
* Timing: Shortly after introduction of **Gluten**-containing weaning foods.
* Presentation: Profound malabsorptive syndrome (faltering growth, abdominal distension, buttock wasting, abnormal stools, irritability).
* *Note: "Classical" form now less common (1 in 3000); asymptomatic/mild forms more frequent.*

### Atypical/Silent Presentation
* Onset: Older children/adults.
* Presentation: Extra-intestinal symptoms (e.g., isolated iron deficiency anaemia, short stature, delayed puberty, recurrent aphthous stomatitis, dental enamel defects).
* Often identified via screening of high-risk groups or first-degree relatives.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Gastrointestinal Symptoms
* Chronic diarrhoea (intermittent or chronic).
* **Steatorrhoea** (脂肪瀉): Pale, bulky, foul-smelling, difficult to flush.
* Abdominal pain, distension, bloating.
* Nausea & vomiting.
* Constipation (paradoxical).

### Malabsorption & Systemic Signs
* **Faltering Growth** (生長遲緩): Weight drops off centiles, followed by height.
* Muscle wasting (prominent buttocks).
* Lethargy, weakness, irritability.
* Anaemia: Iron deficiency (microcytic, resistant to oral iron), folate/B12 deficiency (macrocytic).
* Delayed puberty & short stature.

### Dermatological & Neurological Signs
* **Dermatitis Herpetiformis** (疱疹樣皮炎): Intensely itchy, blistering rash on extensor surfaces (knees, elbows, buttocks). Pathognomonic but rare in young children.
* Neuropathy.
* Osteoporosis / pathological fractures.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### CMPA vs Coeliac Disease
| Feature | Cow's Milk Protein Allergy (CMPA) | Coeliac Disease (乳糜瀉) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Onset** | Early (first few months), post-formula exposure | >6 months, post-**Gluten** introduction |
| **Associated Symptoms** | Prominent skin (eczema), respiratory symptoms (wheeze), bloody stools | Profound malabsorption, **Dermatitis Herpetiformis** |
| **Resolution** | Often resolves by 1-3 years of age | Lifelong condition |

### Toddler's Diarrhoea vs Coeliac Disease
| Feature | Toddler's Diarrhoea (Functional) | Coeliac Disease (乳糜瀉) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Growth** | Thriving, normal centiles | **Faltering Growth**, weight/height drop |
| **Stool** | Frequent loose stools with undigested food (peas/carrots) | **Steatorrhoea** (foul, bulky, pale) |
| **Systemic signs**| Well, active | Lethargic, wasted, anaemic |

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
### Common Differentials
| Category | Conditions |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Allergic** | Cow's Milk Protein Allergy (CMPA) |
| **Infective** | Giardiasis, Post-gastroenteritis syndrome (transient lactose intolerance) |
| **Inflammatory** | Inflammatory Bowel Disease (Crohn's disease) |
| **Genetic/Pancreatic**| Cystic Fibrosis (causes malabsorption & **steatorrhoea**) |
| **Functional** | Toddler's diarrhoea |

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
### Crucial Prerequisite
* **CRITICAL RULE**: All testing MUST be done while child is on a **Gluten**-containing diet.
* Gluten withdrawal prior to testing → gut heals, antibodies clear → false -ve.

### First-Line Serology
* **Total IgA**: Check first (selective IgA deficiency common in Coeliac patients).
* **Anti-Tissue Transglutaminase (Anti-tTG) IgA**: Most sensitive/specific initial screen.
* If Total IgA ↓: Check **Anti-tTG IgG** or **Anti-Deamidated Gliadin Peptide (DGP) IgG**.

### Gold Standard Investigation
* **Endoscopy & Duodenal Biopsy**: Required to confirm diagnosis in most cases.
* Histology reveals: **Villous atrophy**, **crypt hyperplasia**, ↑ **intraepithelial lymphocytes** (Marsh classification).

### ESPGHAN Biopsy-Avoidance Guidelines
* Biopsy can be avoided IF all 3 criteria met:
  1. Classic symptoms of Coeliac Disease.
  2. **Anti-tTG IgA** >10x upper limit of normal.
  3. Second blood sample confirms +ve **Endomysial Antibodies (EMA IgA)**.

### Adjunct Bloods
* FBC (Anaemia check).
* Iron studies, Folate, B12.
* Calcium, Vitamin D, LFTs, Bone profile.

## 8. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Dietary Management
* **ONLY treatment**: Strict, lifelong **Gluten-Free Diet (GFD)** (無麩質飲食).
* Absolute avoidance of wheat, rye, barley.
* Zero tolerance for trace amounts/cross-contamination (shared toasters, fryers) → triggers mucosal damage even if asymptomatic.

### Nutritional Support & Monitoring
* Correct deficiencies: Iron, Calcium, Vitamin D supplements initially.
* HA Protocol: Regular monitoring of growth (height/weight centiles), symptom resolution.
* Repeat **Anti-tTG IgA** to monitor dietary compliance (should normalize on strict GFD).

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
### Dietitian Role
* Absolutely essential. GFD is difficult, expensive, socially isolating.
* Educate family: Reading labels, identifying hidden gluten (soy sauce, sausages, play-dough), avoiding cross-contamination.
* Ensure nutritionally balanced diet.

### Psychosocial Support
* **Coeliac Support Groups**: Crucial for practical tips, recipes, social support.
* **School Liaison**: Inform school/canteen regarding strict GFD requirements to prevent accidental exposure.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Safeguarding Considerations
* N/A for this condition directly.
* Ensure faltering growth is thoroughly investigated to differentiate organic cause (Coeliac) from neglect/NAI.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
### Complications of Untreated Disease
* Osteoporosis, severe short stature, delayed puberty.
* Infertility.
* ↑ risk of **Enteropathy-associated T-cell Lymphoma (EATL)** & small bowel adenocarcinoma in adulthood.
* Associated Autoimmune Conditions: ↑ risk of Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus, Autoimmune Thyroiditis.

### Prognosis
* Excellent on strict GFD.
* Gut mucosa heals completely.
* Symptoms resolve rapidly (mood improves within days/weeks).
* Catch-up growth occurs.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Viva Question: A 14-month-old presents with chronic diarrhoea and faltering growth. You suspect Coeliac disease. The mother tells you she has already started him on a gluten-free diet for the past month and he is improving. What must you advise her before ordering the diagnostic blood tests? Answer: She must reintroduce gluten into his diet for at least 6 weeks before testing. Testing while on a gluten-free diet will result in false-negative serology and biopsies.
* Viva Question: What are the classic histological findings on a duodenal biopsy in a patient with Coeliac disease? Answer: Villous atrophy, crypt hyperplasia, and an increase in intraepithelial lymphocytes.
* Trap: Believing a negative Anti-tTG IgA completely rules out Coeliac disease in all patients. Pearl: Check Total IgA first! Selective IgA deficiency is more common in Coeliacs, which will cause a falsely negative Anti-tTG IgA.
* Pitfall: Diagnosing Coeliac disease purely on mildly elevated serology. Biopsy is usually required unless specific ESPGHAN criteria (tTG IgA >10x ULN and +ve EMA) are strictly met.
* Viva Question: Which skin condition is pathognomonic for Coeliac disease? Answer: Dermatitis Herpetiformis (intensely itchy, blistering rash on extensor surfaces).
