# Sickle Cell Disease (鐮狀細胞病)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: An inherited group of blood disorders causing red blood cells (RBCs) to distort into a sickle or crescent shape. The most severe and common form is HbSS (homozygous).
- **Local Epidemiology**: Extremely rare in the local ethnic Chinese population of Hong Kong, but seen in expatriate and immigrant populations (especially from African, Mediterranean, or Middle Eastern descent). Often tested as a classic textbook hematology topic.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Genetics**: Autosomal Recessive. A point mutation in the $\beta$-globin gene causes Glutamic Acid (hydrophilic) to be replaced by Valine (hydrophobic) at the 6th position.
- **Sickling**: When deoxygenated, the abnormal Hemoglobin S (HbS) undergoes polymerization into stiff rods. This warps the RBC into a sickle shape.
- **Vaso-occlusion**: Sickled RBCs are sticky and inflexible. They physically jam up small capillaries causing excruciating ischemic pain (Vaso-occlusive Crises) and end-organ infarction.
- **Hemolysis**: The rigid cells are destroyed quickly (lifespan ~10-20 days instead of 120), leading to chronic baseline anemia, jaundice, and gallstones.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Usually asymptomatic for the first 6 months of life because **Fetal Hemoglobin (HbF)** prevents sickling.
- First presentation is often at 6-9 months old with Dactylitis (painful, profoundly swollen hands and feet).

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Vaso-occlusive Crisis (VOC)**: Severe, throbbing bone/joint pain, often triggered by hypoxia, dehydration, cold, or infection.
- **Acute Chest Syndrome**: A life-threatening lung crisis mimicking pneumonia (fever, chest pain, new pulmonary infiltrates on CXR). The leading cause of death.
- **Splenic Sequestration Crisis**: Sudden, massive pooling of blood into the spleen causing acute severe anemia, splenomegaly, and rapid hypovolemic shock.
- **Aplastic Crisis**: Sudden halt of RBC production perfectly triggered by **Parvovirus B19** infection.
- **Cerebrovascular Accident (Stroke)**: High risk in childhood without transcranial doppler screening.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from Thalassemia: Thalassemia is a *quantitative* defect (not making enough globin chains), causing profound microcytic anemia without sickling crises. SCD is a *qualitative* defect (making broken globin chains) that sickles.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Osteomyelitis (often difficult to distinguish from a severe bone vaso-occlusive crisis. Note: Salmonella is a classic cause of osteomyelitis in SCD).
- Thalassemia
- Leukemia (bone pain)
- Appendicitis (mesenteric vaso-occlusion mimicking acute abdomen)

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bloods**: 
  - CBC: Hemoglobin usually 6-9 g/dL. High reticulocytes (except in aplastic crisis).
  - Blood smear: Sickle cells, target cells, and **Howell-Jolly bodies** (bits of DNA left in RBCs because the spleen is dead and can't clean them out).
- **Definitive Diagnosis**: Hemoglobin Electrophoresis or High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) showing absent HbA and predominant HbS.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Crises (Acute)**: Aggressive analgesia (Morpheine/Opiates often required), IV hydration, oxygen (if hypoxic), and treating underlying triggers. Exchange transfusion for severe Acute Chest Syndrome or Stroke.
- **Infection Prophylaxis (CRITICAL)**: Because the spleen auto-infarcts itself by age 5, these children have functional *asplenia*. They are completely defenceless against encapsulated bacteria (*S. pneumoniae*, *H. influenzae*, *N. meningitidis*). They MANDATORILY receive **daily prophylactic oral Penicillin** until at least age 5, plus extra vaccines.
- **Disease Modifier**: **Hydroxyurea** (chemotherepeutic agent that forces the bone marrow to turn back on Fetal Hemoglobin [HbF] production, which cannot sickle, dramatically reducing crises).
- **Curative**: Hematopoietic Stem Cell Transplant (HSCT).

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- immense psychosocial burden of chronic, disabling pain and fear of death.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Autosplenectomy by early childhood.
- Avascular necrosis of the femoral head.
- Chronic kidney disease (sickle nephropathy).
- Gallstones (pigment stones from chronic hemolysis).

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Under-dosing pain medication in a VOC because the patient "doesn't look like they are in pain." Chronic pain patients often mask their symptoms. Rely on their self-report and dose generously.
- **Viva Q**: "Why do you see Howell-Jolly bodies on the blood film of a 6-year-old with Sickle Cell Disease?" Answer: Because of repetitive splenic infarctions causing functional asplenia (autosplenectomy). The spleen's normal job involves pitting/removing these nuclear remnants from RBCs. Without a spleen, the Howell-Jolly bodies remain in the circulation.
