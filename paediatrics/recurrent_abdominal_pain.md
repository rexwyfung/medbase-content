
# Recurrent Abdominal Pain (反覆性腹痛)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: At least 3 episodes of abdominal pain, severe enough to affect activities, occurring over a period of at least 3 months (Apley's criteria). Now often classified under Functional Gastrointestinal Disorders (FGIDs) using Rome IV criteria.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Extremely common in HK school-aged children (~10-15%). Often associated with school stress, high academic expectations, and parental anxiety.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Functional (90%)**: No structural, infectious, or biochemical cause.
  - *Brain-Gut Axis*: Altered visceral hypersensitivity (normal bowel movements are perceived as painful) and altered gastrointestinal motility.
  - *Psychosocial*: Strongly linked to stress, anxiety, bullying, or family discord.
- **Organic (10%)**:
  - *GI*: Coeliac disease, Inflammatory Bowel Disease (Crohn's, UC), Peptic Ulcer Disease (H. pylori is common in HK), Eosinophilic Oesophagitis, Constipation.
  - *Renal*: Recurrent UTI, Pelviureteric junction (PUJ) obstruction.
  - *Gynaecological*: Endometriosis, ovarian cysts (in adolescent females).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- See below.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Functional Pain**: Typically periumbilical, dull/crampy, lasts < 1 hour, does not wake the child from sleep, normal growth, completely normal physical exam.
- **Red Flags (Suggest Organic Disease)**:
  - Pain located away from the umbilicus (e.g., RUQ, RLQ).
  - Pain that awakens the child from sleep.
  - Significant weight loss or faltering growth.
  - Unexplained fever, joint pain, or rash.
  - Persistent vomiting (especially bilious) or severe diarrhoea.
  - GI bleeding (haematemesis, melaena, haematochezia).
  - Family history of IBD, Coeliac disease, or Peptic Ulcer Disease.
  - Abnormal physical exam (e.g., right iliac fossa tenderness, perianal skin tags/fissures - Crohn's).
## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Constipation**: Very common cause of recurrent pain. Palpable faecal mass in LIF. Pain often improves after a large bowel movement.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Most Likely**:
  - Functional Abdominal Pain, Constipation.
- **Must-Not-Miss**:
  - Inflammatory Bowel Disease (Crohn's disease can present insidiously with poor growth and vague pain).
  - Coeliac disease.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**:
  - **If NO red flags**: Minimal investigations. Reassurance is key.
  - **Urine dipstick/culture**: To rule out UTI.
  - **Stool**: Occult blood, culture (if diarrhoea).
- **Gold Standard**:
  - **If Red Flags present**:
    - **Bloods**: CBC (anaemia), ESR/CRP (elevated in IBD), Coeliac screen (Anti-tTG IgA + Total IgA), LFTs, Amylase.
    - **Stool Calprotectin**: Excellent non-invasive marker for bowel inflammation (differentiates IBD from functional pain).
    - **Ultrasound Abdomen**: If specific signs (e.g., RUQ pain for gallstones, loin pain for PUJ obstruction).
    - **Endoscopy/Colonoscopy**: The definitive test for IBD, Coeliac, or H. pylori, but reserved for cases with strong clinical suspicion or abnormal screening tests.
- **Monitoring**:
  - Growth monitoring (height and weight centiles).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**:
  - Not applicable.
- **Chronic/Definitive**:
  - **Functional Pain**:
    - **Validate the pain**: Acknowledge that the pain is real, not "in their head".
    - **Biopsychosocial approach**: Explain the brain-gut axis.
    - **Symptom management**: Peppermint oil, antispasmodics (e.g., Mebeverine - use sparingly).
    - **Avoid triggers**: Identify and manage school/social stressors.
    - **Organic**: Treat the specific cause (e.g., laxatives for constipation, gluten-free diet for Coeliac, immunosuppressants for IBD).
## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Clinical Psychologist**: Referral is often necessary for Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) or hypnotherapy if functional pain is severely impacting school attendance.
## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- **School Refusal**: A common complication. Work with the school and MSW to ensure a graded return to school. Emphasise that the child should go to school *despite* the pain.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Functional**: Good prognosis, but symptoms may persist into adulthood (e.g., evolving into Irritable Bowel Syndrome).
- **Organic**: Depends on the disease (e.g., IBD requires lifelong management).

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Ordering an abdominal ultrasound for typical periumbilical functional pain. *Pearl*: Ultrasound has a very low yield in the absence of red flags and can increase parental anxiety.
- **Viva Question**: "What are the 'red flag' symptoms in a child presenting with recurrent abdominal pain?" *Answer*: Pain waking from sleep, pain away from the umbilicus, weight loss, GI bleeding, chronic diarrhoea, unexplained fever, and family history of IBD/Coeliac.
