# Parvovirus B19 (Erythema Infectiosum / Slapped Cheek Syndrome) (微小病毒 B19 / 傳染性紅斑)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A common childhood viral exanthem, also famously known as "Fifth Disease".
- **Local Epidemiology**: Common in school-aged outbreaks in late winter/spring.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Parvovirus B19 explicitly targets and destroys human erythroid progenitor cells (the precursors to red blood cells) in the bone marrow.
- In healthy children, this brief halt in RBC production is unnoticeable because mature RBCs live for 120 days.
- In children with chronic hemolytic anemias (whose RBCs die in 10-20 days), this halt causes a catastrophic, sudden drop in hemoglobin (Aplastic Crisis).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Classic presentation: A completely well-looking 6-year-old who suddenly develops bright red cheeks as if slapped.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Phase 1 (Viremia)**: Mild fever, coryza, headache (7-10 days before rash). THIS is when they are highly catching.
- **Phase 2 (Immune complex deposition)**: 
  - **"Slapped cheeks"**: Intense, confluent erythema on the face with circumoral pallor.
  - **Reticular rash**: A lace-like, pink, macular rash on the trunk and limbs following the cheek rash.
- Note: In teenagers/adults, it often presents without the rash but with severe symmetrical polyarthropathy (joint pains mimicking Rheumatoid Arthritis).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from Scarlet Fever: Scarlet fever has strawberry tongue, a sandpaper-like rash, and extreme sore throat. Parvovirus patients are usually systemically very well once the rash appears.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Rubella (German Measles)
- Measles
- Scarlet fever (Group A Strep)
- Enterovirus infection
- Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) (butterfly rash can mimic slapped cheek)

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- Usually purely a clinical diagnosis.
- **Bloods**: 
  - Parvovirus B19 IgM (indicates acute infection), IgG (indicates past immunity).
  - Complete Blood Count (CBC) with Reticulocyte count: Mandatory if the child is pale or known to have blood disorders. A reticulocyte count of ~0% confirms an Aplastic Crisis.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Healthy Children**: No treatment required. Reassurance only.
- **Aplastic Crisis**: Urgent blood transfusion.
- **Infection Control Constraint**: By the time the classic "slapped cheek" rash appears, the viremic phase is over, and the child is **NO LONGER INFECTIOUS**. They do NOT need to be excluded from school.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Maternal Health aspect**: Pregnant teachers/daycare workers exposed to a child in the prodromal phase must be referred to Obstetrics immediately.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- The bright red cheeks can sometimes literally be mistaken for a physical slap mark (child abuse). Ensure the rash is perfectly symmetrical and usually accompanied by the lacy limb rash.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Aplastic Crisis**: In patients with Sickle Cell Disease, Hereditary Spherocytosis, or Thalassemia.
- **Fetal Hydrops**: If a non-immune pregnant woman is infected in the first half of pregnancy, the virus destroys the fetal RBCs, causing severe fetal anemia and massive heart failure/anasarca (hydrops fetalis).

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Sending a child with slapped cheek rash home from school to "isolate". It's too late; they were infectious last week. 
- **Viva Q**: "A child with known Sickle Cell Disease reports a history of slapped cheeks and is now lethargic with a Hb of 3 g/dL. What test will confirm the cause of the drop?" Answer: A reticulocyte count. It will be near zero, confirming Parvovirus-induced aplastic crisis rather than splenic sequestration (where reticulocytes would be ragingly high).
