# Dengue Fever (登革熱)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: An acute mosquito-borne viral infection caused by the Dengue virus (Flavivirus, 4 sterotypes: DENV 1-4).
- **Local Epidemiology**: Hong Kong is highly susceptible to local outbreaks due to the *Aedes albopictus* mosquito population. Cases peak in late summer/autumn, often clustered around specific hiking trails or construction sites, alongside imported cases from Southeast Asia.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Viremic phase**: Direct viral replication causing systemic inflammation.
- **Critical phase (Plasma leakage)**: Usually starts just as the fever drops (defervescence). Triggered by intense cytokine release causing transient, massive dysfunction of the endothelial glycocalyx.
- **Antibody-dependent enhancement (ADE)**: Sequential infection with a *different* serotype causes existing antibodies to bind the virus but facilitate its entry into macrophages rather than destroying it, leading to vastly worse disease (Severe Dengue / Dengue Hemorrhagic Fever).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- A 10-year-old child returning from a SE Asian holiday with sudden high fever, headache, and severe aching joints, who suddenly deteriorates on day 5 just as their fever settles.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Febrile Phase (Days 1-3)**: High fever, retro-orbital pain (behind the eyes), severe myalgia ("breakbone fever"), facial flushing, positive tourniquet test.
- **Critical Phase (Days 4-6)**: FEVER DROPS, but patient worsens. **Plasma leakage**: pleural effusion, ascites, hemoconcentration (rising hematocrit). **Bleeding**: petechiae, epistaxis, GI bleeds.
- **Recovery Phase (Days 7+)**: Fluid reabsorbs, bradycardia, and the classic "Isles of white in a sea of red" convalescent rash.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from Influenza: Dengue has the characteristic retro-orbital pain and profound thrombocytopenia out of nowhere, plus the tourniquet test is positive.
- Distinct from Malaria: Malaria features cyclical fevers (every 48-72h) and no major rash; Dengue is a continuous fever for days followed by a severe crash.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Malaria
- Typhoid Fever
- Leptospirosis
- Zika / Chikungunya
- Influenza

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**: Tourniquet test (inflate BP cuff halfway between systolic and diastolic for 5 mins; >10 to 20 petechiae per square inch is positive).
- **Bloods**: 
  - **CBC**: *Leukopenia* followed rapidly by profound *thrombocytopenia*, AND a distinctly *rising Hematocrit (HCT)* (the hallmark of plasma leakage).
  - LFTs (transaminitis common).
- **Definitive**: Dengue NS1 Antigen test (positive in first 5 days) or Dengue PCR. Dengue IgM (positive after day 5).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Supportive**: STRICT fluid management is the cornerstone. 
- **Critical Phase Algorithm**: 
  - If HCT is rising without shock: Careful oral rehydration or cautious IV fluids matching the leak.
  - **Dengue Shock Syndrome**: Aggressive 10-20 ml/kg boluses of isotonic crystalloids. If refractory, use colloids.
- **Medications**: ONLY Paracetamol for fever. **ABSOLUTELY NO NSAIDs or Aspirin** due to severe bleeding risk.
- **Recovery Phase**: STOP all IV fluids immediately to prevent fatal pulmonary edema when the leaked plasma naturally returns to the bloodstream.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Notifiable infectious disease to the Centre for Health Protection (CHP) in HK for mosquito control measures (fogging).

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- N/A.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Dengue Shock Syndrome (DSS). Severe mucosal bleeding. Fulminant hepatic failure. 
- Mortality is very low if fluid resuscitation is managed perfectly during the 48-hour critical window.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Relaxing and thinking the child is getting better because their fever has broken. In Dengue, the moment the fever breaks (defervescence) is exactly when the lethal shock phase begins.
- **Viva Q**: "Why is checking the Hematocrit (HCT) along with platelets so crucial in Dengue?" Answer: Because the HCT indicates plasma leakage. The plasma leaks out of the vessels, but the red blood cells stay inside, making the blood thick and sludge-like (high HCT). A rising HCT + dropping platelets forecasts impending shock.
