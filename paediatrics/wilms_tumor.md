# Wilms Tumour (Nephroblastoma) (威爾姆氏腫瘤 / 腎母細胞瘤)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Wilms Tumour
* Most common primary malignant renal tumour of childhood
* Arises from embryonic kidney cells (**metanephric blastema**)
* Typically unilateral (90%) but can be bilateral (10%)

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
* Accounts for **~6%** of all childhood cancers in HK
* Centralised management at **Hong Kong Children's Hospital (HKCH)**
* Peak age of onset: **2–5 years**
* Incidence: ~1 in 10,000 children

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics

### Pathophysiology of Nephroblastoma
* Tumour develops from primitive embryonal renal tissue (blastema, stroma, epithelium)
* Grows rapidly → forms large, fragile, vascular mass → distorts normal kidney architecture
* High propensity for vascular invasion → extending into **renal vein** and **inferior vena cava (IVC)**
* **Metastasis:** Most commonly haematogenous spread to **Lungs (肺部)** (80%), followed by Liver.

### Associated Genetic Syndromes (Wilms Tumour)
| Syndrome | Clinical Features | Associated Gene |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **WAGR Syndrome** | **W**ilms tumour, **A**niridia (absent iris), **G**enitourinary anomalies, **R**etardation (intellectual disability) | **WT1** deletion (11p13) |
| **Beckwith-Wiedemann Syndrome (貝克威思-韋德曼氏症候群)** | Overgrowth syndrome (macrosomia, macroglossia, omphalocele, hemihypertrophy), neonatal hypoglycaemia | **WT2** mutation (11p15) |
| **Denys-Drash Syndrome** | Wilms tumour, early-onset nephrotic syndrome (mesangial sclerosis), male pseudohermaphroditism | **WT1** mutation |

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Classic Clinical Presentation
* Asymptomatic **Abdominal Mass (腹部腫塊)** (most common)
* Incidentally discovered by parents (bathing/dressing) or routine physical exam
* **Paradoxical Appearance:** Child generally appears remarkably well and thriving, contradicting the massive size of the tumour.

### Key Physical Signs
* **Palpable Mass:** Smooth, firm, distinctly unilateral (does **NOT** cross midline)
* **Haematuria (血尿):** Microscopic or macroscopic (~20%) → due to tumour invasion into pelvicalyceal system
* **Hypertension (高血壓):** (~25%) → due to ↑ renin secretion by tumour cells or renal ischaemia from mass compression
* **Systemic Signs:** Fever, abdominal pain, or pallor (if rapid intra-tumoural haemorrhage occurs)
* **Varicocele:** May develop on left side if tumour invades left renal vein (blocks left gonadal vein drainage)

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)

### Wilms Tumour vs Neuroblastoma 
| Feature | Wilms Tumour (Nephroblastoma) | Neuroblastoma (神經母細胞瘤) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Age** | 2–5 years | < 2 years (often infants) |
| **General Health** | Appears well | Systemically unwell (weight loss, irritable) |
| **Mass Characteristics** | Smooth, firm, **unilateral** | Nodular, irregular, **crosses midline** |
| **Systemic Signs** | Hypertension, Haematuria | Bone pain, "Raccoon eyes" (periorbital ecchymosis), opsoclonus-myoclonus |
| **Calcification on XR/CT** | Rare (~10%) | Common (~90%) |
| **Urinary Catecholamines** | Normal | **↑↑ VMA / HVA** |

## 5. Differential Diagnosis

### Differentials for Paediatric Abdominal Mass
* **Neuroblastoma (神經母細胞瘤):** Sympathetic nervous system tumour (adrenal medulla)
* **Hydronephrosis (腎積水):** Obstructed urinary tract forming large cystic renal mass
* **Multicystic Dysplastic Kidney (多囊性發育不良腎):** Congenital non-functioning cystic kidney
* **Autosomal Recessive Polycystic Kidney Disease (ARPKD):** Bilateral flank masses, usually presents neonatally
* **Hepatoblastoma (肝母細胞瘤):** If right-sided upper quadrant mass

## 6. Tiered Investigations

### Bedside & Laboratory Investigations
* **Urinalysis:** Check for microscopic haematuria
* **Blood Pressure:** Mandatory screening for renin-induced hypertension
* **FBC:** Check for anaemia (intra-tumoural bleed)
* **Renal Function (U&E, Creatinine):** Usually normal (contralateral kidney compensates), unless bilateral disease or Denys-Drash
* **Coagulation Screen:** Essential pre-op/pre-biopsy
* **Liver Function Tests (LFTs):** Baseline and check for hepatic metastasis

### First-Line & Staging Imaging
* **Abdominal Ultrasound (超聲波):** * 1st line investigation of choice
  * Confirms intra-renal origin, solid vs cystic nature
  * Crucial: Assesses contralateral kidney and Doppler evaluation of renal vein/IVC for tumour thrombus
* **CT/MRI Abdomen & Pelvis:** * Essential for exact anatomical staging, assessing regional lymphadenopathy, and surgical planning
* **CT Chest:** * Mandatory staging investigation to rule out lung metastases (most common metastatic site)

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)

### Hong Kong HA Oncology Protocol (SIOP)
* HK completely follows the **SIOP (International Society of Paediatric Oncology)** protocol (European model) rather than the COG (Children's Oncology Group - US model).
* **Core Philosophy of SIOP:** Pre-operative chemotherapy based on imaging diagnosis alone (biopsy avoided to prevent tumour seeding).

### Stepwise Management Plan
* **1. Neo-adjuvant Chemotherapy (化學治療):**
  * Given to almost all patients (usually Vincristine + Actinomycin D) for 4–6 weeks
  * Goal: Shrink tumour mass, form a thick fibrous pseudocapsule, ↓ risk of intraoperative rupture, and clear micrometastases
* **2. Radical Nephrectomy (根治性腎切除術):**
  * En bloc removal of affected kidney, peri-renal fat, and regional lymph nodes
  * Contralateral kidney must be visually inspected if bilateral disease suspected
* **3. Post-operative Adjuvant Chemotherapy:**
  * Regimen tailored by exact histological subtype (favourable vs anaplastic) and surgical stage
* **4. Radiotherapy (放射治療):**
  * Reserved for advanced stages (Stage III/IV) or high-risk unfavourable histology (anaplasia)

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care

### Multidisciplinary Team Involvement
* **HKCH Centralised Care:** Managed strictly under joint care of Paediatric Oncology and Paediatric Surgery at HK Children's Hospital
* **Clinical Geneticist:** Crucial for **Genetic Counseling (遺傳諮詢)**, especially in bilateral disease, hypospadias, aniridia, or overgrowth syndromes
* **Medical Social Worker (MSW):** Financial assistance for specialized treatments/transport
* **Clinical Psychologist:** Support for parents coping with sudden malignancy diagnosis in a previously "well" child

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags

### Critical Examination Precautions
* **DO NOT PALPATE VIGOROUSLY:** * Wilms tumour has an extremely friable capsule.
  * Excessive pressure → **Tumour Rupture** → spills malignant metanephric cells into peritoneal cavity.
  * Consequence: Instantly upstages the disease (Stage III) → demands aggressive whole-abdomen radiotherapy + intensive chemotherapy → ↑↑ long-term morbidity.
* **Signage:** "DO NOT PALPATE ABDOMEN" sign must be placed strictly on the patient's bed in the ward.

## 10. Complications & Prognosis

### Disease & Treatment Complications
* **Tumour Rupture:** Pre-op or intra-op, leading to abdominal seeding
* **Vascular:** Tumour embolism to pulmonary artery from IVC thrombus
* **Renal Failure:** Highest risk in bilateral Wilms or underlying Denys-Drash syndrome
* **Late Treatment Effects:** * Anthracycline-induced cardiotoxicity (Doxorubicin)
  * Radiation-induced scoliosis / secondary malignancies

### Prognostic Factors
* **Excellent Overall Prognosis:** >90% 5-year survival rate for localised, favourable histology
* **Poor Prognostic Indicators:** Anaplastic histology (most critical factor), advanced stage (IV/V), loss of heterozygosity at 1p and 16q

## 11. Follow-Up & Survivorship

### Long-Term Surveillance
* **Renal Function:** Serial BP checks, U&Es, urinalysis to protect the single remaining kidney
* **Ultrasound Screening:** For patients with genetic syndromes (e.g., Beckwith-Wiedemann), regular US screening is required to detect early Wilms or hepatoblastoma until age 7
* **Cardiac Echo:** For patients who received anthracyclines

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: A 3-year-old child presents with a painless, smooth, unilateral abdominal mass. The child appears completely healthy. Blood pressure is 130/85. What is the most likely diagnosis? Answer: Wilms Tumour (Nephroblastoma).
* Trap: Performing an immediate percutaneous biopsy of a suspected Wilms tumour to confirm the diagnosis. Pearl: Under the SIOP protocol used in HK, diagnosis is made on imaging alone and neo-adjuvant chemotherapy is started immediately. Biopsy risks tumour rupture and upstaging.
* Pitfall: Allowing medical students to repeatedly palpate the abdomen of a child with a suspected Wilms tumour. Place a "Do Not Palpate" sign on the bed; vigorous palpation can rupture the fragile tumour capsule, spilling malignant cells and upstaging the disease to Stage III.
* Viva Question: What is the most common site of metastasis for a Wilms tumour, and what mandatory imaging is required to check for this? Answer: The lungs. A CT Chest is mandatory for staging.
* Viva Question: Name two major genetic syndromes associated with Wilms tumour. Answer: WAGR syndrome and Beckwith-Wiedemann syndrome.
* Trap: Confusing the clinical presentation of Wilms tumour with Neuroblastoma. Pearl: Wilms tumour typically occurs in well-appearing older toddlers (2-5 years) with a smooth unilateral mass, whereas Neuroblastoma usually affects sick-appearing infants (<2 years) with an irregular mass that crosses the midline.
