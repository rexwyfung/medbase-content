# Wilms Tumour (Nephroblastoma) (威爾姆氏腫瘤 / 腎母細胞瘤)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: The most common primary malignant renal tumour of childhood. It arises from embryonal kidney cells (metanephric blastema).
- **Local Epidemiology**: Accounts for ~6% of all childhood cancers. Peak incidence is between 2 and 5 years of age.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Pathophysiology**: Tumour develops from primitive embryonal renal tissue. It is usually unilateral (90%) but can be bilateral. It tends to grow rapidly, forming a large, fragile mass that distorts the kidney. It can invade the renal vein and extend into the inferior vena cava (IVC).
- **Genetics & Syndromes**: ~10% of cases are associated with specific genetic syndromes:
  - **WAGR Syndrome**: **W**ilms tumour, **A**niridia (absence of iris), **G**enitourinary anomalies, **R**etardation (intellectual disability). Associated with WT1 gene deletion.
  - **Beckwith-Wiedemann Syndrome**: An overgrowth syndrome (macrosomia, macroglossia, omphalocele, hemihypertrophy). Associated with WT2 gene mutation.
  - **Denys-Drash Syndrome**: Wilms tumour, early-onset nephrotic syndrome, male pseudohermaphroditism.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- The most common presentation is an **asymptomatic abdominal mass** discovered incidentally by a parent while bathing or dressing the child, or by a doctor during a routine examination.
- The child usually appears remarkably well, in stark contrast to the size of the tumour.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Abdominal Mass**: Typically smooth, firm, and **does NOT cross the midline** (unlike neuroblastoma).
- **Haematuria**: Microscopic or macroscopic (in ~20% of cases) due to tumour invasion into the collecting system.
- **Hypertension**: Occurs in ~25% of cases due to renin secretion by the tumour or renal ischaemia from compression.
- **Abdominal Pain/Fever**: May occur if there is rapid growth or haemorrhage into the tumour.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Neuroblastoma**: The main differential for a childhood abdominal mass. Neuroblastoma often crosses the midline, is nodular/irregular, and the child is usually systemically unwell (weight loss, bone pain, "raccoon eyes"). Wilms tumour is usually smooth, unilateral, and the child looks well.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Neuroblastoma.
- Hydronephrosis (can present as a large renal mass).
- Polycystic kidney disease.
- Hepatoblastoma (if right-sided).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Abdominal Ultrasound**: The initial investigation of choice. Confirms the mass is intra-renal, solid/cystic, and checks the contralateral kidney and renal vein/IVC for tumour thrombus.
- **CT/MRI Abdomen & Pelvis**: Essential for precise anatomical staging, assessing lymph nodes, and planning surgery.
- **CT Chest**: Mandatory to check for lung metastases (the most common site of distant spread).
- **Urinalysis**: To check for haematuria.
- **Bloods**: FBC, U&E, Creatinine, LFTs, Coagulation screen.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- Management depends on the protocol used (SIOP vs. COG). Hong Kong generally follows the SIOP (International Society of Paediatric Oncology) protocols.
- **SIOP Protocol**:
  1. **Pre-operative Chemotherapy**: Given to almost all patients (based on imaging diagnosis) for 4-6 weeks to shrink the tumour, form a fibrous pseudocapsule, and reduce the risk of intraoperative rupture.
  2. **Surgery**: Radical nephrectomy (removal of the entire affected kidney and surrounding fat/lymph nodes). *Crucial: The surgeon must be extremely careful not to rupture the tumour, as spilling tumour cells into the abdomen upstages the disease and requires abdominal radiation.*
  3. **Post-operative Chemotherapy**: Tailored based on the histological subtype and stage.
  4. **Radiotherapy**: Reserved for advanced stages or specific high-risk histologies.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Paediatric Oncology & Paediatric Surgery**.
- **Genetic Counseling**: Important for patients with associated syndromes or bilateral disease.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- *Crucial Clinical Rule*: **NEVER vigorously palpate a suspected Wilms tumour.** Excessive palpation can cause the fragile tumour capsule to rupture, spilling malignant cells into the peritoneal cavity, instantly upstaging the disease and worsening the prognosis.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Complications**: Tumour rupture, hypertension, renal failure (especially in bilateral cases or Denys-Drash syndrome), complications of chemotherapy/radiotherapy (e.g., secondary malignancies, cardiotoxicity).
- **Prognosis**: Excellent. The overall 5-year survival rate is >90% for localized disease with favourable histology.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "A mother brings her 3-year-old in because she noticed a hard lump on the right side of his tummy while bathing him. The child is completely well. On examination, you feel a smooth mass that does not cross the midline. What is the most likely diagnosis, and what is the most important rule regarding your physical examination?" (Wilms tumour. You must NOT vigorously palpate the mass to avoid rupturing the tumour capsule and upstaging the disease).
- *Viva*: "Name two genetic syndromes associated with Wilms tumour." (WAGR syndrome and Beckwith-Wiedemann syndrome).
