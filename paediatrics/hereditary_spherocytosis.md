# Hereditary Spherocytosis (遺傳性球形紅血球增多症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Hereditary Spherocytosis
* **Inherited hemolytic anemia** (遺傳性溶血性貧血)
* Characterized by defect in RBC membrane cytoskeleton
* Normal biconcave RBCs → rigid spheres (spherocytes)
* Rigid RBCs trapped & destroyed in spleen → **Extravascular hemolysis** (血管外溶血)

### Epidemiology of HS in Hong Kong
* Most common inherited structural RBC membrane defect
* Prevalence: ~1 in 2000-5000 globally; less common in HK Chinese than G6PD deficiency or Thalassemia, but remains the most common RBC membrane disorder locally
* ~75% Autosomal Dominant (AD); 25% Autosomal Recessive (AR) or de novo mutations

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics

### Genetic Inheritance of HS
* **AD inheritance** (75%): Usually defects in **Ankyrin** (*ANK1*) or **Band 3** (*SLC4A1*)
* **AR inheritance** (25%): Usually defects in **Spectrin** (α or β) or Protein 4.2; often presents with more severe clinical phenotype

### Membrane Defect in HS
* Mutations cause deficiency/dysfunction of vertical anchoring proteins
* Disruption between lipid bilayer and underlying cytoskeleton
* → Progressive loss of unsupported lipid membrane (microvesiculation)
* → ↓ Surface area-to-volume ratio

### Mechanism of Hemolysis in HS
* RBC assumes spherical shape to accommodate volume in reduced surface area
* Spherocytes lose deformability
* Trapped in splenic microcirculation (cords of Billroth)
* Premature destruction by splenic macrophages → **Extravascular hemolysis** (血管外溶血) → ↑ Unconjugated bilirubin, ↑ Reticulocytes

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Neonatal Presentation of HS
* **Severe Neonatal Jaundice** (新生兒黃疸): Often presents within 1st 24-48 hrs
* Anemia (may be masked by physiological polycythemia)
* Splenomegaly (less prominent in neonates)

### Childhood Presentation of HS
* **Hemolytic Anemia** (溶血性貧血): Fatigue, pallor, exercise intolerance
* **Intermittent Jaundice** (黃疸): Fluctuates, exacerbated by viral illnesses
* **Pigment Gallstones** (膽色素結石): Can present as biliary colic/cholecystitis in young children/adolescents

### Key Clinical Signs of HS
* **Pallor** (蒼白)
* **Scleral icterus** (鞏膜黃疸)
* **Splenomegaly** (脾臟腫大): Often massive, palpable extending towards RIF
* Frontal bossing / maxillary prominence (rare, only in severe chronic hemolysis)

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)

### HS vs Autoimmune Hemolytic Anemia (AIHA)
* **AIHA**: +ve Direct Antiglobulin Test (DAT / Coombs test), acquired, no family history
* **HS**: -ve DAT, inherited, +ve family history, +ve EMA binding test

### HS vs G6PD Deficiency
* **G6PD**: X-linked recessive (males predominantly), episodic intravascular hemolysis triggered by oxidative stress (fava beans, infections), Heinz bodies/bite cells on smear
* **HS**: AD (both sexes), chronic extravascular hemolysis, spherocytes on smear

## 5. Differential Diagnosis

### Table of Differentials for Spherocytosis
| Condition | Inheritance | Key Differentiating Features | DAT (Coombs) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Hereditary Spherocytosis** | Mostly AD | Chronic, splenomegaly, +ve EMA binding | -ve |
| **AIHA** | Acquired | Acute onset, signs of autoimmunity | **+ve** |
| **ABO Incompatibility** | N/A | Neonates only, mother O / baby A or B | +ve (weak) |
| **Clostridium perfringens** | Acquired | Severe sepsis, massive intravascular hemolysis| -ve |
| **Burns (Severe)** | Acquired | Clear history of thermal injury, microspherocytes| -ve |

## 6. Tiered Investigations

### First-Line Blood Tests for HS
* **Complete Blood Count (CBC)**:
  * Hb: ↓ or normal (compensated)
  * MCV: Normal or slightly ↓
  * **MCHC: ↑** (>36 g/dL) (Highly specific characteristic)
  * RDW: ↑
* **Reticulocyte Count**: ↑ (3-15%)
* **Peripheral Blood Smear** (周邊血液抹片): Dense spherocytes lacking central pallor, polychromasia
* **Hemolysis Screen**:
  * Unconjugated Bilirubin: ↑
  * LDH: ↑
  * Haptoglobin: ↓

### Gold Standard Confirmatory Test for HS
* **EMA (Eosin-5-Maleimide) Binding Test** (Flow cytometry):
  * Replaces old osmotic fragility test in HK HA hospitals
  * Fluorescent dye binds to Band 3 protein; HS RBCs show ↓ fluorescence (↓ binding)
* **DAT (Coombs Test)**: Strictly must be -ve to rule out AIHA

### Supportive Imaging for HS
* **Ultrasound Abdomen** (腹部超聲波):
  * Assess degree of splenomegaly
  * Screen for cholelithiasis (pigment stones)

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)

### Acute Management of Aplastic Crisis
* **Trigger**: Usually **Parvovirus B19** (微小病毒B19) affecting erythroid progenitors
* **Presentation**: Acute severe anemia, ↓↓ reticulocytes (reticulocytopenia)
* **Management**:
  * Urgent hospital admission
  * Packed RBC (PRBC) transfusion if hemodynamically compromised / symptomatic
  * Isolate patient (droplet precautions for Parvovirus)

### Chronic Medical Management
* **Folic Acid Supplementation** (葉酸補充): 1-5 mg daily to support ↑ erythropoiesis
* Monitoring: Routine clinic follow-up for growth, Hb levels, and gallstone surveillance

### Splenectomy Protocol (HA Guidelines)
* **Indication**: Severe anemia, growth retardation, massive symptomatic splenomegaly, recurrent severe crises
* **Timing**: Strictly **defer until >6 years old** (due to high risk of Overwhelming Post-Splenectomy Infection - OPSI in young children)
* **Concurrent surgery**: Cholecystectomy if symptomatic gallstones present

### Post-Splenectomy Sepsis Prevention (HA Protocol)
* **Pre-operative Vaccines** (give ≥2-4 weeks prior):
  * Pneumococcal (PCV13 followed by PPSV23)
  * Meningococcal (MenACWY and MenB)
  * *Haemophilus influenzae* type b (Hib)
* **Lifelong Prophylaxis**: Daily **Penicillin V** (or Erythromycin if allergic)
* **Patient Education**: Carry medical alert card, urgent presentation for any fever ≥38°C

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care

### Multidisciplinary Team Involvement for HS
* **Pediatric Hematologist**: Disease monitoring and crisis management
* **Pediatric Surgeon**: Assessment for splenectomy/cholecystectomy
* **Dietitian**: Nutritional support during high-turnover states

### Psychosocial Support for HS Patients
* Manage chronic fatigue impact on schooling and physical activities
* Psychological support for body image issues (visible jaundice, surgical scars)
* Genetic counseling for parents/patient regarding future offspring

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags

### Safeguarding Considerations in Chronic Hemolysis
* Ensure parental compliance with daily folic acid and post-splenectomy Penicillin V
* Frequent missed appointments or failure to present during febrile episodes post-splenectomy requires social worker involvement (Medical Neglect)
* Note: Splenomegaly increases risk of splenic rupture; counsel against contact sports. If rupture occurs without appropriate trauma history, consider NAI.

## 10. Complications & Prognosis

### Acute Complications of HS
* **Aplastic Crisis** (再生不良性危機): Parvovirus B19 induced
* **Hemolytic Crisis**: Triggered by intercurrent viral/bacterial infections
* **Megaloblastic Crisis**: Due to folate exhaustion (rare if compliant with supplements)

### Chronic Complications of HS
* **Pigment Gallstones** (膽色素結石) → Cholecystitis, biliary obstruction
* **Growth Retardation**: Seen in severe uncompensated cases
* **Extramedullary Hematopoiesis**: Can cause skeletal changes (rare)
* **OPSI**: Post-splenectomy (Capsulated organisms: *S. pneumoniae*, *N. meningitidis*, *H. influenzae*)

### Long-Term Prognosis of HS
* Generally excellent
* Mild cases: Normal life expectancy without surgery
* Severe cases: Splenectomy provides clinical cure (halts hemolysis), normalizing Hb and resolving jaundice, but lifelong infection risk persists

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: What is the most common viral trigger for an aplastic crisis in Hereditary Spherocytosis and what is the key lab finding? Answer: Parvovirus B19; characterized by profound anemia with a low/absent reticulocyte count.
* Trap: Diagnosing HS solely based on spherocytes on a peripheral blood smear. Pearl: Spherocytes are also the hallmark of Autoimmune Hemolytic Anemia (AIHA); you must always check a Direct Antiglobulin Test (DAT/Coombs test), which will be negative in HS and positive in AIHA.
* Viva Question: Why is the MCHC typically elevated in Hereditary Spherocytosis? Answer: Cellular dehydration relative to hemoglobin content, caused by membrane loss and a defective K+/H2O transport out of the RBC.
* Pitfall: Recommending early splenectomy for a 3-year-old child with severe HS. High risk of Overwhelming Post-Splenectomy Infection (OPSI). Hong Kong HA and international protocols mandate delaying splenectomy until at least 6 years of age.
* Trap: Forgetting to manage the gallbladder when planning a splenectomy for HS. Pearl: Always perform a pre-operative ultrasound; if pigment stones are present, a concomitant cholecystectomy should be performed to prevent future biliary complications.
