# Soft Tissue Sarcomas (Rhabdomyosarcoma) (軟組織肉瘤 - 橫紋肌肉瘤)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Rhabdomyosarcoma
* Highly malignant **Soft Tissue Sarcoma (軟組織肉瘤)**
* Arises from primitive mesenchymal cells destined to become skeletal muscle
* Can occur anywhere, even in sites lacking mature skeletal muscle (e.g., bladder, vagina)
* Most common soft-tissue sarcoma in childhood

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
* Centralized management at **Hong Kong Children's Hospital (HKCH) (香港兒童醫院)**
* Peak incidence: Young children (**<5 years**) & adolescents
* Accounts for ~5-8% of all paediatric malignancies
* Slight male predominance

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics

### Histological Subtypes of Rhabdomyosarcoma
* **Embryonal (胚胎型)**:
    * Most common (~70%)
    * Typically affects younger children (<5 yrs)
    * Favorable prognosis (especially botryoid & spindle cell variants)
    * Common sites: Head/neck, genitourinary (GU)
* **Alveolar (腺泡型)**:
    * Less common (~20%)
    * Typically affects older children/adolescents
    * Highly aggressive → worse prognosis
    * Common sites: Trunk, extremities
* **Pleomorphic (多形型)**: Rare in kids, mostly adults

### Genetic Drivers & Translocations
* **Alveolar RMS**: Driven by specific gene fusions
    * **t(2;13)** → **PAX3-FOXO1** fusion protein (more common, poorer prognosis)
    * **t(1;13)** → **PAX7-FOXO1** fusion protein
* **Embryonal RMS**: Loss of heterozygosity (LOH) at chromosome 11p15 (associated with **Beckwith-Wiedemann Syndrome (貝克威斯-韋德曼綜合症)**)

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Head & Neck Presentation (40%)
* **Orbit**: Rapidly expanding, painless **Proptosis (突眼)**, visual changes, extraocular muscle palsy
* **Parameningeal** (nasopharynx, middle ear, paranasal sinuses):
    * Nasal obstruction / epistaxis
    * Chronic blood-stained ear discharge (mimicking otitis media/mastoiditis)
    * Cranial nerve palsies (invasion of skull base)

### Genitourinary Presentation (25%)
* **Bladder/Prostate**: Urinary retention, hematuria, constipation (pelvic mass)
* **Vagina**: **Sarcoma Botryoides (葡萄狀肉瘤)** (Embryonal variant) → Exophytic, fleshy, grape-like mass protruding from introitus, blood-spotted discharge

### Extremity & Trunk Presentation (20%)
* Rapidly growing, firm, deep mass
* Often painless initially
* More likely **Alveolar** subtype
* Regional lymphadenopathy +ve

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)

### Distinguishing Sarcoma from Benign Hematoma
* **History**: Trauma often reported by parents (incidental finding vs. cause)
* **Evolution**:
    * **Hematoma (血腫)**: Shrinks over weeks, color changes (bruising resolves)
    * **RMS**: Relentless growth, firming, skin becomes taut/shiny, fixed to deep structures

### Distinguishing Sarcoma Botryoides from Foreign Body
* **Foreign Body**: Purulent, foul-smelling discharge, sudden onset
* **RMS**: Grape-like fleshy mass visible, hemorrhagic discharge, progressive

## 5. Differential Diagnosis

### Small Round Blue Cell Tumors (SRBCT) Differentials Table
| Diagnosis | Differentiating Clinical & Lab Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Rhabdomyosarcoma** | +ve **Desmin (結蛋白)**, **MyoD1**, **Myogenin**. Soft tissue primary. |
| **Neuroblastoma (神經母細胞瘤)** | Abdominal/neck mass, ↑ Urine catecholamines (VMA/HVA), +ve MIBG scan. |
| **Ewing Sarcoma (尤文氏肉瘤)** | Usually bone primary (can be extraosseous), CD99 +ve, t(11;22). |
| **Lymphoma (淋巴瘤)** | Prominent B-symptoms (fever, night sweats, weight loss), node primary, CD45 +ve. |
| **Abscess / Hematoma** | Signs of inflammation/infection, history of significant trauma, resolves with time/antibiotics. |

## 6. Tiered Investigations

### Baseline Blood Investigations
* **CBC**: Rule out cytopenias (marrow involvement)
* **Renal/Liver Function**: Pre-chemo baseline, check for post-renal AKI if bladder mass
* **LDH & Uric Acid**: ↑ in high cell turnover (tumor lysis risk)

### Local & Systemic Staging Scans
* **MRI Primary Site**: Gold standard for local extent, neurovascular encasement, skull base invasion
* **CT Thorax**: Essential to rule out lung metastases (most common met site)
* **PET-CT / Bone Scan**: Evaluate for bony and distant soft-tissue metastases

### Definitive Pathological Investigations
* **Incisional/Core Needle Biopsy**: Gold standard. Avoid excisional biopsy outside specialized centers (disrupts tumor bed)
* **Histology**: "Small round blue cells"
* **Immunohistochemistry**: Rhabdomyoblasts stain +ve for skeletal muscle markers (**Desmin**, **MyoD1**, **Myogenin**)
* **Bone Marrow Aspirate & Trephine (骨髓穿刺及活檢)**: Mandatory staging to rule out marrow micrometastases

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)

### Surgical Management Protocols
* **Maximum Safe Resection**: Primary aim if feasible without significant functional/cosmetic morbidity
* **Organ Preservation**: Avoid mutilating surgery (e.g., orbital exenteration, total pelvic exenteration) upfront → utilize neo-adjuvant chemo to shrink tumor first
* **Lymph Node Dissection**: Required for clinically +ve nodes or specific sites (e.g., paratesticular)

### Chemotherapy Regimens (HKCH / International Protocols)
* **MANDATORY** for all patients to clear micrometastases
* **Standard Regimens**:
    * **VAC**: **Vincristine (長春新鹼)**, **Actinomycin-D (放線菌素D)**, **Cyclophosphamide (環磷酰胺)**
    * **IVA**: **Ifosfamide (異環磷酰胺)** (often replaces cyclophosphamide in European/HK protocols), Vincristine, Actinomycin-D

### Radiotherapy (Local Control)
* Indicated if tumor is incompletely resected or for specific unfavorable sites (e.g., parameningeal, alveolar subtype)
* **Proton Beam Therapy (質子治療)**: Preferred in young children to spare normal growing tissue (avoids severe late-effects)

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care

### Multidisciplinary Team Operations
* **Core Team**: Paediatric Oncologist, Paediatric Surgeon, Radiation Oncologist, Pathologist, Radiologist
* **Reconstructive Follow-up**: Head/neck RT causes severe facial bone hypoplasia → requires plastic surgery/maxillofacial MDT
* **Medical Social Worker (MSW)**: Financial assistance for CCF (Children's Cancer Foundation) drugs/therapies
* **Clinical Psychologist**: Support for body image issues (hair loss, amputations, facial deformity)

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags

### Differentiating Tumor from Non-Accidental Injury (NAI)
* Parents often attribute soft tissue masses/bruising to a specific minor fall
* **Red Flag for NAI**: Inconsistent history, injuries of different ages, specific bruise patterns (grip marks)
* **Red Flag for Tumor**: Mass is painless, extremely hard, completely disproportionate to the described trauma, rapidly expanding

## 10. Complications & Prognosis

### Prognostic Risk Stratification
* **Favorable Factors**:
    * Site: Orbit, Non-parameningeal head/neck, GU (non-bladder/prostate)
    * Histology: Embryonal
    * Extent: Fully resected, localized
* **Unfavorable Factors**:
    * Site: Parameningeal (CSF spread risk), Extremity, Trunk
    * Histology: Alveolar
    * Extent: Distant metastases (lung, bone marrow, liver)

### Treatment-Related Complications
* **Vincristine**: Peripheral neuropathy, paralytic ileus
* **Alkylating Agents (Cyclophosphamide/Ifosfamide)**: Hemorrhagic cystitis (prevent with Mesna), future infertility
* **Radiotherapy**: Growth retardation of irradiated bones, secondary malignancies

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Trap: Ignoring blood-spotted vaginal discharge in a toddler, assuming it is purely trauma or a foreign body without a thorough pelvic exam under anesthesia.
* Pearl: You must aggressively rule out Sarcoma Botryoides in any young girl with bloody vaginal discharge and a fleshy mass.
* Viva Question: A biopsy of a pediatric tumor stains intensely positive for Desmin and MyoD1. What is the cell of origin?
* Answer: Primitive skeletal muscle mesenchymal cells (pathognomonic for Rhabdomyosarcoma).
* Pitfall: Attempting to fully excise an undiagnosed rapidly growing limb mass in a general pediatric surgical unit. This disrupts the tumor bed, seeds the surrounding tissue, and complicates future limb-salvage surgery. Always refer to a specialized oncology center for a planned core biopsy.
* Viva Question: What is the most common site of distant metastasis in Rhabdomyosarcoma and how is it screened?
* Answer: The lungs. It is screened via a High-Resolution CT Thorax.
* Trap: Assuming a toddler with chronic ear discharge and a facial nerve palsy just has treatment-resistant otitis media.
* Pearl: Always consider parameningeal Rhabdomyosarcoma in the differential for chronic ear issues complicated by cranial nerve deficits.
