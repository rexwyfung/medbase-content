# Soft Tissue Sarcomas (Rhabdomyosarcoma) (軟組織肉瘤 - 橫紋肌肉瘤)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A highly malignant cancer arising from primitive mesenchymal cells destined to become skeletal muscle. It is the most common soft-tissue sarcoma in children.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Most common in young children (<5 years) and adolescents. Two main histological subtypes: Embryonal (more common, younger kids) and Alveolar (older kids, much worse prognosis).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Develops anywhere there is skeletal muscle, but frequently arises in places with *no* mature skeletal muscle (like the bladder or vagina).
- **Alveolar subtype**: Driven by specific gene fusions, typically t(2;13) causing a PAX3-FOXO1 fusion protein.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- A 3-year-old girl with a rapidly expanding, firm, painless mass pushing her right eye outward and downward (proptosis).

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- Symptoms depend entirely on the tumor location (since it can grow anywhere).
- **Head & Neck (40%)**: Orbit (proptosis, visual changes), Parameningeal (nasal obstruction, cranial nerve palsies, chronic ear discharge mimicking mastoiditis).
- **Genitourinary (25%)**: 
  - **Sarcoma Botryoides** (a variant of Embryonal RMS): Presents as an exophytic, fleshy, grape-like mass protruding from the vagina or causing bladder obstruction.
- **Extremities (20%)**: A rapidly growing, firm, deep thigh or arm mass (often Alveolar subtype).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Distinct from Hematomas: Parents often blame a bump/fall for a new extremity mass. Hematomas shrink and change colors over weeks. Rhabdomyosarcoma relentlessly grows and hardens.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Hematoma / Abscess
- Neuroblastoma (if in abdomen/neck)
- Lymphoma
- Ewing Sarcoma (extraosseous)

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Imaging**: MRI of the primary site to assess tissue invasion. CT of lungs/thorax to rule out metastases.
- **Definitive Diagnosis**: Core needle biopsy or open surgical biopsy. Histopathology shows "small round blue cells" and rhabdomyoblasts staining positive for skeletal muscle markers (Desmin, MyoD1, Myogenin).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- Require an aggressive, multimodal approach.
- **Surgery**: Maximum safe surgical resection of the primary tumor (sparing function if possible).
- **Chemotherapy**: MANDATORY for all patients to clear micrometastases (VAC regimen: Vincristine, Actinomycin-D, Cyclophosphamide).
- **Radiation**: High-dose targeted radiation to the primary bed if the tumor was not 100% physically removed.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Head and neck radiation in young children causes severe long-term facial bone hypoplasia and cosmetic deformities requiring reconstructive surgical follow-up.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- N/A.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Prognosis heavily depends on histology (Embryonal > Alveolar) and site (Orbit is favorable; Parameningeal spreading to CSF is very poor).
- Metastasis primarily goes to lungs and bone marrow.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Ignoring blood-spotted discharge from the vagina in a toddler, assuming it is purely trauma or foreign body without doing a proper exam. You must rule out Sarcoma Botryoides.
- **Viva Q**: "A biopsy of a pediatric tumor stains intensely positive for Desmin and MyoD1. What is the cell of origin?" Answer: Skeletal muscle (pathognomonic for Rhabdomyosarcoma).
