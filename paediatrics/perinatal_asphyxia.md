
# Perinatal Asphyxia & Hypoxic Ischaemic Encephalopathy (HIE) (圍產期窒息與缺氧缺血性腦病)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Impaired gas exchange during labour or delivery leading to fetal hypoxia, hypercapnia, and profound metabolic acidosis. **HIE** is the resulting clinical syndrome of neurological dysfunction.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Incidence of moderate-to-severe HIE is ~1-2 per 1000 live births. Managed in regional NICUs (e.g., QMH, PWH, KWH, UCH) which have therapeutic hypothermia capabilities.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Primary Energy Failure**: Immediate drop in ATP due to hypoxia -> cell depolarization, calcium influx, acute cell death.
- **Latent Phase**: A brief period (1-6 hours) of partial recovery. This is the **CRITICAL therapeutic window** for intervention.
- **Secondary Energy Failure**: Occurs 6-48 hours later. Reperfusion injury, oxidative stress, excitotoxicity (glutamate), and apoptosis lead to further brain damage.
- **Multi-organ Involvement**: Asphyxia affects all organs (kidneys -> ATN, heart -> myocardial dysfunction, gut -> NEC, liver -> transaminitis).

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- See below.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **At Birth**: Poor Apgar scores (e.g., < 5 at 5 and 10 minutes), need for prolonged resuscitation.
- **Sarnat Staging of HIE**:
  - *Stage 1 (Mild)*: Hyperalert, irritable, uninhibited reflexes, sympathetic overactivity (tachycardia, dilated pupils). Usually resolves in 24h.
  - *Stage 2 (Moderate)*: Lethargic, hypotonia, weak suck, parasympathetic overactivity (bradycardia, constricted pupils), **seizures** are common.
  - *Stage 3 (Severe)*: Comatose, flaccid, absent reflexes, absent spontaneous breathing, fixed pupils, severe intractable seizures.
## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Metabolic Disorders (e.g., Urea Cycle Defects)**: Can present with encephalopathy and seizures in the first few days, but usually have a normal birth history and Apgar scores, deteriorating only after feeding starts.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Most Likely**:
  - HIE (if clear history of sentinel event like placental abruption, cord prolapse, or profound fetal distress).
- **Must-Not-Miss**:
  - Intracranial Haemorrhage (e.g., massive subgaleal or intraventricular haemorrhage).
  - Neonatal Sepsis / Meningitis.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**:
  - **Cord Blood Gas**: The most objective measure of asphyxia. Severe acidemia (pH < 7.0, Base Deficit > 16 mmol/L) strongly supports the diagnosis.
  - **Amplitude-Integrated EEG (aEEG)**: Bedside continuous brain monitoring. Crucial for detecting subclinical seizures and assessing background brain activity (e.g., burst suppression, flat trace) to determine eligibility for cooling.
- **Gold Standard**:
  - **MRI Brain**: The gold standard for assessing the extent and pattern of brain injury. Usually performed at day 5-7 of life (after rewarming).
  - *Pattern*: Deep grey matter (basal ganglia/thalamus) injury suggests acute profound asphyxia. Watershed (parasagittal) injury suggests prolonged partial asphyxia.
- **Monitoring**:
  - **Multi-organ screening**: U&E (AKI), LFTs, Troponin/CK-MB, Coagulation profile (DIC).

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**:
  - **Neonatal Resuscitation Program (NRP)**: Effective ventilation is the most important step.
- **Chronic/Definitive**:
  - **Therapeutic Hypothermia (Cooling)**: The standard of care in HK HA NICUs for infants ≥ 36 weeks gestation with moderate-to-severe HIE.
  - *Mechanism*: Lowers brain temperature to 33.5°C for 72 hours. Reduces metabolic rate, suppresses excitotoxins, and blunts apoptosis during the secondary energy failure phase.
  - *Timing*: MUST be initiated within **6 hours** of birth.
  - **Seizure Management**: Phenobarbitone is the first-line anti-epileptic drug.
  - **Supportive Care**: Maintain normoglycaemia, normotension, and avoid hyperoxia/hypocapnia (which decrease cerebral blood flow).
## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Long-term neurodevelopmental follow-up is mandatory (CAS referral).
## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Prognostication is difficult in the first few days. Communication must be honest but empathetic, often involving the NICU consultant.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Prognosis**: Depends on Sarnat stage. Mild HIE usually has normal outcomes. Severe HIE has high mortality or severe neurodevelopmental impairment.
- **Long-term Complications**: Cerebral Palsy (often dyskinetic/athetoid if basal ganglia injured, or spastic quadriplegia), intellectual disability, epilepsy, cortical visual impairment, sensorineural hearing loss.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Giving 100% oxygen during neonatal resuscitation. *Pearl*: Resuscitation should start with 21% (room air) for term infants. Hyperoxia generates free radicals which worsen reperfusion injury in the asphyxiated brain.
- **Viva Question**: "What is the therapeutic window for starting cooling in HIE, and why?" *Answer*: Within 6 hours of birth. This targets the 'latent phase' before the onset of secondary energy failure and irreversible apoptosis.
