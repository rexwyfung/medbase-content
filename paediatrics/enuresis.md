# Nocturnal Enuresis (Bedwetting) (夜間遺尿)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: Involuntary urination during sleep in a child aged 5 years or older, in the absence of any congenital or acquired defect of the central nervous system or urinary tract.
- **Classification**:
  - *Primary*: The child has never been consistently dry at night for a period of 6 months.
  - *Secondary*: The child begins bedwetting after having been completely dry at night for at least 6 months.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Very common in Hong Kong. Affects ~15% of 5-year-olds, ~5% of 10-year-olds, and ~1-2% of 15-year-olds. More common in boys. Spontaneous resolution rate is ~15% per year.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Pathophysiology**: Primary nocturnal enuresis is usually multifactorial, involving three main mechanisms:
  1. **Nocturnal Polyuria**: A lack of the normal physiological surge in Anti-Diuretic Hormone (ADH / Vasopressin) during sleep, leading to excessive urine production at night.
  2. **Detrusor Overactivity / Reduced Bladder Capacity**: The bladder contracts prematurely or cannot hold a normal volume of urine.
  3. **High Arousal Threshold**: The child sleeps very deeply and the brain fails to wake them up in response to a full bladder.
- **Genetics**: Highly heritable. If one parent was a bedwetter, the child has a 40% chance; if both parents were, the chance is 70%.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- The parents bring the child to the clinic because they are frustrated with daily wet sheets, or the child is distressed because they want to attend a school camp or sleepover but are embarrassed.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **The History is the most important part of the assessment**:
  - Frequency of wet nights.
  - Fluid intake habits (e.g., drinking large amounts of fluid or caffeinated drinks right before bed).
  - **Daytime symptoms**: Urgency, frequency, daytime wetting, poor stream. *(Crucial: The presence of daytime symptoms suggests a lower urinary tract dysfunction, NOT simple primary nocturnal enuresis).*
  - **Bowel habits**: Constipation is a massive, often overlooked cause of enuresis (a full rectum compresses the bladder).
  - Snoring/Sleep Apnoea (can cause secondary enuresis).
- **Examination**: Usually completely normal. Examine the abdomen (for palpable bladder or faecal mass), lower back/spine (for sacral dimple/tuft of hair indicating spina bifida occulta), and a brief neurological exam of the lower limbs.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Secondary Enuresis**: Bedwetting that restarts after 6 months of dryness. This is a red flag for an underlying medical or psychological issue. Common causes include: **Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)**, **Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus** (polyuria), **Constipation**, or **Psychological stress** (bullying, parental divorce, new sibling).

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Primary Nocturnal Enuresis (most common).
- Constipation (causing bladder compression/overactivity).
- Urinary Tract Infection (UTI).
- Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus or Diabetes Insipidus.
- Lower Urinary Tract Dysfunction (e.g., overactive bladder).
- Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA).
- Spinal cord pathology (e.g., tethered cord).

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Urinalysis**: Mandatory for ALL children presenting with enuresis.
  - *Dipstick*: Check for glucose (Diabetes Mellitus), specific gravity (Diabetes Insipidus), and leukocytes/nitrites (UTI).
- **Bladder Diary**: Ask parents to record fluid intake, daytime voids, and wet/dry nights for 1-2 weeks.
- **Ultrasound Kidneys, Ureters, Bladder (KUB)**: Not routinely required for simple primary nocturnal enuresis. Indicated if there are daytime symptoms, recurrent UTIs, or suspicion of structural anomalies.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Step 1: Education and Behavioral Modification (First-line)**:
  - Reassure the child and parents that it is not the child's fault and they are not being lazy. **Never punish the child.**
  - Fluid management: Ensure adequate fluid intake during the day, but restrict fluids (especially caffeine and sugary drinks) 2 hours before bedtime.
  - Regular toileting: Ensure the child empties their bladder right before going to sleep.
  - Treat underlying constipation aggressively.
- **Step 2: Enuresis Alarm (The most effective long-term treatment)**:
  - A moisture sensor placed in the underwear that sounds a loud alarm when the child starts to wet.
  - *Mechanism*: Conditions the child's brain to wake up to the sensation of a full bladder.
  - Requires high motivation from the child and parents. Takes 2-3 months to work. High long-term success rate and low relapse rate.
- **Step 3: Pharmacological Therapy (Desmopressin / DDAVP)**:
  - A synthetic analogue of ADH. It reduces overnight urine production.
  - Given as an oral melt (sublingual) at bedtime.
  - *Pros*: Works immediately. Excellent for short-term use (e.g., school camps, sleepovers).
  - *Cons*: High relapse rate when stopped.
  - *Crucial Safety Warning*: **Fluid restriction is mandatory** from 1 hour before to 8 hours after taking Desmopressin to prevent life-threatening water intoxication and hyponatraemic seizures.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Psychological Impact**: Enuresis can cause significant shame, low self-esteem, and social isolation. Empathy and positive reinforcement are essential.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Secondary enuresis in a previously dry child can occasionally be a sign of emotional abuse or, rarely, sexual abuse. Always explore the psychosocial history carefully.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Prognosis**: Excellent. The vast majority of cases resolve spontaneously by adolescence.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "A 7-year-old boy who has been dry at night since age 4 suddenly starts wetting the bed every night for the past month. What is the most important initial investigation?" (A urinalysis to rule out a UTI or Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus. This is secondary enuresis, which requires investigation for an underlying cause).
- *Viva*: "What is the most effective long-term treatment for primary nocturnal enuresis, and how does it work?" (The enuresis alarm. It uses classical conditioning to train the child's brain to wake up to the sensation of a full bladder).
- *Pitfall*: Prescribing Desmopressin without explicitly warning the parents to restrict the child's fluid intake overnight, risking severe hyponatraemia.
