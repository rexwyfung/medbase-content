# Gillick Competence & Fraser Guidelines (吉利克勝任能力與弗雷澤準則)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Context
- **Definition**: Legal and ethical principles used in medical law to decide whether a child (under 18 years of age) is able to consent to their own medical treatment without the need for parental permission or knowledge.
- **Local Context**: In Hong Kong, the age of majority is 18. However, under common law (derived from the UK House of Lords ruling in *Gillick v West Norfolk and Wisbech AHA*), minors can consent to medical treatment if they achieve sufficient understanding and intelligence to fully comprehend what is proposed.

## 2. Core Concepts
- **Gillick Competence**: A general principle applying to *any* medical treatment or intervention. A child is considered "Gillick competent" if they have sufficient maturity, intelligence, and understanding to fully comprehend the nature, implications, risks, and alternatives of the proposed treatment.
- **Fraser Guidelines**: Specifically apply to the provision of **contraception and sexual health advice** to minors without parental consent.

## 3. Assessing Gillick Competence
To determine if a child is Gillick competent, the doctor must assess if the child understands:
1. The nature of the medical condition.
2. The nature of the proposed treatment.
3. The potential risks and side effects of the treatment.
4. The alternatives to the treatment.
5. The consequences of refusing the treatment.
- *Note*: Competence is decision-specific. A 14-year-old may be competent to consent to treatment for a minor infection, but not competent to consent to complex, life-altering surgery.

## 4. The Fraser Guidelines (Specific Criteria)
A doctor can provide contraceptive advice/treatment to a minor without parental consent ONLY IF all the following criteria are met:
1. The young person understands the doctor's advice.
2. The doctor cannot persuade the young person to inform their parents or allow the doctor to inform them.
3. The young person is very likely to begin or continue having sexual intercourse with or without contraceptive treatment.
4. Unless they receive contraceptive advice or treatment, their physical or mental health (or both) is likely to suffer.
5. Their best interests require the doctor to give them contraceptive advice, treatment, or both without parental consent.

## 5. Refusal of Treatment
- **Consent vs. Refusal**: While a Gillick competent child can *consent* to treatment, their *refusal* of life-saving treatment can be overridden by a person with parental responsibility or by the courts (the principle of the "child's best interests" remains paramount).
- *Example*: A 15-year-old Jehovah's Witness who is Gillick competent refuses a life-saving blood transfusion. In HK/UK law, the parents or the court can override this refusal to save the child's life.

## 6. Confidentiality
- A Gillick competent child has the same right to medical confidentiality as an adult.
- A doctor cannot disclose medical information to the parents without the child's consent, **UNLESS** there is a significant risk of serious harm to the child or others (e.g., suspected child abuse, statutory rape, suicidal ideation). In these cases, safeguarding procedures override confidentiality.

## 7. Practical Application in Hong Kong
- **Age of Consent for Sexual Activity**: In Hong Kong, the legal age of consent for sexual intercourse is **16**.
- If a doctor sees a patient under 16 seeking contraception, they must assess Fraser guidelines. However, they must also consider the legal implications of underage sex. If there is suspicion of abuse, exploitation, or non-consensual activity, it becomes a child protection issue, and the Medical Social Worker (MSW) and Police must be involved, breaching confidentiality.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "A 15-year-old girl requests the oral contraceptive pill. She explicitly asks you not to tell her parents. Under what guidelines can you prescribe this, and what are the criteria?" (The Fraser Guidelines. You must assess if she understands the advice, cannot be persuaded to tell her parents, is likely to have sex anyway, her physical/mental health will suffer without it, and it is in her best interests).
- *Viva*: "A 14-year-old boy is deemed Gillick competent. He has acute appendicitis but refuses surgery because he is scared of scars. Can you operate?" (Yes. While he is competent to consent, a competent minor's refusal of life-saving or necessary treatment can be overridden by the parents or the courts in the child's best interests).
- *Pitfall*: Assuming that because a child is under 18, parents must always be informed about everything. A Gillick competent child has a right to confidentiality, provided there are no safeguarding concerns.
