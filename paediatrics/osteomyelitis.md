# Acute Haematogenous Osteomyelitis (急性血源性骨髓炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: An acute bacterial infection of the bone and bone marrow. In children, it is almost always "haematogenous," meaning the bacteria reach the bone via the bloodstream.
- **Local Epidemiology**: A relatively common and serious paediatric infection in Hong Kong. Peak incidence is in children under 5 years of age. More common in boys.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Pathogenesis**: 
  - A transient asymptomatic bacteraemia (e.g., from a minor skin abrasion, URTI, or dental issue) occurs.
  - Bacteria lodge in the **metaphysis** of long bones (most commonly the distal femur, proximal tibia, or proximal humerus).
  - *Why the metaphysis?* The metaphyseal capillary loops have sluggish, turbulent blood flow and lack phagocytic cells, making it an ideal environment for bacteria to settle and multiply.
  - Multiplication causes a localized purulent exudate (pus). Because bone is a rigid structure, the pus increases intraosseous pressure, compressing blood vessels and causing bone necrosis.
  - The pus can track outward through the Haversian canals, lifting the periosteum and forming a **subperiosteal abscess**.
- **Causative Organisms**:
  - **Staphylococcus aureus**: The most common organism across all age groups (>80% of cases).
  - **Group A Streptococcus (S. pyogenes)**.
  - *Neonates*: Group B Streptococcus, E. coli.
  - *Sickle Cell Disease*: Salmonella species (a classic board question, though rare in HK).
  - *Puncture wounds to the foot (e.g., stepping on a nail through a sneaker)*: Pseudomonas aeruginosa.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- A child presents with a relatively short history (days) of fever, severe localized bone pain, and refusal to use the affected limb.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Pain**: Severe, constant, and localized to the metaphysis of a long bone.
- **Pseudoparalysis**: The child refuses to move the limb or bear weight due to extreme pain.
- **Systemic Signs**: High fever, rigors, lethargy, toxic appearance.
- **Local Signs**: 
  - **Exquisite point tenderness** over the affected bone (the most reliable clinical sign).
  - Swelling, erythema, and warmth may develop later as the infection breaches the cortex and forms a subperiosteal abscess.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Septic Arthritis**: The infection is *inside* the joint space. Any movement of the joint is excruciatingly painful. In osteomyelitis, the infection is in the bone (metaphysis); gentle movement of the adjacent joint may be tolerated, but palpating the bone itself is extremely painful. *Note: In neonates, the blood vessels cross the growth plate, so osteomyelitis frequently spreads into the joint, causing concurrent septic arthritis.*
- **Bone Tumours (e.g., Ewing Sarcoma, Osteosarcoma)**: Present with bone pain and swelling, but usually have a more insidious (slower) onset over weeks/months, and the child is usually afebrile initially.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Septic Arthritis.
- Cellulitis (superficial skin infection).
- Transient Synovitis (if near the hip).
- Bone tumours (Ewing sarcoma, Osteosarcoma, Leukaemia).
- Trauma / Occult fracture.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bloods**:
  - FBC (Leukocytosis with a left shift).
  - **CRP and ESR**: Markedly elevated. CRP is crucial for monitoring the response to treatment (it falls rapidly with effective antibiotics).
  - **Blood Cultures**: Mandatory BEFORE starting antibiotics. Positive in ~50% of cases.
- **Imaging**:
  - **X-ray**: The initial test, but **usually NORMAL for the first 10-14 days** of the infection. Late signs include periosteal reaction (new bone formation) and osteopenia/bone destruction. X-rays are mainly done to rule out fractures or tumours.
  - **MRI with Gadolinium**: **The gold standard and most sensitive imaging modality**. Can detect bone marrow oedema and subperiosteal abscesses within 24-48 hours of onset.
  - **Ultrasound**: Useful for detecting a subperiosteal abscess or concurrent joint effusion.
- **Bone Aspirate/Biopsy**: If blood cultures are negative and the child is not responding to empirical antibiotics, an orthopaedic surgeon may aspirate the bone to obtain pus for culture.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Intravenous Antibiotics**: The cornerstone of treatment. Must be started empirically immediately after blood cultures are drawn.
  - *Empirical choice (HA guidelines)*: High-dose **IV Cloxacillin** (or Flucloxacillin) to cover MSSA, or **IV Cefazolin**.
  - *If MRSA is suspected (e.g., critically ill, known carrier)*: IV Vancomycin or Clindamycin.
  - *Duration*: Prolonged course. Usually 1-2 weeks of IV antibiotics, followed by a switch to high-dose oral antibiotics (e.g., Cephalexin) once the child is afebrile and the CRP has significantly dropped. Total duration is typically **4 to 6 weeks**.
- **Surgical Management**:
  - Indicated if there is a **subperiosteal abscess** (requires incision and drainage), if the child fails to respond to IV antibiotics within 48-72 hours, or if there is concurrent septic arthritis.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Paediatric Orthopaedics**: Essential for surgical decision-making and long-term follow-up.
- **Infectious Diseases**: For advice on complex cases or resistant organisms (MRSA).

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- **Chronic Osteomyelitis**: If treatment is delayed or inadequate, pieces of dead bone (sequestrum) form, surrounded by new bone (involucrum). This leads to chronic, discharging sinuses and requires extensive surgical debridement.
- **Growth Arrest**: If the infection damages the physis (growth plate), it can cause limb length discrepancy or angular deformities.
- **Pathological Fracture**: The weakened bone may fracture under normal stress.
- **Prognosis**: Excellent if diagnosed early and treated with a full course of appropriate antibiotics.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- *Viva*: "A 4-year-old presents with high fever and severe pain in the distal femur. An X-ray of the femur is completely normal. Does this rule out osteomyelitis?" (Absolutely not. X-rays are typically normal for the first 10-14 days of acute osteomyelitis. MRI is the gold standard for early diagnosis).
- *Viva*: "What is the most common causative organism for acute haematogenous osteomyelitis in a healthy child?" (Staphylococcus aureus).
- *Pitfall*: Stopping antibiotics too early (e.g., after 1 week when the child feels better). Osteomyelitis requires a prolonged 4-6 week course to eradicate the bacteria from the relatively avascular bone tissue and prevent chronic osteomyelitis.
