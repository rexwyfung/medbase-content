# COVID-19 in Children (兒童 2019 冠狀病毒病)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition of Pediatric COVID-19
**SARS-CoV-2** infection in the pediatric population. Usually asymptomatic or mild, but can present with severe acute complications or post-infectious hyperinflammatory syndromes.

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
Massive spikes during HK's 5th wave (Omicron BA.2) in early 2022. Revealed high hospitalization rates for **COVID-induced croup (哮吼)** and **febrile seizures (熱性痙攣)**. Overall mortality is very low, but sheer volume of cases → significant pediatric morbidity & PICU admissions.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Viral Entry Mechanism
SARS-CoV-2 binds to **ACE2 receptors**. Pediatric upper airways have ↓ ACE2 density compared to adults → partially explains generally milder initial disease.

### Variant-Specific Pathophysiology
**Omicron variants** demonstrate ↑ tropism for upper airway & glottic machinery → surge in acute viral croup (subglottic edema).

### Post-Infectious Hyperinflammation Pathophysiology
**PIMS-TS (Pediatric Inflammatory Multisystem Syndrome - Temporally Associated with SARS-CoV-2) / MIS-C (兒童多系統發炎綜合症)**: Occurs 2-6 weeks post-infection. Dysregulated T-cell & macrophage activation → massive cytokine storm (↑ IL-6, ↑ TNF-α) → endothelial dysfunction, capillary leak, & severe myocardial depression.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Mild to Moderate Clinical Presentation
* Often asymptomatic.
* URTI symptoms: Fever (發燒), cough, sore throat, rhinitis.
* GI symptoms (common in kids): Vomiting, diarrhea.
* Anosmia/Ageusia (less reliably reported in toddlers).

### Severe Acute Clinical Presentation
* **Acute COVID Croup**: "Barking" cough, inspiratory stridor, chest indrawing (sternal/subcostal recession).
* **COVID Encephalopathy**: Seizures out of proportion to fever grade, altered mental status, lethargy.
* **Pneumonia**: Tachypnea, hypoxia (SpO2 <92%), grunting.

### PIMS-TS / MIS-C Clinical Presentation
* Persistent high fever (>3 days).
* Kawasaki-like mucocutaneous signs: Non-purulent conjunctivitis, polymorphous rash, red/cracked lips, strawberry tongue.
* Severe GI pain (can mimic appendicitis).
* Shock & Myocardial dysfunction (tachycardia, hypotension, cool peripheries).

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Differentiating Acute Viral Pathogens
Acute COVID-19 is clinically indistinguishable from Influenza A/B, RSV, or Parainfluenza without molecular testing. **Definitive diagnosis requires +ve Antigen/PCR.**

### Differentiating PIMS-TS vs Kawasaki Disease (KD)
* **Age**: PIMS-TS typically affects older children/adolescents; KD mostly <5 years.
* **GI Symptoms**: Severe abdominal pain/diarrhea prominent in PIMS-TS; less common in KD.
* **Lab Markers**: PIMS-TS features profound lymphopenia, massive ↑↑ Ferritin, ↑↑↑ CRP, & severe ↑ Troponin/NT-proBNP compared to classic KD.
* **Cardiac**: PIMS-TS → predominantly global left ventricular (LV) dysfunction/shock; KD → predominantly coronary artery aneurysms (though overlap exists).

## 5. Differential Diagnosis
### Acute Respiratory & Hyperinflammatory Differentials
| Differential Diagnosis | Key Distinguishing Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Influenza A/B (甲型/乙型流感)** | Sudden onset high fever, severe myalgia, headache. -ve SARS-CoV-2 PCR. |
| **RSV Bronchiolitis (呼吸道合胞病毒細支氣管炎)** | Usually <2 years. Prominent wheeze, hyperinflated chest, fine crackles. |
| **Parainfluenza Virus** | Classic cause of viral croup. Indistinguishable without multiplex PCR. |
| **Adenovirus (腺病毒)** | Prolonged fever, purulent conjunctivitis, pharyngitis, +/- diarrhea. |
| **Kawasaki Disease (川崎病)** | Age <5, prominent coronary artery aneurysms early, less profound shock/GI symptoms vs PIMS-TS. |

## 6. Tiered Investigations
### Bedside & Point-of-Care Testing
* **Rapid Antigen Test (RAT)**: Fast, lower sensitivity.
* **ECG**: Evaluate for arrhythmias or conduction blocks if myocarditis/PIMS-TS suspected.

### Diagnostic Laboratory Investigations
* **Gold Standard**: Nasopharyngeal/throat/saliva **Multiplex PCR** (SARS-CoV-2 + Respiratory panel).

### Blood Investigations (For Moderate/Severe Disease or PIMS-TS)
* **FBC**: Leukopenia, prominent **lymphopenia**, thrombocytopenia (in severe disease).
* **Inflammatory Markers**: ↑ CRP, ↑ ESR, ↑ Procalcitonin, ↑↑ Ferritin (hallmark of PIMS-TS).
* **Organ Function**: U&E (AKI), LFT (↑ ALT/AST).
* **Cardiac Biomarkers**: ↑ Troponin, ↑ NT-proBNP (myocarditis / PIMS-TS).
* **Coagulation Profile**: ↑ D-Dimer, ↑ Fibrinogen, ↑ PT/APTT (DIC risk).

### Imaging & Special Investigations
* **CXR**: Bilateral patchy infiltrates or consolidation (pneumonia).
* **Echocardiogram (心臟超聲波)**: Mandatory if PIMS-TS suspected → evaluate LV ejection fraction, coronary artery dilation/aneurysms, pericardial effusion.

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Mild Disease Management
* Home isolation (or community isolation facility per prevailing DH protocol).
* Supportive care: Antipyretics (Paracetamol, Ibuprofen), adequate hydration.

### Acute COVID Croup Management
* **1st Line**: Oral **Dexamethasone** (0.15 mg/kg).
* **Severe (Stridor at rest/Recession)**: Nebulized **Adrenaline (Epinephrine)** (1:1000, 0.5 mL/kg, max 5mL) + continuous SpO2 monitoring. 

### Severe COVID Pneumonia Management
* **Oxygen Therapy**: Nasal cannula, High-Flow Nasal Cannula (HFNC), or mechanical ventilation if respiratory failure.
* **Corticosteroids**: IV Dexamethasone for requiring supplemental O2.
* **Antivirals**: **Remdesivir** (IV) indicated for severe disease OR early in mild/moderate disease for high-risk groups (e.g., immunosuppressed, severe neurodisability) per HA guidelines.

### PIMS-TS / MIS-C Protocol
* MDT approach (PICU + Cardiology + Rheumatology).
* **1st Line Immunomodulation**: **IVIG** (2 g/kg) + **IV Methylprednisolone**.
* **Refractory Disease**: Biologics (e.g., Anakinra or Infliximab - IL-1 inhibitors).
* **Antithrombotic**: Low-dose Aspirin (prevent coronary thrombosis).

### Preventive Vaccination Strategy
* HK Pediatric Societies strongly advocate COVID-19 vaccination (mRNA or inactivated virus) for all eligible children >6 months.
* **Rationale**: Prevents severe acute disease & drastically ↓ risk of developing PIMS-TS.

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Multidisciplinary Team Involvement
* **Clinical Psychologists / MSW**: Critical for addressing parental anxiety, bereavement, and trauma from severe acute admissions.
* **Physiotherapy / Occupational Therapy**: Essential for "Long COVID" rehabilitation (chronic fatigue, post-exertional malaise).

### Community & Educational Impact
* Address severe psychosocial impact of prolonged school closures & quarantine mandates (developmental delay, social isolation, loss of routine).

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Lockdown-Associated Safeguarding Risks
* Severe social isolation during pandemic peaks → documented spikes in undetected **Non-Accidental Injury (NAI)**.
* Lack of school visibility → delayed reporting of neglect or physical abuse.
* Significant deterioration in adolescent mental health → monitor for ↑ self-harm, eating disorders, and depression.

## 10. Complications & Prognosis
### Acute Complications
* Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS).
* Viral Encephalitis / Status Epilepticus.
* Severe airway obstruction (Croup) requiring PICU intubation.
* Myocarditis.

### Post-Acute & Long-Term Complications
* **PIMS-TS / MIS-C**: Life-threatening hyperinflammatory shock developing 2-6 weeks post-infection.
* **Long COVID (長新冠)**: Chronic fatigue, "brain fog", persistent anosmia, sleep disturbances.

### Overall Prognosis
* Excellent for previously healthy children; acute mortality is exceptionally low.
* PIMS-TS has a high morbidity and requires PICU, but mortality remains low with prompt IVIG/steroid therapy.

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Trap: Assuming COVID doesn't cause severe acute disease in kids. Pearl: During the Omicron wave, the incidence of severe, ICU-level croup and acute febrile encephalopathy skyrocketed in the pediatric population.
* Viva Question: A mother asks if her child should get the COVID vaccine, seeing as the acute infection is usually mild. What is your strongest medical argument for vaccination? Answer: While acute disease is generally mild, the vaccine is highly effective at preventing the deadly post-infectious complication, PIMS-TS, which can strike previously completely healthy children weeks after a mild or silent infection.
* Pitfall: Discharging a child with a post-COVID prolonged fever without checking cardiac markers. Always consider PIMS-TS in any child presenting with unremitting fever and GI symptoms 2-6 weeks after a COVID infection, and urgently check CRP, Ferritin, Troponin, and ECG/Echo.
