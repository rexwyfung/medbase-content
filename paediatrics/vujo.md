# Vesicoureteric Junction Obstruction (膀胱輸尿管交界處阻塞)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Definition of VUJO
* Obstruction to urine flow at distal ureter where it enters bladder → primary obstructive megaureter (POM).
* Characterized by ureteric dilatation (>7mm) + varying degrees of hydronephrosis (腎積水).

### Hong Kong Epidemiology
* Incidence: ~1 in 10,000 live births.
* Gender: Male > Female (4:1).
* Laterality: Left > Right (3:1). Bilateral in ~20%.
* Detection: Mostly picked up incidentally on antenatal maternal USG routinely offered in HA Maternal & Child Health Centres (MCHC).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics

### Pathogenesis of Megaureter
* Distal aperistaltic ureteral segment (juxtavesical portion).
* Pathological basis: ↑ collagen to smooth muscle ratio → failure of peristaltic wave transmission → functional obstruction → proximal ureteric dilatation & hypertrophy → backpressure to kidney.

### Genetic & Embryological Associations
* Sporadic occurrence (usually).
* Occurs due to delayed recanalization of the distal ureter during embryogenesis.
* Associations:
  * Contralateral renal agenesis.
  * Multicystic dysplastic kidney (MCDK).
  * Horseshoe kidney.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Antenatal Presentation
* Incidental finding of fetal hydroureteronephrosis on 2nd/3rd-trimester morphology USG.

### Postnatal Clinical Symptoms
* **Asymptomatic** (Most common in modern HA setting due to USG screening).
* **Febrile UTI** (尿道感染): Most common symptomatic presentation.
* **Failure to Thrive** (生長遲緩): Secondary to recurrent infections or chronic kidney disease.
* **Haematuria** (血尿): Microscopic or macroscopic.
* **Abdominal Pain**: Older children, localized to flank/umbilicus.

### Physical Signs
* Usually nil.
* Palpable abdominal/flank mass (ballotable kidney) if extreme hydronephrosis.
* Hypertension (高血壓) (Rare, check BP).

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)

### VUJO vs Pelviureteric Junction Obstruction (PUJO)
* **PUJO**: ↑ Renal pelvis dilatation, but ureter calibre is strictly normal (not visualized on USG).
* **VUJO**: Hydronephrosis + explicitly dilated ureter (megaureter) traced down to pelvis.

### VUJO vs Vesicoureteral Reflux (VUR)
* **VUJO**: Distal obstruction. MCUG shows no reflux. 
* **VUR (膀胱輸尿管返流)**: Incompetent VUJ. MCUG shows +ve retrograde urine flow. (Note: Rare coexistence exists → "refluxing obstructed megaureter").

## 5. Differential Diagnosis

### Differentials for Neonatal Hydroureteronephrosis
| Differential Diagnosis | Key Distinguishing Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Vesicoureteral Reflux (VUR)** | +ve reflux on MCUG, dynamic dilatation. |
| **Posterior Urethral Valves (PUV)** | Males only. Bilateral hydro, thickened bladder wall, "keyhole sign" on USG. Oligohydramnios. |
| **Neurogenic Bladder** | Spina bifida/sacral agenesis. Patulous anus, lower limb neuro signs. |
| **Ureterocele/Ectopic Ureter** | Duplex system. Cystic intravesical structure on USG. |

## 6. Tiered Investigations

### 1st Line Postnatal Screening
* **USG KUB (Kidney, Ureter, Bladder)**:
  * Timing: Postpone until Day 3-7 of life to avoid false -ve from neonatal oliguria.
  * Findings: Megaureter (>7mm), hydronephrosis, normal bladder wall.

### Functional Diagnostic Imaging
* **MAG3 Diuretic Renogram (MAG3 腎圖)**:
  * Gold standard for obstruction.
  * Wait until >4-6 weeks of age (immature neonatal tubular function → false results).
  * Defines Differential Renal Function (DRF) (Normal 45-55%).
  * Defines excretion curve (Obstructive: rising curve despite furosemide).

### Rule-Out Investigation
* **MCUG (Micturating Cystourethrogram)**:
  * Mandatory to exclude VUR and PUV.
  * Requires prophylactic Abx cover (e.g., Trimethoprim) 3 days around procedure.

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)

### Acute Infection Management
* **Febrile UTI**: Admit for IV antibiotics (HA empirical: IV Cefuroxime + Amikacin if <6 months or septic; adjust to culture sensitivities).

### Conservative HA Protocol
* 70-80% of primary VUJO resolves spontaneously by 1-2 years as ureter matures.
* **Prophylactic Antibiotics**:
  * Indicated for severe dilatation to prevent UTI until resolution or surgery.
  * PO Trimethoprim (2mg/kg) OR Cefalexin (12.5mg/kg) OD at night (晚間一次).
* **Serial Surveillance**:
  * USG KUB every 3-6 months.
  * Repeat MAG3 if USG shows worsening hydronephrosis.

### Surgical Management
* **Indications for Surgery**:
  * ↓ DRF to <40% or falling by >5% on serial MAG3.
  * Recurrent breakthrough febrile UTIs despite Abx compliance.
  * Massive/worsening hydroureteronephrosis causing symptoms.
* **Surgical Options**:
  * **Ureteric Reimplantation (輸尿管重植術)**: Excision of aperistaltic segment + reimplantation into bladder (Cohen cross-trigonal or Leadbetter-Politano). ± Ureteric tapering or tailoring if megaureter is massively dilated.
  * **Endoscopic High-Pressure Balloon Dilatation**: Minimally invasive alternative, increasing HA popularity.
  * **Temporary Cutaneous Ureterostomy**: If infant too small/sick for definitive reimplantation.

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care

### Multidisciplinary Approach
* **Paediatric Urology**: Surgical decision making.
* **Paediatric Nephrology**: BP monitoring, CKD management if bilateral involvement.
* **Specialist Nurses**: Catheter care (if temporary stoma/catheter), voiding advice.

### Parental Education
* Reassurance regarding high spontaneous resolution rates.
* Education on strict adherence to prophylactic Abx.
* Red flag counseling: Return to AED immediately for fever >38°C without clear focus.

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags

### Neglect Indicators
* Medical neglect is the primary concern here.
* Non-compliance with HA follow-up imaging leading to preventable loss of renal parenchyma.
* Failure to administer prophylactic antibiotics leading to recurrent renal scarring (UTIs).

## 10. Complications & Prognosis

### Complications
* **Renal Scarring (腎疤痕)**: Consequence of associated UTI + stasis.
* **Hypertension (高血壓)**: RAAS activation from focal renal ischemia.
* **Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)**: Rare unless bilateral or solitary kidney.

### Prognosis
* Spontaneous resolution common (70-80%) within first 2 years of life.
* Excellent long-term renal function if managed effectively and UTIs prevented. Post-surgical success rate >90%.

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: When should the first postnatal USG be performed for antenatally detected hydronephrosis? Answer: Day 3-7 of life. Doing it on Day 1 risks a false negative due to physiological neonatal oliguria and relative dehydration.
* Viva Question: What is the classic finding of VUJO on a MAG3 renogram? Answer: Preserved differential renal function with a continuously rising curve that fails to drop by 50% within 20 minutes after furosemide injection.
* Trap: Assuming a dilated ureter on USG means vesicoureteral reflux (VUR). Pearl: A dilated ureter can be VUR or VUJO; an MCUG is mandatory to differentiate, as VUJO will show no reflux while VUR will be positive.
* Pitfall: Performing a MAG3 renogram in the first week of life. The neonatal kidney has immature tubular function and poor response to diuretics, rendering early MAG3 results inaccurate and uninterpretable. Delay until at least 4-6 weeks of age.
