# Beckwith-Wiedemann Syndrome (貝克威斯-威德曼綜合症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: The most common genetic overgrowth and cancer predisposition disorder.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Approx 1 in 10,000 births.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Highly complex genetics involving the dysregulation of parentally imprinted genes at chromosome **11p15.5**.
- Involves insulin-like growth factor 2 (IGF-2) overexpression or CDKN1C (a growth inhibitor) underexpression. This massive growth drive drives both somatic overgrowth and tumor formation.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Frequently noted on antenatal ultrasound: polyhydramnios, macrosomia, and abdominal wall defects.
- Postnatal presentation: Large, floppy babies with hypoglycaemia.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Diagnostic triad components**: Exomphalos (or umbilical hernia), Macroglossia (massive tongue frequently protruding), Gigantism/Macrosomia.
- Ear creases and ear pits.
- Hemihypertrophy (one side of the body grows larger than the other - strongly correlated with cancer risk).
- Visceromegaly (large liver/kidneys).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- Simpson-Golabi-Behmel starts with overgrowth but has distinctive coarse facies and skeletal anomalies.
- Diabetic embryopathy (Infant of Diabetic Mother) causes macrosomia and hypoglycaemia, but without the cardinal macroglossia / omphalocele.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Infant of a Diabetic Mother (IDM)
- Congenital Hypothyroidism (can cause macroglossia)
- Sotos Syndrome
- Simpson-Golabi-Behmel Syndrome

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside/Bloods**: Urgent CBG/blood glucose in the neonatal period (recurrent profound hypoglycaemia due to hyperinsulinism).
- **Genetic (Definitive)**: Methylation studies and microarray covering chromosome 11p15.
- **Imaging**: Baseline abdominal ultrasound for organs.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute (Neonatal)**: Treat hyperinsulinemic hypoglycaemia aggressively. May require IV dextrose >10%, Diazoxide, or sometimes partial pancreatectomy to prevent irreversible neurological damage.
- **Chronic**: Surgical repair of exomphalos. Tongue reduction surgery if macroglossia obstructs airway or prevents speech.
- **Tumor Surveillance (HA Protocol)**: **Abdominal ultrasound every 3 months until age 8** (to explicitly screen for Wilms tumor and Hepatoblastoma). Alpha-fetoprotein (AFP) screening is sometimes used for hepatoblastoma.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Continuous linkage with Paediatric Oncology, Endocrinology (hyperinsulinism), and Craniofacial teams.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Ensure absolute adherence to the tumor screening protocol. Any parental non-compliance to US screening requires MSW and potentially SWD intervention.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Embryonal tumors (Wilms, hepatoblastoma, neuroblastoma).
- Brain damage secondary to uncontrolled neonatal hypoglycaemia.
- Growth rate generally normalizes by age 8.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Forgetting to look at the ears in a large baby. Always check for ear creases/pits if you see macroglossia.
- **Viva Q**: "Why do we stop tumor screening at age 8?" Answer: The risk of these specific embryonal tumors drops to the general population rate after age 8 as explosive embryonic-type cell division ceases.
