
# Acute Bronchiolitis (急性細支氣管炎)
## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A viral lower respiratory tract infection causing inflammation, oedema, and necrosis of the epithelial cells lining the small airways (bronchioles), leading to mucus production and bronchospasm.
- **Local Epidemiology**: The most common lower respiratory tract infection in infants < 1 year old. Peak incidence is between 3-6 months. In HK, RSV is the predominant cause, with peaks in spring and summer.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- **Pathogen**: **Respiratory Syncytial Virus (RSV)** causes >80% of cases. Others include Rhinovirus, Parainfluenza, Adenovirus, Human Metapneumovirus.
- **Mechanism**: Viral infection of the bronchiolar epithelium causes cell death, sloughing, and copious mucus production. This debris plugs the tiny airways of infants.
- **Air trapping**: The obstruction acts as a ball-valve, allowing air in during inspiration but trapping it during expiration, leading to hyperinflation.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- See below.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- **Prodrome**: 2-3 days of coryza (runny nose), mild fever, and cough.
- **Progression**: Develops tachypnoea, increased work of breathing, and poor feeding.
- **Auscultation**:
  - **Widespread fine inspiratory crackles** (the hallmark sign).
  - High-pitched expiratory wheeze.
  - Prolonged expiratory phase.
- **Inspection**: Hyperinflated chest, subcostal/intercostal recessions, tracheal tug.
- **Apnoea**: A critical presentation, especially in premature infants or those < 2 months old. Can occur even without significant respiratory distress.
## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Viral-induced Wheeze / Asthma**: Bronchiolitis is typically the *first* episode of wheeze/crackles in an infant < 1 year. Asthma/viral wheeze usually presents in older toddlers with recurrent episodes and responds well to Salbutamol. Bronchiolitis does NOT respond to Salbutamol.
- **Pneumonia**: Usually presents with high fever, focal crackles, and consolidation, rather than widespread crackles and hyperinflation.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- **Most Likely**:
  - RSV Bronchiolitis.
- **Must-Not-Miss**:
  - **Apnoea**: High risk in ex-premature infants.
  - **Congenital Heart Disease**: Heart failure can present with tachypnoea, poor feeding, and crackles (pulmonary oedema). Look for a murmur, hepatomegaly, and absent femoral pulses.

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Bedside**:
  - **Clinical Diagnosis**: Bronchiolitis is a clinical diagnosis.
  - **Pulse Oximetry**: Essential to assess severity and need for admission.
  - **Nasopharyngeal Aspirate (NPA)**: For RSV rapid antigen test or respiratory multiplex PCR. Useful for cohorting patients in the ward to prevent cross-infection.
- **Gold Standard**:
  - **Chest X-ray (CXR)**: NOT routinely recommended. Often shows hyperinflation (flattened diaphragms, >6 anterior ribs visible), peribronchial cuffing, and patchy atelectasis (which can be misdiagnosed as pneumonia). Only do CXR if atypical presentation, severe disease, or suspecting complications (e.g., pneumothorax).
- **Monitoring**:
  - Continuous SpO2 monitoring if on oxygen.
  - Strict input/output monitoring (weigh nappies) as poor feeding is a major reason for admission.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Acute**:
  - **Oxygen**: If SpO2 < 92%. Often delivered via nasal cannulae.
  - **High-Flow Nasal Cannula (HFNC) / CPAP**: For severe respiratory distress or recurrent apnoeas. Provides positive end-expiratory pressure (PEEP) to stent open the collapsed bronchioles.
- **Chronic/Definitive**:
  - **Supportive Care is the ONLY proven treatment**.
  - **Hydration**: If unable to take >50% of normal feeds, provide IV fluids or Nasogastric (NG) tube feeding. (NG feeding is often preferred in HK HA hospitals as it is less invasive and doesn't require an IV line).
  - **What NOT to do (Ineffective therapies)**:
    - **NO Salbutamol**: Beta-agonists do not work because the obstruction is due to mucus and cellular debris, not smooth muscle bronchospasm.
    - **NO Corticosteroids**: Neither oral nor inhaled steroids change the course of the disease.
    - **NO Antibiotics**: It is a viral infection.
## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- **Prevention**: **Palivizumab** (Synagis) - a monoclonal antibody against RSV. Given as monthly IM injections during RSV season. Very expensive. *HA Indications*: Restricted to high-risk infants (e.g., extreme prematurity < 28 weeks, haemodynamically significant congenital heart disease, chronic lung disease of prematurity).
## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- Reassure parents that the cough can last for several weeks.
- Advise on smoking cessation (passive smoking worsens the severity).

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Generally self-limiting. Peak severity is usually around day 3-5 of the illness.
- **Complications**: Apnoea, secondary bacterial pneumonia, dehydration, respiratory failure requiring intubation.
- **Long-term**: Increased risk of recurrent viral-induced wheeze in early childhood, though the link to long-term atopic asthma is debated.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Prescribing Salbutamol nebulisers and oral steroids for a 4-month-old with bronchiolitis. *Pearl*: This is a classic OSCE trap. Bronchiolitis management is purely supportive (Oxygen and Fluids). Bronchodilators and steroids are ineffective and not recommended by guidelines.
- **Viva Question**: "Why might an infant with bronchiolitis have a palpable liver edge?" *Answer*: The lungs are hyperinflated, which pushes the diaphragm down, displacing a normal-sized liver downwards, making it palpable. It does not necessarily mean they have heart failure.
