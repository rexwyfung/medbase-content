# Acute Bronchiolitis (急性細支氣管炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Definition of Acute Bronchiolitis
* Viral lower respiratory tract infection (LRTI).
* Causes inflammation, oedema & necrosis of bronchiolar epithelial cells → ↑ mucus production & bronchospasm → airway obstruction.

### Local Epidemiology & Seasonality
* Most common LRTI in infants < 1 year old. Peak incidence: 1-9 months (especially 3-6 months).
* Hospital admission required for 2-3% of all infants.
* HA Context: **Respiratory Syncytial Virus (RSV) (呼吸道合胞病毒)** predominates.
* Peaks in **Spring & Summer** in Hong Kong (unlike Winter peaks in Western countries).

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Causative Pathogens
* **RSV** causes >80% of cases.
* Others: Rhinovirus, Parainfluenza, Adenovirus, Influenza, Human Metapneumovirus (hMPV).
* Co-infection (e.g., RSV + hMPV) → ↑ severity of illness.

### Mechanism of Airway Obstruction
* Viral infection of bronchiolar epithelium → cell death, sloughing & copious mucus production.
* Debris plugs tiny airways of infants.
* **Ball-valve mechanism**: Air enters during inspiration but is trapped during expiration → **Hyperinflation (肺過度充氣)**.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Typical Clinical Progression
* **Prodrome**: 2-3 days of **coryza (鼻水)**, mild fever & cough.
* **Progression**: Develops tachypnoea, ↑ work of breathing (WOB), dry/wheezy cough & poor feeding.
* **Peak severity**: Usually around day 3-5 of illness.

### Key Clinical Signs on Examination
* **Auscultation**: Widespread **fine end-inspiratory crackles (微細吸氣末期喀泡音)** (hallmark) & prolonged expiratory wheeze.
* **Inspection**: Hyperinflated chest (prominent sternum), subcostal/intercostal recessions, tracheal tug.
* **Vitals**: Tachypnoea (>60/min), tachycardia, potential hypoxia (↓ SpO2).

### High-Risk Presentations
* **Apnoea (呼吸暫停)**: Critical presentation, especially in ex-premature infants or those < 2 months old. Can occur without significant respiratory distress.
* Cyanosis or pallor (impending respiratory failure).

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### Bronchiolitis vs. Viral-induced Wheeze / Asthma
| Feature | Acute Bronchiolitis | Viral-induced Wheeze / Asthma |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Age** | Usually < 1 year (first episode) | Older toddlers / children |
| **Auscultation** | **Crackles** + wheeze | Mostly **wheeze** |
| **Pathophysiology**| Mucus/cellular debris plugging | Smooth muscle bronchospasm |
| **Response to Salbutamol**| -ve (No response) | +ve (Good response) |

### Bronchiolitis vs. Pneumonia
* **Pneumonia**: High fever (>38.5°C), *focal* crackles, bronchial breathing, dull percussion.
* **Bronchiolitis**: Low-grade fever, *widespread* crackles, hyperresonant percussion.

## 5. Differential Diagnosis
### Most Likely Diagnoses
* **RSV Bronchiolitis**

### Must-Not-Miss Diagnoses
* **Pneumonia (肺炎)**: Bacterial superinfection can occur.
* **Congenital Heart Disease (CHD) (先天性心臟病)**: Heart failure → tachypnoea, poor feeding, pulmonary oedema (focal crackles), hepatomegaly (liver displaced downwards in bronchiolitis vs true ↑ size in heart failure). Look for murmurs, absent femoral pulses.
* **Pertussis (Whooping Cough) (百日咳)**: Paroxysmal cough, ↑ risk of apnoea in infants.
* **Foreign Body Aspiration (異物吸入)**: Sudden onset, unilateral wheeze/reduced air entry.

## 6. Tiered Investigations
### Bedside Investigations
* **Clinical Diagnosis**: Primarily based on history & examination.
* **Pulse Oximetry (SpO2)**: Essential to assess hypoxia & admission need.
* **Nasopharyngeal Aspirate (NPA)**: For RSV rapid antigen test or multiplex PCR. Critical in HA hospitals for ward cohorting to prevent nosocomial cross-infection.

### Blood Tests & Imaging
* Routine bloods & CXR are **NOT** recommended for typical cases.
* **Capillary Blood Gas (CBG)**: If suspecting respiratory failure (hypercapnia/acidosis).
* **Chest X-ray (CXR)**: Only if atypical presentation, severe disease, or suspecting complications (e.g., pneumothorax). Typical findings: hyperinflation (flattened diaphragm, >6 anterior ribs visible), peribronchial cuffing, patchy atelectasis.

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Hospital Admission Criteria
* Apnoea (observed or reported).
* Persistent SpO2 < 92% in room air.
* Inadequate oral intake (<70% of normal volume) / clinical dehydration.
* Severe respiratory distress: Grunting, severe recession, RR > 70/min.

### Acute Supportive Management
* **Oxygen Therapy**: Humidified O2 via nasal cannulae/head box if SpO2 < 92%.
* **Hydration**: IV fluids or Nasogastric (NG) tube feeding if poor oral intake. (NG feeding preferred in HK HA hospitals; less invasive & preserves IV access).
* **Advanced Respiratory Support**: Heated, Humidified High-Flow Nasal Cannula (HHFNCO) or CPAP for severe distress/hypoxia unresponsive to standard O2, providing PEEP to stent open collapsed bronchioles. Intubation if impending exhaustion.

### Ineffective Therapies (Contraindicated)
* **NO Salbutamol/Ipratropium**: Bronchodilators ineffective (obstruction is mucus/cellular debris, not smooth muscle bronchospasm).
* **NO Corticosteroids**: Oral/inhaled steroids do not change disease course.
* **NO Antibiotics**: Purely viral. Routine use prohibited.

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Infection Control & Isolation
* **Contact & Droplet Precautions**: RSV is highly contagious. Good hand hygiene, gloves/gowns, and cohorting RSV +ve patients together.

### Pharmacological Prevention
* **Palivizumab (Synagis) (帕利珠單抗)**: Monoclonal antibody against RSV.
* **HA Indications** (strict due to cost): Restricted to high-risk infants (e.g., extreme prematurity, haemodynamically significant CHD, chronic lung disease of prematurity). Given as monthly IM injections during RSV season.

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Parental Education & Safety Netting
* Educate that cough may persist for several weeks post-discharge.
* Advise smoking cessation: Passive smoking directly ↑ severity and risk of LRTI in infants.

## 10. Complications & Prognosis
### Acute Complications
* Apnoea (highest risk in ex-prems < 2 months).
* Dehydration.
* Secondary bacterial infection (Pneumonia/Otitis Media).
* Respiratory failure requiring mechanical ventilation.

### Long-Term Prognosis
* Generally self-limiting (resolves within 2 weeks).
* ↑ Risk of recurrent viral-induced wheeze in early childhood (up to 50% of cases).
* Rare: Bronchiolitis obliterans (permanent airway damage, more common post-Adenovirus).

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Trap: Prescribing Salbutamol nebulisers and oral steroids for a 4-month-old with bronchiolitis. Pearl: This is a classic OSCE trap. Bronchiolitis management is purely supportive (Oxygen and Fluids). Bronchodilators and steroids are ineffective and not recommended by guidelines.
* Viva Question: "Why might an infant with bronchiolitis have a palpable liver edge?" Answer: The lungs are hyperinflated, which pushes the diaphragm down, displacing a normal-sized liver downwards, making it palpable. It does not necessarily mean they have heart failure.
* Pitfall: Doing a CXR for every wheezy infant. CXR is not routinely indicated for typical bronchiolitis and patchy atelectasis can easily be misread as consolidation, leading to inappropriate antibiotic use.
