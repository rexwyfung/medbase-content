# Common Variable Immune Deficiency (常見變異型免疫缺陷病)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of CVID
- **Definition**: A highly variable, primary humoral immunodeficiency.
- **Core Characteristic**: Characterized by markedly reduced serum levels of **IgG**, combined with low **IgA** and/or **IgM**.
- **Clinical Phenotype**: Essentially translates to "Adult-onset XLA," though it displays a bimodal age distribution peaking in both childhood (ages 1-5) and adulthood (ages 20-40).

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
- **Incidence**: Affects approximately 1 in 25,000 to 50,000 individuals globally.
- **Local Context**: In Hong Kong, it is one of the most frequently diagnosed primary immunodeficiencies in specialized Hospital Authority (HA) immunology clinics, often picked up late due to the subtle onset of recurrent respiratory infections.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics

### Genetic Basis of CVID
- **Genetics**: Primarily **polygenic** and highly heterogeneous. Monogenic defects are identified in <20% of cases.
- **Common Mutations**: Defects in **TNFRSF13B** (encoding **TACI**), **BAFF-R**, and **ICOS** are among the most frequently identified genetic drivers.

### Pathophysiology of B-Cell Dysfunction
- **Cellular Defect**: **B-cells** physically exist in normal or near-normal numbers in the periphery, but they are functionally defective.
- **Mechanism**: B-cells fail to undergo terminal differentiation into **memory B-cells** and **plasma cells (漿細胞)**.
- **Immunological Consequence**: Inability to perform immunoglobulin class-switching → profound deficiency in producing **IgG**, **IgA**, and **IgM** → inability to mount specific humoral responses to pathogens.

### Co-existing T-Cell Dysfunction
- **T-cell Involvement**: Over 50% of patients exhibit concurrent T-cell defects (e.g., altered CD4/CD8 ratios), driving the **granulomatous** and **autoimmune** complications characteristic of the disease.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Recurrent Sinopulmonary Infections
- **Pathogens**: High susceptibility to encapsulated bacteria (e.g., **S. pneumoniae**, **H. influenzae**, **M. catarrhalis**).
- **Presentation**: Recurrent acute otitis media, chronic sinusitis, and recurrent pneumonias.
- **Atypical Infections**: High risk for GI tract infections by **Giardia lamblia**, **Campylobacter**, and **Salmonella**.

### Autoimmune Manifestations
- **Prevalence**: Occurs in up to 25% of CVID patients.
- **Hematological Autoimmunity**: Extremely high rate of **Immune Thrombocytopenic Purpura (ITP) (免疫性血小板減少性紫癜)** and **Autoimmune Hemolytic Anemia (AIHA) (自體免疫性溶血性貧血)** (Evans syndrome).
- **Other Autoimmunity**: Rheumatoid arthritis, pernicious anemia, vitiligo, and autoimmune thyroiditis.

### Lymphoproliferative Signs
- **Physical Findings**: Unlike XLA (empty nodes), CVID patients frequently present with **generalized lymphadenopathy** and massive **splenomegaly (巨脾症)**.
- **Tissue Infiltration**: Development of non-caseating granulomas in the lungs, liver, spleen, and skin.

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)

### Differentiating CVID from XLA
- **Age of Onset**: CVID typically presents in older children/adults; XLA strictly presents at 6-9 months when maternal IgG wanes.
- **Gender**: CVID affects females and males equally; XLA is X-linked recessive (males only).
- **Cellular Profile**: CVID has **normal CD19+ B-cell counts**; XLA has **absent B-cells**.
- **Anatomy**: CVID features prominent tonsils and lymph nodes; XLA features absent or rudimentary lymphoid tissues.

### Differentiating CVID from Selective IgA Deficiency
- **Immunoglobulin Profile**: CVID features pan-hypogammaglobulinemia (↓IgG + ↓IgA/IgM); Selective IgA deficiency strictly features isolated ↓IgA with normal IgG/IgM.

## 5. Differential Diagnosis

### Core Differential Diagnoses for Hypogammaglobulinemia
| Condition | Key Differentiating Clinical & Laboratory Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **XLA (Bruton's Agammaglobulinemia)** | Males only, onset <1 year, absent CD19+ B-cells (<1%), absent tonsils/lymph nodes. |
| **Selective IgA Deficiency** | Often asymptomatic, isolated ↓IgA, normal IgG/IgM, **normal vaccine response**. |
| **Hyper-IgM Syndrome** | Normal or ↑IgM, ↓IgG/IgA, defective CD40L, high risk of *Pneumocystis jirovecii*. |
| **Secondary Hypogammaglobulinemia** | Driven by protein loss: **Protein-Losing Enteropathy (PLE)** or **Nephrotic Syndrome**. Vaccine response remains **normal**. |
| **Iatrogenic Hypogammaglobulinemia** | History of systemic immunosuppression (e.g., **Rituximab** anti-CD20 therapy). |

## 6. Tiered Investigations

### First-Line Blood Investigations
- **Serum Immunoglobulins**: Reveals significantly **↓IgG** (typically <2 SD for age), combined with **↓IgA** and/or **↓IgM**.
- **Complete Blood Count (CBC)**: Essential to screen for concurrent autoimmune cytopenias (anemia, thrombocytopenia).

### Gold Standard Immunological Investigations
- **Vaccine Response Testing (CRITICAL)**: Diagnosis strictly requires demonstrating an **impaired functional antibody response** to vaccines (e.g., administering **Pneumovax 23** and confirming failure to build adequate specific IgG titers 4 weeks later).
- **Flow Cytometry**: Reveals normal or slightly low **CD19+ B-cells** (ruling out XLA) and drastically reduced switched memory B-cells (CD27+IgD-).

### Essential Baseline Imaging
- **High-Resolution CT (HRCT) Thorax**: Mandatory baseline screening to detect silent **bronchiectasis (支氣管擴張)** or interstitial lung disease.

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)

### Acute Infection Management
- **Aggressive Antibiotics**: Immediate, prolonged courses of broad-spectrum oral or IV antibiotics for any suspected sinopulmonary infections.

### Chronic Immunoglobulin Replacement Therapy
- **Core Therapy**: Lifelong **Intravenous Immunoglobulin (IVIG) (靜脈注射免疫球蛋白)** every 3-4 weeks (hospital day-ward) or **Subcutaneous IG (SCIG)** weekly (at home).
- **Target**: Maintain trough serum IgG levels **> 800 mg/dL** to reliably prevent bronchiectasis progression.

### Management of Autoimmune Complications
- **First-Line**: Systemic corticosteroids for acute flares of ITP/AIHA.
- **Second-Line**: **Rituximab** (anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody) is heavily utilized as a highly effective steroid-sparing agent for refractory cytopenias.

### Routine Screening Protocols
- **Pulmonary**: Regular lung function tests (Spirometry) and interval HRCT scans.
- **Gastrointestinal**: Regular upper/lower GI endoscopies for symptomatic patients to screen for **Gastric Carcinoma (胃癌)** and lymphoma.

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care

### Multidisciplinary Team Involvement
- **Respiratory Physiotherapy**: Crucial for active airway clearance techniques and sputum mobilization in patients with established bronchiectasis.
- **Immunology Specialist Nurses**: Vital for coordinating IVIG infusions and training families for home-based SCIG administration.

### Medical Social Worker (MSW) Support
- **Financial Assistance**: MSW intervention is critical in HA hospitals for applying to the **Samaritan Fund** or similar subsidies to cover the extreme lifelong costs of IVIG/SCIG therapy.

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags

### Safeguarding Against Medical Neglect
- **Compliance Monitoring**: Missing consecutive lifelong monthly IVIG infusions constitutes severe medical neglect, directly exposing the child to life-threatening sepsis and irreversible structural lung damage.
- **Misinterpretation Risk**: Chronic bruising from undiagnosed ITP can mimic physical abuse; accurate CVID diagnosis prevents false Non-Accidental Injury (NAI) accusations.

## 10. Complications & Prognosis

### Respiratory Complications
- **Bronchiectasis (支氣管擴張)**: The predominant cause of long-term morbidity, eventually leading to chronic respiratory failure and cor pulmonale.

### Malignancy Risks in CVID
- **Lymphoma**: Drastically increased risk (up to 50x) of developing **Non-Hodgkin Lymphoma (非霍奇金淋巴瘤)**, frequently extranodal.
- **Gastric Cancer**: High risk of **Gastric Carcinoma (胃癌)**, heavily driven by persistent, un-cleared *H. pylori* infections or underlying pernicious anemia.

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Trap: Missing CVID in a teenager purely presenting with "tough to treat asthma". Pearl: If a teen's "asthma" involves constant green sputum and actual pneumonia consolidations, check their immunoglobulins to rule out underlying CVID.
* Viva Question: Why do we test vaccine responses to diagnose CVID? Answer: Because simply having low IgG isn't enough to prove the immune system is broken (they could just be losing IgG in their urine via Nephrotic Syndrome). Injecting a vaccine and failing to produce specific antibodies proves the B-cells are functionally defective at class-switching and memory formation.
* Pitfall: Misdiagnosing CVID as XLA purely based on low immunoglobulins in a male patient. Always perform flow cytometry to check CD19+ B-cell counts (normal in CVID, completely absent in XLA) to ensure accurate diagnosis and genetic counseling.
