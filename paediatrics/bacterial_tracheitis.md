# Bacterial Tracheitis (細菌性氣管炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition
* Rare, life-threatening, acute bacterial infection of subglottic trachea.
* Characterised by formation of thick, purulent **Pseudomembranes (假膜)** → physical airway plugging.
* Clinically mimics severe **Croup (哮吼)** but fails standard therapies.

### Local Epidemiology (Hong Kong Context)
* Incidence: Rare (<0.1 per 100,000 children), but ↑ mortality if missed.
* Peak age: 3–8 years (older than typical viral croup).
* HA Context: High index of suspicion needed in A&E/Paediatric wards during winter viral peaks when secondary bacterial superinfections ↑.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics

### Primary Aetiology
* Pathogens: 
  * ***Staphylococcus aureus*** (金黃色葡萄球菌) (Most common, inc. MRSA).
  * *Streptococcus pneumoniae* (肺炎鏈球菌).
  * *Moraxella catarrhalis* (卡他莫拉菌).
  * *Haemophilus influenzae* type b (Hib) (Rare post-vaccination).

### Pathogenesis & Airway Obstruction
* **Primary Phase**: Preceding viral URI (e.g., Parainfluenza, Influenza A/B) damages tracheal mucosa.
* **Secondary Phase**: Bacterial superinfection → intense subglottic mucosal inflammation.
* **Obstruction**: Sloughing of necrotic mucosa + thick pus → forms obstructive **Pseudomembranes (假膜)** → critical airway narrowing.

## 3. Clinical Presentation

### Typical Prodrome
* 1–3 days of mild viral URI symptoms.
* Low-grade fever, rhinorrhoea, mild barking cough (mimics viral croup).

### Acute Deterioration Phase
* Sudden, rapid deterioration over hours.
* Fulminant ↑ fever, severe respiratory distress.
* Transition from inspiratory to severe biphasic **Stridor (喘鳴)**.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Vital Signs & General Appearance
* **Fever**: Spiking, often >39°C.
* **Toxaemia**: Child appears highly toxic, lethargic, terrified, exhausted.
* **Positioning**: Prefers lying flat or semi-recumbent (epiglottitis prefers "tripod" sitting).
* **Voice**: Hoarse, but drooling/dysphagia are generally absent.

### Respiratory Signs
* **Stridor (喘鳴)**: Biphasic, progressive, entirely unresponsive to nebulised Adrenaline (腎上腺素).
* **Cough**: Productive of thick, purulent sputum (viral croup is strictly non-purulent).
* **Work of Breathing**: Severe sternal/intercostal recessions, tachypnoea, nasal flaring.
* **Hypoxia**: Late and ominous sign of impending arrest.

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)

### Differentiating Tracheitis vs Viral Croup
* **Treatment Response**: Tracheitis entirely fails to improve with nebulised Adrenaline / IV Dexamethasone.
* **Toxicity**: Tracheitis = profoundly toxic/septic; Croup = non-toxic.
* **Sputum**: Tracheitis = purulent secretions; Croup = no purulence.

### Differentiating Tracheitis vs Epiglottitis
* **Onset**: Tracheitis has a 1-3 day prodrome; Epiglottitis is fulminant over hours.
* **Posture**: Tracheitis patient lies flat/supine; Epiglottitis tripod/sniffing position.
* **Drooling**: Rare in tracheitis; prominent in Epiglottitis.
* **Vaccination**: Hib vaccination makes Epiglottitis extremely rare.

## 6. Differential Diagnosis

### Primary Differentials Table
| Differential Diagnosis | Key Differentiating Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Viral Croup (哮吼)** | Non-toxic, clear secretions, responds to adrenaline. |
| **Acute Epiglottitis (會厭炎)** | Drooling, dysphagia, tripod posture, no cough, fully un-immunised child. |
| **Foreign Body Aspiration (異物吸入)** | Sudden choking history, focal wheeze/unilateral ↓ air entry, afebrile. |
| **Retropharyngeal Abscess (咽後膿腫)** | Torticollis, stiff neck, bulging posterior pharyngeal wall, trismus. |
| **Anaphylaxis (過敏性休克)** | Urticaria, facial oedema, wheeze, known allergen exposure. |

## 7. Tiered Investigations

### Bedside & Golden Rules
* **GOLDEN RULE**: DO NOT agitate the child. Keep with parents.
* DO NOT attempt IV access, throat swabbing, or venepuncture if airway is tenuous → triggers crying → ↑ oxygen demand → sudden complete obstruction.

### Blood Investigations
* Only perform *after* airway is secured:
  * Complete Blood Count (CBC): ↑↑ WCC with neutrophil predominance.
  * C-Reactive Protein (CRP) / Procalcitonin: Markedly ↑.
  * Blood Cultures (血培養): +ve in minority, but essential to guide antibiotics.

### Imaging
* **Lateral/AP Neck X-Ray**: 
  * Only if patient is highly stable (rare). Never delay theatre.
  * May show "shaggy" trachea margins (ragged edges due to pseudomembranes).
  * May show subglottic narrowing (steeple sign).

### Definitive / Special Investigations
* **Rigid Bronchoscopy (支氣管鏡檢查)**: 
  * Diagnostic AND therapeutic.
  * Performed in OT by ENT/Anaesthesia.
  * Findings: Thick, purulent secretions, mucosal ulcerations, pseudomembranes.
  * Swabs/suction traps sent for urgent Gram stain & culture.

## 8. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)

### Immediate Airway Management (Resuscitation)
* **Location**: MUST be managed in the Operating Theatre (OT) or PICU.
* **Personnel**: Call for Senior Anaesthetist & ENT Surgeon IMMEDIATELY.
* **Oxygen**: High-flow O2 via mask if tolerated.
* **Intubation (插管)**: Usually requires a smaller endotracheal tube (ETT) due to severe subglottic oedema.

### Pharmacological Therapy (HA Protocols)
* **Antibiotics**: Commence immediately post-intubation (or before if delayed).
  * 1st Line: IV **Ceftriaxone (頭孢曲松)** + IV **Flucloxacillin (氟氯西林)**.
  * Suspected MRSA / Penicillin Allergy: IV **Vancomycin (萬古黴素)** or IV **Clindamycin (克林黴素)**.
* **Steroids**: IV Dexamethasone (地塞米松) often given to ↓ airway oedema (controversial but widely used).

### Operative & Procedural Management
* **Bronchoscopic Stripping**: Repeated rigid bronchoscopy to physically suction and strip away obstructing pseudomembranes.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care

### Intensive Care (PICU)
* **Ventilation**: Requires PICU admission for mechanical ventilation.
* **Suctioning**: Frequent ETT suctioning required to prevent tube blockage by thick pus.
* **Extubation**: Only attempted when secretions ↓, fever resolves, and air leak around ETT is present (indicating ↓ oedema).

### Psychosocial Support
* **Medical Social Worker (MSW)**: Parental support for sudden, life-threatening critical illness in a previously healthy child.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags

### NAI Relevance
* Generally N/A for bacterial tracheitis.
* Observe for general neglect (e.g., severe malnutrition predisposing to severe infections), but not directly indicative of NAI.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis

### Acute Complications
* **Cardiopulmonary Arrest**: Due to sudden airway obstruction (pseudomembrane dislodgement) or hypoxia.
* **Toxic Shock Syndrome (中毒性休克綜合症)**: If toxin-producing *S. aureus* is the pathogen → hypotension, multi-organ failure.
* **Pneumonia / ARDS**: Infection descending into lower airways.
* **Pneumothorax**: Secondary to high airway pressures.

### Long-Term Complications
* **Subglottic Stenosis (聲門下狹窄)**: Secondary to prolonged intubation + intense tracheal mucosal necrosis.
* **Overall Prognosis**: Excellent if airway is secured promptly. High mortality if misdiagnosed as simple croup and discharged.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls

* Viva Question: A child with croup is given nebulised adrenaline. Instead of improving, they remain severely stridulous and look extremely toxic. What is your next step?
* Answer: Abandon standard croup protocols. Assume Bacterial Tracheitis. Immediately call for senior Anaesthesia and ENT help to secure the airway in the operating theatre.
* Trap: Sending a deteriorating "croup" patient to the X-ray department.
* Pearl: If they have tracheitis, they can fully obstruct and arrest in the radiology scanner away from airway experts. Proceed directly to the operating theatre.
* Pitfall: Attempting vigorous throat examination or IV cannulation in A&E. This causes severe agitation, dramatically ↑ oxygen demand, and can precipitate fatal acute airway obstruction.
