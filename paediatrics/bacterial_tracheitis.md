# Bacterial Tracheitis (細菌性氣管炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
- **Definition**: A rare, acute, dreadful bacterial infection of the subglottic trachea, leading to the formation of thick, purulent pseudomembranes that physically plug the airway.
- **Local Epidemiology**: Extremely rare, but carries a high mortality. It is a critical differential for any child diagnosed with "Croup" who is not improving or appears highly toxic.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
- Usually a secondary bacterial complication following a primary viral URI (like Parainfluenza causing Croup).
- Most common pathogen is **Staphylococcus aureus** (including MRSA), followed by *S. pneumoniae* or *Moraxella*.
- The trachea becomes intensely inflamed, sloughing off necrotic mucosa mixed with thick pus, forming obstructive pseudomembranes.

## 3. Clinical Presentation
- Often starts like classic viral croup (barking cough, stridor, mild fever).
- Then, the child suddenly **deteriorates**. They develop a raging high fever, appear incredibly toxic/septic, and their stridor becomes biphasic and severe.

## 4. Key Symptoms & Signs
- High fever, toxic/septic appearance (child looks terrified and exhausted).
- Thick, purulent sputum (which pure viral croup never has).
- Progression of stridor despite being given standard croup therapy (nebulized adrenaline / oral dexamethasone).
- Child prefers to lie flat or semi-recumbent (unlike epiglottitis where they sit in a "tripod" position).

## 5. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
- **Unlike Viral Croup**: Fails to respond to adrenaline nebulizers, incredibly high fever, toxic appearance.
- **Unlike Epiglottitis**: Tracheitis usually has a prodrome of a few days of cough (epiglottitis is fulminant over hours), sitting position doesn't relieve it, and drooling is less prominent. 

## 6. Differential Diagnosis
- Viral Croup (Laryngotracheobronchitis)
- Acute Epiglottitis
- Foreign Body Aspiration
- Retropharyngeal / Peritonsillar abscess

## 7. Tiered Investigations (Bedside, Bloods, Imaging, Special)
- **Do NOT agitate the child** with blood tests or X-rays if the airway is highly tenuous.
- **Imaging**: If stable enough (never delay securing the airway), AP/lateral neck X-ray may show "shaggy" trachea margins (ragged edges due to pseudomembranes).
- **Definitive/Special**: Direct visualization via **rigid bronchoscopy** in the operating theatre. This allows for both diagnosis and immediate mechanical stripping/suctioning of the deadly pseudomembranes.

## 8. Evidence-Based Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
- **Immediate Priority**: Secure the airway. This MUST be done in the operating theatre with senior Anaesthetist and ENT surgeon present.
- **Airway**: Formal intubation is almost always required (and often difficult, requiring a smaller tube due to severe subglottic swelling).
- **Drugs**: 
  - IV Broad-spectrum antibiotics (e.g., Ceftriaxone + Flucloxacillin or Clindamycin/Vancomycin if MSRA prevalent).
- **Procedure**: Repeated bronchoscopic suctioning of the trachea to remove the sloughed membranes.

## 9. MDT & Psychosocial Care (MSW, MCHC, SWD)
- Total PICU intensive care needed. Intense stress for parents watching a rapidly deteriorating child.

## 10. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
- N/A.

## 11. Complications & Prognosis
- Cardiopulmonary arrest due to sudden catastrophic airway obstruction (pseudomembrane breaking off and fully blocking the trachea).
- Toxic shock syndrome (if Staph aureus toxin-mediated).
- Prolonged intubation required, risking future subglottic stenosis.

## 12. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- **Trap**: Sending a deteriorating "croup" patient to the X-ray department. If they have tracheitis, they can fully obstruct and arrest in the radiology scanner away from airway experts.
- **Viva Q**: "A child with croup is given nebulised adrenaline. Instead of improving, they remain severely stridulous and look extremely toxic. What is your next step?" Answer: Abandon standard croup protocols. Assume Bacterial Tracheitis. Immediately call for senior Anaesthesia and ENT help to secure the airway in the operating theatre.
