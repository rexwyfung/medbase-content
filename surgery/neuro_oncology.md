# Brain Tumours (腦腫瘤)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Brain Tumours
* **Primary Brain Tumours (原發性腦腫瘤)**: Neoplasms arising directly from brain parenchyma, meninges, cranial nerves, or pituitary gland (e.g., Gliomas, Meningiomas, Schwannomas).
* **Secondary/Metastatic Brain Tumours (轉移性腦腫瘤)**: Disseminated malignant cells from extra-cranial primary sites spreading haematogenously to the central nervous system (CNS).
* **Benign vs. Malignant**: Even histologically "benign" tumours (e.g., Meningioma) can be clinically lethal due to mass effect within the rigid cranial vault.

### Brain Tumour Epidemiology in Hong Kong
* **Metastatic Tumours**: Most common intracranial tumours; local prevalence driven by high rates of **Lung Cancer (肺癌)**, **Breast Cancer (乳癌)**, and **Colorectal Cancer (大腸癌)** in Hong Kong.
* **Primary Adult Tumours**: **Meningioma (腦膜瘤)** (mostly benign, female > male) and **Glioblastoma (GBM) (膠質母細胞瘤)** (highly malignant, median age 60s).
* **Primary Paediatric Tumours**: Brain tumours are the 2nd most common childhood malignancy in HK (after leukaemia); most occur in the **Posterior Fossa (後顱窩)** (e.g., Medulloblastoma, Pilocytic Astrocytoma).

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Monro-Kellie Doctrine and Raised ICP (顱內壓增高)
* **Monro-Kellie Doctrine (顱腔定容原理)**: Total cranial volume = Brain parenchyma (80%) + CSF (10%) + Blood (10%).
* **Volume-Pressure Curve**: Initial compensatory mechanisms (CSF displacement to spinal sac, ↓ venous blood volume) maintain normal ICP.
* **Decompensation**: Once compensatory reserve is exhausted, minute ↑ in tumour volume → exponential ↑ in **Intracranial Pressure (ICP)** → critical ↓ in **Cerebral Perfusion Pressure (CPP)** (CPP = MAP - ICP).

### Pathophysiology of Brain Oedema
* **Vasogenic Oedema (血管源性水腫)**: Most common type surrounding brain tumours; driven by breakdown of the **Blood-Brain Barrier (血腦屏障)**.
* **Mechanism**: Tumour secretes VEGF → ↑ capillary permeability → plasma fluid leaks into extracellular white matter tracks.
* **Steroid Responsiveness**: Highly responsive to corticosteroids (e.g., Dexamethasone), which restore endothelial tight junctions.

### Brain Herniation Syndromes (腦疝)
* **Uncal Herniation (鉤回疝)**: Medial temporal lobe (uncus) pushed over tentorial edge → compresses CN III (ipsilateral blown pupil) → compresses cerebral peduncle (contralateral hemiparesis).
* **Subfalcine Herniation (大腦鐮下疝)**: Cingulate gyrus pushed under falx cerebri → compresses Anterior Cerebral Artery (ACA) → contralateral leg weakness.
* **Tonsillar Herniation (小腦扁桃體疝)**: Cerebellar tonsils pushed through foramen magnum → compresses medulla → respiratory arrest/cardiovascular collapse (Cushing's reflex end-stage).

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Classic Triad of Raised ICP
* **Headache (頭痛)**: Typically worse in the morning, awakens patient from sleep, aggravated by coughing, straining, or bending forward (Valsalva maneuvers).
* **Vomiting (嘔吐)**: Often early morning, projectile nature, can occur without preceding nausea.
* **Papilloedema (視乳頭水腫)**: Bilateral optic disc swelling on fundoscopy; represents delayed sign of chronic ↑ ICP.

### Focal Neurological Signs by Lobe
* **Frontal Lobe (額葉)**: Personality changes, abulia (lack of drive), expressive aphasia (Broca's area), contralateral hemiparesis (motor cortex), primitive reflexes (+ve grasping/snout).
* **Parietal Lobe (頂葉)**: Contralateral sensory loss, astereognosis, agraphaesthesia, neglect (right parietal), receptive aphasia (Wernicke's area).
* **Temporal Lobe (顳葉)**: Complex partial seizures (olfactory/gustatory auras), memory impairment, superior homonymous quadrantanopia ("pie in the sky").
* **Occipital Lobe (枕葉)**: Contralateral homonymous hemianopia with macular sparing.
* **Cerebellum (小腦)**: Ipsilateral ataxia, dysmetria, intention tremor, nystagmus, broad-based gait.

### Specific Brain Tumour Syndromes
* **Cerebellopontine Angle (CPA) Syndrome**: Unilateral sensorineural hearing loss, tinnitus, vertigo (CN VIII), absent corneal reflex (CN V), facial palsy (CN VII); classic for **Vestibular Schwannoma (前庭神經鞘瘤)**.
* **Foster Kennedy Syndrome**: Ipsilateral anosmia and optic atrophy (direct tumour compression) + contralateral papilloedema (raised ICP); classic for olfactory groove meningioma.
* **Parinaud Syndrome**: Upward gaze paralysis, convergence-retraction nystagmus, pupillary light-near dissociation; classic for **Pineal Gland Tumours (松果體瘤)**.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Differential Diagnosis of Intracranial Space-Occupying Lesions (SOL)
| Pathology                     | Clinical Course                                                 | CT/MRI Discriminatory Features                                                                                  |
| :---------------------------- | :-------------------------------------------------------------- | :-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Primary High-Grade Glioma** | Weeks to months; progressive deficit, seizures.                 | Irregular thick ring enhancement; crosses corpus callosum ("Butterfly glioma"); significant surrounding oedema. |
| **Brain Metastases**          | Weeks to months; known primary cancer (Lung/Breast/Colon).      | Multiple lesions at grey-white matter junction; extensive surrounding vasogenic oedema.                         |
| **Meningioma**                | Years; often incidental, slowly progressive compression.        | Extra-axial, dural tail sign, hyperdense on plain CT, broad-based on dura, hyperostosis of adjacent bone.       |
| **Brain Abscess (腦膿腫)**       | Days to weeks; fever, systemic source (endocarditis/sinusitis). | Thin, smooth, complete ring enhancement; marked restricted diffusion on DWI MRI.                                |
| **Intracerebral Haemorrhage** | Hyper-acute (minutes); sudden severe deficit/headache.          | Hyperdense on non-contrast CT; minimal oedema acutely; tracking into ventricles.                                |

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup

### Emergency First-Line Imaging
* **Non-Contrast CT Brain (平掃腦部電腦掃描)**: Rapidly rules out acute haemorrhage, gross midline shift, and hydrocephalus. Tumours may appear hypodense (oedema) or hyperdense (meningioma/medulloblastoma).
* **Contrast-Enhanced CT Brain**: Demonstrates blood-brain barrier breakdown (tumour enhancement); utilized if MRI is contraindicated or unavailable acutely.

### Gold Standard Neuroimaging
* **MRI Brain with Gadolinium Contrast (磁力共振掃描)**: Gold standard for characterizing structural anatomy, tumour vascularity, and precise localization.
* **Functional MRI (fMRI)**: Maps eloquent cortex (motor, speech) relative to the tumour to plan safe resection margins.
* **MR Tractography (DTI)**: Visualizes major white matter tracts (e.g., corticospinal tract, optic radiation) to prevent surgical transection.
* **MR Spectroscopy (MRS)**: Differentiates tumour from radiation necrosis or abscess by measuring tissue metabolites (↑ Choline, ↓ NAA in tumours).

### Pre-operative Diagnostic Workup
* **CT Thorax, Abdomen, Pelvis (CT TAP)**: Mandatory for any newly discovered multiple brain lesions to locate an occult primary malignancy.
* **Endocrinological Panel**: Required for parasellar/pituitary lesions (Prolactin, IGF-1, Cortisol, TFTs, Testosterone).
* **Pre-operative Anaesthetic Workup**: ECG, CXR, Clotting profile, G&S, Renal function, and LFTs prior to major craniotomy.

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications

### Acute Medical Resuscitation for Raised ICP
* **Head Elevation**: Prop bed to 30 degrees to optimize venous jugular drainage.
* **Corticosteroids**: IV **Dexamethasone (地塞米松)** (typically 4-16mg daily in divided doses) + PPI cover; rapidly reduces vasogenic oedema around the tumour.
* **Osmotic Diuretics**: IV **Mannitol (甘露醇)** (0.5-1 g/kg) or Hypertonic Saline for acute herniation syndromes; creates osmotic gradient pulling fluid from brain parenchyma.
* **Anti-epileptics**: IV Levetiracetam (Keppra) or Phenytoin indicated ONLY if the patient has had a clinical seizure; HA protocol does not recommend routine prophylactic anticonvulsants for all brain tumours.

### Surgical Resection Indications
* **Maximal Safe Resection**: Primary goal is obtaining tissue diagnosis, relieving mass effect, and cytoreduction while strictly preserving neurological function.
* **Indications for Biopsy Only**:
	* Lesions in deep/eloquent areas (brainstem, thalamus).
	* Primary CNS lymphoma (responds completely to high-dose Methotrexate).
	* Poor functional status (KPS < 70).
* **Emergent Surgery**: Acute hydrocephalus requiring External Ventricular Drain (EVD) or rapidly deteriorating GCS due to imminent herniation.

### Hong Kong Hospital Authority Oncology Protocols
* **Multidisciplinary Team (MDT)**: Mandatory discussion for all definitive neuro-oncology treatment plans.
* **Stupp Protocol for Glioblastoma**: Maximal safe resection → Concurrent Radiotherapy (RT) + oral Temozolomide (TMZ) → Adjuvant TMZ.
* **Stereotactic Radiosurgery (SRS)**: Targetted high-dose radiation (e.g., Gamma Knife, CyberKnife) used for metastases <3cm, inaccessible benign tumours, or recurrent disease.

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Core Operative Principles of Craniotomy (開顱手術)
* **Neuronavigation (神經導航系統)**: Frameless intra-operative stereotactic guidance (using pre-op MRI) to localize the tumour and outline planned surgical trajectories.
* **Awake Craniotomy (清醒開顱手術)**: Indicated for tumours infiltrating eloquent cortex (Broca's/Wernicke's/Motor). Patient is awoken post-craniotomy for intra-operative cortical mapping using bipolar stimulation to avoid permanent deficit.
* **5-ALA Fluorescence (Gliolan)**: Patient drinks 5-Aminolevulinic acid pre-op; high-grade glioma cells fluoresce pink/red under special blue operative microscope light, maximizing extent of resection.
* **Intra-operative Neuromonitoring (IONM)**: SSEP, MEP, and continuous cranial nerve EMG monitoring to preserve functional integrity.

### Informed Consent and Specific Surgical Risks
* **Neurological Deficit**: Permanent weakness, sensory loss, aphasia, or visual field cuts depending on tumour location.
* **Seizures**: De novo post-operative epilepsy secondary to cortical scarring.
* **Infection/Meningitis**: Risk of catastrophic deep space infection or bone flap osteomyelitis.
* **Intracranial Haemorrhage**: Post-operative bleeding into the resection cavity requiring emergent return to theatre.

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Immediate Post-operative Neuro-ICU Care
* **Strict Neurological Observations**: Hourly GCS, pupillary size/reactivity, and focal limb power assessment to detect early post-operative haematoma or severe oedema.
* **Blood Pressure Control**: Maintain strict normotension; hypertension ↑ risk of resection cavity haemorrhage; hypotension ↓ CPP leading to cerebral ischaemia.
* **Seizure Prophylaxis**: Maintain Keppra/Phenytoin for at least 7 days post-op if cortex was significantly manipulated or manipulated.

### Post-operative Pharmacological Management
* **Steroid Weaning**: Rapidly taper Dexamethasone over 1-2 weeks as tolerated to prevent Addisonian crisis and reduce side effects (hyperglycaemia, myopathy, immunosuppression).
* **VTE Prophylaxis**: High-risk for DVT/PE. Use mechanical prophylaxis (TEDS/Flowtrons) immediately. Low Molecular Weight Heparin (LMWH) typically started 24-48 hours post-op after follow-up CT confirms no active bleeding.
* **Early Mobilization**: Aggressive Physiotherapy (PT), Occupational Therapy (OT), and Speech Therapy (ST) input from post-op Day 1.

## 9. Surgical Complications & Prognosis

### Early Post-operative Complications (<48 Hours)
* **Resection Cavity Haematoma**: Presents as sudden GCS drop or new pupil asymmetry; requires immediate non-contrast CT and emergent re-evacuation.
* **Status Epilepticus**: Prolonged continuous seizures requiring IV Lorazepam and Phenytoin loading; causes massive ↑ in cerebral metabolic demand and ICP.
* **CSF Leak**: Clear fluid from surgical wound, nose, or ear; high risk for ascending bacterial meningitis.

### Late Complications and WHO Grading Prognosis
* **Radiation Necrosis**: Delayed local tissue death mimicking tumour recurrence on MRI; often requires PET scan or MRS to differentiate.
* **Hydrocephalus**: Communicating hydrocephalus from proteinaceous debris blocking arachnoid granulations; requires permanent Ventriculoperitoneal (VP) Shunt.
* **Prognosis by WHO Grade**:
  * WHO Grade I (e.g., Pilocytic Astrocytoma, Meningioma): Curative with complete resection.
  * WHO Grade II (e.g., Diffuse Astrocytoma): Median survival 5-8 years; inevitable malignant transformation.
  * WHO Grade III (e.g., Anaplastic Astrocytoma): Median survival 2-3 years.
  * WHO Grade IV (e.g., Glioblastoma): Median survival 12-15 months despite optimal Stupp protocol.

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: What is the classic presentation of a patient with a Vestibular Schwannoma? Answer: Unilateral sensorineural hearing loss, continuous tinnitus, vertigo, and an absent ipsilateral corneal reflex due to CN V compression in the Cerebellopontine Angle. ![image](https://pub-f0606dedf6d94e4bb5b1999cbd065914.r2.dev/paediatrics/c551bfe7edbc3767ca85f95e79ce205d.png)
* Viva Question: Why do brain tumours rarely metastasize outside the central nervous system? Answer: The brain lacks a traditional lymphatic drainage system, and the dural venous sinuses do not easily allow large tumour cells to escape the rigid cranial vault into systemic circulation before the primary intracranial mass becomes lethal.
* Trap: Assuming a patient with a brain tumour presenting with a new focal deficit has had an acute tumour enlargement. Pearl: Rapid, acute deterioration (minutes to hours) in a known brain tumour patient is almost always caused by an acute intratumoral haemorrhage or an acute seizure (Todd's paresis), not tumour growth.
* Trap: Performing a lumbar puncture to rule out meningitis in a patient presenting with headache, vomiting, and a suspected brain tumour. Pearl: Lumbar puncture is absolutely contraindicated in patients with intracranial space-occupying lesions due to the massive risk of precipitating fatal tonsillar herniation; always do a CT brain first.
* Pitfall: Over-prescribing prophylactic anticonvulsants for all brain tumour patients. HA guidelines mandate that anti-epileptic drugs (e.g., Keppra) should only be initiated if the patient has had a documented clinical seizure; routine prophylaxis without prior seizures is not indicated.
* Pitfall: Forgetting to image the spine in paediatric patients with Medulloblastoma. Medulloblastomas spread via CSF ("drop metastases"), so full neuraxis imaging (MRI Brain + MRI Whole Spine) is mandatory for accurate staging before surgery.
