# Post-Tonsillectomy Haemorrhage (扁桃腺切除術後出血)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Classification of Post-Tonsillectomy Haemorrhage (PTH)
* **Primary PTH (原發性出血)**: Occurs <24 hrs post-operatively.
* **Secondary PTH (繼發性出血)**: Occurs >24 hrs post-operatively (peak incidence at 5-10 days).

### Hong Kong HA Epidemiology & Incidence Rates
* **Overall Incidence**: ~2-5% of all tonsillectomies in HK.
* **Secondary PTH**: More common (approx. 3-4%) than Primary PTH (approx. 1%).
* **Risk Factors**: ↑ age (adults > children), recurrent **Tonsillitis (扁桃腺炎)**, use of hot surgical techniques (e.g., monopolar diathermy).

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Arterial Blood Supply to the Tonsillar Fossa (扁桃腺窩)
* **Tonsillar branch** of **Facial artery (面動脈)** (Main supply).
* **Ascending pharyngeal artery (升咽動脈)**.
* **Dorsal lingual artery (舌背動脈)**.
* **Ascending palatine artery (升顎動脈)**.
* **Descending palatine artery (降顎動脈)**.
* **Venous drainage**: Paratonsillar vein → Pharyngeal plexus → Internal Jugular Vein.

### Pathophysiology of Primary Post-Tonsillectomy Haemorrhage
* Inadequate surgical haemostasis intra-operatively.
* Rebound vasodilation as local vasoconstrictors (adrenaline) wear off.
* Undiagnosed underlying **Coagulopathy (凝血病)**.

### Pathophysiology of Secondary Post-Tonsillectomy Haemorrhage
* Sloughing of the healing **Eschar (焦痂)**.
* Local bacterial infection of the tonsillar bed → tissue breakdown.
* Exacerbated by poor oral intake → throat dryness and eschar cracking.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Presenting Symptoms of PTH
* Frank **Haemoptysis (咯血)** or spitting fresh blood.
* **Haematemesis (吐血)** (vomiting swallowed altered "coffee-ground" or fresh blood).
* Frequent swallowing (classic sign of concealed bleeding, especially in children).
* Throat pain (often acutely worsened in secondary PTH).

### Clinical Signs of Active Haemorrhage
* Active pulsatile arterial spurting or venous oozing in **Oropharynx (口咽)**.
* Presence of "liver-like" clot in tonsillar fossa.
* ↑ HR (Tachycardia - earliest sign of hypovolaemia).
* ↓ BP (Hypotension - late/decompensated sign of **Hypovolaemic Shock (低血容量性休克)**).
* Pallor, diaphoresis, prolonged capillary refill time.
* Agitation or ↓ GCS (signs of cerebral hypoperfusion/hypoxia).

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Primary vs Secondary PTH Discriminatory Table
| Feature | Primary PTH (原發性出血) | Secondary PTH (繼發性出血) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Timing** | < 24 hours post-op | > 24 hours (peak Day 5-10) |
| **Aetiology** | Surgical technique, slipped tie, unmasked coagulopathy | Infection, premature eschar sloughing |
| **Clinical State** | Often more severe/rapid blood loss | Often slow ooze, herald bleeds |
| **Management Focus**| Early return to OT for haemostasis | Resuscitation, IV Antibiotics, ± OT if severe |

### Systemic vs Local Bleeding Aetiology
* **Local Surgical Site**: Unilateral clot, active oozing from single pole, eschar disruption.
* **Systemic Diathesis**: Bilateral oozing, petechiae, concurrent epistaxis, known history of vWD/Haemophilia.

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)

### Immediate Bedside Workup for Bleeding Airway
* Assess Airway (A) and Breathing (B) before any labs.
* Weigh spit bowls/swabs to estimate objective blood loss.
* Examine oropharynx carefully with headlamp + tongue depressor (do NOT dislodge stable clots blindly in ward).

### First-Tier Laboratory Investigations
* **CBP**: Hb baseline (acute drop lags behind actual blood loss), Platelets.
* **Clotting Profile**: PT/APTT/INR to r/o coagulopathy.
* **Type & Screen (G&S)**: Mandatory for all PTH.
* **Cross-match (X-match)**: Order 2-4 units PRBCs if active massive bleeding or haemodynamic instability.
* **LRFT**: Baseline renal function for IV contrast/medication dosing.

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)

### HA Immediate Resuscitation Protocol
* **Position**: Upright, leaning forward (prevents airway aspiration).
* **Airway**: Yankauer suction readily available.
* **Breathing**: 100% O2 via Non-Rebreather Mask (15L/min).
* **Circulation**: 2x large-bore IV cannulae (14G/16G) → Rapid fluid bolus (Warm crystalloid 20ml/kg stat).

### Pharmacological & Conservative Management
* **Hydrogen Peroxide (過氧化氫)** gargle (1% diluted) or ice water gargle to induce local vasoconstriction.
* IV **Tranexamic Acid (氨甲環酸)** (TXA) 1g (adult) or 15mg/kg (paediatric).
* IV Broad-spectrum Antibiotics (e.g., **Amoxicillin/Clavulanate (阿莫西林/克拉維酸)**) to treat underlying infection in Secondary PTH.
* Adequate IV Analgesia (e.g., Paracetamol) to lower BP and HR.

### Surgical Indications for Return to OT
* Uncontrollable active bleeding despite medical measures.
* Hemodynamic instability (shock) unresponsive to fluids.
* Re-bleeding episodes ("herald bleeds" followed by massive haemorrhage).
* Uncooperative patient (infant/young child) preventing adequate ward examination.

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Operative Steps for Arrest of PTH (Arrest of Haemorrhage)
* **Anaesthesia**: Rapid Sequence Induction (RSI) with cricoid pressure in head-down position (high risk of aspiration from swallowed blood).
* **Positioning**: **Rose position** (neck extended).
* **Exposure**: Insert **Boyle-Davis gag**.
* **Clearance**: Aggressive suction to remove all clots from tonsillar fossa (clots hide the bleeding source and prevent vessel retraction).
* **Primary Haemostasis**: Identify bleeding point → **Bipolar diathermy (雙極電凝)** or surgical ties.
* **Secondary Haemostasis**: If diffuse oozing → **Pillar suturing (縫合)** (suturing anterior and posterior pillars together with absorbable suture, e.g., Vicryl) ± haemostatic agents (Surgicel/Floseal).
* **Extubate**: Fully awake in lateral position to protect airway.

### Surgical Consent Risks & Complications
* Damage to teeth/lips/tongue from mouth gag.
* Failure to arrest bleeding (rarely requiring external carotid artery ligation).
* Risks of massive blood transfusion.
* Aspiration pneumonia.

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Immediate Post-Operative Monitoring (PACU & Ward)
* Strict vital signs monitoring (Q1H initially).
* Nurse in lateral recovery position until fully alert.
* Keep Nil By Mouth (NBM) until haemostatically stable and fully awake.

### Ward Rehabilitation & Discharge Criteria
* Cold liquid diet progressing to soft diet.
* Regular analgesia to encourage swallowing and prevent eschar dryness.
* **Discharge criteria**: Haemodynamically stable, normal Hb (or transfused to target), tolerating oral diet, strictly no re-bleeding for minimum 24 hours.

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Early and Life-Threatening Complications
* **Airway Obstruction (氣道阻塞)**: From massive clot formation or laryngeal oedema.
* **Aspiration Pneumonia (吸入性肺炎)**: Inhalation of blood intra-op or pre-op.
* **Hypovolaemic Shock (低血容量性休克)**: Leading to end-organ ischaemia.
* **Cardiac Arrest / Death**: Rare, usually secondary to hypoxia (airway clot) or exsanguination.

### Intermediate & Late Complications
* Re-bleeding (High risk if initial bleeding was severe).
* Severe anaemia requiring delayed transfusion or prolonged iron therapy.
* Prognosis is generally excellent once definitive haemostasis is achieved and infection is treated.

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: What is the most dangerous anaesthetic risk in a bleeding post-tonsillectomy patient? Answer: Aspiration of blood during induction; requires Rapid Sequence Induction (RSI) with cricoid pressure and head-down positioning.
* Viva Question: Which artery is the primary source of haemorrhage in the tonsillar fossa? Answer: The tonsillar branch of the facial artery.
* Trap: Assuming a sleeping child post-tonsillectomy is stable. Pearl: Children often continuously swallow blood rather than spitting it out; frequent swallowing or unexplained tachycardia in a sleeping child strongly suggests concealed active bleeding.
* Trap: Attempting to vigorously remove a large tonsillar clot in an uncooperative child on the general ward. Pearl: Clot removal removes the tamponade effect; it should only be done in a controlled environment with airway equipment ready, ideally in the OT.
* Pitfall: Discharging a patient immediately after a minor secondary bleed spontaneously stops. Observation for a minimum of 24 hours in the hospital is mandatory as minor bleeds can act as "herald bleeds" preceding a catastrophic massive haemorrhage.
