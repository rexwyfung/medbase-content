# Priapism (陰莖異常勃起)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Priapism
* **Priapism (陰莖異常勃起):** Prolonged, persistent penile erection lasting >4 hours, unassociated with sexual interest or stimulation.
* **Medical Emergency:** Ischaemic variant requires urgent intervention to prevent irreversible **Corporal Fibrosis (海綿體纖維化)** and permanent **Erectile Dysfunction (勃起功能障礙)**.

### Local Hong Kong Epidemiology & Aetiology
* **Iatrogenic/Pharmacological (Most Common in HK):** Intracavernosal injections (e.g., Alprostadil/Prostaglandin E1), PDE5 inhibitors (Sildenafil), psychotropics (Trazodone, Chlorpromazine), antihypertensives (Alpha-blockers).
* **Haematological:** Leukaemia (especially CML → hyperviscosity). Sickle cell anaemia is classically taught but **rare** in the local HK Chinese population.
* **Neurological:** Spinal cord injury, autonomic neuropathy.
* **Trauma:** Perineal trauma → straddle injury causing arterial-lacunar fistula (Non-ischaemic).

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Surgical Anatomy of Erection
* **Corpora Cavernosa (陰莖海綿體):** Paired dorsal cylinders responsible for erection; uniformly engorged in priapism.
* **Corpus Spongiosum (尿道海綿體) & Glans Penis (陰莖頭):** Ventral cylinder housing the urethra; classically flaccid and spared in ischaemic priapism due to separate venous drainage.
* **Arterial Supply:** Internal pudendal artery → Penile artery → Cavernosal/Helicine arteries.
* **Venous Drainage:** Subtunical venules → Emissary veins → Deep dorsal vein.

### Pathophysiology of Priapism Subtypes
* **Ischaemic (Low-Flow / Veno-occlusive):** Failure of venous outflow → compartment syndrome within tunica albuginea → ↑ intra-cavernosal pressure → cessation of arterial inflow → tissue hypoxia, acidosis, and necrosis.
* **Non-Ischaemic (High-Flow / Arterial):** Disruption of cavernosal artery (usually blunt perineal trauma) → unregulated arterial inflow into lacunar spaces → continuous high flow without ischaemia.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Clinical Presentation of Ischaemic Priapism
* **Pain:** Severe, progressive penile pain.
* **Rigidity:** Extremely rigid, board-like shafts (**Corpora Cavernosa**).
* **Glans Sparing:** Soft, flaccid **Glans Penis** and **Corpus Spongiosum**.

### Clinical Presentation of Non-Ischaemic Priapism
* **Pain:** Painless or minimal discomfort.
* **Rigidity:** Semi-rigid, pulsatile erection; compressible.
* **History:** Recent history of straddle injury or perineal trauma.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Diagnostic Matrix: Ischaemic vs Non-Ischaemic Priapism
| Discriminatory Feature | **Ischaemic (Low-Flow)** | **Non-Ischaemic (High-Flow)** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Pathology** | Veno-occlusive | Arterio-lacunar fistula |
| **Pain Level** | Severe | Painless |
| **Penile Rigidity** | Fully rigid (board-like) | Semi-rigid (compressible) |
| **Cavernosal Blood Gas** | Hypoxic, Hypercapnic, Acidotic | Normoxic, Normocapnic, Normal pH |
| **Doppler USS** | ↓ or Absent arterial flow | ↑ Turbulent arterial flow |

### Stuttering (Recurrent) Priapism
* **Definition:** Recurrent, self-limiting episodes of priapism.
* **Aetiology:** Typically associated with haematological disorders or idiopathic; prophylactic PDE5i paradoxically used for long-term prevention.

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup

### 1st Line Diagnostic Investigation (Gold Standard)
* **Cavernosal Blood Gas (CBG):** Aspirate blood directly from lateral corpus cavernosum.
* **Ischaemic Criteria:** pO2 <30 mmHg, pCO2 >60 mmHg, pH <7.25 (dark, sludgy blood).
* **Non-Ischaemic Criteria:** pO2 >90 mmHg, pCO2 <40 mmHg, pH >7.35 (bright red arterial blood).

### Mandatory Imaging
* **Penile Doppler Ultrasound Scan (USS):** Confirms flow status (Ischaemic = no flow; Non-ischaemic = high flow/fistula). Identifies exact anatomical location of fistula for potential embolization.

### Pre-operative Systemic Bloods
* **Complete Blood Picture (CBP) + Smear:** Exclude leukaemia and severe thrombocytosis.
* **Toxicology Screen:** Detect illicit drugs (cocaine, amphetamines) or psychotropics.
* **Haemoglobin Electrophoresis:** Indicated if patient is of African/Mediterranean descent to rule out Sickle Cell Trait/Disease.

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications

### HA Protocol for Ischaemic Priapism (Urgent)
* **Step 1: Corporal Aspiration:** Insert 19G or 21G butterfly needle into proximal lateral aspect of corpus cavernosum (avoiding dorsal neurovascular bundle and ventral urethra). Aspirate 20-30 mL of sludgy blood until bright red blood appears.
* **Step 2: Corporal Irrigation:** Flush with sterile normal saline to clear micro-thrombi.
* **Step 3: Intracavernosal Sympathomimetics:** Inject **Phenylephrine** (pure α1-agonist) 100-200 mcg every 3-5 mins (Max cumulative dose: 1 mg). Promotes cavernosal smooth muscle contraction and venous outflow.
* **Monitoring Requirement:** Strict continuous BP and ECG monitoring required during Phenylephrine injection (Risk of severe hypertension and reflex bradycardia).

### Surgical Indications for Ischaemic Priapism
* **Indication:** Failure of aspiration, irrigation, and sympathomimetic injections after 1 hour, or prolonged presentation (>24 hours).

### Management of Non-Ischaemic Priapism
* **1st Line:** Conservative (Observation, ice packs, perineal compression). 60% resolve spontaneously.
* **2nd Line:** Super-selective arterial embolization (using absorbable gelatin sponge/Gelfoam) to occlude fistula while preserving overall penile blood supply.

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Surgical Shunting Principles (Step-up Approach)
* **Distal Shunts (Cavernoso-glandular):** Creates fistula between distal corpora cavernosa and glans to allow venous drainage into corpus spongiosum.
  * **Winter’s Procedure:** Percutaneous core biopsy (Tru-Cut) needle via glans into corpora.
  * **Ebbehøj Procedure:** Percutaneous scalpel slit via glans.
  * **Al-Ghorab Procedure:** Open excision of tunica albuginea tips via dorsal glans incision.
* **Proximal Shunts (Cavernoso-spongiosum):**
  * **Quackels Procedure:** Open perineal anastomosis between corpus cavernosum and corpus spongiosum.

### Critical Consent Issues
* **Erectile Dysfunction (勃起功能障礙):** Explicitly warn patient: >90% risk of permanent ED if ischaemia >24 hours, and 100% if >36 hours, regardless of successful detumescence.
* **Surgical Complications:** Shunt failure requiring repeat surgery/proximal shunt, urethral injury, glans necrosis, bleeding, infection.

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Immediate Post-operative Care
* **Penile Care:** Apply firm compressive penile dressing (Coban). Ice packs to perineum/genitalia.
* **Monitoring:** Hourly observation of penile flaccidity. Re-examine immediately if pain returns or rigidity increases.
* **Catheterisation:** Indwelling Foley catheter if urethral injury suspected or patient unable to void due to oedema.

### Medical Rehabilitation
* **Antibiotics:** Prophylactic oral antibiotics (e.g., Co-amoxiclav) to prevent cavernositis.
* **Long-term:** Consider early insertion of malleable penile prosthesis (within weeks) if prolonged ischaemia >48 hours to prevent severe corporal scarring and loss of penile length.

## 9. Surgical Complications & Prognosis

### Early Complications
* **Recurrent Priapism:** Indicates shunt closure; necessitates immediate return to theatre for a more proximal shunt.
* **Bleeding/Haematoma:** From shunt site or aspiration punctures.
* **Wound Infection / Cavernositis:** Potentially leading to systemic sepsis.

### Late Complications & Prognosis
* **Corporal Fibrosis (海綿體纖維化):** Necrosis of smooth muscle replaced by dense fibrotic scar tissue.
* **Permanent Impotence:** Direct result of fibrosis; renders future medical ED treatments (PDE5i, injections) ineffective, mandating surgical penile implant.
* **Penile Shortening/Curvature:** Due to asymmetric fibrotic contracture.

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: How do you clinically differentiate ischaemic from non-ischaemic priapism based strictly on palpation? Answer: Ischaemic priapism presents with fully rigid, board-like corpora cavernosa but a completely soft glans and corpus spongiosum. Non-ischaemic presents with a semi-rigid, pulsatile, and globally compressible penis.
* Trap: Administering intracavernosal phenylephrine without cardiovascular monitoring. Pearl: Phenylephrine is a potent alpha-1 agonist that enters the systemic circulation, causing severe peripheral vasoconstriction (hypertension) and secondary reflex bradycardia. Continuous ECG and BP monitoring is strictly mandatory.
* Pitfall: Delaying surgical shunting to attempt repeated aspirations over several hours. Irreversible smooth muscle necrosis and corporal fibrosis begin after 24 hours of ischaemia. If aspiration and phenylephrine fail within 1 hour, proceed immediately to a distal surgical shunt.
* Viva Question: What is the defining cavernosal blood gas parameter for ischaemic priapism? Answer: The aspirate will be profoundly hypoxic and acidotic (pO2 < 30 mmHg, pCO2 > 60 mmHg, pH < 7.25), resembling dark venous blood.
