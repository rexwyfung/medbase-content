# Tonsillitis & Pharyngitis (扁桃體炎與咽炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Tonsillitis (急性扁桃體炎)
* **Acute Tonsillitis (急性扁桃體炎)**: Acute inflammation of the palatine tonsils, often with purulent exudate. 
* **Pharyngitis (咽炎)**: Inflammation of the posterior oropharynx; frequently co-exists as pharyngotonsillitis.

### Hong Kong Epidemiology & Pathogenic Profile
* **Viral (70-80%)**: Rhinovirus, Adenovirus, Influenza, Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV) (EB病毒).
* **Bacterial (20-30%)**: Group A β-haemolytic Streptococcus (GAS) / *Streptococcus pyogenes* (化膿性鏈球菌) is most common.
* **Epidemiology**: Peak incidence in school-aged children (5-15 yrs); seasonal peaks in HK during winter and spring.

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Surgical Anatomy of Waldeyer's Ring (韋氏環)
* **Components**: Pharyngeal tonsils (adenoids), tubal tonsils, palatine tonsils, lingual tonsils.
* **Palatine Tonsil Bed**: Superior constrictor muscle (上咽縮肌).
* **Arterial Supply**: Tonsillar branch of facial artery (面動脈扁桃體支), ascending pharyngeal artery, dorsal lingual artery.
* **Venous Drainage**: Paratonsillar vein → internal jugular vein (頸內靜脈).
* **Innervation**: Glossopharyngeal nerve (CN IX) (舌咽神經) → causes referred otalgia (反射性耳痛).

### Pathophysiology of Tonsillar Infection
* **Mechanism**: Pathogen invasion → inflammatory cascade → engorgement of lymphoid tissue.
* **Cryptic Involvement**: Accumulation of debris/pus in tonsillar crypts (扁桃體隱窩) → forms visible white exudates.
* **Progression**: Unresolved infection → spread beyond tonsillar capsule → Peritonsillar abscess (Quinsy) (扁桃體周圍膿腫) or deep neck space infections.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Key Symptoms (Patient Presentation)
* **Odynophagia (吞嚥痛)** 
* **Dysphagia (吞嚥困難)**
* **Fever (發燒)** (>38°C)
* **Referred otalgia (反射性耳痛)**
* **Constitutional**: Malaise, headache, anorexia.

### Key Clinical Signs (Physical Examination)
* **Erythematous, enlarged tonsils (扁桃體紅腫)**
* **Purulent tonsillar exudates (膿性分泌物)**
* **Tender cervical lymphadenopathy (頸部淋巴結腫大)** (esp. jugulodigastric node).
* **Fetor oris (口臭)**
* **Voice change**: "Hot potato" voice (if progressing to Quinsy).

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Differential Diagnosis of Sore Throat
| Condition | Key Discriminatory Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Bacterial Tonsillitis** | Exudates, +ve Centor criteria, rapid onset, fever. |
| **Viral Pharyngitis** | Cough, coryza, conjunctivitis, absence of exudates. |
| **Infectious Mononucleosis (傳染性單核細胞增多症)** | EBV, massive tonsils, false membrane, hepatosplenomegaly, atypical lymphocytes. |
| **Peritonsillar Abscess (Quinsy)** | Unilateral swelling, uvula deviation (懸雍垂偏移), trismus (牙關緊閉). |
| **Diphtheria (白喉)** | Grey pseudomembrane (bleeds on peeling), unimmunized patient. |
| **Epiglottitis (會厭炎)** | Drooling, stridor, tripod position, minimal tonsillar swelling. |

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)

### Clinical Scoring: Centor Criteria (Centor評分)
* **C**: Cough absent (+1)
* **E**: Exudates on tonsils (+1)
* **N**: Nodes (tender anterior cervical lymphadenopathy) (+1)
* **T**: Temperature >38°C (+1)
* **Age modifier (Modified McIsaac)**: <15 yrs (+1); >44 yrs (-1). 
* **Score ≥3**: High probability of GAS → consider empirical Abx or RADT.

### Bedside & Laboratory Investigations
* **Rapid Antigen Detection Test (RADT)**: High specificity for GAS, rapid result.
* **Throat Swab Culture (咽拭子培養)**: Gold standard for GAS; guides Abx sensitivity.
* **Complete Blood Count (CBC)**: ↑ WBC, ↑ Neutrophils (bacterial). Atypical lymphocytes (EBV).
* **Monospot Test (單核細胞增多症斑點試驗)**: +ve in EBV infection.

### Imaging Modalities
* **Lateral Neck X-ray**: Rule out epiglottitis (Thumbprint sign).
* **Contrast CT Neck (頸部電腦掃描)**: Indicated if deep neck space infection (深頸部感染) or retro-pharyngeal abscess suspected.

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)

### Conservative & Symptomatic Management
* **Hydration**: Aggressive oral or IV fluids (if severe dysphagia).
* **Analgesia/Antipyretics**: Paracetamol, NSAIDs.
* **Topical**: Benzydamine (Difflam) gargle/spray.

### Medical Management (Hong Kong HA Prescribing Protocol)
* **Viral**: Supportive care only; STRICTLY avoid Abx.
* **Bacterial (1st Line)**: **Oral Penicillin V** x 10 days.
* **Bacterial (Penicillin Allergic)**: **Macrolides** (e.g., Erythromycin, Clarithromycin).
* **Quinsy/Severe Inpatient**: **IV Amoxicillin-Clavulanate (Augmentin)** + needle aspiration/incision & drainage (切開引流).
* **Contraindication**: NEVER give Ampicillin/Amoxicillin if EBV is suspected → causes severe maculopapular rash (斑丘疹).

### Surgical Indications for Tonsillectomy (扁桃體切除術)
* **Recurrent Acute Tonsillitis (SIGN Guidelines)**: 
  * **≥7 episodes** in 1 year.
  * **≥5 episodes/year** for 2 consecutive years.
  * **≥3 episodes/year** for 3 consecutive years.
* **Previous Peritonsillar Abscess (Quinsy)**: Especially if recurrent.
* **Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA) (阻塞性睡眠窒息症)**: Secondary to tonsillar hypertrophy.
* **Suspected Malignancy**: Asymmetrical tonsillar enlargement (rule out lymphoma/squamous cell carcinoma).

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Key Operative Principles of Tonsillectomy
* **Anaesthesia**: General Anaesthesia with endotracheal intubation.
* **Position**: Rose position (neck extended, shoulder roll, Boyle-Davis mouth gag).
* **Technique**: Extracapsular dissection.
* **Modality**: Cold steel (tonsil snare/dissector) + bipolar diathermy haemostasis OR Coblation tonsillectomy (射頻消融術) (increasingly preferred in HA for ↓ thermal damage).

### Pre-operative Workup & Consent Issues
* **Consent - Haemorrhage**: ~1-2% primary, ~3-5% secondary risk.
* **Consent - Dental Injury**: Risk from rigid mouth gag.
* **Consent - Infection**: Secondary infection of tonsillar fossa.
* **Consent - Taste Disturbance**: Rare damage to CN IX.

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Immediate Post-operative Care
* **Airway Monitoring**: Strict observation for respiratory compromise or occult bleeding (frequent swallowing).
* **Analgesia**: Regular Paracetamol + NSAIDs. 
* **Opioid Caution**: Strictly AVOID Codeine in children (risk of fatal respiratory depression in ultra-rapid CYP2D6 metabolizers).

### Dietary Rehabilitation
* **Normal Diet**: Encourage normal/rough diet ASAP.
* **Mechanism**: Chewing and swallowing sloughs off the fibrinous eschar (痂皮) covering the tonsillar fossa → ↓ risk of infection and secondary haemorrhage.

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Early Complications (<24 hours)
* **Primary Haemorrhage (原發性出血)**: Usually occurs within first 6 hours.
* **Cause**: Inadequate surgical haemostasis or slipped ligature.
* **Management**: SURGICAL EMERGENCY. Resuscitate (ABCDE), IV fluids, blood cross-match → Immediate return to theatre for packing/diathermy/ligation.

### Intermediate Complications (24 hours - 14 days)
* **Secondary Haemorrhage (繼發性出血)**: Typically occurs day 5-10 post-op.
* **Cause**: Infection of the slough/tonsillar bed.
* **Management**: Admission, IV Abx (e.g., Augmentin), Hydrogen peroxide gargles. Rarely requires return to theatre unless massive.

### Late Complications & Prognosis
* **Nasopharyngeal Stenosis (鼻咽狹窄)**: Scarring/cicatrization (rare).
* **Voice Alteration**: Velopharyngeal insufficiency (齶咽閉合不全).
* **Prognosis**: Excellent. Eliminates recurrent tonsillitis; significant improvement in OSA parameters.

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: What is the most feared early complication of a tonsillectomy and how does the patient classically present on the ward? Answer: Primary post-tonsillectomy haemorrhage. The patient may not vomit blood initially but will present with tachycardia and continuous, frequent swallowing (swallowing blood).
* Viva Question: A 19-year-old presents with a severe sore throat, massive tonsils, and cervical lymphadenopathy. You prescribe Amoxicillin. Two days later, he returns with a widespread maculopapular rash. What is the diagnosis? Answer: Infectious Mononucleosis (EBV). The rash is a classic amoxicillin-induced maculopapular exanthem, not a true penicillin allergy.
* Trap: Assuming unilateral tonsillar enlargement in an adult is just recurrent tonsillitis. Pearl: Unilateral or asymmetrical tonsil enlargement in adults must be biopsied/excised to rule out tonsillar lymphoma or squamous cell carcinoma.
* Pitfall: Advising a post-tonsillectomy patient to eat only soft foods/ice cream for a week. Tell them to eat a normal, rough diet to physically clear the eschar, which prevents infection and reduces the risk of secondary haemorrhage.
* Viva Question: Which nerve is responsible for the referred otalgia in acute tonsillitis, and what is its anatomical relationship to the tonsil? Answer: The glossopharyngeal nerve (CN IX). It lies deep to the tonsillar bed (lateral to the superior constrictor muscle) and supplies both the tonsil and the middle ear (via Jacobson's nerve).
