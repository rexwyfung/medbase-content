# Recurrent Pyogenic Cholangitis (復發性化膿性膽管炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Recurrent Pyogenic Cholangitis
- Characterized by recurrent bacterial cholangitis (膽管炎).
- Presence of intrahepatic/extrahepatic **pigment stones (色素結石)**.
- Biliary strictures (膽管狹窄) + upstream ductal dilation.

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
- Historically known as "Hong Kong Disease" or Oriental Cholangiohepatitis.
- Endemic in SE Asia; strongly linked to rural areas and poor sanitation.
- ↓ incidence locally in recent decades due to ↑ sanitation, but remains prevalent in older generations & immigrants.
- Strong association with liver fluke **Clonorchis sinensis (中華肝吸蟲)** infection via ingestion of raw freshwater fish (e.g., grass carp).

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Pathophysiology of Pigment Stone Formation
- Portal bacteremia (usually **E. coli**) + biliary stasis (due to parasite/stricture).
- Bacteria produce **β-glucuronidase (β-葡萄糖醛酸酶)**.
- Deconjugates soluble bilirubin diglucuronide → insoluble unconjugated **bilirubin (膽紅素)**.
- Combines w/ calcium → **Calcium bilirubinate stones (鈣膽紅素結石)** (soft, earthy, brown sludge).

### Surgical Anatomy of Intrahepatic Strictures
- Predominantly affects **Left lateral sector (Segments II, III)** & **Right posterior sector (Segments VI, VII)**.
- Due to sharper branching angles of ducts → ↑ bile stasis.
- Chronic cycle: Stricture → stasis → stone → cholangitis → fibrosis → further stricture.
- End-stage → segment atrophy, compensatory hypertrophy of unaffected segments, **biliary cirrhosis (膽汁性肝硬化)**, **portal hypertension (門脈高壓)**.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Acute Cholangitis Presentation Symptoms
- **Charcot’s Triad (夏科氏三聯症)**:
  - RUQ pain (右上腹痛)
  - Fever/chills (發燒/發冷)
  - Jaundice (黃疸)
- **Reynolds' Pentad (雷諾氏五聯症)** (implies severe suppurative cholangitis):
  - +ve Shock (休克) (↓ BP, ↑ HR)
  - +ve Altered mental status

### Chronic/Inter-episode Presentation Symptoms
- Recurrent vague dyspepsia or upper abdominal pain.
- Weight loss, malnutrition, failure to thrive.

### Key Physical Examination Signs
- **RUQ Tenderness (右上腹壓痛)**.
- Hepatomegaly (肝腫大).
- Palpable gallbladder (膽囊可觸及) (if common bile duct obstructed).
- Stigmata of chronic liver disease (if end-stage).
- Scratch marks (excoriations) due to pruritus from obstructive jaundice.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Differential Diagnosis for Obstructive Jaundice & Cholangitis
| Condition | Key Discriminatory Features | Imaging Hallmark |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Recurrent Pyogenic Cholangitis** | Hx of raw fish, recurrent attacks, Asian descent | Intra/extra-hepatic stones, strictures, focal atrophy |
| **Primary Sclerosing Cholangitis (PSC)** | Assoc. w/ IBD (Ulcerative Colitis), autoimmune | "Beaded" bile ducts, intra/extra-hepatic strictures, NO stones initially |
| **Choledocholithiasis (繼發性膽管結石)** | Originates from gallbladder (cholesterol stones) | CBD stone, single stricture at sphincter, normal intrahepatic ducts |
| **Cholangiocarcinoma (CCA) (膽管癌)** | Painless progressive jaundice, severe weight loss | Mass lesion, "shouldering" stricture, upstream dilation only |

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)

### Baseline Laboratory Investigations
- **CBC**: ↑ WBC (neutrophilia), ↓ Hb (anemia of chronic disease).
- **LFTs**: ↑ ALP, ↑ GGT, ↑ Direct Bilirubin (直接膽紅素).
- **Clotting Profile**: ↑ PT/INR (due to ↓ Vitamin K absorption in cholestasis).
- **Tumor Markers**: CA 19-9, CEA (to rule out secondary Cholangiocarcinoma).

### First-Line Diagnostic Imaging
- **USG Hepatobiliary (肝膽超聲波)**:
  - Quick, non-invasive, HA emergency standard.
  - Identifies dilated ducts (膽管擴張), hepatolithiasis (肝內結石) (echogenic foci w/ shadowing).
  - Detects pyogenic liver abscesses.

### Gold Standard Biliary Mapping
- **MRCP (磁力共振膽管胰管造影)**:
  - Non-invasive total biliary tree mapping.
  - Precisely maps stones, strictures, and segmental anatomy for surgical planning.
  - Identifies parenchymal atrophy.

### Therapeutic & Diagnostic Interventions
- **ERCP (內視鏡逆行膽胰管造影)**:
  - 1st-line for acute extrahepatic decompression.
  - Diagnostic (brush cytology) + Therapeutic (sphincterotomy, stone extraction, stenting).
- **PTBD (經皮穿刺肝膽管引流術)**:
  - Indicated if ERCP fails or for isolated intrahepatic obstruction.

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)

### Immediate Sepsis Management (HA Protocol)
- NPO (禁食).
- Aggressive IV fluid resuscitation.
- **Empiric IV Antibiotics**: Cefuroxime + Metronidazole OR Piperacillin/Tazobactam.
- Correct coagulopathy (IV Vitamin K).
- **Urgent Decompression**: ERCP or PTBD within 24h if no clinical improvement.

### Elective Surgical Indications
- Recurrent cholangitis despite endoscopic clearance.
- Refractory intrahepatic stones (肝內結石) behind strictures.
- Destroyed/atrophic liver segments (萎縮肝段) (e.g., Segments II/III).
- High suspicion of concomitant **Cholangiocarcinoma (膽管癌)**.
- Associated liver abscess failing percutaneous drainage.

### Interventional Radiology & Endoscopy Alternatives
- **PTCS (Percutaneous Transhepatic Cholangioscopy)**:
  - Serial dilation of PTBD tract → cholangioscopic electrohydraulic lithotripsy (EHL) or laser stone fragmentation.

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Core Surgical Procedures for RPC
- **Hepatectomy (肝切除術)** (Gold Standard for localized disease):
  - e.g., Left lateral sectionectomy for destroyed Segments II/III.
  - Removes stones, strictures, and the future risk of CCA in that segment.
- **Biliary Bypass (Roux-en-Y Hepaticojejunostomy)**:
  - For extensive extrahepatic strictures & massively dilated CBD.
- **Cutaneous Hepaticojejunostomy (Access loop)**:
  - Suturing the Roux limb to the abdominal wall to allow easy percutaneous endoscopic access for future stone recurrences.
- **Cholecystectomy (膽囊切除術)**:
  - Routinely performed concurrently to remove reservoir of infection.

### Intraoperative Surgical Principles
- Complete stone clearance is mandatory.
- Intraoperative ultrasonography (IOUS) to map remaining stones.
- Intraoperative flexible cholangioscopy (膽管鏡) to inspect ducts & extract residual fragments.

### Pre-operative Surgical Consent
- Standard risks: Bleeding, infection, anesthesia.
- Specific risks: **Bile leak (膽汁漏)**, Post-hepatectomy liver failure (PHLF).
- High recurrence rate of stones (~20-30%) requiring future interventions.
- Possibility of permanent or long-term PTBD tubes.

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Immediate Post-operative Care
- ICU/HDU monitoring for major hepatectomies.
- ERAS protocol: Early mobilization, early enteral feeding when bowel function returns.
- Monitor abdominal drains closely for bleeding or bile (check fluid bilirubin level if suspicious).

### Biliary Drain Management Protocol
- Keep T-tube or external stents open to gravity initially.
- **T-tube Cholangiogram (T型管造影)** at post-op Day 10-14.
- If no leak & good flow to duodenum → trial of clamping.
- Do NOT remove T-tube before 3 weeks (allows mature tract formation to prevent biliary peritonitis).

### Long-term Medical Therapy & Surveillance
- Eradicate parasite: **Praziquantel (吡喹酮)** if active *Clonorchis* infection confirmed via stool O&P.
- Ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA) has limited efficacy for pigment stones but sometimes used.
- Lifelong surveillance (USG/Tumor markers) due to permanent ↑ CCA risk.

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Early Surgical Complications (<30 days)
- **Bile leak (膽汁漏)** → biloma or biliary peritonitis.
- **Subphrenic abscess (膈下膿腫)**.
- Post-hepatectomy liver failure (PHLF) (especially if background biliary cirrhosis).
- Sepsis / Septic shock.

### Late Surgical Complications & Sequelae
- Stricture recurrence at hepaticojejunostomy anastomosis.
- Recurrent intrahepatic stone formation.
- Secondary biliary cirrhosis → portal hypertension → variceal bleeding.

### Oncological Prognosis
- Chronic inflammation → dysplasia → **Cholangiocarcinoma (膽管癌)**.
- ~5-10% lifetime risk.
- Poor prognosis once CCA develops within RPC background due to delayed presentation (symptoms mimic baseline disease).

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
- Viva Question: What is the most common anatomical site for intrahepatic stones in RPC? Answer: Left lateral sector (Segments II and III) and right posterior sector (Segments VI and VII), likely due to sharper angulation of ducts causing stasis.
- Viva Question: Why do we prefer hepatectomy over simple drainage for unilateral localized RPC? Answer: Hepatectomy removes the stones, the primary stricture causing stasis, the atrophic parenchyma, and the localized high-risk tissue for cholangiocarcinoma.
- Trap: Diagnosing RPC as simple gallbladder-derived choledocholithiasis. Pearl: Look for the defining features of RPC: stones are brown pigment (not cholesterol), located intrahepatically (often upstream of strictures), and associated with intrahepatic ductal dilation, which is not seen in simple secondary CBD stones.
- Pitfall: Removing a surgical T-tube too early post-operatively. The cutaneous tract takes at least 14-21 days to mature; premature removal causes catastrophic biliary peritonitis.
- Trap: Assuming a mass in an RPC patient is just an inflammatory pseudotumor. Pearl: Any new mass, distinct stricture with abrupt cut-off, or disproportionate ductal dilation in an RPC patient must be treated as Cholangiocarcinoma until proven otherwise.
