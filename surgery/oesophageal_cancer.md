# Oesophageal Cancer (食道癌)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Definition of Oesophageal Cancer
* **Malignant proliferation** of oesophageal epithelial cells.
* Dominant histological subtypes: **Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC) (鱗狀細胞癌)** and **Adenocarcinoma (AdenoCa) (腺癌)**.

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
* Ranked among top 10 cancer killers in HK men.
* Male-to-female ratio: ~4:1 to 5:1.
* Peak incidence: **60–70 years old**.
* Histological distribution in HK: Historically **>80–90% SCC**, though **AdenoCa** is rising in Westernized cohorts.

### HK Risk Factors for Squamous Cell Carcinoma
* Strongest links: **Chronic alcohol consumption** and **heavy tobacco smoking**.
* Dietary factors: Intake of **very hot soups/drinks**, pickled foods, nitrosamines.
* Underlying conditions: **Achalasia (賁門失弛緩症)**, **Plummer-Vinson syndrome**, prior radiation.

### HK Risk Factors for Adenocarcinoma
* Primary driver: **Gastro-oesophageal Reflux Disease (GORD) (胃食管反流病)**.
* Precursor lesion: **Barrett's Oesophagus (巴雷特食道)** (intestinal metaplasia: stratified squamous → simple columnar epithelium with goblet cells).
* Metabolic factors: **Obesity** and high fat intake.


## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Oesophageal Wall Layers
* Lack of **serosal layer (漿膜層)** (except intra-abdominal segment) → early transmural invasion and local mediastinal spread.
* **Mucosa**: Stratified squamous (SCC origin).
* **Submucosa**: Contains dense, extensive **longitudinal lymphatic networks** → early skip metastases.
* **Muscularis propria**: Inner circular, outer longitudinal.

### Anatomical Segments
* **Cervical**: Cricopharyngeus (C6) to thoracic inlet (T1) (15–18 cm from incisors).
* **Upper Thoracic**: Thoracic inlet to tracheal bifurcation (T4/5) (18–24 cm).
* **Middle Thoracic**: Tracheal bifurcation to lower pulmonary vein (T7/8) (24–32 cm).
* **Lower Thoracic / Junctional**: Lower pulmonary vein to GOJ (T11) (32–40 cm).
![image|407](https://pub-f0606dedf6d94e4bb5b1999cbd065914.r2.dev/paediatrics/7973948d3d8382b57d36b7e04bf040b0.png)

### Lymphatic Drainage Distribution
* Upper third: Drainage to **cervical lymph nodes** (deep cervical, supraclavicular).
* Middle third: Drainage to **mediastinal lymph nodes** (paratracheal, subcarinal, paraoesophageal).
* Lower third: Drainage to **coeliac** and **left gastric lymph nodes**.
![image|341](https://pub-f0606dedf6d94e4bb5b1999cbd065914.r2.dev/paediatrics/dad4b47d75988efbe2f8ed4154ab9fb7.png)

### Advanced Pathophysiology & Longitudinal Spread
* Extensive submucosal lymphatics allow **skip lesions** several centimetres away from primary bulk.
* Local invasion involves critical mediastinal structures:
	* **Trachea/bronchus** (→ tracheooesophageal fistula)
	* **Recurrent laryngeal nerve** (→ hoarseness)
	* **Aorta** (→ catastrophic haemorrhage)

### Siewert Classification of Gastro-oesophageal Junction (GOJ) Tumours
| Class                | Description                                | Management Approach                                              |
| :------------------- | :----------------------------------------- | :--------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Siewert Type I**   | Epicentre **1 to 5 cm above** GOJ          | Treated as oesophageal cancer (Transthoracic Oesophagectomy)     |
| **Siewert Type II**  | Epicentre **1 cm above to 2 cm below** GOJ | Cardia cancer; managed via extended gastrectomy or esophagectomy |
| **Siewert Type III** | Epicentre **2 to 5 cm below** GOJ          | Treated as gastric cancer (Radical Gastrectomy)                  |

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Key Gastrointestinal Symptoms
* Progressive **Dysphagia (吞嚥困難)**: Initially solids, progresses rapidly to liquids.
* **Odynophagia (吞嚥疼痛)**: Indicates deep ulceration or mediastinal invasion.
* Persistent **Regurgitation (反流)** of undigested food.
* **Weight loss (體重減輕)**: Rapid, profound; due to cachexia and mechanical starvation.

### Key Respiratory & Neurological Symptoms
* **Hoarseness of voice (聲音嘶啞)**: Indicates invasion of the **Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN)**.
* Chronic **cough or choking** after swallowing: Suggests development of a **Tracheooesophageal Fistula (TEF)**.
* Persistent, boring **back or retrosternal pain**: Suggests direct spinal or aortic invasion.

### Key Physical Signs
* **Cachexia (惡病質)**: Severe muscle wasting and subcutaneous fat loss.
* Palpable **Virchow's Node (維爾肖黎氏淋巴結)**: Left supraclavicular fossa metastasis.
* **Hepatomegaly**: Indicates advanced haematogenous visceral metastasis.
* **Aspiration pneumonia signs**: Basal crepitations, dullness to percussion.


## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Oesophageal Cancer Differentials and Features
| Diagnosis                          | Discriminatory Clinical & Imaging Features                                                                                              |
| :--------------------------------- | :-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Oesophageal Cancer**             | Progressive dysphagia (solids → liquids), older age, severe weight loss. OGD shows **friable, exophytic mass or malignant ulceration**. |
| **Benign Oesophageal Stricture**   | Long history of GORD, static or slowly progressive dysphagia. OGD shows **smooth concentric narrowing**, biopsy -ve for malignancy.     |
| **Achalasia (賁門失弛緩症)**             | Intermittent dysphagia for *both* solids and liquids simultaneously, paradoxical. Barium swallow shows **"Bird's beak"** appearance.    |
| **Oesophageal Motility Disorders** | Non-cardiac chest pain, intermittent dysphagia triggered by cold liquids. Confirmed via **high-resolution manometry**.                  |
| **Pharyngeal Pouch (Zenker's)**    | Regurgitation of undigested food hours after meals, halitosis, gurgling neck mass. Diagnosed via **barium swallow** (avoid blind OGD).  |


## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup

### First-Line Diagnostic Investigations
* **Oesophago-gastro-duodenoscopy (OGD) (胃鏡檢查)**: Direct visualization of mucosal mass, localization from incisors.
* **Endoscopic Biopsy**: Minimum 4-6 bites for definitive histological classification (SCC vs AdenoCa).
* **Barium Swallow**: Optional; demonstrates mucosal irregularity, length of stricture, and synchronous motility status.

### Locoregional Staging Investigations
* **Endoscopic Ultrasound (EUS) (超聲內鏡)**: Most accurate for **T-staging** (depth of wall invasion) and **N-staging** (perioesophageal nodes).
* **CT Thorax & Abdomen**: Assesses distant visceral metastases (M-stage), bulky lymphadenopathy, and gross invasion of aorta or tracheobronchial tree.

### Systemic Staging & Fitness Workup
* **PET-CT Scan**: Gold standard for detecting occult **distant systemic metastases** and assessing metabolic treatment response post-neoadjuvant therapy.
* **Bronchoscopy**: Mandatory for tumours at or above the carina to rule out **tracheobronchial tree invasion**.
* **Cardiopulmonary Exercise Testing (CPET)** & **Spirometry**: Essential to evaluate pulmonary reserve (FEV1, DLCO) prior to major transthoracic surgery.


## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications

### Management Approach by Staging
* **Tis / T1a (Mucosal)**: Eligible for **Endoscopic Mucosal Resection (EMR)** or **Endoscopic Submucosal Dissection (ESD)**.
* **Locoregional Resectable ($\le$T3, Any N0/+, M0)**: Multimodal therapy: **Neoadjuvant Chemoradiotherapy (nCRT)** (CROSS protocol) or **Neoadjuvant Chemotherapy (nCT)** followed by **Radical Oesophagectomy**.
* **T4b (Invasion of aorta, trachea, spine) or M1**: **Palliative pathway**.
![image](https://pub-f0606dedf6d94e4bb5b1999cbd065914.r2.dev/paediatrics/7001494d79e0ea35a8d00db1fb6a27d0.png)

### Neoadjuvant Protocols (HK Hospital Authority Practice)
* **CROSS Protocol**: Paclitaxel + Carboplatin combined with concurrent radiotherapy (41.4 Gy) before surgery. Significantly improves survival in both SCC and AdenoCa.
* **Perative Nutritional Optimization**: Pre-operative **nasojejunal (NJ) feeding tube** or **percutaneous endoscopic gastrostomy (PEG)** (placed carefully outside future conduit zone) if dysphagia is severe.

### Surgical Indications & Contraindications
* **Indications**: Fit patient, resectable disease without systemic metastasis, structural integrity of airway maintained.
* **Absolute Contraindications**: Distant visceral metastases (M1), encasement of aorta (>90 degrees), airway invasion, major uncorrectable cardiopulmonary disease.


## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### McKeown Three-Stage Oesophagectomy
* **Indication**: Middle and upper thoracic tumours.
* **Stage 1 (Right Thoracotomy/VATS)**: Mobilization of thoracic oesophagus, radical mediastinal lymphadenectomy.
* **Stage 2 (Laparotomy/Laparoscopy)**: Mobilization of stomach, preservation of **right gastroepiploic artery**, formation of gastric conduit.
* **Stage 3 (Left Cervical Incision)**: Delivery of conduit to neck, **cervical anastomosis**.
![image](https://pub-f0606dedf6d94e4bb5b1999cbd065914.r2.dev/paediatrics/9ce016b1b249291dee69d238754f9217.png)

### Ivor Lewis Two-Stage Oesophagectomy
* **Indication**: Lower thoracic and GOJ Siewert I/II tumours.
* **Stage 1 (Laparotomy/Laparoscopy)**: Gastric mobilization, creation of gastric tube, coeliac lymph node dissection.
* **Stage 2 (Right Thoracotomy/VATS)**: Excision of thoracic oesophagus, **intrathoracic anastomosis** above the level of the azygos vein.
![image|406](https://pub-f0606dedf6d94e4bb5b1999cbd065914.r2.dev/paediatrics/b69dd6ea2e570d0203084b136695dda6.png)

### Transhiatal Oesophagectomy
* **Indication**: Lower third/GOJ tumours in patients with compromised pulmonary function (avoids thoracotomy).
* **Approach**: Abdominal and cervical incisions; blunt manual dissection of thoracic oesophagus through widened diaphragmatic hiatus.
* **Disadvantage**: Blind dissection, lower lymph node yield.
![image](https://pub-f0606dedf6d94e4bb5b1999cbd065914.r2.dev/paediatrics/89a03d90b8221c84e506f250b0f43a07.png)

### Key Intra-operative Principles
* **Conduit Selection**: **Stomach** is 1st choice (vascularized by right gastroepiploic arcade); **colon** or **jejunum** used if stomach resected or diseased.
* **Lymphadenectomy**: **Two-field** (abdominal + mediastinal) or **three-field** (abdominal + mediastinal + cervical) lymph node clearance.
* **Margins**: Goal is an R0 resection with >5 cm proximal and distal macro-margins.

### Surgical Consent & Specific Complications
* **Death**: Peri-operative mortality rate ~2-5%.
* **Anastomotic Leak**: Cervical leak (5-15%, lower mortality, manageable bedside); Intrathoracic leak (2-5%, high mortality, sepsis).
* **Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (RLN) Injury**: Temporary or permanent hoarseness, loss of effective cough, ↑ aspiration risk.
* **Chylothorax**: Injury to the **thoracic duct** during mediastinal dissection.


## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Immediate Post-operative Monitoring
* **ICU/HDU Admission**: Continuous invasive hemodynamic monitoring and close ventilator weaning.
* **Chest Drain Monitoring**: Track output volume, color, and presence of air leaks or milky fluid (chyle).
* **Airway Protection**: Strict head-of-bed elevation to **30–45 degrees** to prevent aspiration of passive gastric reflux.

### Enhanced Recovery After Surgery (ERAS) Pathways
* **Early Mobilization**: Out of bed on Post-operative Day (POD) 1.
* **Fluid Restriction**: Maintain euvolemia to prevent gastric conduit oedema and acute lung injury.
* **Delayed Oral Intake**: Nil by mouth (NBM) until an **Anastomotic Contrast Swallow Study** (POD 5-7) confirms absence of a leak.
* **Enteral Nutrition**: Enteral feeding via **jejunostomy tube (J-tube)** started early (POD 1-2) at a low trophic rate, stepped up gradually.


## 9. Surgical Complications & Prognosis

### Early Complications (POD 1 - 7)
* **Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS) / Pneumonia**: Triggered by long operating time, single-lung ventilation, or micro-aspiration.
* **Conduit Necrosis**: Ischaemic failure of gastric fundus due to poor micro-perfusion; requires urgent re-exploration, conduit excision, and cervical esophagostomy (spit fistula).
* **Thoracic Duct Injury (Chylothorax)**: Clear or milky drain output with high **triglyceride levels** (>1.2 mmol/L); requires NPO + MCT/TPN, or surgical re-ligation if >1L/day.

### Intermediate Complications (POD 7 - 30)
* **Anastomotic Leak**: Presents with unexplained tachycardia, tachypnoea, surgical emphysema, or purulent chest drain output.
* **Conduit Outlet Obstruction**: Delayed gastric emptying or pyloric spasm; managed via endoscopic pyloric balloon dilatation or botox injection.

### Late Complications (>30 Days) & Prognosis
* **Anastomotic Stricture**: Presents as recurrent progressive dysphagia; managed via serial **endoscopic balloon dilatations**.
* **Dumping Syndrome**: Due to loss of pyloric sphincter regulation; causes postprandial flushing, palpitations, and osmotic diarrhoea.
* **Prognosis**: 5-year survival overall remains poor (~15-20%); increases to ~40-50% for early-stage resectable disease managed with multimodal protocols.


## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: A patient develops sudden tachycardia, tachypnoea, and new-onset atrial fibrillation on POD 3 after an Ivor Lewis esophagectomy. What is the most critical complication to rule out? Answer: An acute **intrathoracic anastomotic leak**. You must urgently arrange a CT thorax with oral contrast or an urgent contrast swallow.
* Viva Question: Why is the stomach preferred over the colon for oesophageal reconstruction? Answer: The **stomach** requires only a single anastomosis (cervical or high thoracic), has a highly reliable collateral blood supply via the **right gastroepiploic artery**, and involves a shorter operative time compared to colon interposition.
* Trap: Believing that a normal barium swallow completely rules out early oesophageal cancer. Pearl: Early mucosal lesions can be completely flat and missed on contrast swallows; **OGD with biopsy** remains the definitive gold standard for investigation of dysphagia.
* Trap: Forgetting to examine the neck when a lower-third adenocarcinoma is suspected. Pearl: Always palpate for a **Virchow's node** (left supraclavicular), as its presence indicates M1 disease, rendering the patient unsuitable for curative resection.
* Pitfall: Blindly inserting a nasogastric tube (NGT) post-operatively if it accidentally dislodges. A blindly advanced tube can easily perforate the fresh cervical or thoracic anastomosis. Always insert under direct endoscopic or fluoroscopic guidance.
* Pitfall: Failing to identify trachea/bronchus invasion on preoperative CT before scheduling a middle-third squamous cell carcinoma resection. The surgeon will encounter an unresectable tumor fixed to the airway intraoperatively. Always perform a **preoperative bronchoscopy** for any tumor at or above the level of the carina.
