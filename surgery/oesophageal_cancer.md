# Oesophageal Cancer (食道癌)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Oesophageal Malignancy
* **Oesophageal Cancer (食道癌)**: Primary malignant epithelial tumor arising from the oesophageal mucosa.
* Predominant forms: **Squamous Cell Carcinoma (鱗狀細胞癌)** [SCC] and **Adenocarcinoma (腺癌)** [AC].

### Local Epidemiology and Demographics in Hong Kong
* Rank: Top 10-12 leading causes of cancer mortality in **Hong Kong**.
* Gender ratio: Significant male predominance (~4:1 to 5:1).
* Age profile: Peak incidence in 6th to 7th decades of life.
* Crude incidence rate: ~5-6 per 100,000 population annually.

### Distribution of Histological Subtypes
* **Squamous Cell Carcinoma (鱗狀細胞癌)**: Historically accounts for >70-80% of local cases in **Hong Kong**. Strongly associated with smoking, heavy alcohol consumption, hot fluids, nitrosamines, pickled foods, and low socioeconomic status.
* **Adenocarcinoma (腺癌)**: Rising incidence locally, typically located in the lower third/Oesophagogastric Junction (OGJ). Strongly associated with long-standing Gastro-oesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD) and **Barrett's Oesophagus (巴雷斯特食道症)**.

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Surgical Anatomy and Lymphatic Drainage Networks
* Oesophageal length: ~25 cm long from cricoid cartilage (C6) to cardiac orifice (T11).
* Anatomical segments:
  * **Cervical Oesophagus**: Cricoid cartilage to thoracic inlet (~15-18 cm from incisors).
  * **Upper Thoracic**: Thoracic inlet to tracheal bifurcation (~18-24 cm from incisors).
  * **Middle Thoracic**: Tracheal bifurcation to lower border of inferior pulmonary vein (~24-32 cm from incisors).
  * **Lower Thoracic**: Inferior pulmonary vein to cardiac orifice (~32-40 cm from incisors).
* Lymphatic network: Extensive, dense submucosal lymphatic plexus running longitudinally.
* Lymphatic drainage directions:
  * Upper third → **Cervical / Supraclavicular nodes**.
  * Middle third → **Mediastinal / Tracheobronchial nodes**.
  * Lower third → **Coeliac / Left gastric nodes**.
* Propensity: Early skipped metastasis can occur due to longitudinal lymphatic plexuses.

### Absence of Oesophageal Serosa and Clinical Implications
* Histological layers: Mucosa, submucosa, muscularis propria, **adventitia** only.
* Missing layer: Lacks a true **serosa** layer (except the short intra-abdominal segment).
* Clinical consequence: Absent serosa → no anatomical barrier to limit local transmural tumor extension → early direct invasion into adjacent vital mediastinal structures (e.g., trachea, main bronchus, aorta, pericardium).

### Molecular Pathophysiology of Squamous Cell Carcinoma
* Location: Most common in upper and middle thirds of the oesophagus.
* Pathogenesis: Chronic mucosal irritation (tobacco, alcohol) → squamous dysplasia → carcinoma *in situ* → invasive **Squamous Cell Carcinoma**.
* Associated molecular alterations: **TP53** mutations, **EGFR** overexpression, **Cyclin D1** amplification.

### Pathophysiology of Barrett's Oesophagus and Adenocarcinoma
* Location: Arises almost exclusively in the lower third / OGJ.
* Pathogenesis sequence: Chronic gastro-oesophageal reflux → **Metaplasia** (normal stratified squamous epithelium replaced by specialized intestinal columnar epithelium with goblet cells = **Barrett's Oesophagus**) → Low-grade dysplasia → High-grade dysplasia → Invasive **Adenocarcinoma**.
* Associated molecular alterations: **HER2/neu** amplification, **p16/CDKN2A** inactivation, **TP53** mutations.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Progressive Dysphagia Presentation
* **Progressive Dysphagia (吞嚥困難)**: Pathognomonic primary symptom.
* Pattern: Starts with solid foods, progresses sequentially to soft foods, and eventually to liquids.
* Threshold effect: Dysphagia typically manifests only when >two-thirds of the oesophageal lumen circumference is infiltrated and obstructed by the tumor mass.

### Constitutional Symptoms and Nutritional Depletion
* **Weight loss (體重減輕)**: Rapid, profound, multi-factorial (mechanical obstruction + tumor cachexia).
* **Anorexia (食慾不振)**: Contributes to rapid physical decline.
* **Odynophagia (吞嚥疼痛)**: Indicates deep transmural ulceration or local invasion into mediastinal tissues.

### Physical Signs of Advanced or Metastatic Disease
* Early presentation: Often completely normal physical examination findings.
* Cachexia: Severe muscle wasting and subcutaneous fat depletion.
* Palpable lymphadenopathy: Left supraclavicular fossa node enlargement (**Virchow’s node / 三台節**).
* Hepatomegaly: Nodular liver margin indicating haematogenous hepatic metastasis.
* Hoarseness of voice: Indicates invasion of the **Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve (反喉神經)** [RLN], more commonly the left side.
* Coughing after swallowing: Indicates development of a **Tracheo-oesophageal fistula (氣管食道廔管)** from direct anterior erosion.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

| Differential Diagnosis | Discriminatory Clinical Features | Diagnostic Strategy |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Oesophageal Cancer (食道癌)** | Elderly, progressive dysphagia (solids → liquids), profound weight loss, smoking/alcohol history. | **OGD + Biopsy** (diagnostic), CT scan (staging). |
| **Achalasia (食道賁門失弛緩症)** | Younger age, intermittent dysphagia for *both* solids and liquids simultaneously, regurgitation of undigested food, long duration. | **Oesophageal Manometry** (absent peristalsis, incomplete LES relaxation), Barium swallow ("bird's beak"). |
| **Benign Peptic Stricture (良性消化性狹窄)** | Long-standing history of severe heartburn/acid reflux, slow progression of dysphagia, stable weight. | **OGD** (smooth narrowing, rule out malignancy via multiple targeted biopsies). |
| **Diffuse Oesophageal Spasm (瀰漫性食道痙攣)** | Intermittent retrosternal chest pain mimicking angina, precipitated by cold fluids, uncoordinated dysphagia. | **Oesophageal Manometry** ("corkscrew" appearance on barium swallow, high-amplitude simultaneous contractions). |
| **Plummer-Vinson Syndrome (普拉默-文森氏症候群)** | Middle-aged females, profound iron deficiency anaemia, koilonychia, glossitis, proximal oesophageal webs. | **Barium swallow** (lateral view shows web), FBC/Iron studies, **OGD** (increased risk of post-cricoid SCC). |

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup

### First-line Diagnostic Modalities
* **Oesophagogastroduodenoscopy (內視鏡檢查/胃鏡) [OGD]**: **Gold standard** initial investigation.
  * Direct visualization: Direct assessment of tumor location, length, degree of lumen obstruction, distance from the incisors.
  * Tissue acquisition: Minimum of 4-6 targeted **biopsies** for histological confirmation of SCC vs AC.
* **Barium Swallow (鋇劑吞嚥檢查)**: Optional or used when OGD cannot pass.
  * Findings: Irregular filling defect, mucosal destruction, "apple-core" narrowing, or stricture with proximal dilation.

### Locoregional and Distant Staging Investigations
* **CT Thorax, Abdomen and Pelvis (CT TAP)**: 
  * Role: Assesses distant visceral metastases (liver, lung, bone), gross regional lymphadenopathy, and fat-plane blurring between tumor and aorta/tracheobronchial tree.
* **Endoscopic Ultrasound (內視鏡超音波) [EUS]**: 
  * Role: **Most accurate modality for T (tumor depth) and N (regional lymph node) staging**.
  * Detail: Visualizes individual histological layers of the oesophageal wall and allows Fine Needle Aspiration (FNA) of suspicious regional nodes.
* **PET-CT Scan (正電子電腦斷層掃描)**: 
  * Role: Standard staging investigation under **Hong Kong Hospital Authority (HA)** protocols for fit surgical candidates. Highly sensitive for occult distant metastatic disease (M staging) and monitors response to neoadjuvant therapy.
* **Bronchoscopy (支氣管鏡檢查)**: 
  * Indication: Mandatory for all upper and middle third thoracic tumors to rule out direct invasion into the posterior trachea or left main bronchus before surgical resection.

### Siewert Classification of Oesophagogastric Junction (OGJ) Tumours

| Siewert Type | Anatomical Definition | Surgical Management Strategy |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Type I** | AC of distal oesophagus; epicenter 1 cm to 5 cm *above* the anatomic OGJ. | Managed as oesophageal cancer → **Esophagectomy** + partial gastrectomy + 2-field lymphadenectomy. |
| **Type II** | True cardia carcinoma; epicenter 1 cm *above* to 2 cm *below* the OGJ. | Can be managed via extended total gastrectomy OR transhiatal/transthoracic esophagectomy based on size/extent. |
| **Type III** | Subcardial gastric carcinoma; epicenter 2 cm to 5 cm *below* the OGJ, invading upwards. | Managed as gastric cancer → **Radical Total Gastrectomy** + distal esophagectomy + D2 lymphadenectomy. |

### Pre-operative Cardiopulmonary Fitness Optimization
* **Cardiopulmonary Exercise Testing (CPET)**: Quantifies anaerobic threshold and $VO_2$ max to predict perioperative cardiorespiratory mortality before major thoracotomy.
* **Echocardiogram**: Evaluates left ventricular ejection fraction and cardiac function.
* **Pulmonary Function Tests (PFTs)**: Spirometry ($FEV_1$, $FVC$) and $DL_{CO}$ quantification to assess tolerance for single-lung ventilation during thoracotomy.

### TNM Staging System for Oesophageal Malignancy

| Category | Stage Criteria Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **T1** | Tumor invades **lamina propria, muscularis mucosae (T1a)** or **submucosa (T1b)**. |
| **T2** | Tumor invades **muscularis propria**. |
| **T3** | Tumor invades **adventitia**. |
| **T4** | Tumor invades **adjacent structures**: resectable (pleura, pericardium, diaphragm = **T4a**); unresectable (aorta, vertebral body, trachea = **T4b**). |
| **N0** | No regional lymph node metastasis. |
| **N1** | Metastasis in **1–2** regional lymph nodes. |
| **N2** | Metastasis in **3–6** regional lymph nodes. |
| **N3** | Metastasis in **7 or more** regional lymph nodes. |
| **M0** | No distant metastasis. |
| **M1** | Distant metastasis present (including non-regional lymph nodes). |


## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications

### Hong Kong Hospital Authority (HA) MDT Workflow
* All confirmed cases must be discussed in a **Multidisciplinary Team (MDT)** meeting (Surgeons, Medical Oncologists, Radiation Oncologists, Radiologists, Pathologists).
* Treatment intent is categorized into **Curative (根治性)** vs **Palliative (姑息性)** based on TNM stage and patient performance status.

### Indications for Curative Resection
* **Endoscopic Mucosal Resection (EMR) / Endoscopic Submucosal Dissection (ESD)**: Indicated *only* for ultra-early disease (**T1a**, well-differentiated, no lymphovascular invasion, zero node involvement on EUS).
* **Radical Esophagectomy (食道切除術)**: Indicated for medically fit patients with resectable disease (**Stage I, II, and III**; T1b–T4a, any N0-N3, M0).

### Neoadjuvant Oncological Protocols
* Standard of Care for locally advanced resectable disease (**T2-T4a or N+ve, M0**):
* **CROSS Protocol (Neoadjuvant Chemoradiotherapy)**: Used extensively in HK for both SCC and AC.
  * Chemotherapy: Paclitaxel + Carboplatin weekly for 5 weeks.
  * Radiotherapy: Concurrent external beam radiation (41.4 Gy over 23 fractions).
  * Followed by surgery in 6-8 weeks.
* **FLOT Protocol (Perioperative Chemotherapy)**: Alternative approach used for lower third **Adenocarcinomas** and Siewert Type I/II/III tumors.
  * Regimen: 5-Fluorouracil, Leucovorin, Oxaliplatin, Docetaxel.
  * Schedule: 4 cycles pre-operatively + 4 cycles post-operatively.

### Palliative Interventions for Advanced Disease
* Indication: **Stage IV** disease (T4b or M1) or patients medically unfit for major surgery.
* Aim: Restore swallowing capacity, manage pain, optimize quality of life.
* **Self-Expanding Metallic Stent (SEMS) insertion**: Deploys across the malignant stricture via OGD to mechanically maintain lumen patency for oral intake.
* **Palliative Radiotherapy / Chemotherapy**: Cytoreduction to manage dysphagia and localized bone/mediastinal pain.
* **Nutritional access**: Placement of a **Surgical Jejunostomy** or **Radiological Percutaneous Endoscopic Gastrectomy (PEG)** if stent deployment fails or is contraindicated (e.g., high risk of airway perforation).


## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### McKeown Three-Stage Esophagectomy
* **McKeown Esophagectomy (麥氏食管切除術)**: Indicated primarily for tumors located in the **upper or middle third** of the thoracic oesophagus.
* Phase 1: **Right Thoracotomy / Thoracoscopy**: Patient in left lateral position. Mobilization of the thoracic oesophagus from the thoracic inlet down to the diaphragm; radical mediastinal lymphadenectomy.
* Phase 2: **Laparotomy / Laparoscopy**: Patient turned supine. Mobilization of the stomach preserving the **Right Gastroepiploic Artery** (which acts as the primary blood supply for the future conduit); ligation of left gastric artery; coeliac lymphadenectomy; creation of a narrow **gastric tube** (conduit).
* Phase 3: **Left Cervical Incision**: Identification of the cervical oesophagus; mobilization; extraction of the specimen upward into the neck; construction of a **cervical hand-sewn or stapled anastamosis**.

### Ivor Lewis Two-Stage Esophagectomy
* **Ivor Lewis Esophagectomy (艾氏食管切除術)**: Indicated for tumors located in the **lower third** of the oesophagus or at the **OGJ / Siewert Type I**.
* Phase 1: **Laparotomy / Laparoscopy**: Patient supine. Mobilization of the stomach, preservation of the right gastroepiploic arcade, gastric tube preparation, coeliac lymphadenectomy.
* Phase 2: **Right Thoracotomy / Thoracoscopy**: Patient changed to left lateral position. Mobilization of the thoracic oesophagus, excision of the tumor with clear proximal/distal margins, radical mediastinal lymphadenectomy, and construction of an **intrathoracic anastomosis** (stapled or hand-sewn) inside the right hemithorax.

### Lymphadenectomy Extent and Conduit Selection
* **Two-field lymphadenectomy**: Standard practice. Involves radical clearance of abdominal (coeliac, left gastric, common hepatic, splenic zones) and mediastinal (paratracheal, subcarinal, paraoesophageal zones) lymph nodes.
* **Three-field lymphadenectomy**: Includes additional bilateral cervical lymph node clearance. Performed selectively for proximal/cervical lesions showing high node involvement risks.
* **Conduit of choice**: **Stomach** (gastric tube) is preferred due to its robust blood supply and excellent length. If the stomach is unavailable (e.g., previous subtotal/total gastrectomy), a **Colon interposition** (colonic graft based on left colic artery) or **Jejunal free flap** is utilized.

### High-Risk Operative Consent Issues
* **Mortality**: High perioperative mortality rate (~2-5% even in high-volume centres).
* **Anastomotic Leak (吻合口廔管)**: Severe morbidity risk; higher rate in cervical (McKeown) but higher mortality/sepsis risk in thoracic (Ivor Lewis).
* **Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve Injury**: Leading to temporary or permanent hoarseness/aphonia and high risk of aspiration pneumonia.
* **Chylothorax**: Due to accidental injury to the main **Thoracic Duct**.
* **Major Cardiorespiratory Failure**: Respiratory distress syndrome, arrhythmia (atrial fibrillation), myocardial infarction.


## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Enhanced Recovery After Surgery Respiratory Protocols
* **Early Extubation**: Aim for immediate or early extubation in the operating theatre or within hours of ICU admission to minimize positive pressure ventilation stress on an intrathoracic anastomosis.
* **Aggressive Physiotherapy**: Intensive chest physiotherapy, incentive spirometry, and frequent suctioning to prevent atelectasis and retained secretions.
* **Epidural Analgesia / Paravertebral Blocks**: Continuous thoracic epidural or regional block to achieve optimal pain control, enabling deep breathing and early mobilization without systemic opioid-induced respiratory depression.
* **Early Mobilization**: Out of bed sitting on Post-operative Day 1 (POD 1), ambulating by POD 2-3.

### Post-operative Nutritional Support and Jejunostomy Feeding
* **Nil by Mouth (NBM)** status: Typically maintained for 5-7 days post-operatively to protect the integrity of the fresh surgical anastomosis.
* **Enteral Nutrition via Feeding Jejunostomy (FJ)**: Initiated slowly on POD 1-2 with sterile water/saline, escalating gradually to full-strength enteral formula by POD 3-4 to meet caloric and protein goals.
* **Anastomotic Assessment**: A routine contrast swallow study (Water-soluble gastrografin or thin barium) or clinical evaluation is performed around POD 5-7.
* **Oral Intake Escalation**: Once zero leak is confirmed on contrast study/clinical evaluation → clear liquids initiated → graduate slowly to thick fluids → soft diet over several weeks.
* **Jejunostomy Care**: Maintained continuously at discharge until oral intake meets >75% of nutritional requirements.


## 9. Surgical Complications & Prognosis

### Acute Anastomotic Leak Management
* Timing: Typically presents between POD 4 and POD 10.
* Clinical signs: Unexplained tachycardia, tachypnea, low-grade pyrexia, sudden high drain output of enteric/salivary fluid, surgical emphysema in the neck, or rapid clinical shock.
* **Cervical Leak (McKeown)**: Less fatal. Management: Open the neck wound at the bedside, institute gentle digital exploration, pack, and allow to heal by secondary intention while maintaining feeding jejunostomy nutrition.
* **Intrathoracic Leak (Ivor Lewis)**: High mortality due to fulminant **Mediastinitis (縱隔腔炎)** and overwhelming empyema.
* Management: Urgent CT thorax with oral contrast. If small/contained + stable patient → conservative management (NBM, continuous drainage, antibiotics, endoscopic covered stent placement). If large leak + unstable patient with sepsis → urgent re-laparotomy/thoracotomy, aggressive wash-out, patch, or complete diversion/esophagostomy with cervical stoma.

### Chylothorax and Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve Injury
* **Chylothorax (乳糜胸)**:
  * Etiology: Direct intraoperative injury to the **Thoracic Duct** during mediastinal lymph node dissection.
  * Signs: Milky-white fluid draining from the chest tube once enteral feeding/fat intake begins. Confirm by checking drain fluid for elevated **Triglycerides** and presence of chylomicrons.
  * Management: Initial trial of conservative management (NBM + Total Parenteral Nutrition [TPN] OR medium-chain triglyceride [MCT] diet). If high output (>1000 mL/day for >3-5 days) → surgical re-exploration and direct **Thoracic Duct Ligation** superior to the diaphragm.
* **Recurrent Laryngeal Nerve Injury**:
  * Etiology: Thermal or traction injury during dissection around the tracheoesophageal groove, most common in McKeown cervical phase.
  * Impact: Vocal cord paralysis → hoarse voice, weak cough, loss of laryngeal protection → severe **Aspiration Pneumonia**.
  * Management: Speech therapy, assessment via laryngoscopy, temporary vocal cord injection/medialization if aspiration is intractable.

### Late Benign Anastomotic Strictures and Survival Prognosis
* **Benign Anastomotic Stricture**: Occurs months post-op, often secondary to chronic low-grade ischemia or healed micro-leaks at the anastomosis.
  * Management: Sequential endoscopic **balloon dilation**.
* Overall 5-Year Survival Rate:
  * Early localized disease (Stage I): ~60-70%.
  * Locally advanced node-positive disease (Stage III): ~15-25% without neoadjuvant therapy, improves to ~35-45% with CROSS neoadjuvant chemoradiotherapy.
  * Distant metastatic disease (Stage IV): Poor prognosis, median survival <12 months.


## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: Why does oesophageal cancer have an inherently worse prognosis and higher rate of early local invasion compared to gastric or colonic cancer? Answer: The oesophagus lacks a protective outer **serosal layer** (adventitia only), allowing early direct invasion into adjacent mediastinal vital organs. Additionally, it contains an extensive longitudinal submucosal lymphatic plexus that facilitates early skipped nodal metastases.
* Viva Question: A patient develops sudden atrial fibrillation and new-onset tachycardia on Post-operative Day 5 following an Ivor Lewis esophagectomy. What is your primary differential diagnosis and immediate action? Answer: Anastomotic leak with secondary mediastinitis. New-onset arrhythmias or unexplained tachycardia are frequently the earliest signs of a leak before frank sepsis occurs. Immediate action requires an urgent CT thorax with oral water-soluble contrast and broad-spectrum antibiotic coverage.
* Trap: Believing that a normal physical examination rules out resectable oesophageal cancer. Pearl: Most patients with potentially resectable (curative-stage) oesophageal cancer exhibit completely unremarkable physical findings; physical findings like Virchow's node, hoarseness, or cachexia signify advanced, often incurable disease.
* Trap: Assuming that a patient with an upper-third thoracic squamous cell carcinoma can be routinely treated with an Ivor Lewis esophagectomy. Pearl: Upper-third tumors require a McKeown three-stage approach because an intrathoracic anastomosis cannot achieve adequate proximal oncological margins; a cervical anastomosis is mandatory.
* Pitfall: Failure to perform a pre-operative bronchoscopy on patients with upper or middle third thoracic oesophageal tumors. This can lead to an intraoperative discovery of an unresectable tumor that has completely eroded into the posterior membranous trachea or main bronchus. Always perform a bronchoscopy pre-operatively for these anatomical locations to rule out tracheobronchial tree invasion.
* Pitfall: Discharging an esophagectomy patient post-operatively without detailed nutritional planning and jejunostomy education. These patients experience significant early satiety, delayed gastric emptying, and weight loss due to vagotomy and loss of gastric reservoir function. Ensure the feeding jejunostomy is maintained functional at home until oral caloric goals are reliably attained.
