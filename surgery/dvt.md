# Deep Vein Thrombosis (深層靜脈血栓)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Deep Vein Thrombosis
* **Definition**: Formation of blood clot (thrombus) within deep veins, most commonly affecting lower extremities (calf/thigh) → obstruction of venous return & risk of embolization.
* **Classification**: Proximal DVT (popliteal vein or above) vs Distal/Calf DVT (below knee). Proximal carries ↑ risk of Pulmonary Embolism (PE) (肺栓塞).

### Hong Kong Epidemiology & HA Trends
* **Incidence**: Historically lower in Asians vs Caucasians, but ↑ rapidly in HK due to aging population, sedentary lifestyle, & ↑ major surgeries (e.g., TKR/THR).
* **Local HA Focus**: VTE prophylaxis (Venous Thromboembolism) is a mandatory quality metric in all HA surgical departments (e.g., Caprini score use).

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Virchow's Triad Pathophysiology (血栓形成三大因素)
* **Venous Stasis (靜脈瘀滯)**: Immobility (>3 days), prolonged flights, obesity, pelvic mass compressing Iliac Vein.
* **Endothelial Injury (內皮損傷)**: Surgery (esp. orthopaedic/pelvic), trauma, central venous catheters (CVC), prior DVT.
* **Hypercoagulability (血液高凝狀態)**: Malignancy (Trousseau's syndrome), pregnancy/OCP, inherited thrombophilia (Factor V Leiden, Protein C/S deficiency).

### Lower Limb Deep Venous Anatomy
* **Calf Veins**: Anterior/posterior tibial, peroneal veins.
* **Popliteal Vein (膕靜脈)**: Formed by confluence of calf veins at lower border of popliteus.
* **Femoral Vein (股靜脈)**: Continuation of popliteal vein at adductor hiatus. 
* **Iliac Veins (髂靜脈)**: External iliac joins internal iliac → Common Iliac. Left Common Iliac crossed by Right Common Iliac Artery → risk of May-Thurner Syndrome (Cockett Syndrome).

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Classic Clinical Presentation
* **Unilateral Leg Swelling (單側下肢腫脹)**: Most reliable symptom; often dependent pitting edema.
* **Calf/Thigh Pain**: Described as deep ache or cramp; ↑ with standing/walking.
* **Erythema & Warmth**: Overlying skin may be red and warm.
* **Superficial Venous Dilation**: Prominent collateral superficial veins.

### Specific Pathognomonic Signs
* **Phlegmasia Cerulea Dolens (疼痛性股藍腫)**: Massive iliofemoral DVT → near-total venous occlusion → severe cyanosis, pain, edema → risk of venous gangrene/amputation. Emergency!
* **Phlegmasia Alba Dolens (疼痛性股白腫)**: "Milk leg" in pregnancy; severe DVT + arterial spasm → pale, cool, swollen leg.
* **Homan's Sign**: Calf pain on passive dorsiflexion of foot (Unreliable: low sens/spec; rarely used in HA practice).

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Primary Differential Diagnoses

| Differential Diagnosis | Key Discriminatory Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Cellulitis (蜂窩組織炎)** | High fever, severe erythema, sharply demarcated borders, ↑ WBC/CRP. |
| **Ruptured Baker's Cyst** | Posterior knee pain pop, crescent sign (bruising below medial malleolus), confirm via US. |
| **Superficial Thrombophlebitis** | Palpable, tender, erythematous cord along superficial vein (e.g., Long Saphenous Vein). |
| **Lymphedema (淋巴水腫)** | Non-pitting (chronic), painless, Stemmer's sign +ve, bilateral or insidious onset. |
| **Heart Failure (Cor Pulmonale)** | Bilateral pitting edema, ↑ JVP, dyspnea, hepatomegaly. |

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)

### Two-Level DVT Wells Score Assessment

| Clinical Feature | Points |
| :--- | :--- |
| Active cancer (treatment within 6 months or palliation) | +1 |
| Paralysis, paresis, or recent plaster immobilization | +1 |
| Recently bedridden >3 days or major surgery within 12 weeks | +1 |
| Localized tenderness along deep venous system | +1 |
| Entire leg swollen | +1 |
| Calf swelling >3 cm compared to asymptomatic leg | +1 |
| Pitting edema confined to symptomatic leg | +1 |
| Collateral superficial veins (non-varicose) | +1 |
| Previously documented DVT | +1 |
| Alternative diagnosis at least as likely as DVT | -2 |
| **Scoring Interpretation** | **DVT Likely (≥2 points), DVT Unlikely (<2 points)** |

### First-Line Laboratory Workup
* **D-Dimer (D-二聚體)**: Highly sensitive, poor specificity. Useful ONLY to rule out DVT in "DVT Unlikely" Wells Score. Do NOT order if Wells ≥2.
* **Baseline Coagulation Profile**: PT/INR, APTT before starting anticoagulation.
* **Renal/Liver Function**: U&E, LFT to guide DOAC/LMWH dosing.

### Gold Standard Imaging Studies
* **Compression Duplex Ultrasound (多普勒超聲波)**: 1st line. +ve finding = Non-compressibility of deep veins (most sensitive sign), absent color flow, visible echogenic thrombus.
* **CT Venography (CTV)**: Used for iliac/IVC thrombosis evaluation (e.g., suspected May-Thurner) or if ultrasound is equivocal.
* **MR Venography (MRV)**: Reserved for pregnant patients where pelvic DVT is suspected and US is negative.

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)

### HA First-Line Anticoagulation Protocol
* **Direct Oral Anticoagulants (DOACs)**: Apixaban (阿哌沙班) or Rivaroxaban (利伐沙班) are 1st line in HA for unprovoked/provoked DVT. No bridging needed.
* **Low Molecular Weight Heparin (LMWH)**: Enoxaparin (Clexane) used for cancer-associated thrombosis (though DOACs gaining traction), pregnancy, or severe renal impairment (CrCl <15 mL/min → switch to Unfractionated Heparin).
* **Warfarin (華法林)**: 2nd line due to INR monitoring (Target 2.0-3.0). Requires LMWH overlap ≥5 days until INR >2.0 for 24 hours.

### Indications for Surgical Intervention / Thrombolysis
* **Phlegmasia Cerulea Dolens**: Impending venous gangrene.
* **Iliofemoral DVT**: Severe, symptomatic, acute (<14 days) in patients with low bleeding risk and good functional status.
* **Failure of Anticoagulation**: Thrombus propagation despite therapeutic anticoagulation.

### IVC Filter (下腔靜脈濾網) Indications
* Absolute contraindication to anticoagulation (e.g., active GI bleed, recent neurosurgery).
* Recurrent PE despite optimal therapeutic anticoagulation.
* Note: Routine use strictly discouraged; retrievable filters preferred and must be removed once anticoagulation is safe.

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Catheter-Directed Thrombolysis (CDT) Principles
* **Access**: US-guided popliteal vein puncture (prone position).
* **Technique**: Multi-sidehole catheter placed into thrombus → infusion of tPA (Alteplase) over 24-48 hrs + concurrent low-dose Heparin.
* **Adjuncts**: Pharmacomechanical thrombectomy (e.g., AngioJet) to aspirate clot and ↓ tPA dose. Balloon venoplasty/stenting if underlying stricture (May-Thurner).

### IVC Filter Insertion Steps & Consent
* **Approach**: Transjugular or transfemoral percutaneous deployment under fluoroscopy.
* **Positioning**: Filter apex placed infra-renal (below renal veins) to prevent renal vein thrombosis.
* **Consent Risks**: Migration of filter, IVC perforation, access site hematoma, ↑ long-term DVT risk if left in situ.

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Immediate Post-Procedural Care
* **Mobilization**: Early ambulation strongly encouraged. Bed rest is OBSOLETE and ↑ risk of propagation.
* **Monitoring**: Frequent neurovascular checks if post-CDT. Monitor access site for hematoma.

### Long-Term Rehabilitation & Secondary Prevention
* **Duration of Anticoagulation**: Provoked (transient risk) → 3 months. Unprovoked/Recurrent → Extended/Indefinite. Cancer → Long-term LMWH/DOAC.
* **Compression Stockings (漸進式壓力襪)**: Routine use to prevent Post-Thrombotic Syndrome (PTS) is controversial (SOX trial showed no benefit), but HA physios often prescribe for symptomatic relief of chronic edema.

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Acute Life-Threatening Complications
* **Pulmonary Embolism (PE) (肺栓塞)**: Clot dislodges → PA tree. Pleuritic chest pain, hemoptysis, tachycardia, Right Heart Strain (S1Q3T3 on ECG).
* **Venous Gangrene**: Consequence of untreated Phlegmasia Cerulea Dolens → requires amputation.
* **Major Bleeding**: Anticoagulation/Thrombolysis complication (e.g., ICH, GI bleed).

### Chronic Long-Term Complications
* **Post-Thrombotic Syndrome (PTS) (創傷後靜脈血栓症候群)**: Occurs in ~20-50%. Chronic venous hypertension due to valve destruction. Symptoms: Chronic pain, edema, hyperpigmentation (hemosiderin), venous ulcers (medial malleolus).
* **Recurrent DVT**: High risk in unprovoked cases without indefinite anticoagulation.

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: What is the primary difference in management between an isolated distal calf DVT and a proximal DVT? Answer: Proximal DVT mandates formal therapeutic anticoagulation due to high PE risk. Isolated calf DVT may be managed with serial duplex ultrasound surveillance for 2 weeks if asymptomatic and low risk, though anticoagulation is often still prescribed.
* Trap: Ordering a D-Dimer for a patient with a visibly swollen, painful, unilateral leg. Pearl: D-Dimer is only useful to rule out DVT in low-probability patients. A high-probability patient (Wells score ≥2) must proceed directly to a Compression Duplex Ultrasound.
* Pitfall: Stopping LMWH bridging the moment Warfarin is initiated. LMWH must be continued for at least 5 days AND until the INR is ≥2.0 for 24 hours to prevent Warfarin-induced skin necrosis (due to rapid initial drop in Protein C).
* Viva Question: What anatomical variant classically causes left-sided iliofemoral DVT in young women? Answer: May-Thurner Syndrome, where the right common iliac artery compresses the left common iliac vein against the lumbar spine.
* Pitfall: Advising strict bed rest for a patient newly diagnosed with a DVT. Early mobilization combined with therapeutic anticoagulation does NOT increase the risk of PE and helps reduce pain and swelling.
