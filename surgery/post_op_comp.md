# Post-operative Complications (術後併發症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Post-operative Complications
* Adverse events occurring within 30 days following a surgical procedure
* Classified by **Clavien-Dindo Classification (Clavien-Dindo 併發症分級)** to standardize severity reporting
* Categorized by timing: **Immediate** (≤24h), **Early** (Days 1-7), **Late** (>Day 7)
* Categorized by system: General (e.g., VTE) vs. Surgery-specific (e.g., anastomotic leak)

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
* Hong Kong Hospital Authority (HA) audits (Surgical Outcomes Monitoring & Improvement Programme - SOMIP) report overall ↓ mortality but rising morbidity due to aging population
* Geriatric patients (>65 yrs) highly prevalent in HK public hospitals → ↑ risk of cardiopulmonary complications (e.g., pneumonia, myocardial infarction)
* High local prevalence of Hepatitis B/cirrhosis → ↑ risk of post-op hepatic decompensation and coagulopathy

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Pathophysiology of Surgical Stress Response
* Tissue trauma → activation of Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal (HPA) axis & Sympathetic Nervous System (SNS)
* ↑ **Catecholamines, Cortisol, Glucagon** → Hyperglycemia, catabolism, negative nitrogen balance
* ↑ **Cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α)** → Systemic Inflammatory Response Syndrome (SIRS), fever, acute phase reactants (CRP)

### Pathophysiology of Post-operative Hypercoagulability
* Endothelial injury + Venous stasis (immobilization) + Hypercoagulability (Virchow's Triad)
* Surgery induces ↓ antithrombin III, ↓ Protein C/S, ↑ platelet reactivity → ↑ risk of **Deep Vein Thrombosis (深層靜脈血栓)** & **Pulmonary Embolism (肺栓塞)**

### Pathophysiology of Wound Dehiscence (傷口爆裂)
* Failure of fascial healing due to:
  * **Systemic factors:** Malnutrition (↓ albumin), diabetes, steroid use, malignancy, uremia
  * **Local factors:** Infection, poor blood supply, excess tension, hematoma, poor surgical technique (e.g., bites too close/far)
* ↑ Intra-abdominal pressure (e.g., coughing, ileus, COPD) pushes weakened fascia apart → evisceration

### Pathophysiology of Paralytic Ileus (麻痺性腸梗阻) vs. Mechanical Obstruction
* **Ileus:** Sympathetic hyperactivity + local inflammation + opioids → ↓ bowel motility (global). Usually resolves in 3-5 days.
* **Mechanical:** Adhesions (bands) form early post-op → physical blockage of bowel lumen → localized dilation and upstream collapse.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Key Symptoms & Signs of Post-operative Fever (術後發燒)
* **Fever (發燒):** Temp > 38°C
* **Tachycardia (心跳過速):** HR > 100 bpm (early sign of sepsis/hypovolemia)
* **Tachypnea (呼吸急促):** RR > 20 /min (early sign of atelectasis/PE/sepsis)
* **Hypotension (血壓低):** SBP < 90 mmHg (late sign of sepsis/bleeding)
* **Oliguria (少尿):** Urine output < 0.5 mL/kg/hr

### Key Symptoms & Signs of Wound Complications
* **Surgical Site Infection (SSI) (手術部位感染):** Erythema, induration, localized pain, purulent discharge, crepitus (if necrotizing)
* **Wound Dehiscence/Evisceration (傷口爆裂):** Sudden serosanguinous discharge (salmon-pink fluid) from wound, visible bowel/omentum protruding
* **Hematoma/Seroma (血腫/血清腫):** Fluctuant swelling under incision, ecchymosis, overlying skin necrosis

### Key Symptoms & Signs of Anastomotic Leak (吻合口瘻)
* Sudden ↑ abdominal pain, generalized peritonism (rebound +ve, guarding)
* Tachycardia out of proportion to fever
* Purulent/feculent discharge from intra-abdominal drains
* Prolonged/worsening ileus

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Discriminatory Features of Post-operative Fever (The "5 Ws" Timeline)

| Post-op Day | The "W" | Primary Differential Diagnosis | Discriminatory Clinical Features |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Day 1-2** | **Wind** | **Atelectasis (肺不張)** / Pneumonia | ↓ breath sounds at bases, fine crepitations, mild hypoxia. Normal CXR initially. |
| **Day 3-5** | **Water** | **UTI (尿道感染)** | Dysuria, frequency, suprapubic pain. History of urinary catheterization. |
| **Day 5-7** | **Wound** | **SSI / Anastomotic Leak** | Wound erythema/pus. Sudden abdominal pain, peritonism, feculent drain output. |
| **Day 7-10** | **Walking** | **DVT / PE (深層靜脈血栓 / 肺栓塞)** | Unilateral calf swelling/pain. Sudden pleuritic chest pain, SOB, hemoptysis, right heart strain. |
| **Anytime** | **Wonder Drugs** | **Drug Fever / Transfusion Rxn** | New medication (e.g., antibiotics). Rash, eosinophilia. Occurs shortly after blood product infusion. |

### Differential Diagnosis of Post-operative Breathlessness
* **Atelectasis/Pneumonia:** Fever, productive cough, localized crepitations
* **Pulmonary Embolism:** Sudden onset, pleuritic pain, clear CXR, ↑ D-dimer, tachycardia
* **Fluid Overload / Pulmonary Edema:** Basal crepitations, elevated JVP, bilateral pleural effusions on CXR (common in elderly HK patients post-resuscitation)
* **Myocardial Infarction (心肌梗塞):** Atypical silent ischemia in diabetics, ECG changes, ↑ Troponin

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)

### Baseline Septic Workup for Post-op Complications
* **Blood:** CBP (↑ WBC), LRFT (renal impairment, acute liver injury), Clotting profile, CRP, Procalcitonin
* **Microbiology:** Blood culture (x2 sets, before Abx), Mid-stream urine (MSU) culture, Sputum culture, Wound swab / Drain fluid C&ST
* **Arterial Blood Gas (ABG):** Check PaO2, PaCO2, lactate (↑ in sepsis/bowel ischemia)

### Radiological Investigations for Specific Complications
* **Chest X-Ray (CXR):** First-line for respiratory symptoms (consolidation, effusions, pneumothorax, subdiaphragmatic free gas)
* **CT Abdomen & Pelvis with IV/Oral Contrast:** Gold standard for suspected **Anastomotic Leak** (extravasation of contrast, fluid collections) or intra-abdominal abscess
* **CT Pulmonary Angiogram (CTPA):** Gold standard for suspected **Pulmonary Embolism**
* **Ultrasound Doppler of Lower Limbs:** First-line for suspected **DVT** (non-compressible veins, thrombus visualization)

### Clavien-Dindo Classification Assessment
* **Grade I:** Any deviation from normal post-op course without need for pharmacological/surgical/endoscopic intervention (e.g., antiemetics, antipyretics)
* **Grade II:** Requiring pharmacological treatment (e.g., blood transfusion, antibiotics for SSI)
* **Grade III:** Requiring surgical, endoscopic, or radiological intervention (IIIa: local anesthesia; IIIb: general anesthesia)
* **Grade IV:** Life-threatening complication requiring ICU management (IVa: single organ; IVb: multi-organ)
* **Grade V:** Death of patient

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)

### Immediate Sepsis Management (HA Sepsis 6 Protocol)
* Deliver within 1 hour:
  * Administer high-flow 100% O2
  * Take blood cultures (before Abx)
  * Give broad-spectrum IV antibiotics (e.g., Augmentin + Metronidazole, or Piperacillin-Tazobactam as per local HA antibiogram)
  * Give IV fluid resuscitation (Hartmann's or Normal Saline, 30mL/kg)
  * Measure serum lactate
  * Measure urine output (insert Foley catheter)

### Management of Wound Complications
* **Superficial SSI:** Open wound staples/sutures, drain pus, pack with saline gauze (allow healing by secondary intention). Antibiotics only if surrounding cellulitis or systemic signs.
* **Superficial Dehiscence:** Conservative management with VAC (**Vacuum-Assisted Closure**) dressing to promote granulation.
* **Complete Evisceration (Burst Abdomen):**
  * Immediate: Cover exposed bowel with sterile saline-soaked packs. Do NOT push bowel back in ward.
  * Definitive: Urgent return to operating theatre (OT) for washout and mass closure.

### Indications for Urgent Re-laparotomy
* **Hemorrhage:** Hemodynamic instability despite active resuscitation, rapid blood in drains (>100mL/hr)
* **Anastomotic Leak:** Generalized peritonitis, overt sepsis, severe physiological derangement
* **Bowel Ischemia:** Severe pain out of proportion to signs, ↑ lactate, acidosis
* **Burst Abdomen (Evisceration):** As above

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Principles of Emergency Re-operation for Complications
* **Source Control:** Identify and stop bleeding, close/exteriorize leak, drain pus. This is the absolute priority over anatomical restoration.
* **Damage Control Surgery:** If patient is hemodynamically unstable, acidotic, and coagulopathic (The Lethal Triad): do rapid control, temporary abdominal closure (e.g., Bogota bag / VAC), and transfer to ICU for resuscitation before definitive repair.
* **Stoma Formation vs. Primary Anastomosis:** In presence of gross contamination/peritonitis from a leak, breakdown the anastomosis, bring out ends as a stoma (e.g., Hartmann's procedure), and thoroughly wash out. NEVER construct a new primary anastomosis in a grossly septic field.

### Pre-operative Consent Issues (Warning of Complications)
* Must explicitly document warning of general risks in HA consent forms:
  * Bleeding requiring transfusion
  * Wound infection, organ space abscess
  * Deep Vein Thrombosis / Pulmonary Embolism
  * Injury to adjacent structures (e.g., ureter, bowel)
  * Need for intensive care (ICU), potential death

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Enhanced Recovery After Surgery (ERAS) Protocols
* **Pre-op:** Carbohydrate loading (Nutricia drinks up to 2h pre-op), optimize Hb and nutrition, smoking cessation.
* **Intra-op:** Minimally invasive techniques, targeted goal-directed fluid therapy, multimodal opioid-sparing analgesia (epidural/TAP blocks), avoid prophylactic drains/NG tubes.
* **Post-op:**
  * **Early mobilization (早期下床活動):** Reduces DVT, atelectasis, and insulin resistance.
  * **Early enteral feeding:** Stimulates gut motility, reduces ileus, maintains gut mucosal barrier.
  * **Early catheter removal:** Reduces UTI risk.

### VTE Prophylaxis (HA Caprini/Padua Guidelines)
* **Mechanical:** Thromboembolic Deterrent (TED) stockings, Sequential Compression Devices (SCD) for all patients intra/post-op unless contraindicated (e.g., severe PVD).
* **Pharmacological:** Low Molecular Weight Heparin (LMWH - e.g., Enoxaparin/Clexane) given post-op (usually 6-12 hrs after surgery) based on risk scoring, adjusting for renal function (e.g., 40mg daily if CrCl > 30, 20mg if CrCl < 30).

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Immediate Complications (Within 24 Hours)
* **Primary Hemorrhage:** Slipped ligature, unrecognized vessel injury.
* **Reactionary Hemorrhage:** BP rises as patient wakes up from anesthesia/re-warms, dislodging a clot.
* **Aspiration Pneumonitis (Mendelson's Syndrome):** Aspiration of gastric contents during intubation/extubation.
* **Acute Kidney Injury (AKI):** Pre-renal causes due to intra-op hypovolemia or massive blood loss.

### Early/Intermediate Complications (Days 1 to 30)
* **Infectious:** UTI, SSI, Pneumonia, Intra-abdominal abscess, Central Line-Associated Bloodstream Infection (CLABSI).
* **Gastrointestinal:** Paralytic ileus, Anastomotic leak, Clostridioides difficile colitis (due to broad-spectrum Abx).
* **Thromboembolic:** DVT, PE, Stroke, MI.
* **Wound:** Seroma, hematoma, superficial dehiscence.

### Late Complications (Months to Years)
* **Incisional Hernia (切口疝):** Bowel protruding through healed weakened fascial defect.
* **Adhesive Small Bowel Obstruction (粘連性腸梗阻):** Lifetime risk following laparotomy.
* **Stoma Complications:** Prolapse, stricture, parastomal hernia, skin excoriation.
* **Recurrence:** Of primary disease (e.g., malignancy).

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: A patient develops a sudden gush of salmon-pink fluid from their midline laparotomy wound on post-op day 6. What is the diagnosis and immediate management?
* Answer: Diagnosis is wound dehiscence (impending evisceration). Immediate management is applying sterile saline-soaked gauze to the wound, administering IV fluids, broad-spectrum antibiotics, analgesia, and urgently booking the patient for theatre for formal wound exploration and tension-free mass closure.
* Trap: Assuming every patient with post-operative oliguria (<0.5ml/kg/hr) is hypovolemic and blindly bolusing IV fluids.
* Pearl: Always rule out a blocked urinary catheter first. Flush the Foley catheter with sterile saline. If patent, then assess fluid status clinically (JVP, CRT) before bolusing.
* Pitfall: Removing an intra-abdominal drain simply because fluid volume is decreasing. Check the characteristics of the fluid; if it suddenly turns purulent or feculent, it indicates an anastomotic leak, and the drain is currently controlling the fistula.
* Viva Question: What are the criteria for SIRS (Systemic Inflammatory Response Syndrome)?
* Answer: Two or more of: Temp >38°C or <36°C, HR >90 bpm, RR >20 breaths/min or PaCO2 <32 mmHg, WBC >12,000/mm3 or <4,000/mm3, or >10% bands.
* Trap: Discharging a post-appendectomy patient on Day 4 who has spiked a new fever, assuming it is just a mild viral illness.
* Pearl: A new fever on Day 4-7 post-op is an intra-abdominal pelvic collection/abscess until proven otherwise. Examine the abdomen, perform a DRE (for boggy pelvic swelling), and order a CT Abdo/Pelvis with contrast.
* Pitfall: Constructing a primary anastomosis in a hemodynamically unstable patient with generalized fecal peritonitis. High risk of leak and death; perform damage control surgery with a stoma instead.
