# Diverticular Disease (憩室病)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Diverticular Disease
* **Diverticulosis (憩室症)**: Presence of asymptomatic acquired herniations of colonic mucosa/submucosa through the muscularis propria.
* **Diverticulitis (憩室炎)**: Inflammation/infection of diverticula.
* **Diverticular Bleeding (憩室出血)**: Painless lower gastrointestinal hemorrhage from vasa recta erosion.

### Hong Kong Epidemiology & Demographics
* **Asian Phenotype**: ↑↑ Prevalence of **right-sided diverticular disease (右側憩室病)** in Hong Kong Chinese (~70% of cases) vs left-sided predominance in Western populations.
* **Age Distribution**: ↑ Incidence with advancing age (predominantly >50 years).
* **Dietary Factors**: Strongly associated with ↓ dietary fiber intake, ↑ red meat consumption, and ↑ refined carbohydrates (Westernization of HK diet).
* **Genetic Predisposition**: Significant genetic component influencing connective tissue integrity in Asian populations leading to right-sided dominance.

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Surgical Anatomy of Colonic Diverticula
* **Pseudodiverticula (假性憩室)**: Consist only of mucosa & submucosa (lacks muscularis propria).
* **Location of Outpouching**: Occur at points of weakness where **vasa recta (直血管)** penetrate the circular muscle layer (between mesenteric and anti-mesenteric taeniae coli).
* **Right Colon (右半結腸)**: Thinner wall, larger lumen, genetic propensity in Asians.
* **Sigmoid Colon (乙狀結腸)**: Narrowest lumen, highest intraluminal pressure.

### Pathophysiology of Diverticulosis
* **Law of Laplace**: Tension = Pressure × Radius (T = PR). ↓ Lumen radius (sigmoid) → ↑ intraluminal pressure.
* **Colonic Segmentation**: Exaggerated haustral contractions create high-pressure localized chambers → mucosal herniation.
* **Connective Tissue Changes**: Elastin deposition + collagen cross-linking → ↓ colonic wall compliance with age.

### Pathophysiology of Acute Diverticulitis
* **Fecalith Obstruction**: Inspissated feces obstruct diverticular neck.
* **Microperforation**: ↑ Pressure + local ischemia → mucosal ulceration → microperforation into pericolic fat.
* **Macroperforation**: Progression of microperforation → frank rupture → **fecal peritonitis (糞性腹膜炎)** or **purulent peritonitis (化膿性腹膜炎)**.
* **Abscess Formation**: Contained microperforation walled off by mesentery/omentum.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Symptoms of Uncomplicated Diverticulosis
* **Majority**: 70-80% asymptomatic.
* **Incidental Findings**: Discovered on screening colonoscopy or CT for other indications.
* **Symptomatic Uncomplicated Diverticular Disease (SUDD)**: Altered bowel habits, mild left/right lower quadrant discomfort, bloating.

### Symptoms of Acute Diverticulitis
* **Abdominal Pain (腹痛)**: Constant, progressive. **Right Iliac Fossa (右下腹)** in right-sided (mimics appendicitis); **Left Iliac Fossa (左下腹)** in left-sided.
* **Systemic Symptoms**: Fever, malaise, chills.
* **Gastrointestinal Symptoms**: Nausea, vomiting, anorexia, constipation, or diarrhea.
* **Urinary Symptoms**: Dysuria/frequency if inflamed diverticulum irritates the bladder.

### Clinical Signs of Acute Diverticulitis
* **Vitals**: Tachycardia, pyrexia.
* **Palpation**: Localized tenderness, guarding, rebound tenderness (+ve peritonism).
* **Mass**: Palpable inflammatory phlegmon/abscess in RIF or LIF.
* **Bowel Sounds**: ↓ or absent if generalized ileus/peritonitis.
* **Rectal Examination**: Pelvic tenderness on digital rectal examination (DRE).

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Differentials for Right-Sided Diverticulitis
| Differential Diagnosis | Discriminatory Clinical & Investigation Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Acute Appendicitis (急性闌尾炎)** | Migratory pain (umbilicus → RIF), younger demographic, normal cecum on CT |
| **Right-sided Diverticulitis (右側憩室炎)** | Older demographic, lack of migratory pain, CT shows inflamed cecal/ascending colon diverticula |
| **Cecal Carcinoma (盲腸癌)** | Chronic anemia, weight loss, insidious onset, irregular mass on CT/colonoscopy |
| **Crohn's Disease (克隆氏症)** | Chronic diarrhea, skip lesions, transmural inflammation, younger demographic |

### Differentials for Left-Sided Diverticulitis
| Differential Diagnosis | Discriminatory Clinical & Investigation Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Colorectal Malignancy (大腸癌)** | Weight loss, altered bowel habits +ve PR bleeding, chronic symptoms |
| **Ischemic Colitis (缺血性腸炎)** | "Thumbprinting" on AXR, cardiovascular risk factors, sudden bloody diarrhea |
| **Gynecological Emergencies** | Ovarian torsion, ectopic pregnancy, pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) |
| **Renal Colic (腎絞痛)** | Loin-to-groin radiation, non-tender abdomen, hematuria, calculus on CT KUB |

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)

### Laboratory Investigations for Diverticulitis
* **CBC**: ↑ WBC (neutrophilia).
* **Inflammatory Markers**: ↑ CRP (correlates with severity/perforation risk).
* **Renal Function (RFT)**: Dehydration assessment, baseline for IV contrast.
* **Group & Screen (G&S)**: Preparation for potential surgical intervention.
* **Urinalysis**: Rule out UTI; may show sterile pyuria due to bladder irritation.

### Gold Standard Imaging for Diverticulitis
* **CT Abdomen & Pelvis with IV Contrast (電腦斷層掃描)**:
* **Sensitivity/Specificity**: >95%.
* **Diagnostic Signs**: Colonic wall thickening (>4mm), pericolic fat stranding, visualization of diverticula.
* **Complication Identification**: Free gas (pneumoperitoneum), pericolic fluid, abscesses, fistulae.

### Endoscopic Evaluation in Diverticular Disease
* **Acute Phase**: Colonoscopy is **ABSOLUTELY CONTRAINDICATED** (↑ risk of iatrogenic perforation).
* **Interval Endoscopy**: Colonoscopy recommended **6-8 weeks** post-resolution of acute attack to rule out underlying **colorectal malignancy (大腸癌)**.

### Hinchey Classification System
| Stage | Clinical/Radiological Findings | Management Approach |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Hinchey I** | Localized pericolic or mesenteric abscess | IV Antibiotics (Ia); Percutaneous drainage if >3-4cm (Ib) |
| **Hinchey II** | Walled-off pelvic, intra-abdominal, or retroperitoneal abscess | Percutaneous drainage + IV Antibiotics |
| **Hinchey III** | Generalized purulent peritonitis (化膿性腹膜炎) | Urgent Surgical Intervention (Laparoscopic lavage vs Resection) |
| **Hinchey IV** | Generalized fecal peritonitis (糞性腹膜炎) | Emergency Surgery (Hartmann's Procedure) |

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)

### Uncomplicated Diverticulitis Management (HA Protocols)
* **Bowel Rest**: Clear fluid diet (NPO if severe nausea/vomiting).
* **IV Fluid Resuscitation**: Correct electrolyte imbalances.
* **Antibiotic Therapy**: Broad-spectrum targeting gram-negative & anaerobes. Local HA protocol → **IV Amoxicillin/Clavulanate (Augmentin)** OR **IV Cefuroxime + Metronidazole**.
* **Duration**: Transition to oral antibiotics upon clinical improvement (5-7 days total).

### Complicated Diverticulitis Management (Hinchey I-II)
* **Abscess <3-4cm**: Trial of IV antibiotics alone.
* **Abscess >4cm**: **Percutaneous CT/US-guided drainage (經皮穿刺引流)** + IV antibiotics.
* **Failure to Improve**: If clinical deterioration despite drainage → surgery.

### Surgical Indications for Diverticular Disease
* **Emergency Indications**: Hinchey III/IV peritonitis, uncontained perforation, uncontrolled sepsis, massive uncontrollable diverticular hemorrhage.
* **Urgent Indications**: Failure of medical therapy/percutaneous drainage after 48-72 hours, intestinal obstruction.
* **Elective Indications**: Fistula formation (colovesical/colovaginal), recurrent severe attacks (individualized decision), stricture/stenosis causing chronic obstruction, inability to rule out carcinoma.

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Operative Principles for Emergency Resection
* **Goal**: Resect diseased segment containing perforation; control sepsis.
* **Hartmann's Procedure (哈特曼氏手術)**:
* **Step 1**: Resection of diseased colon (usually sigmoid).
* **Step 2**: Formation of **end colostomy (結腸造口術)**.
* **Step 3**: Closure of rectal stump.
* **Advantage**: Avoids high-risk anastomosis in a contaminated/septic field.

### Operative Principles for Primary Anastomosis
* **Damage Control Surgery**: Primary anastomosis generally avoided in Hinchey IV.
* **Alternative to Hartmann's**: Resection + primary anastomosis with a **defunctioning loop ileostomy (保護性迴腸造口)** (increasingly preferred in stable patients with Hinchey III).
* **Laparoscopic Lavage**: Controversial; limited to highly selected Hinchey III cases (washout without resection).

### Surgical Consent for Diverticulitis Surgery
* **Procedure Details**: Laparotomy vs Laparoscopy (convertible), bowel resection.
* **Stoma Consent (CRITICAL)**: Must counsel on high likelihood of temporary or permanent colostomy/ileostomy.
* **General Risks**: Bleeding, infection, damage to surrounding structures (ureter, bladder, spleen).
* **Specific Risks**: Anastomotic leak (if primary anastomosis planned), pelvic abscess.

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Immediate Post-operative Care (ERAS Protocol)
* **Analgesia**: Multi-modal (Paracetamol, NSAIDs, PCA/Epidural); minimize opioids to prevent ileus.
* **Nutrition**: Early enteral feeding (sips of water Day 0, advance as tolerated).
* **Mobilization**: Early ambulation out of bed Day 1 to prevent DVT/atelectasis.
* **DVT Prophylaxis**: LMWH (Clexane) + TED stockings + sequential compression devices (SCD).
* **Catheter**: Early removal of Foley catheter (unless pelvic dissection risks retention).

### Stoma Care and Rehabilitation
* **Stoma Nurse Involvement**: Pre-operative siting (if possible) and post-operative education.
* **Appliance Management**: Training patient on changing bags, managing leaks.
* **Reversal Planning**: Discuss timing for Hartmann's reversal (usually 3-6 months post-op, requires evaluation of rectal stump + patient fitness).

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Early Surgical Complications
* **Anastomotic Leak (吻合口滲漏)**: Presenting with fever, peritonism, feculent drain output (Days 3-7).
* **Surgical Site Infection (SSI)**: Wound infection, dehiscence.
* **Paralytic Ileus**: Prolonged absence of bowel function.
* **Bleeding**: Intraperitoneal hemorrhage or GI bleed from anastomosis.

### Late Surgical Complications
* **Fistula Formation**: Colovesical (pneumaturia/fecaluria), colovaginal.
* **Stricture**: Anastomotic stricture causing obstructive symptoms.
* **Hernia**: Parastomal hernia (旁造口疝氣) or incisional hernia.
* **Hartmann's Reversal Complications**: High morbidity procedure; many patients end up with permanent stomas.

### Prognosis and Recurrence Rates
* **Conservative Rx Prognosis**: 1/3 of patients will have a recurrent attack after initial non-operative management.
* **Recurrence Severity**: Subsequent attacks are NOT inevitably more severe, contrary to historical dogma.
* **Post-Resection**: Recurrence rate is <5-10% after definitive resection of the affected segment.

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Viva Question: What is the most common cause of massive painless lower GI bleeding? Answer: Diverticular disease (diverticulosis).
* Trap: Assuming left-sided diverticulitis is the most common presentation in Hong Kong. Pearl: Right-sided diverticular disease is significantly more prevalent in the Asian/HK population and often mimics acute appendicitis.
* Pitfall: Ordering a colonoscopy during an acute attack of diverticulitis. Avoidance: Colonoscopy is strictly contraindicated in the acute phase due to high perforation risk; defer for 6-8 weeks.
* Viva Question: How do you differentiate Hinchey III and Hinchey IV clinically? Answer: You cannot definitively differentiate them clinically or on CT; both present as generalized peritonitis. The diagnosis is made intra-operatively (purulent fluid = III, gross feces = IV).
* Trap: Recommending primary anastomosis in an unstable patient with fecal peritonitis. Pearl: Hartmann's procedure remains the safest and gold standard operation for Hinchey IV peritonitis with hemodynamic instability.
* Pitfall: Forgetting to obtain consent for a stoma in emergency diverticulitis surgery. Avoidance: Always thoroughly counsel and explicitly document consent for a potential stoma formation before emergency abdominal surgery for suspected perforated diverticulitis.
* Viva Question: What specific symptom suggests a colovesical fistula complication? Answer: Pneumaturia (passing gas in urine) or fecaluria (feces in urine), recurrent mixed-flora UTIs.
