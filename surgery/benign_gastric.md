# Benign Gastric Pathologies (良性胃部病變)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Definition of Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD)
* **Peptic Ulcer Disease (消化性潰瘍病)**: Mucosal defect in stomach/duodenum extending through muscularis mucosae into deeper layers.
* **Gastric Ulcer (GU) (胃潰瘍)**: Predominantly along lesser curvature (Incisura angularis - Type I most common).
* **Duodenal Ulcer (DU) (十二指腸潰瘍)**: Predominantly in 1st part of duodenum (D1).

### Definition of Rare Benign Gastric Lesions
* **Gastric Volvulus (胃扭轉)**: Abnormal rotation of stomach >180° → closed-loop obstruction/strangulation.
* **Dieulafoy's Lesion**: Abnormally large submucosal artery tortuously protruding through minute mucosal defect → massive hemorrhage.
* **Mallory-Weiss Syndrome (馬洛里-魏斯綜合症)**: Longitudinal mucosal tear at gastroesophageal junction (GEJ) post-retching.

### Hong Kong Epidemiology of Peptic Ulcer Disease
* **Endemic H. pylori**: Hong Kong has high **Helicobacter pylori (幽門螺旋桿菌)** prevalence (~50% adults).
* **DU > GU**: DUs historically more common, but shifting due to ↑ NSAID/Aspirin use in aging HK population.
* **Malignancy Risk**: High prevalence of **Gastric Adenocarcinoma (胃腺癌)** in HK mandates rigorous exclusion of malignancy in all GUs.

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Arterial Supply of the Stomach (胃動脈供應)
* **Left Gastric Artery (LGA)**: Branch of Celiac Trunk → supplies lesser curvature.
* **Right Gastric Artery (RGA)**: Branch of Common Hepatic Artery → supplies lesser curvature.
* **Right Gastroepiploic Artery (RGEA)**: Branch of Gastroduodenal Artery (GDA) → supplies greater curvature.
* **Left Gastroepiploic Artery (LGEA)**: Branch of Splenic Artery → supplies greater curvature.
* **Short Gastric Arteries**: Branch of Splenic Artery → supplies fundus.

### Pathophysiology of Peptic Ulcer Disease
* **Imbalance**: Aggressive factors (Acid, Pepsin) > Defensive factors (Mucus, Bicarbonate, Prostaglandins, Blood flow).
* **H. pylori (幽門螺旋桿菌)**: Gram -ve microaerophilic bacillus → produces urease → ammonia neutralizes acid → mucosal injury + hypergastrinemia.
* **NSAIDs/Aspirin**: Systemic inhibition of COX-1 → ↓ mucosal prostaglandins → ↓ mucus/bicarbonate production.

### Pathophysiology of Bleeding and Perforation
* **Bleeding (出血)**: Posterior DU erosion into **Gastroduodenal Artery (GDA)**; Lesser curve GU erosion into **Left Gastric Artery (LGA)**.
* **Perforation (穿孔)**: Anterior DU/GU erodes through serosa → spillage of gastric/biliary contents → chemical peritonitis → bacterial peritonitis.

### Pathophysiology of Gastric Volvulus
* **Organoaxial (70%)**: Rotation around longitudinal axis (Cardia to Pylorus) → highly associated with paraesophageal hernia.
* **Mesenteroaxial (30%)**: Rotation around transverse axis (Lesser to Greater curvature) → typically no hiatus hernia, ischemia common.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Symptoms of Uncomplicated Peptic Ulcer Disease
* **Dyspepsia (消化不良)**
* **Epigastric Pain (胃痛)**
* **Nausea (噁心)**
* **Early Satiety (早飽)**

### Symptoms of Complicated Peptic Ulcer Disease
* **Hematemesis (吐血)**
* **Melena (黑糞症)**
* **Sudden Onset Severe Generalized Abdominal Pain (突發性劇烈腹痛)**
* **Post-prandial Vomiting (飯後嘔吐)** (Sign of Gastric Outlet Obstruction)

### Physical Signs of Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding
* **Tachycardia (心跳過速)**
* **Hypotension (低血壓)**
* **Pallor (蒼白)**
* **Fresh Blood / Coffee-ground material on DRE (直腸指檢)**

### Physical Signs of Perforated Peptic Ulcer
* **Board-like Abdominal Rigidity (腹部僵硬)**
* **Rebound Tenderness (反跳痛)**
* **Loss of Liver Dullness** (Pneumoperitoneum)
* **Systemic Inflammatory Response Syndrome (SIRS)**

### Borchardt's Triad (Pathognomonic for Gastric Volvulus)
* **Severe Epigastric Pain**
* **Intractable Retching without Vomiting**
* **Inability to Pass Nasogastric Tube (NGT)**

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Differential Diagnosis of Epigastric Pain
| Condition | Pain Timing | Key Discriminatory Features | Diagnostic Modality |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Gastric Ulcer (GU)** | Worsens *with* food | Weight loss, anorexia | OGD + Biopsy |
| **Duodenal Ulcer (DU)** | Relieved by food, wakes at night | Weight gain, pain 2-3h post-meal | OGD (Biopsy not strictly req.) |
| **Gastric Cancer (胃癌)** | Constant, unremitting | Cachexia, Virchow's node, Anemia | OGD + Multiple Biopsies |
| **Biliary Colic (膽絞痛)** | Post-prandial (fatty meals) | RUQ pain radiating to right shoulder | USG Hepatobiliary |
| **Acute Pancreatitis (急性胰腺炎)**| Constant, radiating to back | Relief on leaning forward, ↑ Amylase | Contrast CT Abdomen |

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)

### Laboratory Investigations
* **Complete Blood Count (CBC)**: ↓ Hb (chronic/acute bleeding), ↑ WBC (perforation/infection).
* **Renal Function Test (RFT)**: ↑ Urea disproportionate to Creatinine (digested blood in UGI bleed), pre-renal AKI.
* **Liver Function Test (LFT)**: Rule out biliary pathology / portal hypertension.
* **Coagulation Profile (PT/APTT)**: Correct coagulopathy pre-op/pre-endoscopy.
* **Group and Screen (G&S) / Cross-Match**: Essential for hemorrhagic shock.
* **Venous Blood Gas (VBG)**: Assess Lactate (ischemia/sepsis) and pH.

### Gold Standard Diagnostic Investigation (Endoscopy)
* **Oesophagogastroduodenoscopy (OGD) (胃鏡檢查)**: Diagnostic & Therapeutic.
* **Biopsy Protocol**: MUST biopsy all GUs (exclude cancer). Typically 6-8 biopsies from ulcer margins.
* **Rapid Urease Test (CLO test)**: Direct testing for H. pylori during OGD.

### Radiological Investigations
* **Erect Chest X-Ray (CXR)**: Look for free gas under diaphragm (Pneumoperitoneum) in perforation (70% sensitive).
* **CT Abdomen & Pelvis with IV Contrast**: Highly sensitive for concealed perforation, extraluminal contrast leak, gastric volvulus, ruling out malignancy.

### Non-Invasive H. pylori Testing
* **Urea Breath Test (UBT) (尿素呼氣試驗)**: Test of choice to confirm eradication (HA protocol mandates off PPI for 2 weeks prior).
* **Stool Antigen Test**: Alternative to UBT.

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)

### Medical Management & H. pylori Eradication (HA Protocol)
* **Proton Pump Inhibitor (PPI)**: Omeprazole / Pantoprazole (oral or IV).
* **First-line Triple Therapy (14 days)**: Standard HA regimen = PPI BD + Clarithromycin 500mg BD + Amoxicillin 1g BD (or Metronidazole 400mg BD if Penicillin allergic).
* **Second-line Bismuth Quadruple Therapy**: For refractory H. pylori (PPI + Bismuth + Metronidazole + Tetracycline).

### Endoscopic Management of Bleeding Peptic Ulcers
* **Forrest Classification**: Guides risk of rebleeding.
* **Dual Therapy Hemostasis**: Adrenaline injection (1:10,000) PLUS mechanical (Hemoclip) OR thermal (Heater probe / Coagrasper) method.
* **High-dose IV PPI**: 80mg bolus → 8mg/hr infusion for 72 hours post-hemostasis.

### Surgical Indications for Bleeding Peptic Ulcer
* **Refractory Shock**: Hemodynamic instability despite active resuscitation.
* **Failure of Endoscopic Hemostasis**: 2 failed attempts at OGD.
* **Ongoing transfusion requirement**: >6 units packed red blood cells (PRBC) over 24 hours.

### Surgical Indications for Perforated Peptic Ulcer
* **Absolute Indication**: All surgically fit patients with evidence of generalized peritonitis / systemic sepsis.
* **Conservative (Non-operative) Management**: Highly selected cases ONLY (sealed-off perforation on contrast swallow, hemodynamically stable, minimal abdominal signs, strict NPO + IV antibiotics + PPI).

### Management of Gastric Outlet Obstruction (GOO)
* **Initial Medical Management**: NPO, rigid NGT decompression, IV hydration, correct hypokalemic hypochloremic metabolic alkalosis, IV PPI.
* **Endoscopic/Surgical**: Endoscopic balloon dilatation. If refractory → Laparoscopic Gastroenterostomy.

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Operative Steps for Perforated Peptic Ulcer (Laparoscopic/Open)
* **Thorough Peritoneal Lavage**: Massive irrigation (6-10L warm saline) of all 4 quadrants to clear purulent/biliary ascites.
* **Modified Graham Patch Omentoplasty (大網膜修補術)**: 
* **Step 1**: Identify perforation (usually anterior D1).
* **Step 2**: Take full-thickness bites across defect using absorbable sutures (e.g., 2/0 Vicryl).
* **Step 3**: Draw up a vascularized pedicle of omentum.
* **Step 4**: Tie sutures over the omentum to plug the leak (do not close the edges of the ulcer directly).
* **Step 5**: Leave surgical drain in Morrison's pouch.

### Operative Steps for Bleeding Duodenal Ulcer
* **Duodenotomy**: Longitudinal incision over D1.
* **Underrunning of GDA (U-stitch/Three-point ligation)**:
* **Step 1**: Suture cranial end of GDA.
* **Step 2**: Suture caudal end of GDA.
* **Step 3**: Suture medial transverse branch to stop collateral back-bleeding.
* **Closure**: Pyloroplasty (close longitudinal incision transversely) to prevent stricture.

### Definitive Acid-Reducing Surgeries (Historical/Refractory Cases)
* **Truncal Vagotomy + Drainage (Pyloroplasty / Gastrojejunostomy)**: Denervates stomach + guarantees emptying.
* **Highly Selective Vagotomy (HSV)**: Denervates parietal cell mass, preserves Latarjet nerve (pyloric function intact).
* **Distal Gastrectomy**: Antrectomy (removes G-cells) + Billroth I (Gastroduodenostomy) or Billroth II (Gastrojejunostomy).

### Surgical Consent and Key Risks
* **General Risks**: Bleeding, Infection, DVT/PE, Cardiopulmonary complications.
* **Specific Risks**: Re-leak of patch (peritonitis), recurrent bleeding, injury to CBD/bowel, conversion from laparoscopic to open laparotomy (剖腹手術), incisional hernia.

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Immediate Post-operative Ward Care
* **Strict NPO (Nil Per Os)**: Until bowel sounds return / passage of flatus.
* **Nasogastric Tube (NGT)**: Keep on free drainage/low continuous suction to decompress stomach and protect repair.
* **Medications**: IV PPI (high dose), Broad-spectrum IV Antibiotics (e.g., Augmentin / Cefuroxime + Metronidazole), Adequate analgesia.
* **Fluid Balance**: Strict I/O monitoring, target UO >0.5ml/kg/hr.

### ERAS Pathway and Diet Advancement
* **Day 1-2**: NPO, early mobilization, respiratory physiotherapy.
* **Day 3-4**: If flatus passed and NGT output ↓ and non-bilious → remove NGT → sips of water.
* **Day 5+**: Stepwise advancement: Fluids → Soft diet → Normal diet.

### Long-term Follow-up and Surveillance
* **Repeat OGD**: MANDATORY repeat OGD at 6-8 weeks for ALL gastric ulcers to document healing and re-biopsy (rule out underlying Gastric Cancer).
* **H. pylori Eradication Check**: UBT at 4-6 weeks post-completion of antibiotics (must be off PPI for 2 weeks).
* **Discontinuation of NSAIDs**: Switch to alternative analgesia or co-prescribe long-term PPI if unavoidable.

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Early Surgical Complications (Day 1-7)
* **Omental Patch Leak / Blow-out**: Causes recurrent peritonitis → requires emergency re-laparotomy.
* **Re-bleeding**: Intraluminal bleeding.
* **Intra-abdominal Collection / Abscess**: Presents with spiking fever, paralytic ileus → requires USG/CT-guided drainage.
* **Wound Infection / Dehiscence**.

### Intermediate & Late Surgical Complications
* **Gastric Outlet Obstruction (GOO)**: Due to scarring/fibrosis at the pylorus or D1 post-healing.
* **Incisional Hernia**: High risk in emergency midline laparotomies.
* **Complications of Radical Resection (Billroth I/II)**:
* **Dumping Syndrome**: Rapid gastric emptying of hyperosmolar chyme (Early: 30 mins, vasomotor; Late: 1-3 hrs, reactive hypoglycemia).
* **Alkaline Reflux Gastritis**: Bile reflux.
* **Blind Loop Syndrome**: Bacterial overgrowth in afferent limb.
* **Nutritional Deficiencies**: B12, Iron, Calcium.

### Prognosis of Benign Gastric Pathologies
* **Uncomplicated PUD**: Excellent with >90% healing rate post-H. pylori eradication.
* **Perforated PUD**: Mortality highly dependent on age, comorbidities, and delay to surgery (Boey Score: >24h delay, pre-op shock, severe comorbidity).

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: What is the defining anatomical landmark separating upper from lower GI bleeding? Answer: The Ligament of Treitz (Suspensory muscle of duodenum).
* Viva Question: Why do we perform a 3-point U-stitch ligation for a bleeding duodenal ulcer? Answer: To control the Gastroduodenal artery (GDA) proximally, distally, and its transverse pancreatic branch which causes retrograde collateral bleeding.
* Viva Question: What is the most common site for a benign gastric ulcer versus a malignant one? Answer: Benign usually on lesser curvature (Type I); Malignant can occur anywhere but frequently greater curvature, cardia, or fundus.
* Trap: Assuming a patient with a perforated duodenal ulcer needs a biopsy of the ulcer edge during the acute omental patch repair. Pearl: Never biopsy a duodenal ulcer during an acute emergency repair; DUs are almost never malignant, and excising the edge enlarges the defect unnecessarily.
* Trap: Discharging a patient after successful endoscopic hemostasis of a Gastric Ulcer without booking an outpatient OGD. Pearl: All Gastric Ulcers mandate a repeat OGD in 6-8 weeks to confirm mucosal healing and rule out an ulcerated Gastric Adenocarcinoma.
* Pitfall: Removing the NGT too early post-op in a perforated ulcer repair. The stomach must remain decompressed to prevent gastric distension, which places lethal tension on the fresh omental patch repair leading to a blow-out.
* Pitfall: Diagnosing Gastric Volvulus but delaying NGT insertion. Immediate NGT decompression is crucial to prevent gastric ischemia and necrosis.
