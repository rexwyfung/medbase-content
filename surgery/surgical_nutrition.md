# Surgical Nutrition (外科營養)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Definition of Surgical Malnutrition (外科營養不良)
*   State of deficiency in energy, protein, or macronutrients → adverse effects on tissue form/function & clinical outcomes
*   Associated with ↑ post-op morbidity, ↑ mortality, ↑ length of stay (LOS), ↑ healthcare costs
*   Defined clinically as unintentional weight loss >10% in 3-6 months OR BMI < 18.5 kg/m²

### Local Epidemiology & Hong Kong Hospital Authority (HA) Guidelines
*   Malnutrition prevalence in HK HA surgical wards: 20-30%
*   HA mandate: Universal nutritional screening for all admissions within 24 hours
*   Standard HA screening tool: **Malnutrition Universal Screening Tool (MUST)**

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Physiological Response to Starvation (飢餓生理學)
*   ↓ Insulin, ↑ Glucagon
*   Early: Glycogenolysis (depleted in 24h)
*   Mid: Gluconeogenesis from muscle protein (amino acids)
*   Late: Ketogenesis from adipose tissue (lipolysis) → protein sparing

### Metabolic Response to Surgical Stress (手術壓力代謝反應)
*   Characterized by systemic inflammatory response syndrome (SIRS) & altered neuroendocrine profile
*   Triggered by tissue injury, pain, hypovolemia, infection
*   Cytokine release: ↑ IL-1, ↑ IL-6, ↑ TNF-α
*   Hormonal shift: ↑ Cortisol, ↑ Catecholamines, ↑ Glucagon, ↑ Growth Hormone
*   Net effect: Severe hypercatabolism, insulin resistance, hyperglycemia, muscle wasting

### The Ebb Phase of Surgical Stress (退潮期)
*   Timing: 0-48 hours post-injury/surgery
*   Hemodynamics: Shock state, ↓ tissue perfusion, ↓ metabolic rate, ↓ core temperature
*   Priority: Resuscitation & hemodynamic stability (NOT nutrition)

### The Flow Phase of Surgical Stress (漲潮期)
*   Timing: 48 hours to weeks post-injury/surgery
*   Catabolic state: ↑ Metabolic rate, ↑ core temperature, ↑ O2 consumption, severe muscle proteolysis
*   Anabolic state (Recovery): Gradual restoration of protein synthesis & fat stores (can take months)

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### History Indicating Nutritional Risk (營養不良病史)
*   Inadequate intake > 5 days
*   Unintentional weight loss > 10% over 6 months
*   Chronic dysphagia (吞嚥困難)
*   Protracted vomiting or severe diarrhea
*   Underlying hypercatabolic state (sepsis, burns, major trauma)

### Physical Signs of Malnutrition (營養不良體徵)
*   **Wasting signs:** Temporalis muscle wasting, thenar/hypothenar atrophy, loss of buccal fat pads
*   **Deficiency signs:** Glossitis (B12/folate/iron), angular stomatitis (B vitamins), poor wound healing (Vitamin C/Zinc), coagulopathy (Vitamin K)
*   **Fluid shifts:** Peripheral edema, ascites (hypoalbuminemia)
*   **Anthropometry:** Triceps skinfold thickness ↓, Mid-arm muscle circumference (MAMC) ↓

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Etiology of Surgical Malnutrition (外科營養不良病因分類)

| Category | Mechanism | Common Surgical Examples |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Decreased Intake (攝入減少)** | Mechanical obstruction, dysphagia, anorexia | Esophageal cancer, gastric outlet obstruction, head/neck tumors |
| **Decreased Absorption (吸收減少)** | Loss of absorptive surface, enzyme deficiency | Short bowel syndrome (短腸綜合症), severe acute pancreatitis, biliary fistula |
| **Increased Losses (流失增加)** | High-output fistulas, protein-losing enteropathy | Enterocutaneous fistula (腸皮瘻管), severe burn exudate, open abdomen |
| **Hypermetabolism (高代謝狀態)** | ↑ Basal metabolic rate due to stress | Major trauma, severe sepsis, extensive burns, hyperthyroidism |

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)

### Objective Nutritional Screening (MUST Score Workup)
*   **MUST Score Components:**
    *   BMI (>20 = 0; 18.5-20 = 1; <18.5 = 2)
    *   Unplanned weight loss in past 3-6 months (<5% = 0; 5-10% = 1; >10% = 2)
    *   Acute disease effect (No/minimal intake >5 days = 2)
*   **Action based on MUST:**
    *   0 (Low risk): Routine clinical care
    *   1 (Medium risk): Observe, document dietary intake x 3 days
    *   ≥2 (High risk): Urgent HA Dietitian referral, formulate specialized nutrition plan

### Baseline Biochemical Workup (血液化驗)
*   **C-Reactive Protein (CRP):** ↑ indicates active systemic inflammation (invalidates albumin as nutritional marker)
*   **Albumin (白蛋白):** Negative acute-phase reactant; half-life 20 days; marker of chronic severity/inflammation, NOT acute nutritional status
*   **Pre-albumin:** Half-life 2 days; better marker for short-term response to nutritional support
*   **Transferrin:** Half-life 8 days; limited by iron deficiency states
*   **Baseline Electrolytes:** Na, K, Mg, PO4 (Crucial baseline before feeding to prevent refeeding syndrome)
*   **Micronutrients (if indicated):** Vitamin B12, Folate, Iron, Zinc, Copper

### Pre-operative Organ Function Assessment
*   Liver Function Tests (LFTs): Check baseline before starting Total Parenteral Nutrition (TPN)
*   Renal Function Tests (RFTs): Assess urea/creatinine for protein loading capacity
*   Fasting Blood Glucose / HbA1c: Assess baseline insulin resistance

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)

### Calculation of Nutritional Requirements (營養需求計算)
*   **Energy Goal:** 25-30 kcal/kg/day (Target ideal body weight if BMI > 30)
*   **Protein Goal:** 1.0-1.5 g/kg/day (↑ up to 2.0 g/kg in severe burns/sepsis)
*   **Fluid Goal:** 30-35 mL/kg/day
*   **Lipid/Carbohydrate Ratio:** Generally 30% lipids / 70% carbohydrates

### Route Selection Algorithm (營養途徑選擇)
*   **Rule of Thumb:** "If the gut works, use it." (Preserves gut mucosal barrier, prevents bacterial translocation)
*   Step 1: Oral supplementation (Sip feeds, Fortisip)
*   Step 2: Enteral Nutrition (EN) via tube (Nasogastric, Nasojejunal, Gastrostomy, Jejunostomy)
*   Step 3: Parenteral Nutrition (PN/TPN) via central or peripheral vein

### Enteral vs. Parenteral Nutrition Comparison

| Feature | Enteral Nutrition (EN - 腸道營養) | Total Parenteral Nutrition (TPN - 全靜脈營養) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Indications** | Functional GI tract but inadequate oral intake | Non-functional GI tract (ileus, obstruction, high-output fistula) |
| **Route** | NGT, NJT, PEG, Jejunostomy | Central Venous Catheter (CVC), PICC |
| **Pros** | Cheaper, physiological, maintains gut mucosa integrity, ↓ infection risk | Guaranteed caloric delivery, resting the gut |
| **Cons** | Aspiration risk, diarrhea, tube dislodgement | Expensive, requires central line, ↑ sepsis risk, liver steatosis |
| **Contraindications**| Intestinal ischemia, mechanical obstruction, peritonitis | Functional GI tract, terminal palliation (relative) |

### HA Protocols for Pre-operative Nutrition
*   Elective surgery for severely malnourished patients (weight loss >10%): Delay surgery 7-14 days for pre-op nutritional optimization via EN/PN

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Enteral Access Procedures (腸道造口術)
*   **Percutaneous Endoscopic Gastrostomy (PEG - 經皮內視鏡胃造口術):**
    *   Indication: Need for EN > 4 weeks (e.g., severe head injury, obstructing esophageal CA)
    *   Principle: Transillumination via endoscope, percutaneous puncture, placement of mushroom-tipped catheter
    *   Consent: Risk of bleeding, viscous perforation, necrotizing fasciitis at puncture site
*   **Feeding Jejunostomy (空腸造口術):**
    *   Indication: Gastroparesis, recent esophagectomy/gastrectomy, proximal fistulas
    *   Principle: Surgical or laparoscopic insertion of Witzel tube into proximal jejunum (~20cm distal to DJ flexure)
    *   Consent: Risk of bowel volvulus around tube, intraperitoneal leakage → peritonitis

### Parenteral Access Procedures (靜脈導管植入術)
*   **Central Venous Catheter (CVC - 中心靜脈導管):**
    *   Indication: Administration of highly osmolar TPN solutions (osmolarity > 900 mOsm/L)
    *   Principle: Subclavian or internal jugular vein puncture under ultrasound guidance. Tip must lie at superior vena cava (SVC) / right atrium junction (confirm with CXR)
    *   Consent Issues: Risk of pneumothorax, arterial puncture, hemothorax, air embolism, catheter-related bloodstream infection (CRBSI)

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Enhanced Recovery After Surgery (ERAS) Nutritional Protocols (術後加速康復)
*   **Pre-operative Fasting Avoidance:**
    *   Solid food allowed up to 6 hours pre-op
    *   Clear fluids (Carbohydrate-loading drinks) allowed up to 2 hours pre-op → ↓ post-op insulin resistance & catabolism
*   **Post-operative Feeding:**
    *   Early oral feeding (within 24 hours) strongly advocated, even after bowel anastomosis
    *   Reduces paralytic ileus duration, ↓ LOS, ↓ anastomotic leak rates
    *   Discard traditional "nil by mouth (NBM) until flatus" dogma

### Refeeding Syndrome Monitoring Protocol
*   Identify high-risk patients: BMI < 16, weight loss > 15% in 3-6 months, little/no food > 10 days, ↓ baseline K/Mg/PO4
*   Start feeding slowly (10 kcal/kg/day), titrate up over 4-7 days
*   Prophylactic Pabrinex (IV Thiamine/B-complex) 30 mins before feeding starts
*   Daily monitoring of K, Mg, PO4, Ca for the first 1 week

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Complications of Enteral Nutrition (EN)
*   **Early:** Pulmonary aspiration (fatal), tube malposition, epistaxis (NGT trauma)
*   **Intermediate:** Osmotic diarrhea, bloating, vomiting, electrolyte imbalances
*   **Late:** Tube blockage, PEG site infection/leakage

### Complications of Parenteral Nutrition (TPN)
*   **Early (Catheter-related):** Pneumothorax, arterial puncture, arrhythmia during insertion
*   **Intermediate (Metabolic):** Hyperglycemia (requires insulin sliding scale), hypertriglyceridemia, electrolyte disturbances
*   **Late (Infective/Organ):** Catheter-Related Bloodstream Infection (CRBSI) (often Coagulase-negative Staphylococci or Candida), TPN-associated liver disease (steatosis/cholestasis), acalculous cholecystitis

### Pathophysiology of Refeeding Syndrome (再餵食綜合症)

| Component | Pathophysiological Shift | Clinical Consequence |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Insulin Surge** | Starvation (fat metabolism) → Carbohydrate load → Massive insulin release | Cellular uptake of K, Mg, PO4 |
| **Hypophosphatemia (↓PO4)** | Hallmark of syndrome; ↓ ATP, ↓ 2,3-DPG production | Respiratory failure, heart failure, rhabdomyolysis |
| **Hypokalemia (↓K)** | Rapid intracellular shift | Arrhythmias, cardiac arrest, paralysis |
| **Hypomagnesemia (↓Mg)** | Rapid intracellular shift | Tetany, seizures, QT prolongation |
| **Thiamine Deficiency** | Depleted stores used rapidly for carb metabolism | Wernicke's encephalopathy, lactic acidosis |

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: Why is albumin a poor marker for acute nutritional assessment in surgical patients? Answer: Albumin is a negative acute-phase reactant with a long half-life (20 days). In acute surgical stress or sepsis, vascular permeability increases and liver synthesis shifts to CRP, causing hypoalbuminemia regardless of actual nutritional intake.
* Viva Question: A patient with a high-output enterocutaneous fistula requires nutrition. What is the preferred route? Answer: Total Parenteral Nutrition (TPN), because enteral feeding will stimulate GI secretions, increase fistula output, and fail to be absorbed.
* Viva Question: What is the classic biochemical hallmark of Refeeding Syndrome? Answer: Severe hypophosphatemia, caused by an insulin surge driving phosphate into cells for ATP synthesis after glucose reintroduction.
* Trap: Assuming "Nil By Mouth" is mandatory until flatus passes after bowel anastomosis. Pearl: ERAS protocols strongly recommend early oral feeding within 24 hours, which actually promotes bowel motility and does not increase anastomotic leak rates.
* Pitfall: Starting full-calorie TPN immediately in a severely malnourished patient. This risks fatal Refeeding Syndrome. Always start at 10 kcal/kg/day, give prophylactic IV thiamine (Pabrinex), and closely monitor/replace PO4, K, and Mg.
* Trap: Giving enteral feeds to a hemodynamically unstable patient on high-dose inotropes. Pearl: This risks non-occlusive mesenteric ischemia. Always achieve hemodynamic stability (end of the Ebb phase) before commencing enteral feeding.
* Pitfall: Using an NGT for feeding a patient with a basal skull fracture. The tube may enter the cranial vault. Use an orogastric tube or alternative access.
