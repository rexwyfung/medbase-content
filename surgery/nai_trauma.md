# Non-Accidental Injury (NAI) & Paediatric Trauma (非意外創傷與兒童創傷)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition of Non-Accidental Injury (NAI)
* **Non-Accidental Injury (NAI) (非意外創傷)**: Deliberate physical injury inflicted on a child by a caregiver. Often falls under the broader umbrella of **Child Abuse (虐待兒童)** (physical, sexual, emotional abuse, or neglect).
* **Shaken Baby Syndrome (SBS) (嬰兒搖晃症候群)**: A severe form of NAI involving violent shaking → acceleration-deceleration forces → triad of subdural haematoma, retinal haemorrhages, and encephalopathy.

### Hong Kong Epidemiology and Social Welfare Context
* **Incidence**: Rising awareness in HK; physical abuse and neglect are the most common presentations to Hospital Authority (HA) Emergency Departments.
* **Vulnerable Groups**: Premature infants, disabled children, children with chronic illnesses, and stepchildren.
* **Local Authorities**: Managed jointly by HA medical teams, **Medical Social Workers (MSW) (醫務社工)**, **Social Welfare Department (SWD) (社會福利署)**, and the **Hong Kong Police Force (HKPF) (香港警務處)**.

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology
### Pathophysiology of Shaken Baby Syndrome (SBS)
* **Mechanism**: Infant’s head is large/heavy relative to body + weak neck muscles + unmyelinated fragile brain tissue.
* **Vascular Injury**: Violent shaking → shearing forces → rupture of **Bridging Veins (橋靜脈)** → **Subdural Haematoma (SDH) (硬膜下血腫)**.
* **Ocular Injury**: Vitreoretinal traction → classic **Retinal Haemorrhages (視網膜出血)** (often bilateral, multi-layered).

### Pathophysiology of NAI Skeletal Trauma
* **Metaphyseal Corner Fractures (干骺端骨折)**: Pathognomonic for NAI. Caused by twisting/pulling of limbs. The periosteum is tightly adherent at the metaphysis → avulsion fragment (classic "bucket-handle" or "corner" appearance).
* **Rib Fractures (肋骨骨折)**: Highly specific for NAI (especially posterior). Caused by tight squeezing of the infant's chest (AP compression) → levers the posterior ribs over the transverse processes.

### Mechanisms of Suspicious Thermal Injuries
* **Immersion Burns (浸洗燙傷)**: Forced immersion in hot water → sharp line of demarcation ("glove and stocking" distribution), sparing of flexion creases, absence of splash marks.
* **Contact Burns (接觸性燙傷)**: Punched-out circular lesions (cigarette burns) or distinct object imprints (e.g., iron, heater grill).

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Red Flag History Patterns in Suspicious Trauma
* **History/Injury Mismatch**: Mechanism described by parents is incompatible with the developmental age (e.g., "the 2-month-old rolled off the sofa") or the severity of the injury.
* **Delayed Presentation**: Unexplained delay in seeking medical attention for a significant injury.
* **Inconsistent History**: Story changes between different caregivers or upon repeated questioning.

### Pathognomonic Physical Signs of Abuse
* **Bruising Patterns**: Bruises in non-ambulant infants ("those who don't cruise, rarely bruise"). Bruises over soft tissues (cheeks, abdomen, buttocks, genitals, ears, neck). Multiple bruises in various stages of healing.
* **Bite Marks (咬痕)**: Intercanine distance >3cm suggests an adult bite.
* **Torn Frenulum (系帶撕裂)**: Caused by forceful bottle feeding or direct trauma to the mouth in a crying infant.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features
### Diagnostic Differentials for Suspected NAI
| Pathology | Discriminatory Clinical Features | Laboratory / Imaging Findings |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Non-Accidental Injury (NAI)** | Inconsistent Hx, specific fracture types, multi-stage bruises | +ve Skeletal survey, +ve Retinal haemorrhage |
| **Accidental Trauma (意外創傷)** | Hx matches injury, ambulatory child, bruises on bony prominences (shins, forehead) | Single fracture, clear mechanism |
| **Osteogenesis Imperfecta (成骨不全症)** | Blue sclera, +ve family Hx, frequent fractures from trivial trauma | Osteopenia, wormian bones, COL1A1/COL1A2 mutation |
| **Bleeding Disorders (e.g., Haemophilia)** | Spontaneous haemarthrosis, prolonged bleeding, petechiae | ↑ APTT (Haemophilia) or ↑ PT/INR, ↓ Platelets |
| **Mongolian Spots (蒙古斑)** | Present from birth, non-tender, stable appearance over time, common in Asian populations | Normal coagulation, fades over years |
| **Cupping/Coining (拔罐/刮痧)** | Traditional Chinese Medicine practices, symmetrical circular or linear purpura | Normal coagulation, culturally specific history |

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)
### Standardized NAI Radiological Workup
* **Skeletal Survey (骨骼X光普查)**: Mandatory for children <2 years with suspected NAI. Includes AP/Lat skull, AP/Lat spine, AP chest, AP pelvis, and AP of all long bones/hands/feet. (Note: Often repeated at 10-14 days to detect healing fractures/callus).
* **CT Brain (Non-Contrast)**: 1st line imaging for acute neurological signs, ALOC, or suspected SBS → identifies acute SDH, cerebral oedema, subarachnoid haemorrhage.
* **MRI Brain & Spine**: Gold standard for delineating exact timing/extent of intracranial parenchymal injuries and spinal subdural haematomas (performed once stabilized).

### Essential Laboratory Investigations for NAI
* **Coagulation Screen**: PT, APTT, INR, Platelets, Fibrinogen → to rule out coagulopathies.
* **Bone Profile**: Calcium, Phosphate, Alkaline Phosphatase (ALP), Vitamin D → to rule out rickets/osteogenesis imperfecta.
* **Toxicology Screen**: Urine/blood for illicit substances or inappropriate medications (sedatives).

### Mandatory Multi-Specialty Consultations
* **Ophthalmology Consult**: Dilated fundoscopy by a senior ophthalmologist to document retinal haemorrhages.
* **Paediatric Consult**: General systemic examination, documentation, and admission.
* **Clinical Photography**: Medico-legal documentation of all visible injuries using measuring tapes (performed by designated HA medical photographers).

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)
### Hospital Authority (HA) NAI Protocol & MDT Approach
* **Immediate Admission**: ALL suspected NAI cases MUST be admitted to a paediatric ward as a **Place of Safety (安全庇護所)**. NEVER discharge home from AED if NAI is suspected.
* **Multi-Disciplinary Case Conference (MDCC) (多專業個案會議)**: Convened involving HA Doctors, MSW, SWD officers, Police, and school representatives to formulate a welfare plan.
* **Care and Protection Order (C&P Order) (照顧及保護令)**: Applied via Juvenile Court by SWD/Police if parents refuse admission or if return to family is deemed unsafe.

### Indications for Acute Surgical Intervention
* **Neurosurgery**: Decompressive craniectomy or SDH evacuation indicated for severe raised Intracranial Pressure (ICP), midline shift, or rapid neurological deterioration.
* **Orthopaedics**: Open Reduction and Internal Fixation (ORIF) or closed reduction + casting for displaced long bone fractures (e.g., femoral shaft fractures).
* **General/Trauma Surgery**: Exploratory laparotomy for hollow viscus injury (e.g., duodenal haematoma, bowel perforation from blunt abdominal trauma).

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues
### Consent and Legal Authority in NAI Surgery
* **Emergency Life-Saving Surgery**: Can proceed under the doctrine of necessity without parental consent if delaying would cause death/irreversible harm (two senior clinicians must sign).
* **Non-Emergency Intervention**: If parents refuse consent but NAI is highly suspected, intervention must be delayed until a C&P Order is obtained granting the Director of Social Welfare legal guardianship to consent.

### Operative Principles for NAI-Associated Trauma
* **Meticulous Documentation**: Intra-operative findings must be exhaustively documented as they will be used as evidence in criminal proceedings.
* **Sparing Fixation in Paediatrics**: Use flexible intramedullary nails or K-wires rather than rigid plates to avoid physeal injury and accommodate future growth.

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation
### Immediate Post-Operative Safe Placement
* **Strict Ward Security**: Restrict/monitor parental visitation rights according to Police and SWD directives.
* **Nursing Observation**: 1-to-1 nursing may be required; document all caregiver-child interactions.

### Long-Term MDT Rehabilitation
* **Discharge Planning**: Patient is ONLY discharged once SWD has secured a safe environment (e.g., foster care, residential home, or safe relatives).
* **Neuro-rehabilitation**: Long-term physiotherapy, occupational therapy, and special education for brain-injured children.

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis
### Complications of Severe NAI Trauma
* **Early**: Refractory intracranial hypertension, status epilepticus, haemorrhagic shock.
* **Intermediate**: Hydrocephalus (requiring VP shunt), fracture non-union, infection.
* **Late**: **Cerebral Palsy (腦性麻痺)**, severe neurodevelopmental delay, cortical blindness, permanent physical deformity.

### Prognostic Indicators in NAI
* **Mortality**: Severe abusive head trauma (AHT) carries a mortality rate of up to 25%.
* **Morbidity**: >50% of AHT survivors suffer permanent, severe neurological deficits.
* **Recurrence Risk**: Very high without SWD intervention; sentinel injuries (minor bruises) often precede fatal events if missed.

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Viva Question: What is the classic triad of Shaken Baby Syndrome (Abusive Head Trauma)? Answer: Subdural haematoma, retinal haemorrhages, and encephalopathy.
* Trap: Attributing a mid-shaft femoral fracture in a non-walking 6-month-old infant to a "simple fall" from a bed. Pearl: Femoral fractures in non-ambulant infants are NAI until proven otherwise; a simple low fall does not generate enough force.
* Pitfall: Discharging a child with suspicious injuries from the AED because the parents seem "nice" and promise to follow up. NEVER discharge a suspected NAI; immediately admit to the ward as a Place of Safety.
* Viva Question: Which specific rib fractures are highly indicative of non-accidental compression injury? Answer: Posterior rib fractures (near the costovertebral junction).
* Trap: Diagnosing Osteogenesis Imperfecta based solely on blue sclera in a neonate. Pearl: Blue sclera can be a normal physiological finding in normal neonates; look for other signs like osteopenia or multiple unexplainable fractures.
* Pitfall: Failing to order a coagulation screen before accusing parents of abuse in a bruising child. Always exclude bleeding disorders (e.g., Haemophilia, ITP) medically first.
* Viva Question: What is the pathognomonic radiological sign of limb NAI on a skeletal survey? Answer: Metaphyseal corner fractures (or bucket-handle fractures).
