# Ingested Foreign Bodies (異物誤吞)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Foreign Body Ingestion
* **Ingested Foreign Bodies (異物誤吞):** Non-dietary objects or impacted food boluses lodged within the gastrointestinal (GI) tract.
* **True Foreign Bodies:** Non-edible items (e.g., coins, button batteries, toys, dentures).
* **Food Impaction:** Physiologically edible material (e.g., meat bolus, fish bone) stalled in the alimentary canal, typically the esophagus.

### Hong Kong Epidemiology and Demographics
* **Bimodal Age Distribution:** Peaks in pediatrics (6 months–3 years; coins, toys) & elderly (edentulous, psychiatric patients, prisoners).
* **HK Local Context:** **Fish bone ingestion (鯁魚骨)** is exceptionally common due to the local dietary habit of consuming whole steamed fish.
* **Adult Presentations:** Often linked to underlying esophageal pathology (e.g., strictures, rings, eosinophilic esophagitis, malignancy) or intoxication.
* **Body Packers (Drug Mules):** Occasionally encountered at HK International Airport; ingestion of illicit drug packets (e.g., cocaine, heroin).
* **Spontaneous Passage:** 80–90% pass spontaneously through the GI tract; 10–20% require endoscopic retrieval; <1% require surgical intervention.

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Anatomical Constriction Points of the Esophagus
* **Upper Esophageal Sphincter (UES) / Cricopharyngeus (環咽肌):** 15 cm from incisors (most common site of impaction, ~75% of pediatric cases).
* **Aortic Arch (主動脈弓):** 22 cm from incisors.
* **Left Main Bronchus (左主支氣管):** 27 cm from incisors.
* **Lower Esophageal Sphincter (LES) / Diaphragmatic Hiatus (橫膈膜裂孔):** 40 cm from incisors.

### Pathophysiology of Tissue Injury
* **Mechanical Obstruction:** → pooling of secretions → aspiration risk (吸入性肺炎).
* **Pressure Necrosis:** Impaction >24 hours → local ischemia → mucosal ulceration → **Esophageal Perforation (食道穿孔)**.
* **Chemical / Electrical Burn:** **Button Batteries (鈕扣電池)** → conduct current upon mucosal contact → rapid generation of hydroxide (OH-) at the negative pole → liquefactive necrosis (液化性壞死) within 2 hours.
* **Chemical Toxicity:** Leaking drug packets (Body Packers) → massive sympathomimetic/opiate toxicity → cardiovascular collapse/respiratory arrest.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Classic Patient Presentation
* **Dysphagia (吞嚥困難):** Inability to swallow solids/liquids.
* **Odynophagia (吞嚥痛):** Sharp pain upon swallowing, localizing to neck or retrosternal area.
* **Hypersalivation / Drooling (流口水):** Pathognomonic for complete esophageal obstruction.
* **Foreign Body Sensation (異物感):** Feeling of something stuck in the throat.
* **Respiratory Symptoms:** Stridor, wheezing, or choking (due to tracheal compression by esophageal FB).

### Red Flag Signs of Perforation or Complications
* **Surgical Emphysema (皮下氣腫):** Crepitus in the neck.
* **Systemic Toxicity:** Tachycardia (心跳過速), fever (發燒), hypotension.
* **Severe Pain:** Unrelenting neck, chest, or abdominal pain.
* **Hematemesis (吐血):** Aortoenteric fistula or severe mucosal laceration.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Differential Diagnosis for Dysphagia and Foreign Body Sensation
| Differential Diagnosis | Discriminatory Clinical Features |
|---|---|
| **Globus Pharyngeus (喉球症)** | Intermittent sensation; NO true dysphagia; normal swallowing of food; psychological correlation. |
| **Esophageal Malignancy (食道癌)** | Progressive dysphagia (solids → liquids); significant weight loss; elderly patients; smoking/alcohol history. |
| **Eosinophilic Esophagitis** | Young males; atopic history (asthma, eczema); recurrent food bolus impactions. |
| **Achalasia (賁門失弛緩症)** | Dysphagia to both solids & liquids; bird-beak sign on barium swallow; absent peristalsis. |
| **GERD / Peptic Stricture** | Chronic heartburn history; gradual onset of solid food dysphagia. |

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)

### First-Line Bedside and Laboratory Tests
* **Vitals & Resuscitation:** ABCDE approach; strict monitoring of airway patency.
* **Blood Profile:** CBC (↑ WBC in perforation), CRP, RFT, Coagulation profile, Group & Save.
* **ECG:** Rule out acute myocardial infarction in elderly presenting with retrosternal pain.

### Gold Standard Imaging for Ingested Foreign Bodies
* **Plain Radiographs (Neck, Chest, Abdominal X-ray):**
 * **Views:** AP and Lateral views are mandatory.
 * **Button Battery vs Coin:** On AP view, battery shows "halo" or "double-ring" sign; coin is solid. On Lateral view, battery shows a "step-off" border.
 * **Limitations:** Many FBs are radiolucent (e.g., fish bones, plastic, wood, glass).
* **CT Neck and Thorax (Non-contrast):**
 * **Indication:** High clinical suspicion of radiolucent FB (e.g., sharp fish bone), negative X-ray but persistent symptoms, or suspected perforation (pneumomediastinum).
 * **HA Protocol:** Low threshold for CT in elderly presenting to AED with neck pain and fever post-ingestion.

### Endoscopic Pre-operative Evaluation
* **Oesophagogastroduodenoscopy (OGD) (胃鏡):** Diagnostic AND therapeutic. Gold standard for evaluating mucosal integrity and retrieving the object.
* **Barium Swallow:** **ABSOLUTELY CONTRAINDICATED** if suspecting complete obstruction or prior to OGD (barium obscures endoscopic visualization and risks severe aspiration). Water-soluble contrast (Gastrografin) only if suspecting perforation *after* negative CT.

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)

### Emergency Indications for Immediate Endoscopy (<2 Hours)
* **Button Battery** located in the esophagus.
* **Sharp/Pointed Object** (e.g., open safety pin, razor blade) in the esophagus.
* **Complete Esophageal Obstruction** (unable to swallow saliva, massive drooling).
* **Any FB with Respiratory Compromise.**

### Urgent Indications for Endoscopy (<24 Hours)
* **Food Bolus Impaction** without complete obstruction.
* **Blunt Objects** in the esophagus >24h or unknown duration.
* **Sharp Objects** in the stomach or duodenum (high risk of perforation during transit).
* **Button Battery** in the stomach IF symptomatic or >15mm in a child <6 years old.

### Conservative Management Protocol
* **Asymptomatic Blunt Objects (e.g., coins):** If passed into the stomach, monitor with serial X-rays (1-2 weeks). Usually pass spontaneously.
* **Body Packers (Drug Packets):** Strictly conservative (Whole Bowel Irrigation with PEG). Wait for spontaneous passage. Surgical laparotomy ONLY if intestinal obstruction, perforation, or packet rupture (toxicity).

### Management Algorithm for Specific Foreign Bodies
| Foreign Body Type | Anatomic Location | Endoscopic Timing | Preferred Method / Adjunct |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Button Battery** | Esophagus | Emergent (<2h) | Rigid/Flexible OGD + direct extraction |
| **Sharp Fish Bone** | Pharynx / Esophagus | Urgent | Direct Laryngoscopy / OGD + Overtube |
| **Meat Bolus** | Esophagus | Urgent | Push into stomach OR fragment & extract |
| **Coin** | Stomach (Asymptomatic) | Conservative | Serial X-rays; OGD if retained >2-4 weeks |
| **Illicit Drug Packets** | GI Tract | **CONTRAINDICATED** | Whole Bowel Irrigation; Surgery if toxic |

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Essential Informed Consent Components
* **Diagnosis:** Impacted esophageal/gastric foreign body.
* **Proposed Procedure:** Endoscopic removal (OGD) +/- rigid esophagoscopy under General Anesthesia (GA).
* **Material Risks:** Failure of retrieval, mucosal laceration, **esophageal perforation** (life-threatening), aspiration pneumonia, bleeding, conversion to open surgery (thoracotomy/laparotomy) if perforation occurs.

### Standard Operative Steps for Endoscopic Retrieval
* **Anesthesia:** Elective endotracheal intubation (GA) to protect the airway, especially for proximal FBs or high aspiration risk.
* **Positioning:** Left lateral decubitus.
* **Equipment Selection:**
 * **Rat-tooth forceps / Snares:** For coins and hard objects.
 * **Roth Net:** For food boluses and batteries.
 * **Overtube / Protector Hood:** **MANDATORY** when extracting sharp objects (e.g., fish bones, razor blades) to protect the esophageal mucosa and UES during withdrawal.
* **Technique (Food Bolus):** Attempt gentle push into stomach. If resistance met, do NOT force; fragment using snare/net and extract piecemeal. Always inspect mucosa post-retrieval to rule out underlying stricture or eosinophilic esophagitis.

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Immediate Post-operative Monitoring
* **NBM (Nil By Mouth):** Fasting until clinical reassessment.
* **Airway Monitoring:** Observe for stridor or respiratory distress secondary to procedural edema.
* **Vitals Check:** Q1H monitoring for signs of sepsis/perforation (tachycardia, fever).
* **Post-op Imaging:** CXR / Erect AXR if any suspicion of iatrogenic perforation or if the patient develops chest pain/surgical emphysema.

### Dietary Rehabilitation Protocol
* **Uncomplicated Retrieval:** Resume clear fluids at 4 hours post-op → upgrade to soft diet (congee/puree) → normal diet by day 1.
* **Mucosal Injury / Ulceration:** IV PPI (Proton Pump Inhibitors) + Sucralfate. Liquid diet for 24-48 hours.
* **Suspected Perforation:** Strict NBM, broad-spectrum IV antibiotics, urgent CT contrast / Gastrografin swallow, surgical consultation.

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Early Surgical Complications (<24 Hours)
* **Esophageal Perforation:** Highest risk with sharp bones, >24h impaction, or iatrogenic during extraction. High mortality if unrecognised.
* **Airway Compromise:** Secondary to tracheal compression or post-intubation edema.
* **Aspiration Pneumonitis / Pneumonia:** Due to pooled secretions above the obstruction.

### Intermediate and Late Surgical Complications
* **Mediastinitis:** Lethal complication of perforation → rapid sepsis and multi-organ failure.
* **Aortoenteric Fistula:** Rare but fatal complication from chronic pressure necrosis of a mid-esophageal FB eroding into the aorta → massive upper GI bleed.
* **Esophageal Stricture:** Late complication following mucosal ulceration and fibrotic healing (especially common after button battery burns).

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: How do you differentiate a coin from a button battery on an AP chest radiograph? Answer: A button battery exhibits a characteristic "double-ring" or "halo" sign on the AP view, whereas a coin appears as a uniform, solid radiopaque disc.
* Trap: Assuming a negative plain radiograph rules out an ingested fish bone. Pearl: Many fish bones and plastic objects are radiolucent; if the patient is symptomatic with dysphagia or localized neck pain, proceed to CT neck/thorax or direct endoscopy.
* Pitfall: Attempting to endoscopically retrieve ingested drug packets in body packers. This risks puncturing the packet, leading to massive drug absorption and fatal toxicity; management must be conservative or surgical (laparotomy) if complications arise.
* Viva Question: What is the mandatory adjunct required when endoscopically retrieving a sharp foreign body like a razor blade or large fish bone? Answer: An overtube or a latex protector hood must be used to prevent laceration of the esophageal mucosa and upper sphincter during withdrawal.
* Trap: Performing a Barium swallow to locate an unknown foreign body. Pearl: Barium coats the mucosa, completely obscuring subsequent endoscopic visualization and drastically increasing the risk of severe chemical pneumonitis if aspirated; use non-contrast CT instead.
