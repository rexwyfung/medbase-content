# Acute Arterial Embolism & Vascular Emergencies (急性動脈栓塞與血管急症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Definition of Acute Limb Ischaemia (急性下肢缺血)
* **Acute Limb Ischaemia (ALI)**: Sudden ↓ in limb perfusion threatening limb viability → presents within 14 days of symptom onset.
* **Arterial Embolism (動脈栓塞)**: Distal migration of blood clot/debris occluding a downstream vessel.
* **Golden Window**: Irreversible muscle necrosis begins at **6 hours** of ischaemia.

### Hong Kong Epidemiology & Demographics
* **Age & Comorbidities**: ↑ Incidence in HK's ageing population; high local prevalence of **Atrial Fibrillation (心房顫動)**, **Diabetes Mellitus (糖尿病)**, and smoking.
* **Embolic Source**: 80% cardiogenic (AF, post-MI mural thrombus, prosthetic valves).
* **Thrombotic Source**: Superimposed on pre-existing **Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD) (周邊動脈阻塞疾病)**.

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Common Sites of Arterial Embolism
* Emboli typically lodge at arterial bifurcations (vessel calibre ↓ abruptly).
* **Femoral bifurcation** (Common Femoral → SFA/Profunda): ~43% (Most common).
* **Iliac bifurcation**: ~18%.
* **Aortic bifurcation (Saddle Embolus)**: ~9% → Causes bilateral lower limb ischaemia.
* **Popliteal trifurcation**: ~15%.
* **Upper Limb**: Brachial bifurcation (~10%).

### Pathophysiology of Embolisation and Thrombosis
* **Cardiogenic Embolism**: Stasis in Left Atrial Appendage (LAA) in AF → clot formation → dislodgement → sudden occlusion of healthy distal artery → poor collateral circulation → profound ischaemia.
* **In-situ Thrombosis (血栓形成)**: Chronic atherosclerosis → plaque rupture/haemorrhage → platelet aggregation → acute occlusion → pre-existing collaterals limit ischaemia severity.
* **Atheroembolism (Trash Foot)**: Micro-emboli (cholesterol crystals) from proximal aortic plaque → lodge in digital arteries → blue toes with palpable pedal pulses.

### Ischaemic Cascade and Reperfusion Injury Pathophysiology
* Prolonged hypoxia → ↓ ATP production → failure of Na+/K+ ATPase pump → intracellular cellular swelling + Ca2+ influx → cell death.
* **Reperfusion Injury (再灌注損傷)**: Restoration of blood flow → flushes toxic metabolites (**K+, lactic acid, myoglobin, Reactive Oxygen Species (ROS)**) into systemic circulation → **Arrhythmias**, **Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)**, and **ARDS**.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### The 6 Ps of Acute Limb Ischaemia
* **Pain (疼痛)**: Sudden onset, severe, unrelenting; initially distal, worsens with elevation.
* **Pallor (蒼白)**: Cadaveric, waxy pale skin; empties venous gutters.
* **Pulselessness (無脈搏)**: Absent arterial pulses distal to occlusion.
* **Perishingly cold (冰冷)**: Demarcation line of coldness (usually 1 joint below actual occlusion level).
* **Paraesthesia (感覺異常)**: Sensory nerve ischaemia (early sign of impending viability loss); loss of light touch.
* **Paralysis (癱瘓)**: Motor nerve/muscle ischaemia (late/irreversible sign); foot drop, rigid "wooden" muscles.

### Evolution of Clinical Signs over Time
* **<6 hours**: Pain, pallor, coldness, normal motor/sensory function.
* **6-12 hours**: Capillary refill >3s, sensory loss (numbness), muscle weakness.
* **>12-24 hours**: Fixed mottling (non-blanching), muscle rigor, blistering, gangrene (壞疽).

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Embolus vs. In-Situ Thrombosis Discriminatory Table
| Feature | Acute Embolism (急性栓塞) | In-situ Thrombosis (原位血栓) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Onset** | Sudden, dramatic (seconds/mins) | Gradual/insidious (hours/days) |
| **Past Medical Hx** | AF, recent MI, rheumatic heart disease | Claudication, Smoking, DM, CAD |
| **Contralateral Leg** | Normal pulses | Weak/absent pulses, hair loss |
| **Severity** | Profound (no time for collaterals) | Less severe (collaterals present) |
| **Angiography** | "Meniscus" sign (sharp cutoff) | Diffuse atherosclerosis, ragged occlusion |
| **Treatment** | Embolectomy | Thrombolysis / Bypass / Angioplasty |

### Other Vascular Emergencies Differentials
* **Aortic Dissection (主動脈剝離)**: Tearing back pain, unequal BP in arms, widened mediastinum.
* **Phlegmasia Cerulea Dolens (疼痛性股藍腫)**: Massive DVT → venous outflow completely obstructed → secondary arterial compromise → massively swollen, cyanotic, painful leg (differentiates from pale/flat embolic leg).
* **Acute Compartment Syndrome**: Hx of trauma/fracture, disproportionate pain on passive stretch, pulses often still *present* until late stage.

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)

### Bedside & Initial Investigations
* **Handheld Doppler**: Assess audible arterial (biphasic/triphasic = normal, monophasic = poor, absent = occluded) and venous signals.
* **ECG**: Crucial to identify AF or acute MI.
* **CXR**: Cardiomegaly, pulmonary oedema.

### Gold Standard Imaging for Vascular Emergencies
* **CT Angiogram (CTA) Aorta & Lower Limbs**: Gold standard in HA protocol.
* **Identifies**: Site of occlusion, clot burden, status of run-off vessels, presence of proximal aneurysms/plaques.
* **Constraint**: Must *not* delay emergent surgery in Rutherford Category IIb/III patients.

### Pre-operative Blood Panel
* **Creatine Kinase (CK)**: Baseline for muscle necrosis.
* **Renal Function (U&E)**: Baseline Cr, hyperkalaemia risk.
* **Clotting Profile**: Baseline INR/APTT before systemic heparinisation.
* **Group & Save (G&S)**: Potential major blood loss during bypass/embolectomy.

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)

### Rutherford Classification of Acute Limb Ischaemia Table
| Category | Prognosis | Sensory Loss | Motor Deficit | Doppler (Art/Ven) | Primary Management |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **I. Viable** | Not immediately threatened | None | None | +ve / +ve | Imaging → Elective revasc |
| **IIa. Marginally Threatened** | Salvageable if treated promptly | Minimal (toes) | None | -ve / +ve | Imaging → Urgent revasc (Thrombolysis/Surgery) |
| **IIb. Immediately Threatened** | Salvageable with immediate intervention | Rest pain, profound loss | Mild/Mod | -ve / +ve | Emergency Surgery (Embolectomy) |
| **III. Irreversible** | Major tissue loss, permanent damage | Profound, anaesthetic | Paralysis, rigor | -ve / -ve | Amputation (截肢) |

### Initial Resuscitation and Medical Therapy (HA Protocol)
* **Oxygen**: 100% O2 to ↑ tissue oxygenation.
* **Analgesia**: IV Opiates (e.g., Morphine).
* **Systemic Anticoagulation**: **Stat IV Heparin 5000 units** → Prevents propagation of distal thrombus (Does *not* dissolve existing clot).
* **IV Fluids**: Aggressive hydration to protect kidneys from myoglobinuria.
* **Positioning**: Keep limb dependent (head up, legs slightly down); avoid heating pads.

### Surgical/Interventional Indications
* **Emergency Fogarty Embolectomy**: 1st line for Category IIb (Cardiogenic Embolus).
* **Catheter-Directed Thrombolysis (CDT) (導管溶血栓治療)**: Uses tPA/Urokinase. Indicated for Category I/IIa with *In-situ thrombosis* (symptom onset <14 days) and no absolute contraindications (e.g., recent stroke, active bleeding).
* **Open Bypass/Endarterectomy**: Failed endovascular, complex atherosclerotic disease.
* **Primary Amputation**: Category III (Dead limb) → Revascularising a dead limb causes fatal systemic reperfusion syndrome.

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Principles of Transfemoral Fogarty Embolectomy
* **Approach**: Local +/- Sedation or GA. Vertical groin incision over common femoral artery (CFA).
* **Arteriotomy**: Transverse arteriotomy (easier to close without narrowing).
* **Catheter Insertion**: Fogarty balloon catheter passed proximally (into Iliac) and distally (into SFA/Profunda).
* **Clot Extraction**: Balloon inflated with saline (not air) past the clot → gently withdrawn to pull clot out.
* **Completion**: Flushing with heparinised saline; assess back-bleeding and forward flow; consider on-table completion angiogram.
![image|204](https://pub-f0606dedf6d94e4bb5b1999cbd065914.r2.dev/paediatrics/ba276226e058ff4ef7a57cbf57472506.png)

### Indications and Technique for Prophylactic Fasciotomy (筋膜切開術)
* **Indications**: Ischaemia >4-6 hours, delayed reperfusion, severe calf swelling, young patients (tight fascial compartments).
* **Technique**: Two-incision 4-compartment fasciotomy of the lower leg.
* **Lateral Incision**: Decompresses Anterior and Lateral compartments.
* **Medial Incision**: Decompresses Superficial Posterior and Deep Posterior compartments.
![image](https://pub-f0606dedf6d94e4bb5b1999cbd065914.r2.dev/paediatrics/ce8846bfbe6f5ad709fdfbf0622746b0.png)

### Key Surgical Consent Complications
* Failure to revascularise → Amputation.
* Reperfusion syndrome → AKI, Arrhythmias, Death.
* Bleeding, groin haematoma, lymphatic leak.
* Fasciotomy scars and need for future skin grafting.

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Immediate Post-operative Monitoring
* **Vascular Checks**: Hourly neurovascular observations (Pulses, Doppler signals, capillary refill, motor/sensory function).
* **Renal Protection**: Strict fluid input/output monitoring; aim urine output >0.5-1 ml/kg/hr.
* **Cardiac**: Continuous ECG monitoring for hyperkalaemia-induced arrhythmias.

### Systemic Anticoagulation Protocol
* Continue IV Heparin infusion targeting APTT 1.5-2.5x normal.
* Bridge to long-term oral anticoagulation: **Warfarin (華法林)** or **DOACs (e.g., Apixaban, Rivaroxaban)** for lifelong secondary prevention of AF-related emboli.

### Long-term Vascular Rehabilitation
* Optimise cardiovascular risk factors (BP, BM, Lipids).
* Smoking cessation (HA Smoking Cessation Clinics).
* Physiotherapy and occupational therapy for mobilisation or post-amputation prosthetic fitting.

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Early: Reperfusion Syndrome (再灌注綜合症) and Systemic Toxicity
* **Mechanism**: Washout of toxic metabolic byproducts from dead muscle.
* **Hyperkalaemia (高鉀血症)** → Peaked T waves, wide QRS, VF/asystole.
* **Metabolic Acidosis (代謝性酸中毒)** → Myocardial depression.
* **Myoglobinuria (肌紅蛋白尿)** → Rhabdomyolysis → ATN (Acute Tubular Necrosis) → AKI.
* **Management**:
	* Aggressive IV hydration
	* Alkalinise urine (Sodium Bicarbonate)
	* IV Calcium Gluconate (cardioprotection)
	* Insulin/Dextrose for K+
	* Continuous renal replacement therapy (CRRT) if refractory.

### Early: Compartment Syndrome (腔室症候群)
* Reperfusion → capillary leak → oedema within non-yielding fascial compartments.
* ↑ Intra-compartmental pressure >30 mmHg → occludes venous outflow → stops arterial inflow → secondary necrosis.
* **Management**: Immediate 4-compartment fasciotomy.

### Intermediate to Late Complications
* **Recurrent Embolism**: Poorly controlled AF or inadequate anticoagulation.
* **Wound Infection**: Groin incisions are high risk (near perineum, lymphatics).
* **Chronic Pain / Neuropathy**: Permanent ischemic nerve damage despite successful revascularisation.

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: What is the most reliable clinical sign to differentiate between a viable limb (Category I/IIa) and an immediately threatened limb (Category IIb)? Answer: The presence of sensory loss (numbness/paraesthesia) or motor deficit (weakness) indicates Category IIb and demands immediate surgery.
* Viva Question: A patient with AF presents with acute limb ischaemia. Why do we immediately prescribe IV Heparin if it does not dissolve the embolus? Answer: It prevents the propagation of secondary secondary in-situ microvascular thrombus distal to the obstruction, preserving the collateral and run-off vessels.
* Viva Question: What is the classic angiographic finding of an arterial embolus versus in-situ thrombosis? Answer: An embolus shows a sharp, inverted "meniscus sign" in an otherwise normal-looking artery, whereas thrombosis shows diffuse, ragged atherosclerotic disease with rich collaterals. ![image|552](https://pub-f0606dedf6d94e4bb5b1999cbd065914.r2.dev/paediatrics/4dbbb839a9fa6730c1c2e5e1fbd92b06.png)
* Trap: Revascularising a Category III (Irreversible) limb with fixed mottling and rigor mortis of the calf muscles. Pearl: Revascularising dead muscle will cause massive release of potassium and myoglobin, leading to fatal reperfusion syndrome. The correct treatment is primary amputation.
* Trap: Delaying emergency Fogarty embolectomy in a Category IIb patient to wait for a CT Angiogram. Pearl: If the diagnosis of cardiogenic embolism is clinically obvious and the limb is immediately threatened, proceed directly to the operating theatre. Time is muscle.
* Pitfall: Forgetting to perform a completion angiogram or assess back-bleeding after extracting an embolus. Residual distal clot or a secondary embolus may still be present in the run-off vessels. Always confirm flow before closing the arteriotomy.
* Pitfall: Failing to recognize early compartment syndrome post-embolectomy because pedal pulses are present. Compartment pressure >30mmHg causes muscle necrosis long before arterial pressure (>100mmHg) is occluded. Base diagnosis on disproportionate pain and tight compartments, not pulses.
