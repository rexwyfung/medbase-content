# Carcinoma of Kidney (腎癌)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Renal Cell Carcinoma
* **Renal Cell Carcinoma (RCC) (腎細胞癌)** is a primary malignant adenocarcinoma arising from the renal tubular epithelium, predominantly located within the renal cortex.
* Accountable for approximately 80% to 85% of all primary renal neoplasms.

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
* Represents a steadily rising malignancy in Hong Kong, ranking among the top ten most common cancers in males.
* Male-to-female ratio is approximately 2:1.
* Peak incidence occurs between **50 to 70 years of age**.
* **Incidentalomas (偶然發現的腫瘤)** account for over 60% of new diagnoses due to the widespread utilization of routine transabdominal ultrasound scan (USS) and computed tomography (CT) in the Hong Kong Hospital Authority (HA) network.

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Surgical Anatomy and Fascial Layers
* Kidneys are retroperitoneal organs extending from the **T12 to L3 vertebrae**.
* Enclosed by the fibroelastic **Gerota's fascia (腎周筋膜)**, which separates the perinephric fat from the paranephric fat and provides a temporary anatomical barrier against local tumor extension.
* The **right renal vein** is short and direct, whereas the **left renal vein** is longer, crosses anterior to the aorta, and receives the left gonadal and left adrenal veins.
* Lymphatic drainage tracks primarily to the **para-aortic** and **paracaval lymph nodes**.

### Histological Classifications of Renal Cell Carcinoma
* **Clear Cell RCC (透明細胞腎細胞癌)**: Most common subtype (75% to 80%); arises from the **proximal convoluted tubule**; characterized by clear cytoplasm filled with glycogen and lipids; heavily driven by **VHL gene inactivation**.
* **Papillary RCC (乳頭狀腎細胞癌)**: Second most common (10% to 15%); arises from proximal convoluted tubules; can be multifocal or bilateral; split into Type 1 (better prognosis) and Type 2 (aggressive).
* **Chromophobe RCC (嫌色細胞腎細胞癌)**: Accounts for 5%; arises from the **intercalated cells of the collecting duct**; optimal prognosis among the major subtypes; cells feature prominent cell membranes and perinuclear halos.

### Advanced Pathophysiology & Genetic Mechanisms
* **Von Hippel-Lindau (VHL) Gene Inactivation**: Mapped to chromosome **3p25**; loss or inactivation of both alleles prevents the destruction of **Hypoxia-Inducible Factor 1-alpha (HIF-1α)**.
* Accumulation of HIF-1α -> overproduction of angiogenic drivers including **Vascular Endothelial Growth Factor (VEGF)** and **Platelet-Derived Growth Factor (PDGF)** -> extreme hypervascularity and neocapillary networks characteristic of clear cell tumors.
* **Tumor Thrombus Propagation**: Unique biological propensity to directly invade the **renal vein (腎靜脈)** and propagate intraluminally into the **Inferior Vena Cava (IVC) (下腔靜脈)**, potentially extending up into the right atrium.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### The Classic Presentation Triad
* **The Classic Triad (經典三聯徵)** consists of:
* **Visible Haematuria (顯性血尿)**
* **Loin Pain (腰痛)** (constant, gnawing pain caused by capsular distension or colicky clot passage)
* **Palpable Loin Mass (可觸及的腰部腫塊)**
* Triad is present in <10% of modern cases; denotes locally advanced, late-stage disease.

### Paraneoplastic Syndromes (副腫瘤綜合徵)
* **Erythrocytosis**: Driven by ectopic **Erythopoietin (EPO)** production by tumor cells.
* **Hypercalcaemia**: Secondary to tumor secretion of **Parathyroid Hormone-related Peptide (PTHrP)** or osteolytic bony metastases.
* **Hypertension**: Induced by excessive **Renin** secretion or direct mechanical compression of the renal artery.
* **Stauffer's Syndrome**: Reversible hepatic dysfunction without liver metastases, presenting with elevated **alkaline phosphatase (ALP)**, prolonged prothrombin time, and hepatomegaly, mediated by systemic Interleukin-6 (IL-6).

### Physical Exam Signs of Advanced Disease
* **Non-reducing Left-sided Varicocele (左側精索靜脈曲張)**: Does not collapse when the patient lies supine; caused by tumor thrombus obstruction of the left renal vein where the left gonadal vein drains.
* Bilateral lower limb **pitting oedema**: Signifies extensive IVC thrombosis obstructing systemic venous return.
* Cachexia, supraclavicular lymphadenopathy, and fixed loin mass.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Differential Diagnoses of Solid and Cystic Renal Masses
| Differential Diagnosis | Clinical Characteristics | Discriminatory Features on Contrast CT |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Renal Cell Carcinoma** | Older adults, smoker, macroscopic visible hematuria, constitutional symptoms. | Hyperattenuating mass with **heterogeneous contrast enhancement** and rapid washout; tissue necrosis common. |
| **Angiomyolipoma (AML)** | Young to middle-aged females, associated with **Tuberous Sclerosis**. | Pathognomonic presence of **intralesional macroscopic fat** (< -20 Hounsfield Units). |
| **Oncocytoma** | Benign cortical tumor; clinically indistinguishable from RCC pre-operatively. | Presence of a **stellate central scar** on contrast imaging; requires surgical excision due to biopsy overlap with chromophobe RCC. |
| **Transitional Cell Carcinoma (TCC) of Renal Pelvis** | Highly associated with smoking, occupational dyes; centered in the collecting system. | Infiltrative mass within the **pelvicalyceal system** showing minimal enhancement; produces early hydronephrosis. |
| **Renal Abscess** | Present with spiking fevers, pyuria, history of diabetes or urolithiasis. | Well-defined fluid collection with a **thick, rim-enhancing wall**; perinephric fat stranding. |

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup

### First-line Diagnostic Imaging
* **Transabdominal Ultrasound Scan (USS)**: Initial screening modality; differentiates cystic from solid masses; solid tumors appear hyperechoic or heterogeneous; detects gross hydronephrosis.
* **Triple-Phase Contrast-Enhanced CT Abdomen and Pelvis**: Gold standard diagnostic tool. Phases include:
* **Corticomedullary phase** (30-70s post-injection): Maximizes visualization of hypervascular cortical RCC lesions.
* **Nephrographic phase** (80-120s post-injection): Optimal phase to define the margins of the tumor within the renal parenchyma.
* **Excretory phase** (3-5 mins post-injection): Clarifies involvement of the renal pelvicalyceal system and excludes upper tract TCC.

### Staging and Structural Mapping Investigations
* **CT Chest**: Mandatory for all confirmed renal tumors to rule out pulmonary cannonball metastases.
* **Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) Abdomen**: Specifically indicated if renal insufficiency precludes iodinated CT contrast, or to map the precise cranial extension of an **IVC tumor thrombus**.
* **Renal Scintigraphy (DMSA / DTPA scan)**: Indicated prior to partial nephrectomy to accurately quantify individual split renal function when the contralateral kidney is diseased.
* **Renal Tumor Biopsy**: **Not routinely performed** for resectable masses due to sampling error and low diagnostic yield; indicated strictly if a secondary metastasis, abscess, or lymphoma is suspected, or prior to active surveillance / ablative therapies.

### Laboratory Workup
* Full Blood Count (FBC): Detects anemia of chronic disease or paraneoplastic erythrocytosis.
* Renal Function Tests (Urea, Creatinine, eGFR): Establishes baseline global renal capacity.
* Serum Calcium: Screens for paraneoplastic hypercalcemia.
* Liver Function Tests (LFT): Assesses for ALP elevations seen in bone metastases or Stauffer's syndrome.
* Coagulation Profile: Mandated pre-operatively.

### TNM Staging Table for Renal Cell Carcinoma
| Stage | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **T1a** | Tumor ≤ 4 cm in greatest dimension, limited to the kidney. |
| **T1b** | Tumor > 4 cm but ≤ 7 cm in greatest dimension, limited to the kidney. |
| **T2a** | Tumor > 7 cm but ≤ 10 cm in greatest dimension, limited to the kidney. |
| **T2b** | Tumor > 10 cm, limited to the kidney. |
| **T3a** | Tumor extends into the renal vein or its segmental branches, or invades perirenal/sinus fat but not beyond Gerota's fascia. |
| **T3b** | Tumor grossly extends into the IVC below the diaphragm. |
| **T3c** | Tumor grossly extends into the IVC above the diaphragm or invades the wall of the IVC. |
| **T4** | Tumor invades beyond Gerota's fascia (including contiguous extension into the ipsilateral adrenal gland). |
| **N1** | Metastasis in regional lymph node(s). |
| **M1** | Distant metastasis present. |

### Bosniak Classification of Renal Cysts (CT-based)
| Category | Radiographic Description | Malignancy Risk | Clinical Management |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Bosniak I** | Simple benign cyst: hair-thin wall, no septa, calcification, or contrast enhancement. | ~0% | Ignore / No follow-up |
| **Bosniak II** | Benign cyst: few thin septa, fine calcification; high-attenuation non-enhancing cysts < 3 cm. | ~0% | Ignore / No follow-up |
| **Bosniak IIF** | Minimally complex cyst: multiple thin septa, minimal thick calcification, no measurable contrast enhancement. | ~5-10% | Serial imaging follow-up (CT/MRI) |
| **Bosniak III** | Indeterminate cystic mass: thickened, irregular walls or septa with **measurable contrast enhancement**. | ~50% | Surgical excision (Partial/Radical Nephrectomy) |
| **Bosniak IV** | Malignant cystic mass: clearly defined Bosniak III features plus **independent enhancing nodular solid components**. | >90% | Surgical excision |

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications

### Management of Localized Disease (Stages I - III)
* **Partial Nephrectomy (Nephron-Sparing Surgery)**: Preferred gold standard for **T1a and T1b tumors** up to 7 cm, provided the tumor location is anatomically feasible (exophytic). Absolutely indicated in patients with a solitary kidney, bilateral renal tumors, or pre-existing chronic kidney disease.
* **Radical Nephrectomy**: Indicated for large **T2-T4 tumors**, or centrally located T1 tumors unsuitable for nephron-sparing approaches. Can be performed via laparoscopic, robotic-assisted, or open routes.

### Management of Small Renal Masses in Frail Patients
* **Active Surveillance**: Reserved for elderly or comorbid patients with solid tumors **< 3 cm** demonstrating a slow growth rate (< 0.3 cm/year).
* **Thermal Ablative Therapies**: Cryoablation or Radiofrequency Ablation (RFA) are viable options for T1a tumors (< 3 cm) in patients unfit for major surgery. High risk of local recurrence compared to resection.

### Systemic Therapy for Advanced / Metastatic Disease (Stage IV)
* RCC is highly **resistant to conventional chemotherapy and radiotherapy**.
* **Cytoreductive Nephrectomy**: Performed within Hong Kong HA protocols only in highly selected patients with metastatic disease who possess excellent performance status and a resectable primary tumor, prior to launching systemic therapy.
* **First-line Systemic Regimens**: Combination of **Immune Checkpoint Inhibitors (ICI)** (e.g., Pembrolizumab + Axitinib, or Nivolumab + Cabozantinib). Tyrosine Kinase Inhibitors (TKI) targeting the VEGF pathway (e.g., Sunitinib, Pazopanib) are utilized based on IMDC risk scores.

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Operative Steps for Laparoscopic Radical Nephrectomy
* Patient placed in the **lateral decubitus position** with the table flexed to expand the loin corridor.
* Insertion of ports: umbilical/pararectal (camera), subcostal, and anterior axillary line ports.
* Mobilization of the colon medially via incision along the **white line of Toldt** to expose the retroperitoneal space.
* Dissection and exposure of the ureter; tracing cephalad to locate the renal hilum.
* **Early vascular control (先結紮血管)**: Strict anatomical priority is to isolate and double-ligate the **renal artery first**, followed by the **renal vein**. This prevents tumor embolization and minimizes renal engorgement/bleeding.
* En-bloc mobilization of the kidney within **Gerota's fascia and the perinephric fat**.
* Division of the ureter with surgical clips.
* Specimen extraction utilizing an endobag through a Pfannenstiel or expanded port-site incision.

### Operative Principles for Partial Nephrectomy
* Mobilization of the kidney to isolate the specific margins of the renal mass.
* Dissection of the renal artery to prepare for vascular clamping.
* **Warm Ischaemia Time (WIT) (熱缺血時間)**: Application of a bulldog clamp to the renal artery. Tumor excision and renorraphy must be completed strictly **within 20 to 25 minutes** to prevent irreversible ischemic acute tubular necrosis of the remnant nephrons.
* Excision of the tumor with a 2-3 mm normal tissue margin.
* Renorraphy: Two-layer closure of the defect (inner continuous layer for the collecting system and vessels; outer layer with bolster clips for parenchyma).

### Key Consent and Surgical Risk Issues
* **Hemorrhage**: High risk due to proximity to the aorta, IVC, and major hilar branches; may mandate emergency conversion to open surgery.
* **Adjacent Organ Damage**: Injury to the spleen or pancreas (left-sided) or duodenum and liver (right-sided); pleural breach causing pneumothorax.
* **Post-operative Renal Failure**: Dialysis dependence if the contralateral organ has poor baseline function.
* **Urine Leak**: Specific to partial nephrectomy if the pelvicalyceal system is breached.

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Immediate Post-operative Monitoring
* Hourly monitoring of urine output; strict target **> 0.5 mL/kg/hour** to verify structural adequacy and perfusion of the remaining nephrons.
* Continuous hemodynamic tracking to watch for hidden retroperitoneal hemorrhage.
* Daily monitoring of serum creatinine and electrolytes.

### Enhanced Recovery After Surgery (ERAS) Protocols
* **Avoidance of Routine Retroperitoneal Drains**: Drains removed early unless high fluid volume suggests a urinary leak after partial nephrectomy.
* **Early Mobilization**: Out of bed on Post-op Day 1 to reduce thromboembolic events and hypostatic pneumonia.
* **Early Oral Intake**: Clear fluids within hours post-op; rapid escalation to a solid diet to minimize paralytic ileus.
* **Multimodal Analgesia**: Opioid-sparing strategies, heavy reliance on regional nerve blocks (TAP block) and regular paracetamol/tramadol; **NSAIDs are strictly contraindicated** to protect residual renal function.

### Long-term Surveillance and Rehabilitation
* Regular monitoring of eGFR to track chronic kidney disease status.
* Lifestyle modification counseling: strict blood pressure control (< 130/80 mmHg), cessation of smoking, avoidance of nephrotoxic medications (NSAIDs, aminoglycosides), and low-protein diet if renal insufficiency develops.
* HA protocol surveillance: Abdominopelvic CT and chest X-ray at 6 months, then annually for 5 years to monitor for local recurrence or distant lung/bone drop metastases.

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Early Complications
* **Primary Hemorrhage**: Hilum clip slippage or raw parenchymal surface bleeding; requires urgent re-laparoscopy or angioembolization.
* **Pneumothorax**: Accidental injury to the diaphragm during upper pole dissection; managed with intraoperative air aspiration or chest tube insertion.
* **Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)**: Secondary to prolonged warm ischemia or hypovolemia.

### Intermediate Complications
* **Urinary Fistula / Urinoma**: Persistent urine leak from the collecting system after partial nephrectomy; presents as persistent drain output or localized peritonitis; managed by **retrograde double-J (DJ) stent** insertion and urethral catheterization to lower system pressures.
* Secondary hemorrhage due to **pseudoaneurysm formation** on the resection margin; presents with sudden gross hematuria; treated with selective renal artery embolization.
* Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) or Pulmonary Embolism (PE).

### Late Complications
* **Incisional Hernia**: Higher incidence with flank/open anterior subcostal approaches.
* **Chronic Kidney Disease (CKG)**: Accelerated nephron loss over time.
* **Tumor Recurrence / Port-site Metastasis**.

### Prognostic Indicators
* Governed primarily by the **Stage** at presentation and the **Fuhrman / ISUP Nuclear Grade** (Grades 1-4 based on nuclear size and nucleolar prominence).
* 5-year survival for Stage I (T1) disease exceeds 90% to 95%.
* 5-year survival for Stage IV (metastatic) disease drops sharply to < 10% to 15%, though modified heavily by newer immunotherapeutic dual-agent responses.

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: Why must the renal artery be isolated and clamped before the renal vein during a radical nephrectomy? Answer: Clamping the artery first cuts off inflow, reducing parenchymal engorgement and severe bleeding, and minimizes the risk of tumor cell dissemination during kidney manipulation.
* Viva Question: A patient presents with persistent drainage of clear yellow fluid 5 days post partial nephrectomy. What is the test to confirm the diagnosis and the management? Answer: Test the fluid for creatinine; if significantly higher than serum levels, a urine leak is confirmed. Manage by placing an internal retrograde double-J stent and indwelling Foley catheter to decompress the collecting system.
* Trap: Assuming a solid renal mass with a central stellate scar on CT is safely benign oncocytoma and omitting surgery. Pearl: Clear cell RCC can look identical to oncocytoma with central necrosis; any suspicious solid mass requires resection for definitive histology.
* Trap: Performing an urgent biopsy on a resectable 5 cm vascular renal cortical mass to confirm RCC before offering a radical nephrectomy. Pearl: Biopsies are not indicated for clearly resectable renal masses due to high false-negative rates and risks of bleeding; management is guided by cross-sectional imaging features.
* Pitfall: Forgetting to stop regular NSAID prescriptions for post-operative pain relief after a radical or partial nephrectomy. Ensure multi-modal non-nephrotoxic analgesia is ordered, as NSAIDs compromise the single remaining kidney by blocking afferent arteriolar vasodilation.
* Pitfall: Ordering a plain X-ray KUB to evaluate or rule out an incidentially suspected malignant renal mass. X-ray KUB cannot visualize soft tissue renal parenchymal tumors; a triple-phase contrast CT is always mandatory.
