# Pancreas Transplantation (胰臟移植)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Pancreas Transplantation
* **Pancreas Transplantation (胰臟移植)**: Surgical replacement of a diseased pancreas with a healthy cadaveric donor organ to restore normal glucose homeostasis and prevent/halt secondary diabetic complications.
* Usually combined with kidney transplant as **Simultaneous Pancreas-Kidney (SPK)** transplant.

### Hong Kong Local Epidemiology of Pancreas Transplantation
* Highly restricted due to extreme shortage of cadaveric donors in HK.
* Centralised exclusively at **Queen Mary Hospital (瑪麗醫院)** under **Hospital Authority (HA)**.
* **SPK** >90% of cases; reserved primarily for young **Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus (第一型糖尿病)** patients with **End-Stage Renal Failure (末期腎衰竭, ESRF)**.
* 1-year graft survival: >85%; Patient survival: >95%.

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Surgical Anatomy of the Donor Pancreas
* **Arterial Supply**: Dual supply requiring bench reconstruction.
* **Superior Mesenteric Artery (腸繫膜上動脈, SMA)** → Supplies **Head/Uncinate** via inferior pancreaticoduodenal artery.
* **Splenic Artery (脾動脈)** → Supplies **Body/Tail**.
* **Venous Drainage**: **Portal Vein (門靜脈)** via superior mesenteric vein (SMV) & splenic vein.

### Pathophysiology of Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus Requiring Transplant
* Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic **β-cells (β細胞)** → Absolute insulin defciency.
* Chronic hyperglycaemia → Microvascular complications:
* **Diabetic Nephropathy (糖尿病腎病)** → Glomerulosclerosis → **ESRF**.
* **Diabetic Retinopathy (糖尿病視網膜病變)** → Blindness.
* **Diabetic Neuropathy (糖尿病神經病變)** → Autonomic failure → **Hypoglycaemic Unawareness (低血糖無覺察症)**.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Clinical Presentation of End-Stage Diabetic Nephropathy
* **Uraemia (尿毒症)**: Lethargy, pruritus, metallic taste, nausea.
* **Fluid Overload**: Peripheral oedema, pulmonary oedema, ↑ JVP.
* **Electrolyte Imbalance**: ↑ K+, ↑ PO4(3-), ↓ Ca2+, metabolic acidosis.

### Signs of Autonomic Neuropathy & Hypoglycaemic Unawareness
* Severe recurrent unprovoked **hypoglycaemic attacks (低血糖發作)** without adrenergic warning signs (sweating, palpitations, tremors).
* Sudden neuroglycopenia → Confusion, seizures, coma.
* Gastroparesis: Recurrent vomiting, early satiety.
* Orthostatic hypotension, neurogenic bladder.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Comparison of Diabetes Surgical Management Options
| Modality | Patient Profile | Advantages | Disadvantages |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Simultaneous Pancreas-Kidney (SPK)** | T1DM + ESRF | Reverses diabetes & uraemia simultaneously, best graft survival | Major surgery, high morbidity, requires systemic immunosuppression |
| **Pancreas After Kidney (PAK)** | T1DM with previous functioning kidney graft | Avoids dialysis, restores euglycaemia | Exposes to 2nd major surgery, lower pancreas graft survival vs SPK |
| **Pancreas Transplant Alone (PTA)** | T1DM + hyperlabile glucose/hypoglycaemic unawareness (no ESRF) | Cures life-threatening hypoglycaemia | Obligates life-long immunosuppression for non-lethal (but morbid) disease |
| **Islet Cell Transplant (胰島細胞移植)** | T1DM + brittle control, unfit for major surgery | Minimally invasive (portal vein infusion) | Usually requires multiple infusions, insulin independence wanes over time |

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)

### Immunological & HLA Workup for Transplant Recipients
* **ABO Blood Grouping**: Must be compatible.
* **Human Leukocyte Antigen (HLA) Typing**: HLA-A, B, DR matching.
* **Panel Reactive Antibody (PRA)**: Assess pre-existing alloantibodies.
* **Cross-matching**: Complement-dependent cytotoxicity (CDC) or Flow cytometry → Must be -ve to prevent hyperacute rejection.

### Cardiovascular & Anatomical Screening Workup
* **Cardiology Assessment**: ECG, Echocardiogram, Myocardial perfusion scan / Coronary angiogram (critical due to high silent IHD risk in DM).
* **Vascular Imaging**: CT / Doppler US of iliac vessels → Assess calcification/patency for recipient anastomosis.
* **Infectious Disease Screen**: CMV, EBV, HIV, HBV, HCV, Syphilis, VZV serology.

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)

### Hong Kong Hospital Authority (HA) Indications for Pancreas Transplant
* **Confirmed T1DM** with insulin dependence.
* **ESRF** on dialysis or eGFR <15 mL/min (for SPK).
* Frequent, severe metabolic complications (ketoacidosis, hypoglycaemic unawareness).
* Age generally <55 years (due to physiological stress of SPK).
* BMI <30 kg/m² (obesity ↑ technical complications and insulin resistance).

### Contraindications to Pancreas Transplantation
* Absolute:
* Uncorrectable severe coronary artery disease (CAD).
* Active active infection or untreated malignancy.
* Severe peripheral vascular disease (precluding iliac anastomosis).
* Active substance abuse or documented non-compliance.
* Relative:
* BMI >30, active smoking, severe uncorrectable coagulopathy.

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Donor Bench Surgery & Vascular Reconstruction
* **Vascular Y-Graft (Y型血管移植)**: Donor common iliac artery (CIA) bifurcating into internal/external iliac arteries (IIA/EIA) is used.
* Donor IIA anastomosed to graft **Splenic Artery**.
* Donor EIA anastomosed to graft **Superior Mesenteric Artery (SMA)**.
* Converts dual arterial supply into a single CIA inflow.

### Recipient Implantation & Anastomotic Principles
* Pancreas graft routinely placed in **Right Iliac Fossa (右髂窩)**; Kidney graft in **Left Iliac Fossa**.
* **Arterial Anastomosis**: Donor Y-graft CIA → Recipient Right Common or External Iliac Artery.
* **Venous Anastomosis**: Donor Portal Vein → Recipient Right Common/External Iliac Vein (Systemic drainage) OR SMV (Portal drainage - more physiologic but technically harder).

### Comparison of Exocrine Drainage Techniques
| Technique | Anastomosis | Pros | Cons |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Enteric Drainage (腸道引流)** | Donor duodenum to recipient jejunum (Roux-en-Y or side-to-side) | Most physiologic, avoids urinary complications | Cannot monitor urine amylase for rejection, bowel leak is catastrophic |
| **Bladder Drainage (膀胱引流)** | Donor duodenum to recipient bladder dome | Easy to monitor **urine amylase** for early rejection diagnosis | Chemical cystitis, severe dehydration, metabolic acidosis (loss of HCO3-) |

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Immediate Post-operative Monitoring Protocol
* Strict bed rest initially to prevent graft thrombosis.
* Continuous IV Insulin infusion until graft function establishes (usually works within 24-48 hrs → sudden drop in insulin requirement).
* Daily **Amylase / Lipase (澱粉酶/脂肪酶)** monitoring.
* Routine Doppler US of iliac fossa on Day 1-2 → Confirm arterial inflow and venous outflow.
* Prophylactic Anticoagulation (Heparin / Aspirin) → Critical to prevent graft thrombosis.

### Maintenance Immunosuppression Regimen
* **Induction Phase**: Basiliximab (IL-2 receptor antagonist) or Anti-thymocyte globulin (ATG).
* **Maintenance Phase (Triple Therapy)**:
* **Calcineurin Inhibitor**: **Tacrolimus (他克莫司)** (target trough levels required).
* **Antimetabolite**: **Mycophenolate Mofetil (MMF)**.
* **Corticosteroid**: **Prednisolone (類固醇)** (rapid taper if possible to avoid steroid-induced diabetes).
* **Infection Prophylaxis**: Valganciclovir (CMV), Co-trimoxazole (PCP), Nystatin (Candida).

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Early Surgical Complications of Pancreas Transplant
* **Vascular Thrombosis (血管栓塞)** (2-10%): Most feared early complication. Usually venous. Leads to sudden acute graft swelling, dark haemorrhagic drainage, and complete graft failure. Requires urgent graft pancreatectomy.
* **Transplant Pancreatitis (移植胰臟炎)**: Ischaemia-reperfusion injury → transient ↑ amylase/lipase.
* **Anastomotic Leak**: Duodeno-enteric leak → intra-abdominal sepsis; Duodeno-vesical leak → haematuria, peritonitis.

### Intermediate and Late Immunological Complications
* **Acute Rejection (急性排斥)**:
* Often discordant (pancreas rejected while kidney unaffected, or vice versa).
* Exocrine dysfunction (↓ urine amylase in bladder-drained) precedes endocrine dysfunction (hyperglycaemia).
* Diagnosed via percutaneous US-guided core biopsy or cystoscopic duodenal biopsy (if bladder drained).
* **CMV Infection (巨細胞病毒感染)**: Occurs 1-6 months post-op; causes graft dysfunction, pneumonitis, enteritis.

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: What is the most devastating early non-immunological complication of pancreas transplantation? Answer: Venous thrombosis of the graft, typically occurring within the first 48 hours, which invariably requires immediate graft pancreatectomy.
* Viva Question: How do you perform bench reconstruction of the donor pancreas arterial supply? Answer: A donor iliac Y-graft is constructed by anastomosing the donor internal iliac artery to the graft splenic artery, and the external iliac artery to the graft superior mesenteric artery.
* Viva Question: In a bladder-drained pancreas transplant, what is the earliest biochemical marker of acute cellular rejection? Answer: A sudden drop in urinary amylase levels, which predates any rise in serum glucose.
* Trap: Relying on rising blood glucose as an early warning sign of pancreas graft rejection. Pearl: Endocrine failure (hyperglycaemia) is a very late sign of rejection indicating extensive β-cell destruction; exocrine dysfunction occurs much earlier.
* Pitfall: Underestimating the risk of severe dehydration and metabolic acidosis in bladder-drained pancreas recipients. The donor pancreas secretes high volumes of bicarbonate-rich fluid into the bladder, causing massive obligatory fluid and base losses requiring heavy oral or IV bicarbonate supplementation.
* Trap: Assuming normal kidney function implies a healthy pancreas in SPK transplantation. Pearl: Pancreas and kidney grafts can undergo discordant acute rejection; normal creatinine does not rule out acute pancreas rejection.
* Pitfall: Using standard subcutaneous heparin doses for DVT prophylaxis without considering graft thrombosis risk. Pancreas grafts are uniquely prone to thrombosis due to low flow state; low-dose systemic heparin infusion or aggressive antiplatelet therapy is often required in the strict HA protocol.
