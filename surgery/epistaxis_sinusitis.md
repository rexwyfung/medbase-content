# Epistaxis & Sinusitis (鼻出血與鼻竇炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Definition of Epistaxis and Rhinosinusitis
* **Epistaxis (鼻出血)**: Acute hemorrhage from the nasal cavity, nostrils, or nasopharynx.
* **Rhinosinusitis (鼻竇炎)**: Inflammation of the mucosal lining of the nasal cavity and paranasal sinuses (acute <4 weeks, chronic >12 weeks).

### Local Hong Kong Epidemiology of Epistaxis and Rhinosinusitis
* **Bimodal Age Distribution**: Epistaxis incidence peaks at <10 years and >50 years of age.
* **Hong Kong High-Yield Context**: Due to endemic Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV) in Southern China, **Nasopharyngeal Carcinoma (NPC) (鼻咽癌)** MUST be excluded in any adult Hong Kong patient presenting with unilateral epistaxis or blood-stained post-nasal drip.
* **Seasonal Variation**: ↑ Sinusitis and epistaxis presentations to HA Emergency Departments during winter months (low humidity) and influenza seasons.

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Anterior Epistaxis Anatomy (Little's Area)
* **Little's Area / Kiesselbach's Plexus (克氏靜脈叢)**: Located at the anterior-inferior nasal septum. Source of 90% of epistaxis events.
* **Anterior Ethmoidal Artery (AEA)**: Contributes to Little's Area; branches from Ophthalmic Artery → Internal Carotid Artery (ICA).
* **Sphenopalatine Artery (SPA)**: Contributes to Little's Area; branches from Maxillary Artery → External Carotid Artery (ECA).
* **Superior Labial Artery**: Contributes to Little's Area; branches from Facial Artery.
* **Greater Palatine Artery**: Terminal branch supplying the septum.

### Posterior Epistaxis Anatomy (Woodruff's Plexus)
* **Woodruff's Plexus**: Venous/arterial plexus located in the posterior nasal cavity.
* **Primary Source**: Predominantly fed by the **Sphenopalatine Artery (蝶腭動脈)**.
* **Pathophysiological Association**: Highly associated with uncontrolled hypertension and atherosclerosis, → profuse, difficult-to-control hemorrhage.

### Pathophysiology of Rhinosinusitis
* **Ostiomeatal Complex (OMC) Obstruction**: Blockage of the common drainage pathway for maxillary, anterior ethmoid, and frontal sinuses.
* **Cascade Event**: OMC obstruction → stasis of sinus secretions → ↓ mucociliary clearance → local hypoxia → secondary bacterial overgrowth and mucosal inflammation.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Clinical Presentation and Signs of Epistaxis
* **Anterior Bleed**: Unilateral dripping of fresh blood from the nares.
* **Posterior Bleed**: Frequent swallowing, haematemesis (vomiting swallowed blood), bilateral nares involvement, blood visually dripping down the posterior pharynx.
* **Haemodynamic Instability Signs**: Tachycardia (+ve), hypotension (+ve), prolonged capillary refill time (>2s).

### Diagnostic Symptom Criteria for Rhinosinusitis
* **Diagnostic Requirement**: Requires ≥2 major symptoms OR 1 major + ≥2 minor symptoms.
* **Major Symptom: Nasal Obstruction/Congestion (鼻塞)**.
* **Major Symptom: Purulent Nasal Discharge (膿性鼻涕)** (anteriorly or as post-nasal drip).
* **Major Symptom: Facial Pain/Pressure (面部疼痛)** (classically exacerbated on bending forward).
* **Major Symptom: Hyposmia/Anosmia (嗅覺減退)**.
* **Minor Symptoms**: Headache, halitosis (bad breath), dental pain, chronic cough, ear fullness.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Etiological Differentials for Epistaxis
| Category | Key Differentials | Discriminatory Clinical Features |
|---|---|---|
| **Local (Trauma/Inflammation)** | Digital trauma, Allergic rhinitis, Septal deviation | Anterior bleed, young age, prominent crusting at Little's area. |
| **Neoplastic (HK Critical)** | **Nasopharyngeal Carcinoma (NPC) (鼻咽癌)**, Juvenile Nasopharyngeal Angiofibroma (JNA) | Unilateral symptoms, middle ear effusion, male adolescent (JNA), cervical lymphadenopathy. |
| **Systemic/Coagulopathy** | Antiplatelets/DOACs, Liver cirrhosis, Haemophilia, HHT | Bilateral/recurrent bleeds, petechiae, deranged LFTs/clotting profile, telangiectasias (HHT). |
| **Cardiovascular** | Uncontrolled Hypertension | Usually posterior bleed, highly pulsatile, older demographic. |

### Discriminatory Features for Rhinosinusitis Subtypes
* **Viral vs Bacterial**: Viral symptoms peak at day 3 and resolve by day 10. Bacterial is suspected if symptoms persist >10 days or demonstrate "double sickening" (worsening after initial improvement).
* **Allergic Fungal Rhinosinusitis (AFRS)**: Features thick "peanut butter" eosinophilic mucin, unilateral nasal polyps, and bone erosion on CT without invasive tissue destruction.

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)

### Laboratory Workup for Epistaxis and Sinusitis
* **Complete Blood Count (CBC)**: Assess for dilutional anemia (Hb ↓) and underlying thrombocytopenia (Plt ↓).
* **Coagulation Profile (PT/APTT/INR)**: Mandatory if the patient is on anticoagulants, has a known bleeding disorder, or has clinical hepatic dysfunction.
* **Group & Save / Crossmatch**: Essential for severe posterior bleeds anticipating significant blood volume loss and transfusion.

### Endoscopic and Imaging Modalities for Epistaxis and Sinusitis
* **Flexible Nasoendoscopy (鼻內視鏡檢查)**: Gold standard in the outpatient/ED setting to identify the exact bleeding source, rule out NPC, or visualize nasal polyps/purulence.
* **CT Paranasal Sinuses (Coronal Cuts)**: Gold standard preoperative mapping for FESS. Crucial to assess OMC anatomy, lamina papyracea integrity, and skull base depth (Keros classification).
* **MRI Head & Neck**: Specifically indicated if NPC is suspected or to delineate intracranial/orbital extension of aggressive sinus disease.

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)

### HA Acute Epistaxis Management Algorithm
* **1st Line (First Aid)**: **Trotter's Method** (patient leans forward, firmly pinches cartilaginous nose for 15 mins, applies ice pack to forehead/nares).
* **2nd Line (Chemical Cautery)**: **Silver Nitrate (AgNO3)** cautery for a visible anterior bleeding point.
* **3rd Line (Anterior Packing)**: Insertion of Merocel or BIPP (Bismuth Iodoform Paraffin Paste) pack. Leave in situ for 24-48 hrs + prescribe prophylactic oral antibiotics (e.g., Amoxicillin/Clavulanate) to prevent Toxic Shock Syndrome.
* **4th Line (Posterior Packing)**: Foley catheter balloon inflated in the post-nasal space + anterior pack. Mandates ward admission and continuous SpO2 monitoring.
* **5th Line (Surgical/Endovascular)**: Endoscopic surgical ligation or Interventional Radiology (IR) embolization.

### Medical Management Algorithm for Rhinosinusitis
* **Acute Bacterial Rhinosinusitis**: Intranasal corticosteroids + normal saline douching + oral antibiotics (Amoxicillin/Clavulanate for 7-14 days per HA antimicrobial stewardship guidelines).
* **Chronic Rhinosinusitis (CRS)**: Long-term high-dose intranasal steroids + large volume saline irrigation.

### Indications for Surgical Intervention (FESS / Ligation)
* **Epistaxis Surgical Indications**: Failure of bilateral packing to control hemorrhage, recurrent massive bleeds with haemodynamic instability, or patient intolerance to packing.
* **Sinusitis Surgical Indications**: Failure of maximal medical therapy (>12 weeks), complicated sinusitis (orbital cellulitis, subperiosteal abscess), or massive polyposis causing complete airway obstruction.

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Operative Principles for Endoscopic Sphenopalatine Artery (SPA) Ligation
* **Approach**: Transnasal endoscopic visualization.
* **Exposure**: Identify the middle turbinate; make a vertical incision at the posterior fontanelle to elevate a mucoperiosteal flap.
* **Ligation**: Expose the sphenopalatine foramen, isolate the SPA (and all its anatomical branches), and apply titanium clips securely.

### Operative Principles for Functional Endoscopic Sinus Surgery (FESS)
* **Core Concept**: Restore normal physiological ventilation and mucociliary clearance by surgically opening the blocked OMC.
* **Step 1: Uncinectomy**: Removal of the uncinate process to expose the bulla ethmoidalis.
* **Step 2: Middle Meatal Antrostomy**: Widening the natural maxillary ostium.
* **Step 3: Ethmoidectomy**: Clearing the anterior and posterior ethmoid air cells.
* **Step 4: Sphenoidotomy / Frontal Recess Clearance**: Performed only as indicated by the extent of the disease on CT.

### Informed Consent and Critical Surgical Risks
* **Orbital Injury**: Breach of the lamina papyracea → orbital hematoma, diplopia (double vision), or irreversible blindness (視力喪失).
* **Skull Base Injury**: Breach of the cribriform plate → **Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF) Leak (腦脊液外漏)**, tension pneumocephalus, or meningitis.
* **Bleeding**: Catastrophic injury to the anterior ethmoidal artery or internal carotid artery (during sphenoid surgery).
* **Synechiae (Adhesions)**: Post-operative scarring requiring revision surgery.

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Immediate Post-operative Airway and Hemodynamic Management
* **Airway Monitoring**: Continuous SpO2 and respiratory rate tracking on the ward, especially if nasal packing (anterior or posterior) is retained.
* **Analgesia & Anti-emetics**: Aggressively minimize post-operative nausea and vomiting (PONV) to prevent sudden spikes in venous pressure that precipitate re-bleeding.
* **Pack Removal**: HA protocols generally favor early pack removal (24-48 hrs) to facilitate safe discharge, provided haemostasis is secure.

### Rehabilitation and Lifestyle Modifications Post-Surgery
* **Nasal Hygiene**: Initiate large-volume normal saline **Nasal Douching (鼻腔沖洗)** 24-48 hrs post-FESS to clear blood clots, remove crusts, and prevent synechiae formation.
* **Activity Restriction**: Strictly avoid hot fluids, hot showers, heavy lifting, and strenuous cardiovascular exercise for 2-4 weeks to prevent ↑ systemic blood pressure and delayed re-bleeding.
* **Medication Adjustment**: Temporarily cease NSAIDs and Aspirin (if cardiologically cleared by physicians) to minimize hemorrhage risk during the mucosal healing phase.

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Early Surgical Complications (0-7 Days)
* **Toxic Shock Syndrome (TSS)**: Life-threatening complication due to retained nasal packs fostering Staphylococcus aureus exotoxin. Signs: high fever, diffuse macular rash, hypotension.
* **Orbital Hematoma**: Post-FESS surgical emergency presenting with proptosis and ophthalmoplegia. Requires immediate lateral canthotomy and cantholysis.
* **Re-bleeding**: Often from an unidentified or unclipped posterior branch of the SPA.

### Intermediate to Late Surgical Complications
* **Septal Perforation (鼻中隔穿孔)**: Iatrogenic complication from bilateral simultaneous silver nitrate cautery. Causes crusting, recurrent minor bleeds, and a whistling sound on inspiration.
* **Synechiae Formation**: Fibrous adhesions bridging the middle turbinate and lateral nasal wall, causing recurrent sinus obstruction and failure of FESS.
* **Empty Nose Syndrome**: Rare, paradoxical sensation of severe nasal obstruction despite widely patent nasal cavities following aggressive turbinate resection.

### Long-Term Prognosis for Epistaxis and Sinusitis
* **Epistaxis Prognosis**: Excellent control rate (>90%) with endoscopic SPA ligation for refractory posterior bleeds.
* **Sinusitis Prognosis**: Good symptomatic relief with FESS for chronic cases; however, Chronic Rhinosinusitis with Nasal Polyps (CRSwNP) has a high long-term recurrence rate requiring lifelong topical steroid maintenance.

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: What is the most critical condition to rule out in a 55-year-old Hong Kong man presenting with unilateral epistaxis, aural fullness, and a neck lump? Answer: Nasopharyngeal Carcinoma (NPC) (鼻咽癌).
* Trap: Applying silver nitrate cautery to both sides of the nasal septum simultaneously to control bilateral bleeding. Pearl: Only cauterize one side at a time, allowing weeks between procedures, to prevent bilateral mucosal ischemic necrosis and subsequent septal perforation.
* Pitfall: Discharging a patient with an anterior nasal pack without prophylactic antibiotics. This significantly increases the risk of Staphylococcal Toxic Shock Syndrome; always prescribe a prophylactic oral antibiotic (e.g., Amoxicillin/Clavulanate) while the pack is in situ.
* Viva Question: During FESS, the surgeon observes yellow orbital fat protruding into the nasal cavity. What anatomical structure has been iatrogenically breached? Answer: The lamina papyracea (紙樣板).
* Trap: Assuming a posterior epistaxis is adequately controlled with just an anterior Merocel pack. Pearl: Blood will track backwards down the posterior pharynx silently; a Foley catheter balloon or dedicated posterior pack must be deployed, and the patient's airway/SpO2 must be closely monitored in an inpatient setting.
* Pitfall: Proceeding with Functional Endoscopic Sinus Surgery (FESS) without personally reviewing the pre-operative coronal CT scans. Coronal CT scans are mandatory to assess the depth of the olfactory fossa (Keros classification) and skull base integrity to prevent catastrophic cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) leaks.
