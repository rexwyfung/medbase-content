# Adrenal Tumours (腎上腺腫瘤)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Definition of Adrenal Tumours
* **Adrenal Tumours (腎上腺腫瘤)**: Neoplastic growths arising from **Adrenal Cortex (腎上腺皮質)** or **Adrenal Medulla (腎上腺髓質)**
* Broadly classified into:
  * **Functional (分泌性)** vs **Non-functional (非分泌性)**
  * **Benign (良性)** vs **Malignant (惡性)**
* **Incidentalomas (意外發現的腎上腺瘤)**: Asymptomatic masses >1cm discovered serendipitously on imaging for unrelated reasons

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong (Hospital Authority Data)
* **Incidentalomas**: Found in ~4-5% of all abdominal CTs in HK HA hospitals
* Most common type: **Non-functioning Cortical Adenoma (無功能性皮質腺瘤)** (~80%)
* Age: Incidence ↑ with age (peaks in 50-70s)
* **Pheochromocytoma (嗜鉻細胞瘤)**: Rare (<1% of hypertensive pts in HK)
* **Adrenocortical Carcinoma (ACC) (腎上腺皮質癌)**: Extremely rare (1-2 per million), highly aggressive

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Surgical Anatomy of Adrenal Glands (腎上腺解剖)
* **Location**: Retroperitoneal, superior/medial to kidneys, enclosed in Gerota’s fascia
* **Arterial Supply** (Highly vascular, 3 sources):
  * Superior adrenal artery ← Inferior phrenic artery
  * Middle adrenal artery ← Abdominal aorta
  * Inferior adrenal artery ← Renal artery
* **Venous Drainage (CRITICAL SURGICAL ANATOMY)**:
  * **Right Adrenal Vein (右腎上腺靜脈)**: Very short (1-5mm) → drains DIRECTLY into **Inferior Vena Cava (IVC) (下腔靜脈)** posteriorly. High risk of avulsion during surgery.
  * **Left Adrenal Vein (左腎上腺靜脈)**: Longer → joins inferior phrenic vein → drains into **Left Renal Vein (左腎靜脈)**.

### Pathophysiology of Pheochromocytoma (嗜鉻細胞瘤)
* Origin: Chromaffin cells of adrenal medulla
* Secretes: **Catecholamines (兒茶酚胺)** (Noradrenaline > Adrenaline)
	* → Continuous/episodic α & β adrenergic receptor stimulation
	* → Vasoconstriction, ↑ HR, ↑ contractility
* **Rule of 10s**:
	* 10% bilateral.
	* 10% malignant.
	* 10% extra-adrenal (paragangliomas).
	* 10% pediatric.
	* 10% familial (MEN2, VHL, NF1).

### Pathophysiology of Primary Hyperaldosteronism / Conn's Syndrome (原發性醛固酮增多症)
* Origin: Zona Glomerulosa of adrenal cortex
* Secretes: Autonomous **Aldosterone (醛固酮)**
	* → ↑ Na+ reabsorption & ↑ K+/H+ excretion in distal renal tubules
	* → Volume expansion, suppression of Plasma Renin Activity (PRA)
* Causes: Solitary adenoma (~40%), Bilateral adrenal hyperplasia (~60%)

### Pathophysiology of Cushing's Syndrome (庫欣氏症候群)
* Origin: Zona Fasciculata of adrenal cortex
* Secretes: Autonomous **Cortisol (皮質醇)**
* ACTH-independent (adrenal adenoma/carcinoma) vs ACTH-dependent (pituitary adenoma = Cushing's Disease, or ectopic ACTH)
* → Altered fat distribution, protein catabolism, immunosuppression

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Clinical Presentation of Pheochromocytoma
* **Classic Triad**:
  * **Episodic Headache (陣發性頭痛)**
  * **Sweating/Diaphoresis (出汗)**
  * **Palpitations (心悸)**
* Other signs:
  * Paroxysmal or refractory **Hypertension (高血壓)**
  * Postural hypotension (due to volume depletion)
  * Tremors, anxiety, panic attacks

### Clinical Presentation of Conn's Syndrome
* Most patients asymptomatic initially
* **Resistant Hypertension (頑固性高血壓)** (often requiring ≥ 3 anti-hypertensives)
* **Hypokalemia (低血鉀)** (only present in ~50%):
  * Muscle weakness (肌肉無力)
  * Cramps
  * Polyuria/polydipsia (Nephrogenic diabetes insipidus)

### Clinical Presentation of Cushing's Syndrome
* **Central Obesity (中央肥胖)**
* **Moon Face (月亮臉)** & Buffalo Hump (水牛肩)
* **Purple Striae (紫紋)** (>1cm wide on abdomen)
* Proximal muscle weakness
* Easy bruising, thin skin
* Hirsutism, menstrual irregularities

### Clinical Presentation of Adrenocortical Carcinoma (ACC)
* Rapid onset of Cushing's or Virilization (雄性化)
* Mixed hormonal secretion common
* **Mass effect**: Abdominal pain, palpable mass, early satiety

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Discriminatory Features of Adrenal Masses on Imaging

| Feature            | Benign Adenoma                       | Pheochromocytoma          | Adrenocortical Carcinoma (ACC)         | Metastasis |
| :----------------- | :----------------------------------- | :------------------------ | :------------------------------------- | :--------- |
| **Size**           | Usually < 4cm                        | Variable (often 3-5cm)    | Usually > 4-6cm                        | Variable   |
| **Shape/Margin**   | Smooth, well-defined                 | Smooth or cystic/necrotic | Irregular, ill-defined, lobulated      | Irregular  |
| **Lipid Content**  | High (low unenhanced CT attenuation) | Low                       | Low                                    | Low        |
| **Vascularity**    | Mild                                 | Highly vascular           | Highly vascular, necrotic core         | Variable   |
| **Local Invasion** | Absent                               | Rare                      | Common (IVC thrombus, adjacent organs) | Possible   |

### Differential Diagnosis of Refractory Hypertension in Young Patients
* Renal artery stenosis (Renovascular disease)
* Coarctation of the aorta
* Thyroid dysfunction (Hyper/Hypothyroidism)
* Essential hypertension (diagnosis of exclusion)

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)

### Biochemical Workup for Adrenal Incidentaloma (Functional Screen)
* ALL incidentalomas MUST be screened for hypersecretion (HA Protocol):
	1. **Hypercortisolism**: 1mg Overnight Dexamethasone Suppression Test (ONDST). Failure to suppress serum cortisol <50 nmol/L = +ve.
	2. **Pheochromocytoma**: 24-hour urine fractionated metanephrines & catecholamines OR plasma free metanephrines.
	3. **Hyperaldosteronism**: (Only if hypertensive or hypokalemic) Aldosterone-to-Renin Ratio (ARR).
	4. **Virilization**: Serum DHEAS, testosterone, 17-OH progesterone (if clinically indicated or mass >4cm).
![image](https://pub-f0606dedf6d94e4bb5b1999cbd065914.r2.dev/paediatrics/59d851f9066923f19294b38f0220cbc3.png)

### Confirmatory Functional Tests
* **Conn's Confirmatory Test**: Saline infusion test (failure to suppress aldosterone).
* **Conn's Lateralization**: **Adrenal Venous Sampling (AVS) (腎上腺靜脈採樣)** - Gold standard to differentiate unilateral adenoma from bilateral hyperplasia before surgery.

### Radiological Investigations (Imaging Protocol)
* **1st Line: Adrenal Protocol Non-contrast CT (腎上腺電腦掃描)**
  * Assesses lipid content (Hounsfield Units - HU)
  * **Adenoma**: <10 HU (lipid-rich)
* **Contrast Washout CT**:
  * Absolute washout >60% OR Relative washout >40% at 15 mins → **Benign Adenoma**
  * Delayed washout → Pheo, ACC, or Metastasis
* **MRI Abdomen**: Useful for distinguishing pheochromocytoma (hyperintense on T2 = "lightbulb sign") and evaluating local invasion/IVC thrombus in ACC.
* **Functional Imaging**:
  * **MIBG Scan (碘代苄胍掃描)**: Localizes pheochromocytoma/paraganglioma.
  * **FDG-PET Scan**: Differentiates benign from malignant/metastatic disease.

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)

### Surgical Indications for Adrenalectomy (腎上腺切除術)
* **Functional tumours**: ALL functional tumours (Pheo, Conn's adenoma, Cushing's adenoma) regardless of size.
* **Non-functional tumours**: Size $\ge$ 4cm (due to ↑ risk of ACC).
* **Suspicious imaging**: High HU >20, delayed washout, irregular margins, necrosis.
* **Metastasis**: Solitary adrenal metastasis (e.g., from NSCLC) with controlled primary.

### Medical Optimization for Pheochromocytoma (CRITICAL HA PROTOCOL)
* Goal: Prevent intraoperative hypertensive crisis.
* **Step 1: Alpha-blockade (Must start 10-14 days pre-op)**
  * Non-selective: Phenoxybenzamine (traditional, profound hypotension risk).
  * Selective: **Prazosin / Doxazosin (多沙唑嗪)** (Common in HK HA due to availability & fewer side effects).
* **Step 2: Beta-blockade (Start ONLY AFTER adequate alpha-blockade)**
  * **Propranolol (普萘洛爾)** (usually 2-3 days pre-op).
  * Prevents reflex tachycardia. *NEVER give β-blockers first (causes unopposed ɑ-stimulation → hypertensive crisis).*
* **Step 3: Volume Expansion**
  * High salt diet + vigorous IV fluid hydration pre-op to prevent post-resection profound hypotension.

### Medical Optimization for Conn's & Cushing's
* **Conn's**: Spironolactone (potassium-sparing diuretic) to normalize K+ and control BP. K+ supplements.
* **Cushing's**: Control diabetes & hypertension. Consider perioperative prophylactic heparin (high prothrombotic state) and stress-dose intra-op steroids.

### Conservative Management for Incidentalomas
* Criteria (ALL):
	* Non-functional.
	* Benign features on CT (<10 HU).
	* Size < 4cm.
* Follow-up Protocol:
  * Repeat Non-contrast CT at 6-12 months.
  * Repeat biochemical screening annually for up to 4-5 years.
  * Surgery if size increases by >1cm or becomes functional.

### Operative Approaches for Adrenalectomy
* **Minimally Invasive (Gold Standard)**:
  * **Laparoscopic Transabdominal Adrenalectomy (腹腔鏡腎上腺切除術)**: Familiar anatomy, larger working space. Good for tumours <6-8cm.
  * **Posterior Retroperitoneoscopic Adrenalectomy (PRA) (後腹腔鏡腎上腺切除術)**: Avoids intra-abdominal adhesions, faster recovery, avoids bowel mobilization. Patient prone. Ideal for bilateral tumours or previous abdominal surgery.
* **Open Adrenalectomy (傳統開刀手術)**:
  * Indicated for: Suspected ACC, massive tumours (>8-10cm), evidence of local invasion, IVC thrombus.

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Key Surgical Steps & Principles
* **Minimal Manipulation**: "No-touch" technique to prevent massive catecholamine release (Pheo) or tumour rupture (ACC).
* **Early Venous Ligation (早期靜脈結紮)**: Crucial step. Ligate the main adrenal vein BEFORE mobilizing the gland to prevent systemic hormone surge.
  * Right side: High risk of IVC injury. Use clips securely.
  * Left side: Identify junction with left renal vein.

### Consent Issues & Pre-op Counseling
* Conversion to open surgery.
* **Bleeding/Transfusion**: Risk of major venous hemorrhage (IVC, renal vein).
* **Adjacent Organ Injury**: Liver, duodenum (Right); Spleen, pancreas tail (Left).
* Need for **Post-op Steroid Replacement** (especially in Cushing's or bilateral adrenalectomy).
* Risk of post-op hemodynamic instability requiring ICU stay.

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Immediate Post-operative Care
* **Location**: Mandatory HDU or ICU monitoring for 24-48 hours (especially Pheochromocytoma).
* **Hemodynamic Monitoring**: Watch for profound hypotension (due to abrupt removal of catecholamines + down-regulated receptors) → treat with large volume IV fluids, rarely vasopressors.
* **Hypoglycemia Risk**: Monitor blood glucose (rebound hyperinsulinemia post-pheo resection).

### Endocrine Replacement & Monitoring Protocol
* **For Cushing's Adenoma Resection**:
  * Contralateral adrenal gland is atrophied (suppressed by ↑ cortisol).
  * Require **Intra-op Hydrocortisone (氫化可的松)** followed by strict post-op weaning regimen.
  * May take months for the HPA axis to recover.
* **For Conn's Syndrome**:
  * Stop Spironolactone and K+ supplements immediately post-op.
  * Monitor K+ levels (risk of transient hyperkalemia).

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Early Surgical Complications
* **Hemorrhage**: IVC avulsion (Right) or splenic tear (Left). Life-threatening.
* **Cardiovascular Collapse**: Refractory hypotension post-tumor removal.
* **Pneumothorax / Pleural Effusion**: Due to close proximity to diaphragm.
* **Adrenal Crisis (腎上腺危象)**: If inadequate steroid replacement in Cushing's or bilateral resection.

### Late Complications
* **Adrenal Insufficiency**: Permanent requirement for fludrocortisone and hydrocortisone if bilateral resection.
* **Hernia**: Incisional hernia (higher risk in open cases or Cushing's patients with poor tissue healing).
* **Recurrence**: Local recurrence in malignant tumours or incomplete resection of paraganglioma.

### Prognosis of Adrenal Tumours
* **Benign Adenomas (Conn's/Cushing's/Pheo)**: Excellent. Cure rate for Conn's hypertension is ~50-60% (though K+ normalizes in 100%).
* **Adrenocortical Carcinoma (ACC)**: Dismal prognosis. 5-year survival <30%. High rate of local recurrence and distant mets (liver, lung). Requires post-op **Mitotane (米托坦)** therapy.

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: Why is the right adrenal vein anatomically more dangerous to ligate than the left? Answer: The right adrenal vein is extremely short (1-5mm) and drains directly into the posterior aspect of the IVC, making it prone to avulsion and catastrophic hemorrhage if retracted blindly.
* Trap: Giving a beta-blocker (e.g., Propranolol) as first-line therapy for a tachycardic patient with a newly diagnosed Pheochromocytoma. Pearl: NEVER give beta-blockers before establishing adequate alpha-blockade. Beta-blockade abolishes skeletal muscle vasodilation, leaving alpha-mediated vasoconstriction unopposed, precipitating a lethal hypertensive crisis.
* Pitfall: Rushing to operate on a patient with a functional adrenal mass. Adequate medical optimization is strictly required; pheochromocytomas need 10-14 days of alpha-blockade and fluid loading, while Conn's requires potassium normalization with spironolactone.
* Viva Question: What is the rule of 10s for Pheochromocytoma? Answer: 10% malignant, 10% bilateral, 10% extra-adrenal (paragangliomas), 10% pediatric, and 10% familial (MEN2, VHL, NF1).
* Trap: Assuming a patient with newly resected Cushing's adenoma can be discharged without steroids because "the other gland is fine". Pearl: The contralateral adrenal cortex is profoundly suppressed by long-term high cortisol levels. Immediate intra-operative and post-operative steroid replacement is mandatory to prevent a fatal Addisonian crisis.
* Viva Question: A 45-year-old man presents with a 5cm incidentally found right adrenal mass on CT. Non-contrast attenuation is 35 HU. What is the next step in imaging? Answer: Contrast-enhanced CT with delayed washout protocol to assess for benign adenoma (>60% absolute washout) versus malignant/pheo (delayed washout).
* Pitfall: Performing a percutaneous biopsy of an indeterminate adrenal mass before biochemical testing. Biopsying an undiagnosed pheochromocytoma can trigger a fatal catecholamine crisis, and biopsying an ACC can cause needle-tract seeding. Always rule out pheo biochemically first.
