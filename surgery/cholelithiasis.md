# Gallstone Disease (膽石症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Cholelithiasis
* **Cholelithiasis**: Presence of concretions (**gallstones**) within the **gallbladder (膽囊)** (GB).
* **Choledocholithiasis**: Presence of gallstones within the **Common Bile Duct (總膽管)** (CBD).
* **Acute Cholecystitis (急性膽囊炎)**: Inflammation of the GB typically secondary to stone impaction at the GB neck or cystic duct.

### Hong Kong Epidemiology of Gallstones
* Prevalence: ~10% of the adult population in Hong Kong.
* Age: ↑ incidence with age (esp. >40 years).
* Gender: Female > Male (2:1 ratio).
* Epidemiological Shift: Historically, brown pigment stones were predominant in Asia (associated with parasitic infections). Currently, **cholesterol stones** are the most common in HK due to the adoption of a Westernized, high-fat diet.

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Surgical Anatomy of the Hepatocystic Triangle (Calot's Triangle)
* **Calot's Triangle (卡洛氏三角)** is the critical landmark for surgical dissection to avoid **Bile Duct Injury (BDI)**.
* Boundaries of the modern Hepatocystic Triangle:
	* Medial: **Common Hepatic Duct (肝總管)** (CHD)
	* Lateral: **Cystic Duct (膽囊管)**
	* Superior: **Inferior surface of the liver (肝臟下緣)**
* Contents: **Cystic artery (膽囊動脈)**, Lund's node (often enlarged in cholecystitis), lymphatics.

### Pathophysiology of Gallstone Formation
* **Cholesterol Supersaturation**: Liver secretes ↑ cholesterol / ↓ bile salts.
* **Biliary Stasis**: GB dysmotility → incomplete emptying → sludge formation.
* **Nucleation**: Mucin hypersecretion accelerates cholesterol crystallization.
* **Infection/Hemolysis**: ↑ unconjugated bilirubin → binds with calcium → pigment stones.

### Pathophysiological Types of Gallstones

| Stone Type | Composition | Key Risk Factors | Appearance & X-ray |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Cholesterol (膽固醇結石)** | >50% cholesterol | **4Fs**: Female, Fat, Forty, Fertile; OCP use, rapid weight loss | Yellow/green; Mostly radiolucent |
| **Black Pigment (黑膽色素結石)** | Calcium bilirubinate | Chronic hemolysis (Thalassemia common in HK), liver cirrhosis | Black, hard; 50% radiopaque |
| **Brown Pigment (棕膽色素結石)** | Calcium bilirubinate, fatty acids | Biliary stasis, bacterial infection, *Clonorchis sinensis* (中華肝吸蟲) | Brown, earthy; Radiolucent, common in CBD |

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Symptoms of Asymptomatic Gallstones
* 70-80% of patients remain asymptomatic.
* Discovered incidentally on USG/CT for other reasons.

### Symptoms & Signs of Biliary Colic (膽絞痛)
* Pain: Episodic, severe **Right Upper Quadrant (RUQ) (右上腹)** or epigastric pain.
* Radiation: To right shoulder or back (**Boas sign**).
* Trigger: Post-prandial (especially 30-60 mins after a **fatty meal**).
* Duration: Plateaus within 1 hour, resolves within 6 hours.
* Signs: Mild RUQ tenderness, afebrile, -ve peritonism.

### Symptoms & Signs of Acute Cholecystitis (急性膽囊炎)
* Pain: Continuous RUQ pain > 6 hours.
* Systemic: Fever, nausea, vomiting, anorexia.
* **Murphy's Sign (墨菲氏徵)**: +ve (arrest of inspiration upon palpation of the RUQ).
* Palpation: RUQ guarding / localized peritonism.
* Mass: Palpable, tender GB (in delayed presentations).

### Symptoms & Signs of Choledocholithiasis & Cholangitis (膽管炎)
* **Jaundice (黃疸)**: Scleral icterus, dark urine, pale stools.
* **Charcot's Triad (夏科氏三聯症)**: RUQ pain + Jaundice + Fever/Rigors (indicates Acute Cholangitis).
* **Reynolds' Pentad (雷諾氏五聯症)**: Charcot's Triad + Hypotension (Shock) + Altered mental status.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Discriminatory Features of RUQ / Epigastric Pain

| Differential Diagnosis | Key Discriminatory Features (Signs / Investigations) |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Peptic Ulcer Disease (消化性潰瘍)** | Related to meals (empty stomach = DU, after meal = GU). Endoscopy confirms ulcer. |
| **Acute Pancreatitis (急性胰臟炎)** | Pain radiates straight to back. ↑↑ Serum Amylase/Lipase (Lipase >3x upper limit). |
| **Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease** | Heartburn, acid regurgitation. Relieved by PPIs / antacids. |
| **Acute Appendicitis (High-riding)** | Initially peri-umbilical → RUQ/Right flank pain. USG/CT shows inflamed appendix. |
| **Right Lower Lobe Pneumonia** | Cough, sputum, pleuritic chest pain. Chest X-ray shows consolidation. |
| **Acute Hepatitis (急性肝炎)** | Diffuse liver enlargement, significant ↑ ALT/AST (often >1000 U/L), +ve Viral serology. |

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)

### Routine Blood Tests for Gallstone Disease
* **CBC**: ↑ WBC with left shift in acute cholecystitis / cholangitis.
* **LFT (Liver Function Test)**:
	* Normal in simple biliary colic / acute cholecystitis.
	* ↑ Bilirubin, ↑ ALP, ↑ GGT suggests biliary obstruction (Choledocholithiasis).
* **Amylase / Lipase**: Rule out gallstone pancreatitis.
* **Clotting profile (PT/INR)**: Essential pre-op, may be deranged if prolonged biliary obstruction (↓ Vitamin K absorption).

### First-Line Imaging for Gallstone Disease
* **Transabdominal Ultrasound (USG Hepatobiliary) (超聲波)**:
	* **Gold standard** and 1st line.
	* Finds stones (echogenic foci with posterior acoustic shadowing).
	* Signs of Acute Cholecystitis: GB wall thickening (>3mm), pericholecystic fluid, sonographic Murphy's sign +ve.
	* Measures CBD diameter (normal <6mm, >8mm highly suggestive of obstruction).

### Advanced Imaging for Suspected CBD Stones
* **MRCP (Magnetic Resonance Cholangiopancreatography)**: Non-invasive, highly sensitive for detecting CBD stones.
* **EUS (Endoscopic Ultrasound)**: Highly sensitive for microlithiasis/distal CBD stones, requires sedation.
* **ERCP (Endoscopic Retrograde Cholangiopancreatography) (內視鏡逆行性膽管胰管造影術)**: Therapeutic & diagnostic. Used if pre-op probability of CBD stone is high (with intent to extract).

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)

### Management of Asymptomatic Gallstones
* **Conservative**: Observation / Reassurance.
* **Prophylactic Surgery Indications**:
	* Porcelain GB (high cancer risk)
	* Sickle cell disease (difficulty distinguishing crisis vs cholecystitis)
	* Immunocompromised
	* Massive gallstones (>3cm)

### Management of Symptomatic Gallstones (Biliary Colic)
* Acute attack: Analgesia (NSAIDs, e.g., Diclofenac), anti-emetics.
* Definitive: **Elective Laparoscopic Cholecystectomy (腹腔鏡膽囊切除術)** as day surgery or brief admission.
* Dietary advice: Low-fat diet pending surgery.

### Hong Kong HA Protocol for Acute Cholecystitis
* **Resuscitation**: NBM, IV fluids.
* **Antibiotics**: IV broad-spectrum (e.g., Augmentin / Cefuroxime + Metronidazole per local HA antibiogram).
* **Surgical Strategy (Tokyo Guidelines compliant)**:
	* **Early Laparoscopic Cholecystectomy**: Within 72 hours (up to 1 week) of symptom onset is the standard of care in HA hospitals.
	* **Delayed / Interval Cholecystectomy**: If patient presents late (>1 week) with severe inflammation/phlegmon, treat with IV Abx, discharge, and perform surgery at 6-8 weeks.
	* **High-Risk / Unfit Patients (Severe Sepsis, poor ASA)**: **Percutaneous Transhepatic Gallbladder Drainage (PTGBD) (經皮穿肝膽囊引流術)** under local anesthesia to decompress GB, followed by delayed surgery or observation.

### Management of Choledocholithiasis (CBD Stones)
* **Two-stage approach (Most common in HK)**: Pre-operative **ERCP** with sphincterotomy & stone extraction, followed by Elective Laparoscopic Cholecystectomy.
* **One-stage approach**: Laparoscopic Cholecystectomy + Laparoscopic Common Bile Duct Exploration (LCBDE). Requires advanced laparoscopic skills.

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Critical View of Safety (CVS) in Laparoscopic Cholecystectomy
* Mandatory technique to unequivocally identify the cystic structures and prevent **Bile Duct Injury (BDI)**.
* The 3 criteria for CVS:
	* Hepatocystic triangle is cleared of all fat and fibrous tissue.
	* The lower one-third of the GB is separated from the cystic plate (liver bed).
	* **Two and only two** structures are seen entering the GB (Cystic duct and Cystic artery).\

### Key Operative Steps of Laparoscopic Cholecystectomy
* General Anesthesia, supine position (often French position with surgeon between legs or American position on patient's left).
* Establish **pneumoperitoneum (氣腹)** (12-15 mmHg).
* Insert ports: Umbilical (camera), epigastric (working), two right subcostal (retraction).
* Retract GB fundus cephalad over liver edge.
* Retract infundibulum inferolaterally to open Calot's triangle.
* Achieve **Critical View of Safety (CVS)**.
* Clip (proximal x2, distal x1) and divide Cystic Artery.
* Clip (proximal x2, distal x1) and divide Cystic Duct.
* Dissect GB off liver bed (cystic plate) using electrocautery.
* Extract GB in an endobag via the umbilical port.

### Informed Consent for Laparoscopic Cholecystectomy
* **General risks**: Bleeding, infection, DVT/PE, risks of GA.
* **Specific risks**:
	* **Bile Duct Injury (0.2-0.5%)**: Major complication, may require hepaticojejunostomy.
	* Bile leak (from cystic duct stump or ducts of Luschka).
	* Bowel/vascular injury during port insertion.
	* Retained CBD stones.
* Conversion to **Open Cholecystectomy (傳統開腹膽囊切除術)**: ~5% (higher in acute cholecystitis, previous abdominal surgery, male sex).

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### ERAS Protocol for Laparoscopic Cholecystectomy
* **Diet**: Clear fluids immediately post-op, escalate to regular diet as tolerated within 4 hours.
* **Mobilization**: Early ambulation on the day of surgery to prevent VTE and respiratory complications.
* **Analgesia**: Multi-modal (Paracetamol, NSAIDs). Avoid opioids if possible. Local anesthetic infiltration at port sites.
* **Discharge**: Same day or Post-Op Day 1 if criteria met (tolerating diet, pain controlled, mobilizing).
* **Return to work**: Generally 1-2 weeks for desk jobs, 4 weeks for heavy lifting.

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Early Complications of Laparoscopic Cholecystectomy
* **Haemorrhage**: From slipped clip on cystic artery or liver bed oozing. Presents with hypovolemic shock / excessive drain output.
* **Major Bile Duct Injury (BDI)**: Usually presents days later with progressive jaundice, sepsis, or biliary peritonitis.
* **Bile Leak**: From slipped cystic duct clip or undiagnosed duct of Luschka (accessory duct in liver bed). Presents with localized RUQ pain / biloma on USG.

### Late Complications of Laparoscopic Cholecystectomy
* **Post-Cholecystectomy Syndrome**: Persistent RUQ pain or dyspepsia post-surgery. Causes: retained CBD stone, sphincter of Oddi dysfunction, or misdiagnosed non-biliary pain (e.g., PUD).
* **Retained CBD Stones**: Presents with jaundice/colic months later.
* **Port-site Hernia**: Especially at the umbilical port (10-12mm port). Requires surgical repair if symptomatic.

### Prognosis of Gallstone Disease
* Excellent following cholecystectomy.
* The GB is not essential for digestion; bile simply flows continuously into the duodenum. Mild post-op steatorrhea is rare and usually self-limiting.

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: What is the most common organism cultured in bile in acute cholecystitis or cholangitis in Hong Kong? Answer: E. coli, followed by Klebsiella and Enterococcus.
* Trap: Ordering an MRCP for every patient with gallstones. Pearl: MRCP is strictly indicated ONLY if there is biochemical (deranged LFTs, specifically ↑ ALP/GGT/Bilirubin) or sonographic (dilated CBD) suspicion of concurrent choledocholithiasis.
* Pitfall: Rushing to clip structures in severe acute cholecystitis when anatomy is obscured. If the Critical View of Safety cannot be achieved, perform a subtotal cholecystectomy or convert to an open procedure to avoid devastating Bile Duct Injury (BDI).
* Viva Question: What are the ultrasound criteria for diagnosing acute cholecystitis? Answer: Gallbladder wall thickening > 3mm, pericholecystic fluid, sonographic Murphy's sign, and the presence of stones/sludge. ![image|405](https://pub-f0606dedf6d94e4bb5b1999cbd065914.r2.dev/paediatrics/1d4450b45178f4977a782c018459a63f.png)
* Trap: Assuming prophylactic cholecystectomy is needed for diabetic patients with asymptomatic gallstones. Pearl: Current guidelines do not support prophylactic surgery in diabetics unless they develop biliary symptoms; conservative management is standard.
* Pitfall: Delaying surgery for acute cholecystitis beyond 1 week. Attempting surgery in the 2nd to 6th week is dangerous due to maximal tissue friability and hypervascular adhesions (phlegmon). If delayed > 7 days, cool down with antibiotics and operate electively at 6-8 weeks.
