# Congenital Anomalies (兒科先天性異常)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Definition of Major Paediatric Anomalies
* **Esophageal Atresia (食道閉鎖) / Tracheoesophageal Fistula (氣管食道瘻管) [EA/TEF]**: Congenital interruption of esophagus, often communicating with trachea.
* **Congenital Diaphragmatic Hernia (先天性橫膈疝氣) [CDH]**: Defect in diaphragm (usually posterolateral Bochdalek) allowing abdominal viscera into thorax → pulmonary hypoplasia.
* **Hirschsprung's Disease (巨結腸症) [HD]**: Aganglionosis of distal bowel → functional obstruction.
* **Biliary Atresia (膽道閉鎖) [BA]**: Idiopathic fibrosing obliteration of extrahepatic biliary tree → biliary cirrhosis.

### Hong Kong & HA Epidemiology
* **BA**: Endemic in Asian populations. HK incidence ~1/5000 (higher than Caucasian populations).
* **EA/TEF**: ~1/3000 live births. Frequently managed at **Hong Kong Children's Hospital (香港兒童醫院)** (HKCH) or regional NICUs.
* **Gastroschisis (腹裂) vs Omphalocele (臍膨出)**: Gastroschisis ↑ incidence in young mothers (<20y); Omphalocele strongly associated with chromosomal anomalies (Trisomy 13, 18, 21).

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Pathophysiology of Foregut Anomalies (EA/TEF & Duodenal Atresia)
* **EA/TEF**: Defective lateral septation of embryonic foregut (weeks 4-5). Type C (proximal atresia, distal fistula) accounts for 85%.
* **Duodenal Atresia (十二指腸閉鎖)**: Failure of duodenal recanalization (weeks 8-10). Classically distal to Ampulla of Vater → bilious vomiting.

### Pathophysiology of Abdominal Wall Defects
* **Gastroschisis**: Vascular accident (involuting right umbilical vein) → full-thickness right paraumbilical defect. Bowel exposed to amniotic fluid → inflammatory peel, dysmotility.
* **Omphalocele**: Failure of midgut to return to abdominal cavity (week 10). Viscera covered by sac (amnion + peritoneum) with umbilical cord inserting *into* the sac.

### Pathophysiology of Hindgut Anomalies
* **HD**: Failure of neural crest cell craniocaudal migration (weeks 5-12). Lacks Meissner (submucosal) and Auerbach (myenteric) plexuses → continuous spastic contracted segment.
* **Anorectal Malformations (無肛症) [ARM]**: Defective urorectal septum development. Divided into high/intermediate/low lesions relative to **Puborectalis sling (恥骨直腸肌)**.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Clinical Presentation of EA/TEF & CDH
* **EA/TEF**: Polyhydramnios on antenatal USS. Postnatal frothing, choking with feeds, respiratory distress, cyanosis. Inability to pass NGT (stops at ~10cm).
* **CDH**: Immediate neonatal respiratory distress. **Scaphoid abdomen (舟狀腹)**. Shifted apex beat. Bowel sounds in hemithorax.

### Clinical Presentation of Neonatal Bowel Obstruction
* **High Obstruction (e.g., Duodenal Atresia)**: Early bilious vomiting. Non-distended/mildly distended upper abdomen.
* **Low Obstruction (e.g., HD, Ileal Atresia)**: Late vomiting, massive progressive abdominal distension. Failure to pass **Meconium (胎糞)** >24-48 hours.

### Clinical Presentation of Hepatobiliary Anomalies
* **BA**: Prolonged neonatal jaundice (>14 days). Acholic (pale) stools. Dark urine. Hepatomegaly.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Discriminatory Features of Gastroschisis vs Omphalocele
| Feature | Gastroschisis (腹裂) | Omphalocele (臍膨出) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Defect Location** | Right of umbilicus | Central (umbilical ring) |
| **Sac Presence** | No | Yes (unless ruptured) |
| **Umbilical Cord** | Normal insertion (to left of defect) | Inserts into the sac |
| **Liver Herniation** | Rare | Common |
| **Associated Syndromes** | Rare (usually isolated GI issues) | Common (Cardiac, Beckwith-Wiedemann, Trisomies) |
| **Bowel Condition** | Matted, edematous, thickened | Usually normal appearance |

### Discriminatory Features of Neonatal Bilious Vomiting
| Condition | Radiographic Hallmark | Pathological Mechanism |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Duodenal Atresia** | "Double Bubble" sign (no distal gas) | Failure of recanalization |
| **Malrotation + Volvulus (腸扭轉)** | Bird-beak on upper GI contrast | Narrow mesenteric base twist |
| **Jejunoileal Atresia** | Multiple dilated loops + fluid levels | Intrauterine vascular accident |
| **Hirschsprung's Disease** | Transition zone on contrast enema | Aganglionosis |

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)

### Antenatal & Immediate Postnatal Imaging
* **Antenatal USS**: Polyhydramnios (EA, duodenal atresia), absent stomach bubble, free-floating bowel (Gastroschisis).
* **Postnatal AXR (Thoracoabdominal)**:
  * NGT curled in upper pouch + stomach gas = EA with distal TEF (Type C).
  * Double bubble = Duodenal Atresia.
  * Loops in chest = CDH.

### Contrast Imaging for Gastrointestinal Obstruction
* **Upper GI Contrast (UGI)**: Gold standard to rule out **Malrotation (腸旋轉不良)** (Ligament of Treitz on right of midline + corkscrew duodenum).
* **Contrast Enema**: Evaluates microcolon (Ileal atresia/Meconium ileus) or transition zone (HD).

### Tissue Diagnosis & Functional Workup
* **Rectal Suction Biopsy**: Gold standard for HD. Taken 2cm, 3cm, 5cm above dentate line. Shows absent ganglion cells + hypertrophic nerve trunks (↑ Acetylcholinesterase +ve staining).
* **ECHO / Renal USS / Spinal US**: Mandatory screening for VACTERL associations in EA/TEF and ARM patients.

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)

### Immediate Neonatal Resuscitation Protocols
* **Airway/Breathing**: Endotracheal intubation immediately for CDH (Avoid bag-valve-mask to prevent bowel inflation in chest).
* **Gastric Decompression**: Sump suction (Replogle tube) in upper pouch for EA/TEF. Large-bore OG/NGT for bowel obstructions.
* **Fluid Resuscitation**: IV crystalloids + strict I/O. Place bowel in silo/bowel bag for Gastroschisis to prevent heat/fluid loss.

### Surgical Algorithm for CDH
* **Pre-operative**: Permissive hypercapnia, gentle ventilation (HFOV). Inhaled Nitric Oxide (iNO) for Pulmonary Hypertension (PHTN).
* **ECMO (體外膜氧合)**: Indicated if failure to oxygenate despite maximal ventilatory support.
* **Surgery**: Delayed repair (after cardiopulmonary stabilization, typically >48-72 hrs). Subcostal incision → reduction of viscera → primary closure or prosthetic patch.

### Surgical Algorithm for Hirschsprung's Disease
* **Initial Management**: Rectal washouts (saline irrigations) to decompress bowel and prevent **Enterocolitis (小腸結腸炎)**.
* **Definitive Surgery**: Primary transanal endorectal pull-through (TERPT) if stable; staged procedure (diverting colostomy → later pull-through) if enterocolitis, massively dilated colon, or failure to decompress.

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Operative Principles of EA/TEF Repair
* **Access**: Right extrapleural thoracotomy (preserves lung function, avoids empyema if leak occurs) or Thoracoscopic approach.
* **Steps**: Ligation and division of Azygos vein → Identification of vagus nerve → Division and transfixion of TEF → Primary esophageal anastomosis with absorbable sutures over a trans-anastomotic feeding tube.

### Operative Principles of Biliary Atresia (Kasai Portoenterostomy)
* **Timing**: Must be performed ideally <60 days of life to prevent irreversible cirrhosis.
* **Procedure**: Excision of fibrotic biliary remnants at porta hepatis → Roux-en-Y jejunal loop anastomosed directly to the liver capsule to drain microscopic bile ducts.

### Consent Issues in Major Neonatal Surgery
* **Mortality/Morbidity**: High risk of sepsis, anesthetic neurotoxicity, and specific complications (e.g., recurrent laryngeal nerve injury in TEF).
* **Stoma Formation**: Parents must be explicitly consented for possible temporary or permanent colostomy/ileostomy in any bowel obstruction surgery.

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Immediate Post-operative NICU Care
* **Ventilation**: Elective post-operative ventilation often required (especially CDH, large abdominal wall defects to prevent abdominal compartment syndrome).
* **Nutrition**: Early Total Parenteral Nutrition (TPN) via central line. Slow enteric feeds initiation after clinical signs of bowel function (stool output, ↓ gastric aspirates).
* **Chest Drain Management**: Do NOT place drains on strong suction post-EA/TEF repair (risks anastomotic breakdown).

### Long-term Follow-up & Rehabilitation
* **EA/TEF**: Requires long-term monitoring for gastroesophageal reflux (GERD), tracheomalacia, and esophageal strictures (may need serial balloon dilatations).
* **ARM / HD**: Long-term bowel management program (daily enemas, laxatives) to manage chronic constipation or fecal incontinence.

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Complications of Foregut Surgery (EA/TEF)
* **Early**: Anastomotic leak (presents as pneumothorax/saliva in chest drain), recurrent TEF.
* **Late**: Esophageal stricture, severe GERD, **Tracheomalacia (氣管軟化)** ("barking" cough).

### Complications of Kasai Procedure (BA)
* **Early**: Ascending cholangitis (requires prolonged IV antibiotics post-op).
* **Late**: Portal hypertension, progressive liver failure. (Kasai is often a bridge to ultimate **Liver Transplant (肝臟移植)**).

### Complications of Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS)
* Arises post-massive resection for Volvulus, Gastroschisis, or extensive Atresias.
* **Complications**: TPN-associated liver disease (cholestasis), central line-associated bloodstream infections (CLABSI), chronic malabsorption.

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Trap: Diagnosing a neonate with bilious vomiting as having "feeding intolerance" or simple reflux. Pearl: Bilious vomiting in a neonate is Malrotation with Midgut Volvulus until proven otherwise and requires an urgent upper GI contrast study.
* Viva Question: What is the VACTERL association? Answer: Vertebral anomalies, Anorectal malformations, Cardiac defects, Tracheoesophageal fistula, Esophageal atresia, Renal anomalies, Limb defects. Must screen all EA/TEF patients.
* Pitfall: Vigorously bagging a cyanotic neonate with a suspected Congenital Diaphragmatic Hernia. Bag-valve-mask ventilation inflates the herniated stomach/bowel in the thorax, further compressing the hypoplastic lungs. You must intubate immediately.
* Viva Question: How do you clinically differentiate between a high and low imperforate anus (Anorectal Malformation) in a male? Answer: Wait 24 hours. The presence of meconium at the perineum or median raphe indicates a low lesion (perineal fistula), whereas meconium in the urine (rectourinary fistula) indicates a high lesion.
* Trap: Assuming a newborn with a scaphoid abdomen and respiratory distress has a pneumothorax. Pearl: A scaphoid abdomen combined with respiratory distress is the classic presentation of a Congenital Diaphragmatic Hernia.
* Viva Question: Why do we leave the umbilical cord long in an infant with suspected Omphalocele or Gastroschisis? Answer: To preserve umbilical vessels for potential emergent venous/arterial access, as peripheral access can be extremely difficult.
* Pitfall: Delaying the Kasai procedure for Biliary Atresia to "observe" the jaundice. The success rate of Kasai portoenterostomy drops precipitously if performed after 60-90 days of life, leading to biliary cirrhosis requiring a liver transplant.
