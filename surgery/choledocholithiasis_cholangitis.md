# Choledocholithiasis & Cholangitis (膽總管結石與膽管炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Choledocholithiasis and Cholangitis
* **Choledocholithiasis (膽總管結石)**: Presence of gallstones within the **Common Bile Duct (CBD) (膽總管)**
* **Acute Cholangitis (急性膽管炎)**: Ascending bacterial infection of the biliary tree usually secondary to obstruction

### Hong Kong Epidemiology and Recurrent Pyogenic Cholangitis
* High local prevalence of **Gallstone Disease (膽石症)** in HK population
* Endemic history of **Recurrent Pyogenic Cholangitis (RPC) (復發性化膿性膽管炎)** / Oriental Cholangiohepatitis in older demographics
* RPC linked to parasitic infection by **Clonorchis sinensis (中華肝吸蟲)** → strictures, intrahepatic stones (brown pigment stones), biliary stasis
* Common isolated pathogens in HA hospitals:
	* **Escherichia coli (大腸桿菌)**
	* **Klebsiella spp. (克雷伯氏菌)**
	* **Enterococcus (腸球菌)**

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Surgical Anatomy of the Biliary Tree
* **Cystic Duct (膽囊管)**: Joins Common Hepatic Duct → forms **CBD (膽總管)**
* Normal CBD diameter: <6mm (up to 10mm post-cholecystectomy or in elderly)
* Blood supply to CBD: Axial branches from **Right Hepatic Artery (右肝動脈)** and **Gastroduodenal Artery (胃十二指腸動脈)**
* 3 o'clock & 9 o'clock vessels → risk of stricture if stripped excessively during surgery

### Pathophysiology of Choledocholithiasis
* **Secondary Stones** (80%): Formed in **Gallbladder (膽囊)** (cholesterol/black pigment) → migrate down via cystic duct
* **Primary Stones** (20%): Formed *de novo* in biliary tree (brown pigment) → associated with biliary stasis, infection, RPC, altered biliary anatomy (e.g., post-Roux-en-Y)

### Pathophysiology of Acute Cholangitis
1. Biliary Obstruction (Stones, stricture, tumor, parasites)
2. Biliary Stasis + Bacterial Colonization
3. ↑ Intraductal Pressure (>20 cmH2O)
4. Disruption of cholangiovenous junctions
5. Bacterial translocation into portal and systemic circulation → **Sepsis (敗血症)** / Septic Shock

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Symptoms of Choledocholithiasis and Cholangitis
* **Colicky Right Upper Quadrant (RUQ) Pain (右上腹痛)**
* **Fever and Chills (發燒及發冷)**
* **Dark Urine (茶色尿)**
* **Pale Stools (灰白便)**
* **Pruritus (痕癢)**

### Clinical Signs of Cholangitis
* **Jaundice (黃疸)** (Scleral icterus, yellowish skin)
* **Charcot’s Triad (夏科氏三聯徵)** (Present in ~50-70% cases):
  * **RUQ Pain**
  * **Fever** (with rigors)
  * **Jaundice**
* **Reynolds’ Pentad (雷諾氏五聯徵)** (Indicates severe suppurative cholangitis / shock):
  * **RUQ Pain**
  * **Fever**
  * **Jaundice**
  * **Hypotension (低血壓)**
  * **Altered Mental Status (神智不清)**

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Differential Diagnosis Table for Jaundice and Right Upper Quadrant Pain
| Condition | Key Discriminatory Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Choledocholithiasis/Cholangitis** | Charcot's triad, ↑ALP, ↑Bilirubin, CBD dilatation on USG. |
| **Acute Cholecystitis (急性膽囊炎)** | Murphy's sign +ve, thick GB wall, usually NORMAL bilirubin/ALP. |
| **Mirizzi Syndrome (米里齊綜合症)** | Gallstone impacted in cystic duct/Hartmann's pouch compressing CBD. |
| **Cholangiocarcinoma (膽管癌)** | Painless progressive jaundice, palpable mass, weight loss, cachexia. |
| **Periampullary Malignancy (壺腹周圍癌)** | Painless jaundice, Courvoisier's sign (palpable non-tender gallbladder). |
| **Acute Hepatitis (急性肝炎)** | Extremely high ALT/AST (>1000), viral serology +ve, no CBD dilatation. |

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)

### Blood Tests and Laboratory Investigations
* **Complete Blood Count (CBC)**: ↑ WCC (Neutrophilia)
* **Liver Function Tests (LFT)**: Cholestatic pattern (↑↑ALP, ↑↑GGT, ↑Conjugated Bilirubin). Mild-moderate ↑ALT/AST early on.
* **Renal Function Tests (RFT)**: Assess for AKI (Hepatorenal syndrome / sepsis-induced)
* **Coagulation Profile (PT/INR)**: Prolonged due to ↓ Vitamin K absorption in cholestasis
* **Blood Cultures (血液細菌培養)**: Take BEFORE antibiotics
* **Amylase / Lipase**: Rule out gallstone pancreatitis

### Radiological Imaging for Biliary Tract
* **Ultrasound Abdomen (USG) (腹部超聲波)**: 1st line. Detects GB stones, CBD dilatation (>6mm). Poor sensitivity for actual CBD stones due to bowel gas.
* **Magnetic Resonance Cholangiopancreatography (MRCP) (磁力共振胰膽管造影)**: Gold standard diagnostic. Non-invasive. 95% sensitivity/specificity. Used if USG indeterminate but intermediate clinical suspicion.
* **CT Abdomen with Contrast (電腦掃描)**: Assesses complications (abscess, mass). Less sensitive for purely radiolucent stones.
* **Endoscopic Ultrasound (EUS) (內視鏡超聲波)**: Highly sensitive for micro-lithiasis in distal CBD. Alternative to MRCP.

### Tokyo Guidelines 2018 (TG18) Diagnostic Criteria
* **A: Systemic Inflammation**:
  * Fever / shaking chills
  * Lab data (↑ WCC or ↑ CRP)
* **B: Cholestasis**:
  * Jaundice (T-Bil ≥2.0 mg/dL)
  * Abnormal LFTs (↑ ALP, GGT, AST, ALT)
* **C: Imaging**:
  * Biliary dilatation
  * Evidence of etiology (stricture, stone, stent)
* *Diagnosis confirmed if one item from A + one from B + one from C are present.*

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)

### Initial Resuscitation and Medical Management
* **NPO (禁食)**
* **Intravenous Fluids (靜脈輸液)**: Aggressive crystalloid resuscitation for septic shock
* **Intravenous Antibiotics (靜脈抗生素)**: HA Protocol based on local resistance patterns
  * 1st Line: **Amoxicillin-Clavulanate (Augmentin)** OR **Cefuroxime + Metronidazole**
  * Severe/Septic Shock: **Piperacillin-Tazobactam (Tazocin)** OR **Meropenem**
* **Vitamin K (維他命K)**: Intravenous to correct coagulopathy
* **Analgesia (止痛藥)**

### Tokyo Guidelines 2018 (TG18) Severity Grading and Management Table
| Grade | Criteria | Management Strategy |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Grade III (Severe)** | Organ dysfunction (Hypotension requiring inotropes, altered mental status, PaO2/FiO2 <300, oliguria/Cr >2.0, INR >1.5, Platelets <100k) | Urgent biliary drainage (<24 hours) + ICU care + broad-spectrum Abx. |
| **Grade II (Moderate)** | WCC >12k or <4k, Fever ≥39°C, Age ≥75, Hyperbilirubinemia (T-Bil ≥5.0), Hypoalbuminemia. No organ failure. | Early biliary drainage (24-48 hours) + Abx. |
| **Grade I (Mild)** | Does not meet Grade II or III criteria. | Medical treatment first. Elective biliary drainage if non-responsive. |

### Biliary Decompression and Drainage Options
* **ERCP (內視鏡逆行性胰膽管造影術)**: 1st line therapeutic intervention. Diagnostic + Therapeutic (Sphincterotomy + stone extraction + stenting).
* **PTBD (經皮穿肝膽管引流術)**: 2nd line if ERCP fails (e.g., altered anatomy, impacted large stone, impacted ampulla).
* **Open / Laparoscopic Surgical Decompression**: Used if endoscopic/radiological approaches fail or are contraindicated.

### Definitive Surgical Management and Timing
* **Laparoscopic Cholecystectomy (LC) (腹腔鏡膽囊切除術)**
* Indicated to prevent recurrence of choledocholithiasis/cholangitis
* Timing: Wait until cholangitis resolves, LFTs normalize, and sphincterotomy site heals (usually same admission or within 2-6 weeks in HA protocol, depending on cluster resources).

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Operative Principles of ERCP (Endoscopic Retrograde Cholangiopancreatography)
* **Positioning**: Prone or semi-prone
* **Access**: Side-viewing duodenoscope passed to D2 (Ampulla of Vater)
* **Cannulation**: Selective CBD cannulation via major papilla
* **Sphincterotomy**: Electrocautery incision of biliary sphincter to widen orifice
* **Extraction**: Dormia basket (網籃) or Fogarty balloon sweep
* **Stenting**: Plastic stent insertion if stone irretrievable or for purulent cholangitis decompression

### Operative Principles of Laparoscopic Common Bile Duct Exploration (LCBDE)
* **Indications**: Failed ERCP, altered anatomy (e.g., Roux-en-Y), massive stones (>2cm), simultaneous LC + CBD exploration preferred
* **Transcystic approach**: For small (<6mm), few, distal CBD stones. Choledochoscope passed through cystic duct.
* **Choledochotomy approach**: For large, multiple, proximal stones. Direct longitudinal incision on supraduodenal CBD.
* **Closure**: Primary closure vs **T-tube (T型管)** insertion depending on duct size and distal clearance

### Informed Consent and Operative Risks
* **ERCP Risks**:
  * **Bleeding (出血)** (Sphincterotomy site)
  * **Perforation (穿孔)** (Duodenum or biliary tree)
  * **Post-ERCP Pancreatitis (PEP) (內視鏡術後胰臟炎)** (up to 5-10%)
  * **Cholangitis/Sepsis**
* **Cholecystectomy / LCBDE Risks**:
  * **Bile Duct Injury (BDI) (膽管損傷)**
  * **Bile Leak (膽漏)**
  * **Retained Stones (殘留結石)**
  * **Conversion to Open Surgery (轉開腹手術)**

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Immediate Post-operative Monitoring
* **Vital Signs**: Monitor for sepsis, shock, peritonitis
* **Abdominal Examination**: Check for signs of post-ERCP pancreatitis (epigastric pain radiating to back) or surgical bile leak
* **Laboratory Tests**: Day 1 Amylase (if post-ERCP), LFTs to ensure decreasing bilirubin trend
* **Diet**: Gradual upgrade from clear fluids once bowel sounds return / nausea settles

### T-Tube Management Protocol
* Required if Choledochotomy performed during LCBDE/Open exploration
* **Purpose**: Prevents biliary stasis, allows decompression of healing CBD, provides access for post-op imaging
* **Drainage Monitoring**: Note volume and character of bile (normal ~200-500ml/day initially)
* **T-Tube Cholangiogram (T型管膽管造影)**: Performed at Day 10-14 post-op to confirm CBD clearance and no leak
* **Removal Test**: Clamp T-tube for 24-48 hours. If asymptomatic + normal LFTs → remove tube

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Early Surgical Complications
* **Post-ERCP Pancreatitis** (manage with NPO, IV hydration, analgesia)
* **Bleeding** (Sphincterotomy site, often self-limiting, may require adrenaline injection/clipping)
* **Duodenal Perforation** (Retroperitoneal air, often requires surgical patch repair if free fluid/peritonitis)
* **Septic Shock** (from failed drainage or forceful contrast injection pushing bacteria into systemic circulation)

### Late Surgical Complications and Prognosis
* **Biliary Stricture (膽管狹窄)**: Ischemic or inflammatory injury leading to obstruction
* **Recurrent CBD Stones (復發性膽管結石)**: Due to stasis (e.g., sphincter of Oddi dysfunction, ampullary stenosis, foreign body like permanent suture)
* **Cholangiocarcinoma**: Long-term risk in patients with Recurrent Pyogenic Cholangitis (RPC)
* **Prognosis**: Excellent if drained early. Untreated severe cholangitis carries near 100% mortality.

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: What is the classic triad and pentad for acute cholangitis? Answer: Charcot's triad (Fever, RUQ pain, Jaundice) and Reynolds' pentad (+ Hypotension, Altered mental status).
* Viva Question: What is the empirical antibiotic of choice for acute cholangitis in the HA system? Answer: Amoxicillin-Clavulanate (Augmentin) or Cefuroxime + Metronidazole.
* Trap: Delaying biliary drainage while waiting for blood cultures and antibiotics to "work". Pearl: Prompt source control via ERCP/PTBD is the definitive treatment for severe acute cholangitis; antibiotics are only an adjunct.
* Pitfall: Performing a laparoscopic cholecystectomy without addressing the CBD stone first. The retained CBD stone will cause post-operative obstruction, biliary leak from the cystic duct stump, or cholangitis.
* Viva Question: What is the most common and feared complication of ERCP? Answer: Post-ERCP Pancreatitis (PEP).
* Trap: Assuming a patient with right upper quadrant pain, fever, and markedly elevated liver enzymes but NO jaundice does not have a CBD stone. Pearl: Jaundice is a late sign. Transient obstruction can cause "transaminitis" (high ALT/AST) and high ALP before bilirubin significantly rises.
* Pitfall: Removing a T-tube too early after surgical CBD exploration. A tract takes at least 2-3 weeks to mature; early removal can result in catastrophic intraperitoneal biliary leak.
* Viva Question: In a patient with altered GI anatomy (e.g., post-Whipple or Roux-en-Y gastric bypass), how do you access the biliary tree if they develop cholangitis? Answer: Percutaneous Transhepatic Biliary Drainage (PTBD) or Laparoscopic/Open surgical exploration.
