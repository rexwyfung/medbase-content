# Surgical Fluid and Electrolytes (外科水與電解質)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Surgical Fluid Management
* **Surgical Fluid Management (外科輸液管理)**: Administration of IV fluids to maintain tissue perfusion, electrolyte balance, and replace abnormal losses (e.g., bleeding, third-spacing, GI losses) during perioperative period.
* **Goal**: Euvolaemia (正常血容量) + Electrolyte Homeostasis.

### Hong Kong Epidemiology of Fluid Imbalance in Surgical Wards
* **Hypervolaemia (高血容量)**: Common post-op day 1-3 due to ↑ ADH & aldosterone (surgical stress response) + excessive intra-op crystalloids.
* **Hypokalaemia (低血鉀症)**: Most common post-op electrolyte derangement in Hospital Authority (HA) surgical wards (secondary to GI losses, NBM status, stress response).
* **High-risk HA Demographics**: Elderly (↓ renal reserve), oncology patients (malnutrition), emergent laparotomies.

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Body Fluid Compartments (體液分佈)
* **Total Body Water (TBW)**: ~60% body weight (♂), 50% (♀), ↓ in elderly/obese.
* **Intracellular Fluid (ICF, 細胞內液)**: 2/3 TBW. Major ions: **K+**, **Mg2+**, **PO4 3-**.
* **Extracellular Fluid (ECF, 細胞外液)**: 1/3 TBW. Major ions: **Na+**, **Cl-**, **HCO3-**.
* **ECF Subdivisions**:
  * **Interstitial Fluid**: 3/4 ECF.
  * **Intravascular Fluid (Plasma)**: 1/4 ECF.

### Pathophysiology of Surgical Stress Response (外科應激反應)
* **Neuroendocrine Activation**: Surgical trauma → ↑ Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal (HPA) axis → ↑ Cortisol, ↑ Catecholamines.
* **RAAS Activation**: Hypovolaemia/pain → ↑ Renin → ↑ Angiotensin II → ↑ Aldosterone → **Na+ & H2O retention**, **K+ excretion**.
* **ADH Secretion**: Pain, opioids, hypovolaemia → ↑ ADH → ↑ free H2O reabsorption → Oliguria (少尿), dilutional hyponatraemia.

### Third Space Fluid Loss (第三空間體液流失)
* **Mechanism**: Tissue trauma/inflammation (e.g., peritonitis, bowel handling) → ↑ capillary permeability → ECF shifts into non-functional interstitial spaces.
* **Clinical Consequence**: Intravascular hypovolaemia despite ↑ TBW or +ve fluid balance. Mobilizes back to circulation ~48-72h post-op.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Signs of Hypovolaemia (低血容量)
* **Cardiovascular**: Tachycardia (心跳過速) (>100 bpm), hypotension (SBP <90 mmHg), narrow pulse pressure, delayed capillary refill (>2s).
* **Renal**: Oliguria (<0.5 ml/kg/hr), concentrated urine.
* **General**: Altered mental status (late sign), dry mucous membranes, ↓ skin turgor, collapsed neck veins.

### Signs of Hypervolaemia (高血容量)
* **Pulmonary**: Basal crepitations (肺底喀音), hypoxia (↓ SpO2), orthopnoea.
* **Cardiovascular**: Elevated JVP, bounding pulse, S3 gallop.
* **Peripheral**: Pitting oedema (凹陷性水腫), ascites, chemosis, ↑ daily weight.

### Symptoms of Electrolyte Derangement (電解質紊亂)
* **Hypokalaemia (<3.5 mmol/L)**: Muscle weakness, ileus (腸阻塞), arrhythmias.
* **Hyperkalaemia (>5.5 mmol/L)**: Peaked T waves, VF/asystole, ascending paralysis.
* **Hyponatraemia (<135 mmol/L)**: Nausea, headache, seizures, coma (<120 mmol/L).
* **Hypocalcaemia (低血鈣症)**: Perioral tingling, **Chvostek's sign** (+ve), **Trousseau's sign** (+ve), tetany.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Discriminatory Features of Shock (休克)
| Shock Type | Heart Rate | Blood Pressure | JVP | Skin | Primary Defect |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Hypovolaemic (低血容量性)** | ↑ | ↓ | ↓ | Cold/Clammy | ↓ Preload (Bleeding/Fluid loss) |

### Causes of Post-operative Hyponatraemia (低血鈉症)
* **Hypovolaemic Hyponatraemia**: Vomiting, NG tube aspiration, diuretics, salt-wasting nephropathy.
* **Euvolaemic Hyponatraemia**: SIADH (pain, opiates, pneumonia, CNS pathology), hypothyroidism.
* **Hypervolaemic Hyponatraemia**: Heart failure, liver cirrhosis, excessive D5W administration.

### Causes of Post-operative Hypokalaemia (低血鉀症)
* **GI Losses**: High-output stoma, severe diarrhoea, NG suction, biliary fistula.
* **Renal Losses**: Diuretics (Loop/Thiazide), hyperaldosteronism (stress response).
* **Intracellular Shifts**: Alkalosis, insulin therapy, β-agonists.

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)

### First-line Laboratory Investigations for Fluid Status
* **CBP**: ↑ Haematocrit (Hct) implies haemoconcentration/dehydration. ↓ Hb suggests acute haemorrhage (may be falsely normal acutely).
* **RFT**: ↑ Urea > ↑ Creatinine ratio (>100:1 in SI units) indicates prerenal acute kidney injury (AKI) / dehydration.
* **Electrolytes**: Na+, K+, Ca2+, Mg2+, PO4 3- baseline levels.
* **ABG/VBG**: **Lactate** (marker of tissue hypoperfusion), base excess, acid-base status.

### Advanced Hemodynamic Monitoring Investigations
* **CVP (Central Venous Pressure)**: Trend > absolute value (normal 8-12 mmHg). Guides resuscitation.
* **Arterial Line**: Continuous BP monitoring, pulse pressure variation (PPV) to assess fluid responsiveness.
* **Urine Output**: Foley catheter standard for strict I/O monitoring (target ≥0.5 ml/kg/hr).
* **Point-of-Care Ultrasound (POCUS)**: IVC diameter & collapsibility to assess volume status and fluid responsiveness.

### Diagnostic Imaging for Fluid Overload
* **CXR**: Cardiomegaly, bilateral pleural effusions, Kerley B lines, alveolar infiltrates (pulmonary oedema).

### Resuscitation Fluid Protocols (復甦輸液)
* **Indication**: Hypovolaemia, shock, acute bleeding.
* **1st Line**: Balanced crystalloids (**Hartmann's / Plasma-Lyte**).
* **Dose**: 250-500 ml bolus stat over <15 mins.
* **Reassessment**: Check HR, BP, U/O, capillary refill, mentation. Repeat if fluid responsive; escalate to inotropes/blood products if refractory.

### Maintenance Fluid Requirements (維持輸液)
* **Rule**: 4-2-1 Rule for hourly rate OR 25-30 ml/kg/day (adults).
* **Daily Needs (70kg adult)**:
  * **H2O**: 2000-2500 ml/day.
  * **Na+**: 1 mmol/kg/day (~70-100 mmol).
  * **K+**: 1 mmol/kg/day (~70-100 mmol).
  * **Glucose**: 50-100 g/day to prevent starvation ketosis.
* **HA Standard Maintenance Regimen**: 1L 0.9% NaCl + 2L 5% Dextrose over 24 hours (add KCL 20-40 mmol per bag based on daily RFT).

### Potassium Replacement Protocols (補鉀協議)
* **Oral Route**: 1st line if gut functioning (e.g., Slow-K, KCl liquid).
* **IV Route Peripheral Line**: Max rate **10 mmol/hour**. Max concentration **40 mmol/L**.
* **IV Route Central Line**: Required for rates >10 mmol/hr (max 20 mmol/hr) + Continuous ECG monitoring (ICU/HDU setting).

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Intra-operative Fluid Management Principles
* **Basal Deficit**: NBM hours x maintenance rate.
* **Ongoing Losses**: Bleeding (replace with blood 1:1 or crystalloid 3:1), insensible losses.
* **Surgical Trauma Evaporation**:
  * Minor surgery: 2-4 ml/kg/hr
  * Major open laparotomy: 6-8 ml/kg/hr

### Goal-Directed Fluid Therapy (GDFT)
* **Concept**: Utilizing advanced cardiac output monitors (e.g., PiCCO, LiDCO, Oesophageal Doppler) to optimize stroke volume.
* **Target**: Administer fluid boluses until Stroke Volume Variation (SVV) is minimized (flat portion of Frank-Starling curve), avoiding blind fluid overloading.

### Consent Issues in Massive Transfusion
* **Massive Transfusion Protocol (MTP)**: Indicated for anticipated loss >1 blood volume (10 units) in 24 hrs.
* **Consent**: Must obtain pre-op consent for blood products (紅血球、血漿、血小板). Discuss risks of transfusion-associated circulatory overload (TACO), TRALI, infection, allergic reactions.
* **Refusal**: Jehovah's Witness patients require strict advanced directives; employ cell salvage (if acceptable) and optimize pre-op Hb.

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### ERAS Fluid Protocols (加速康復外科輸液協議)
* **Pre-op**: Carbohydrate loading drink (e.g., Nutricia pre-Op) up to 2 hours before induction (avoids NBM dehydration & insulin resistance).
* **Intra-op**: Avoid mechanical bowel prep (dehydrating); use GDFT to achieve zero balance.
* **Post-op**: Discontinue IV fluids early (Day 0/1); immediate initiation of oral clear fluids.

### Post-operative Monitoring Parameters
* **Vitals**: Q4H temp, HR, BP, SpO2.
* **Strict Intake & Output (I/O) Chart**: Include all drains (NG, surgical drains, chest tubes, stoma output, Foley).
* **Daily Weights**: Best indicator of fluid status.
* **Daily Labs**: CBP, RFT, LFT, Bone (Ca, PO4, Mg) if high risk.

### Transition to Enteral Fluids
* **Criteria**: Resolution of nausea, return of bowel function (flatus/bowel sounds), clear chest, hemodynamic stability.
* **Step-up Protocol**: Sips of water → 30 ml/hr → Free clear fluids → Full fluids → Soft diet.

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Early Complications of Fluid Mismanagement
* **Acute Pulmonary Oedema**: Due to over-resuscitation with crystalloids. → Hypoxia, CPAP/intubation requirement, loop diuretics.
* **Anastomotic Oedema**: Gut wall swelling from excess 0.9% NaCl → poor healing → ileus, ↑ risk of anastomotic leak (吻合口瘻).
* **Coagulopathy**: Dilutional coagulopathy from massive un-balanced crystalloid infusion.

### Intermediate Complications of Fluid Mismanagement
* **Hyperchloraemic Metabolic Acidosis**: High Cl- in 0.9% NaCl drives HCO3- intracellularly (strong ion difference). → ↑ renal vasoconstriction, ↓ GI perfusion.
* **AKI (Acute Kidney Injury)**: Prerenal AKI from inadequate resuscitation OR congestive nephropathy from venous congestion (fluid overload).

### Prognostic Indicators of Severe Electrolyte Imbalance
* **Lactate Clearance**: Failure to clear lactate >10% per hour indicates ongoing occult tissue hypoperfusion.
* **Refractory Hyponatraemia**: Associated with prolonged ICU stay and ↑ post-op delirium.

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: How do you clinically assess fluid responsiveness in a hypotensive post-op patient? Answer: Passive leg raise test (transfers ~300ml venous blood) coupled with continuous stroke volume/pulse pressure variation monitoring; a >10% increase indicates fluid responsiveness.
* Viva Question: What is the maximum safe rate of correcting chronic hyponatraemia? Answer: Max 8-10 mmol/L in 24 hours.
* Trap: Correcting chronic hyponatraemia too rapidly. Pearl: Rapid correction risks Central Pontine Myelinolysis (Osmotic Demyelination Syndrome), leading to irreversible flaccid paralysis and locked-in syndrome.
* Trap: Using 5% Dextrose (D5W) for intravascular resuscitation in hypovolaemic shock. Pearl: D5W rapidly metabolizes, leaving free water that distributes evenly across all compartments (ICF and ECF). Only ~1/12 stays intravascular, making it useless for resuscitation. Use Hartmann's or Normal Saline.
* Pitfall: Prescribing standard maintenance potassium (e.g., KCl 40mmol/day) to a post-op Day 1 patient with oliguria. Avoid K+ supplementation until adequate urine output is confirmed to prevent life-threatening hyperkalaemia.
* Pitfall: Failing to recognize high-output stoma fluid composition. Ileostomy fluid is high in Na+ and HCO3-. Replace losses milliliter for milliliter with balanced crystalloids to avoid metabolic acidosis and hyponatraemia.
