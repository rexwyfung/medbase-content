# Soft Tissue Tumours (軟組織腫瘤)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Definition of Soft Tissue Tumours
* **Soft Tissue Tumours (軟組織腫瘤)**: Neoplasms arising from extraskeletal non-epithelial tissues of the body (mesoderm)
* Encompasses muscle, fat, fibrous tissue, blood vessels, and peripheral nerves
* **Benign Tumours (良性腫瘤)**: Extremely common (e.g., **Lipoma (脂肪瘤)** >100x more common than sarcoma)
* **Soft Tissue Sarcomas (軟組織肉瘤)**: Malignant mesenchymal tumours. Rare but high mortality

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong Hospital Authority (HA)
* Incidence: Rare, ~1% of all adult malignancies; ~200-300 new cases/year in HK
* Age distribution: Bimodal (Peaks in childhood/adolescence e.g., Rhabdomyosarcoma, and adults >55 yrs e.g., Liposarcoma, Undifferentiated Pleomorphic Sarcoma)
* Specialized centralized care: HA mandates management in designated Sarcoma Centers with Orthopaedic/General Surgical Oncology MDTs (e.g., PWH, QEH, QMH)

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Embryological Origin of Soft Tissue Tumours
* Derived from embryonic **Mesoderm (中胚層)** (exception: peripheral nerve sheath tumours derive from neuroectoderm)

### Pathophysiology and Spread of Soft Tissue Sarcomas
* Local growth: Centrifugal expansion, compresses surrounding normal tissue forming a **Pseudocapsule (假包膜)**
* Pseudocapsule contains microscopic finger-like tumour extensions (satellite cells) → Enucleation guarantees recurrence
* Metastatic spread: Primarily **Hematogenous (血源性傳播)**
* Primary target organ for metastasis: **Lungs (肺部)** (up to 80% of metastases)

### Sarcoma Subtypes with Lymphatic Spread
* Lymphatic spread generally rare (<5%), EXCEPT for 5 specific subtypes
* Mnemonic: **RSECA**
* **R**habdomyosarcoma (橫紋肌肉瘤)
* **S**ynovial sarcoma (滑膜肉瘤)
* **E**pithelioid sarcoma (上皮樣肉瘤)
* **C**lear cell sarcoma (透明細胞肉瘤)
* **A**ngiosarcoma (血管肉瘤)

### Genetic Syndromes Associated with Soft Tissue Sarcomas
* **Li-Fraumeni Syndrome**: TP53 mutation → ↑ risk of soft tissue sarcomas, osteosarcoma, breast CA
* **Neurofibromatosis Type 1 (NF1)**: NF1 mutation → ↑ risk of Malignant Peripheral Nerve Sheath Tumour (MPNST)
* **Familial Adenomatous Polyposis (FAP)**: APC mutation → ↑ risk of **Desmoid Tumours (硬纖維瘤)** (Gardner's Syndrome)

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Classic Presentation of Soft Tissue Sarcoma
* **Painless enlarging mass (無痛性腫塊)**: Most common presentation
* Pain occurs late (due to nerve compression, rapid growth/necrosis, or bone involvement)
* Deep-seated: Usually located deep to the deep fascia (e.g., intramuscular)
* Size: Often >5cm at presentation due to deep location accommodating growth without early detection

### Red Flag Signs for Malignancy in Soft Tissue Tumours
* Size **>5cm** (golf ball size)
* **Increasing in size (體積增大)** rapidly
* Deep to the deep fascia
* Painful (+ve rest pain or night pain)
* Fixation to surrounding structures (skin, bone, neurovascular bundles)
* Skin changes: Overlying erythema, dilated veins, ulceration (late)

### Specific Regional Signs of Soft Tissue Tumours
* Extremities (75%): Asymmetric limb swelling, palpable firm mass, neurological deficit distal to mass (nerve compression)
* Retroperitoneum (15%): Vague abdominal pain, early satiety, palpable abdominal mass, unilateral leg edema (IVC compression), hydronephrosis (ureteric compression)

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Differential Diagnoses of Soft Tissue Masses

| Category | Conditions | Key Discriminatory Features |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Benign Neoplasms** | Lipoma (脂肪瘤) | Soft, mobile, subcutaneous, slip sign +ve, typically <5cm |
| | Neurofibroma / Schwannoma | Associated with nerve trajectory, positive Tinel's sign, mobile side-to-side but NOT longitudinally |
| | Hemangioma | Compressible, bluish discoloration, may have phleboliths on XR |
| **Malignant Neoplasms** | Soft Tissue Sarcoma | Firm/hard, fixed to underlying tissue, >5cm, deep to fascia |
| | Metastatic Carcinoma | Hx of primary malignancy, multiple lesions, hard, rapidly growing |
| | Lymphoma | Associated lymphadenopathy, B symptoms (fever, night sweats, weight loss) |
| **Infective/Inflammatory**| Abscess (膿腫) | Erythema, warmth, tenderness, fluctuance, fever, ↑ WBC |
| **Trauma** | Hematoma (血腫) | Hx of recent trauma, bruising, resolves/shrinks over time |
| | Myositis Ossificans | Post-traumatic calcification in muscle, firm, identifiable on plain XR |

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)

### First-Line Imaging for Soft Tissue Tumours
* **Ultrasound (USG)**: Initial test for superficial masses to differentiate cystic vs solid, assess vascularity, confirm lipoma vs complex mass

### Gold Standard Local Imaging for Soft Tissue Sarcoma
* **MRI with IV Contrast (磁力共振掃描)**: Gold standard for local staging
* Mandatory for ALL suspected sarcomas >5cm or deep to fascia
* Advantages: Superior soft tissue contrast, defines anatomical compartments, assesses neurovascular bundle/bone involvement, plans surgical approach & biopsy tract

### Biopsy Principles for Soft Tissue Tumours
* **Core Needle Biopsy (CNB) (粗針穿刺活檢)**: Gold standard initial tissue diagnosis. Image-guided (USG/CT). Multiple passes required.
* **Incisional Biopsy**: Indicated ONLY if CNB non-diagnostic.
* **Excisional Biopsy**: Strictly reserved ONLY for superficial masses strongly suspected to be benign AND <3cm. NEVER for suspected sarcoma >5cm.

### Crucial Surgical Technique for Soft Tissue Sarcoma Biopsy
* **Incision Orientation**: MUST be **longitudinal** (parallel to long axis of limb)
* Rationale: Allows subsequent en bloc resection of the biopsy tract during definitive wide local excision without compromising local flap options
* Meticulous hemostasis: Prevent hematoma formation (hematoma tracks tumour cells, contaminating anatomical compartments)

### Staging Investigations for Soft Tissue Sarcoma
* **CT Thorax (胸部電腦掃描)**: Mandatory to rule out lung metastases (most common site)
* CT Abdomen/Pelvis: For retroperitoneal sarcomas or subtypes with intra-abdominal spread (e.g., Myxoid Liposarcoma)
* PET-CT: For highly aggressive subtypes, assessing distant metastases or local recurrence

### FNCLCC Histological Grading System for Soft Tissue Sarcoma
* Most crucial prognostic factor. Based on 3 parameters:
* 1. **Tumour Differentiation (腫瘤分化程度)**: Score 1-3
* 2. **Mitotic Count (有絲分裂數)**: Score 1-3
* 3. **Tumour Necrosis (腫瘤壞死)**: Score 0 (none), 1 (<50%), 2 (>50%)
* Total Score: Grade 1 (low), Grade 2 (intermediate), Grade 3 (high)

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)

### HA Referral Criteria for Soft Tissue Tumour Suspected Sarcoma
* Urgent referral to specialized Sarcoma MDT if "Golf Ball Rule" applies:
* Mass >5cm (golf ball size) OR
* Deep to deep fascia OR
* Increasing in size rapidly OR
* Painful

### Surgical Resection Principles for Soft Tissue Sarcoma
* **Wide Local Excision (WLE) (廣泛局部切除術)**: Mainstay of curative treatment
* Objective: **R0 Resection (Negative microscopic margins)**
* Must resect tumour en bloc with a cuff of normal tissue AND the previous biopsy tract
* Minimum margin: >1cm of normal healthy tissue, OR an intact anatomical barrier (e.g., un-breached deep fascia, periosteum, epineurium)

### Radiotherapy (RT) Indications in Soft Tissue Sarcoma
* Essential for local control, allows limb-sparing surgery instead of amputation
* Indicated for: Tumours >5cm, deep, Grade 2/3, or close/positive surgical margins
* **Pre-operative RT**: Preferred in HA protocols. Smaller radiation field, better oxygenation (↑ RT efficacy), thickens pseudocapsule (easier resection). Cons: ↑ wound complication rate
* **Post-operative RT**: Used if pre-op RT omitted or unexpected positive margins. Cons: Requires larger radiation field, permanent joint stiffness/fibrosis

### Chemotherapy Indications in Soft Tissue Sarcoma
* Controversial for adult soft tissue sarcomas; generally limited survival benefit
* Regimens: **Doxorubicin (阿黴素)** + **Ifosfamide (異環磷酰胺)** based
* Indications: Highly chemosensitive paediatric subtypes (Rhabdomyosarcoma, Ewing's), large/high-grade extremity sarcomas (neo-adjuvant/adjuvant to prevent micro-metastasis), or palliative for metastatic disease

### Management of Retroperitoneal Sarcomas
* Extremely challenging due to late presentation and proximity to vital organs
* Requires en bloc compartmental resection (often en bloc nephrectomy, bowel resection, or major vessel resection/reconstruction)
* Pre-operative RT frequently utilized
* Very high local recurrence rates compared to extremity sarcomas

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Limb-Sparing Surgery vs Amputation for Soft Tissue Sarcoma
* **Limb-Sparing Surgery**: Standard of care for >90% of extremity sarcomas. Combined with RT, survival equals amputation but with superior functional outcome.
* **Amputation Indications**: 
* 1. Massive tumour encasing major neurovascular bundles precluding functional reconstruction
* 2. Extensive skin/muscle involvement preventing closure or reconstruction
* 3. Recurrence after previous maximum RT dose
* 4. Palliation for intractable pain/fungation in advanced disease

### Soft Tissue Reconstruction Options Post-Sarcoma Excision
* Plastic surgery input vital for dead space obliteration and wound closure
* Options: Skin grafts (Split thickness/Full thickness), Local fasciocutaneous flaps, Pedicled muscle flaps (e.g., Gastrocnemius flap for upper tibia), Free tissue transfer (e.g., ALT free flap)

### Operative Consent for Soft Tissue Sarcoma Resection
* General surgical risks: Bleeding, infection, VTE, GA risks
* Specific local risks: Injury to named major nerves (e.g., sciatic/femoral nerve → permanent paralysis/foot drop), vascular injury (requiring bypass/graft)
* Reconstruction risks: Flap necrosis, graft failure, donor site morbidity
* Oncological risks: Positive margins requiring re-excision/RT, local recurrence, distant metastasis

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Immediate Post-operative Care for Sarcoma Flaps
* **Flap Monitoring**: Hourly checks (color, temperature, capillary refill, handheld Doppler for perforator signals)
* **Drain Management**: Closed suction drains (e.g., Jackson-Pratt). Crucial to prevent hematoma/seroma which jeopardizes flap and delays adjuvant RT
* Immobilization: Splinting of the operated limb to protect flap/anastomosis tension
* DVT Prophylaxis: High risk patient group (malignancy + major surgery + immobilization) → LMWH + mechanical prophylaxis

### Physiotherapy and Functional Rehabilitation Post-Sarcoma Surgery
* Multi-disciplinary rehab (Physiotherapy, Occupational Therapy, Orthotists)
* Graduated mobilization based on plastic surgeon's flap protocol
* Range of motion exercises, muscle strengthening of remaining synergist muscles
* Provision of orthoses (e.g., Ankle-Foot Orthosis [AFO] for iatrogenic foot drop)

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Early Complications of Soft Tissue Sarcoma Treatment
* **Wound Complications**: Highly prevalent (up to 35% with pre-op RT). Dehiscence, necrosis, infection
* Hematoma / Seroma formation
* Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) / Pulmonary Embolism (PE)
* Neurovascular deficits (iatrogenic or secondary to massive edema)

### Late Complications of Soft Tissue Sarcoma Treatment
* **Local Recurrence (局部復發)**: 10-20% rate. Requires re-excision or amputation
* **Distant Metastasis (遠處轉移)**: Usually to lungs. High mortality
* **Radiation-induced Toxicity**: Pathological fractures, joint contractures/stiffness, lymphedema, severe soft tissue fibrosis
* **Radiation-induced Sarcoma**: Extremely rare late complication (>5 years post-RT) within the radiation field

### Prognostic Factors for Soft Tissue Sarcoma
* **Histological Grade**: Most critical prognosticator for distant metastasis and overall survival
* **Tumour Size**: >5cm correlates with significantly worse prognosis
* Tumour Depth: Deep tumours carry worse prognosis than superficial
* Surgical Margins: Positive margins strongly predict local recurrence

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: What is the most important prognostic factor for a newly diagnosed soft tissue sarcoma? Answer: Histological Grade (assessed by differentiation, mitosis, and necrosis).
* Viva Question: Where do soft tissue sarcomas most commonly metastasize to, and what imaging is mandatory? Answer: Lungs. A CT Thorax is mandatory for staging.
* Trap: Performing a transverse incision for an incisional biopsy of a suspected extremity sarcoma. Pearl: Incisions MUST be longitudinal. A transverse incision contaminates adjacent fascial compartments and severely limits subsequent local flap reconstruction options or wide local excision.
* Trap: Assuming a rapidly growing, painful, 6cm soft tissue mass on the thigh is an abscess without proper imaging. Pearl: Sarcomas can undergo rapid internal necrosis simulating an abscess. Always perform USG/MRI and biopsy before any incision and drainage.
* Pitfall: "Shelling out" or enucleating a suspected lipoma that is deep to the deep fascia. This is almost guaranteed to leave gross residual disease if it is actually a sarcoma. Any mass >5cm or deep to fascia must undergo MRI and Core Needle Biopsy prior to definitive surgery.
* Viva Question: What is the minimum margin required for a wide local excision of a soft tissue sarcoma? Answer: At least 1cm of normal tissue, or an intact anatomical fascial barrier (like periosteum or deep fascia).
