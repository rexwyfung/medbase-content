# Gastrointestinal Bleeding (腸胃出血)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Definition of Upper vs Lower GI Bleeding
* **Upper GI Bleeding (UGIB)**: Hemorrhage originating proximal to **Ligament of Treitz (特賴氏韌帶)** (suspensory muscle of duodenum).
* **Lower GI Bleeding (LGIB)**: Hemorrhage originating distal to Ligament of Treitz.
* **Obscure GI Bleeding**: Bleeding from unknown source despite negative upper & lower endoscopy (often small bowel origin).

### Hong Kong Epidemiology of GI Bleeding
* **Peptic Ulcer Disease (消化性潰瘍) (PUD)**: Most common cause of UGIB. Endemic **H. pylori (幽門螺旋桿菌)** prevalence in HK drives high incidence.
* **Esophageal Varices (食道靜脈曲張)**: Secondary to **Liver Cirrhosis (肝硬化)**. High prevalence in HK due to endemic **Hepatitis B Virus (HBV) (乙型肝炎)**.
* **Aging Population Trends**: ↑ NSAID/Aspirin use → ↑ PUD bleeding. ↑ age → ↑ incidence of **Colorectal Cancer (大腸癌)**, **Diverticular Disease (憩室病)**, and **Angiodysplasia (血管發育不良)** in LGIB.

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology
### Anatomical Landmark: Ligament of Treitz
* Demarcates duodenojejunal (DJ) flexure.
* Separates physiological foregut (UGIB) from midgut/hindgut (LGIB).

### Pathophysiology of Peptic Ulcer Bleeding
* **Gastric Ulcer (胃潰瘍)**: Mucosal defense breakdown (NSAIDs, H. pylori, ↓ blood flow). Often lesser curvature → erosion into **Left Gastric Artery (胃左動脈)**.
* **Duodenal Ulcer (十二指腸潰瘍)**: Acid hypersecretion. Usually posterior wall of D1 (first part of duodenum) → erosion into **Gastroduodenal Artery (胃十二指腸動脈)**.

### Pathophysiology of Variceal Bleeding
* Portal hypertension (Portal pressure >10-12 mmHg) → Porto-systemic anastomosis engorgement.
* **Esophageal Varices**: Left gastric vein (portal) anastomoses with esophageal veins (systemic/azygos).
* **Gastric Varices**: Short gastric veins (portal) anastomoses with systemic veins.

### Pathophysiology of Lower GI Bleeding
* **Diverticular Disease**: Herniation of mucosa/submucosa at vasa recta penetration sites → segmental weakness of vasa recta → arterial rupture into lumen.
* **Angiodysplasia**: Degenerative AV malformations in submucosa, typically right colon (cecum/ascending colon) due to ↑ wall tension (Laplace's Law).

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Presentation of Upper GI Bleeding
* **Hematemesis (吐血)**: Vomiting fresh red blood (active bleed) or "coffee-ground" (altered by gastric acid).
* **Melena (黑糞症)**: Tarry, foul-smelling black stool (requires ~50-100mL blood + 14h GI transit to degrade Hb to hematin).
* **Hematochezia (便血)**: Fresh blood per rectum. Usually LGIB, but indicates **Massive UGIB** if transit is rapid (>1000mL blood loss).

### Presentation of Lower GI Bleeding
* **Hematochezia**: Bright red/maroon blood.
* Blood mixed with stool → Distal colon source.
* Blood on toilet paper/dripping after stool → Anorectal source [e.g., Hemorrhoids (痔瘡)].

### Signs of Hemodynamic Instability & Shock
* **Tachycardia**: HR >100 bpm (earliest sign).
* **Hypotension**: SBP <90 mmHg.
* **Postural Drop**: SBP ↓ >20 mmHg or HR ↑ >20 bpm upon standing.
* **Oliguria (少尿)**: Urine output <0.5 mL/kg/hr.

### Stigmata of Chronic Liver Disease (肝病體徵)
* **Palmar Erythema (紅斑掌)**
* **Spider Angiomas (蜘蛛痣)**
* **Ascites (腹水)**
* **Caput Medusae (海蛇頭)**
* **Jaundice (黃疸)**

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features
### Differential Diagnosis of Upper GI Bleeding
| Etiology | Pathogenesis / Key Features | Endoscopic Finding |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Peptic Ulcer Disease** | H. pylori, NSAIDs. Epigastric pain. | Ulcer with visible vessel / clot. |
| **Esophageal Varices** | Liver cirrhosis, portal HTN. Massive painless bleed. | Engorged, tortuous veins. |
| **Mallory-Weiss Tear (馬洛里-魏斯氏撕裂)** | Severe retching/vomiting (e.g., alcoholics) → mucosal tear at GE junction. | Longitudinal tear at GE junction. |
| **Gastritis / Erosions** | NSAIDs, alcohol, stress (burns/trauma). | Diffuse mucosal erythema/erosions. |
| **Gastric Cancer (胃癌)** | Weight loss, anorexia, chronic anemia. | Fungating/ulcerated mass. |
| **Dieulafoy's Lesion** | Aberrant submucosal artery in proximal stomach. | Pumping artery from normal mucosa. |

### Differential Diagnosis of Lower GI Bleeding
| Etiology | Pathogenesis / Key Features | Patient Profile |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Diverticulosis (憩室病)** | Most common LGIB. Painless, self-limiting massive bleed. | Elderly, low fiber diet, L-sided diverticula (but R-sided bleeds more). |
| **Angiodysplasia** | Venous malformation, chronic slow bleed or recurrent hematochezia. | Elderly, associated with Aortic Stenosis (Heyde's Syndrome). |
| **Colorectal Cancer (大腸癌)** | Altered bowel habit, weight loss, iron deficiency anemia. | Elderly, +ve FHx. |
| **Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)** | Bloody diarrhea, tenesmus, abdominal pain. | Young adults (Crohn's or UC). |
| **Hemorrhoids (痔瘡)** | Painless bright red blood spotting/dripping. | Constipation, straining. |
| **Ischemic Colitis** | Watershed areas (Splenic flexure). "Thumbprinting" on AXR. Bloody diarrhea + pain. | Elderly, cardiovascular risk factors. |

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)
### Initial Resuscitation & Lab Workup
* **Type and Screen / Crossmatch (交叉配血)**: Crucial for early transfusion.
* **Complete Blood Count (CBC)**: Hb (may be normal acutely due to hemoconcentration), Plt.
* **Renal Function Test (RFT)**: ↑ Urea disproportionate to Creatinine (Urea:Cr ratio >100) indicates UGIB (digested blood protein).
* **Liver Function Test (LFT) & Clotting Profile (PT/APTT/INR)**: Rule out coagulopathy/cirrhosis.
* **Venous Blood Gas (VBG)**: Lactate (marker of tissue hypoperfusion), rapid Hb check.

### Scoring Systems for Upper GI Bleeding
| Score | Purpose | Key Parameters |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Glasgow-Blatchford Score (GBS)** | Pre-endoscopy triage. Identifies low-risk patients for safe outpatient management (Score = 0). | Urea, Hb, SBP, HR, Melena, Syncope, Hepatic/Cardiac failure. |
| **Rockall Score** | Post-endoscopy mortality risk prediction. | Age, Shock, Comorbidities, Endoscopic diagnosis, Stigmata of recent hemorrhage. |

### Gold Standard Diagnostic Investigations
* **Esophagogastroduodenoscopy (OGD) (胃鏡)**: Diagnostic and therapeutic for UGIB. Target within 24h for stable, immediately for unstable.
* **Colonoscopy (大腸內視鏡)**: Diagnostic and therapeutic for LGIB. Requires bowel prep (Polyethylene glycol) for adequate visualization.

### Radiological Investigations for Obscure or Massive Bleeding
* **CT Mesenteric Angiogram (CTMA) (電腦掃描血管造影)**: Highly sensitive. Detects active bleeding rate ≥0.3 mL/min. First-line for massive LGIB when colonoscopy is unsafe/impractical.
* **Digital Subtraction Angiography (DSA) (血管造影)**: Diagnostic & Therapeutic (Embolization). Detects bleeding ≥0.5 mL/min.
* **Tc-99m RBC Scintigraphy (紅血球掃描)**: Most sensitive (≥0.1 mL/min) but poor anatomical localization. Used for intermittent, obscure obscure bleeds.

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)
### Immediate Resuscitation Protocol (ABCDE)
* **Airway**: Protect airway, consider elective intubation if massive hematemesis (prevent aspiration) or encephalopathy.
* **Breathing**: Supplemental O2.
* **Circulation**: Insert 2x large-bore IV cannulae (16G or 14G).
* **Fluid**: Crystalloid resuscitation (Normal Saline or Ringer's Lactate).
* **Transfusion Threshold**: Transfuse PRBC if Hb <7 g/dL (target 7-9 g/dL). If massive bleed, activate Massive Transfusion Protocol (MTP) [1:1:1 ratio of PRBC:FFP:Platelets].

### Medical Management of Non-Variceal Upper GI Bleeding
* **Proton Pump Inhibitor (PPI) (質子泵抑製劑)**: Promotes clot stability by keeping intragastric pH >6.
* **HA Protocol**: IV Omeprazole/Pantoprazole 80mg bolus → followed by 8mg/hr continuous infusion for 72 hours.
* **Tranexamic Acid (TXA)**: Routine use NO LONGER recommended (HALT-IT trial showed no mortality benefit, ↑ VTE risk).

### Endoscopic Therapy for Peptic Ulcer Bleeding
* Dual therapy is standard of care.
* **Injection Therapy**: Adrenaline 1:10,000 (causes vasoconstriction & tamponade).
* **PLUS Mechanical/Thermal**: Hemoclips (Endoclips) OR Heater probe/Argon plasma coagulation (APC).
* Forrest Classification dictates intervention: Active spurting (Ia), Oozing (Ib), Visible vessel (IIa) mandate endoscopic therapy.

### Medical Management of Variceal Upper GI Bleeding
* **Vasoactive Agents**: IV Terlipressin, Somatostatin, or Octreotide immediately (reduces splanchnic blood flow & portal pressure).
* **Prophylactic Antibiotics**: IV Ceftriaxone 1g daily (↓ mortality, prevents SBP/sepsis).
* **Endoscopy**: Variceal Band Ligation (VBL) (靜脈曲張結紮術) for esophageal varices. Cyanoacrylate (Histoacryl) glue injection for gastric varices.
* **Rescue Therapy**: Sengstaken-Blakemore tube (balloon tamponade) as bridge. TIPS (Transjugular Intrahepatic Portosystemic Shunt).

### Management Algorithm for Lower GI Bleeding
* Resuscitate → Exclude UGIB (OGD or NGT aspirate if rapid hematochezia).
* **Stable Patient**: Bowel prep → Colonoscopy.
* **Unstable Patient/Massive Bleed**: CTMA.
    * If +ve target lesion → Interventional Radiology (IR) for angioembolization.
    * If IR fails or unavailable → Surgery.

### Surgical Indications for Peptic Ulcer Bleeding
* Massive bleeding not stabilized by resuscitation.
* Failure of 2 attempts at endoscopic hemostasis.
* Continuous bleeding requiring >6 units PRBC in 24 hours.
* Co-existing perforation or suspected malignancy.

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues
### Operative Principles for Bleeding Peptic Ulcer
* **Duodenal Ulcer (Posterior D1)**: Under-running of the Gastroduodenal Artery.
    * Longitudinal duodenotomy.
    * 3-point U-stitch (Vicryl/Silk) closure: 1 stitch superior (ligates GDA), 1 inferior, 1 U-stitch directly over bleeding vessel (transverse pancreatic branch).
    * Close duodenotomy transversely (Heineke-Mikulicz principle) to prevent stricture.
* **Gastric Ulcer**: Excision/wedge resection of the ulcer (mandatory to exclude underlying gastric cancer) + primary closure.

### Operative Principles for Massive Lower GI Bleeding
* **Segmental Colectomy**: Indicated if bleeding source is definitively localized via CTMA/Colonoscopy.
* **Subtotal Colectomy with End Ileostomy**: Last resort if bleeding is massive, patient is unstable, and source CANNOT be localized. Avoid blind hemicolectomy (high re-bleeding rate).

### Pre-operative Consent and Risks
* **General Risks**: Bleeding, infection (wound/intra-abdominal), damage to surrounding structures.
* **Specific Risks (Ulcer Surgery)**: Duodenal leak, biliary injury (CBD proximity), stricture, re-bleeding.
* **Specific Risks (Colonic Surgery)**: Anastomotic leak, stoma creation (temporary/permanent), bowel dysfunction.

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation
### Immediate Post-operative Monitoring
* High Dependency Unit (HDU) or ICU care for continuous hemodynamic monitoring.
* Monitor NGT drainage (amount & color) and surgical drains.
* Strict fluid balance & hourly urine output.

### Dietary Rehabilitation & Medical Optimization
* NBM initially, clear liquids once bowel sounds return/flatus passed.
* Eradicate **H. pylori** post-op (Triple Therapy: PPI + Clarithromycin + Amoxicillin/Metronidazole for 14 days) to prevent recurrence.
* Avoid NSAIDs. If essential, use COX-2 inhibitors + PPI.

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis
### Early Post-operative Complications (0-7 days)
* **Re-bleeding**: Often inadequate hemostasis or unrecognized second lesion.
* **Duodenal/Anastomotic Leak**: High morbidity. Presents with peritonitis, ↑ inflammatory markers, biliary drain output.
* **Acute Pancreatitis**: From deep U-stitches capturing pancreatic duct or direct trauma.
* Medical complications: AMI, Stroke, Pneumonia (secondary to hypovolemic shock).

### Intermediate to Late Post-operative Complications (>7 days)
* **Gastric Outlet Obstruction (GOO)**: Due to fibrosis/stricture at duodenotomy closure site.
* **Incisional Hernia (切口疝)**
* **Short Bowel Syndrome** (rare, only in extensive resections).

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Viva Question: What is the most common artery responsible for a massively bleeding duodenal ulcer? Answer: The Gastroduodenal Artery (GDA), lying immediately posterior to the first part of the duodenum (D1).
* Trap: Assuming bright red blood per rectum (hematochezia) is strictly a lower GI bleed. Pearl: A massive upper GI bleed with rapid transit time can present as hematochezia; always consider upper GI source and insert an NGT or perform urgent OGD to exclude it.
* Pitfall: Rushing an unstable patient with LGIB to colonoscopy without adequate bowel preparation. You will see nothing but blood and stool, risking perforation. Resuscitate, perform CTMA for active bleeding, and leave colonoscopy for stable patients after prep.
* Viva Question: What is the mechanism of action of Terlipressin in variceal bleeding? Answer: It acts as a vasopressin analogue, causing splanchnic vasoconstriction, which decreases portal venous inflow and thereby lowers portal venous pressure.
* Trap: Giving routine prophylactic antibiotics to all patients with upper GI bleeding. Pearl: Prophylactic IV Ceftriaxone is strictly indicated only for cirrhotic patients with suspected variceal bleeding to prevent Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis (SBP) and improve survival.
* Pitfall: Performing a blind right hemicolectomy for a hemodynamically unstable lower GI bleed of unknown origin. The source might be on the left colon, leading to continued bleeding. Perform a subtotal colectomy with end ileostomy if the source is completely unlocalized.
* Viva Question: What three structures must be ligated in the classic 3-point under-running of a bleeding duodenal ulcer? Answer: The proximal gastroduodenal artery (superior), distal gastroduodenal artery (inferior), and transverse pancreatic artery (deep).
