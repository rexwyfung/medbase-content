# Other Congenital Anomalies in Plastic Surgery (其他整形外科先天性異常)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Microtia (小耳症)
* **Microtia**: Congenital hypoplasia or aplasia (anotia) of the external ear (pinna).
* Often associated with aural atresia (absence of external auditory canal) and middle ear anomalies.

### Clinical Definition of Congenital Hand Anomalies
* **Syndactyly (併指)**: Congenital fusion of adjacent digits (webbed fingers).
* **Polydactyly (多指)**: Presence of supernumerary digits.

### Clinical Definition of Vascular Anomalies
* **Infantile Haemangioma (嬰兒血管瘤)**: Benign vascular endothelial tumor. Present absent/faint at birth, rapid proliferation, then slow involution.
* **Vascular Malformations (血管畸形)**: Structural abnormalities of capillaries, veins, arteries, or lymphatics. Present at birth, grow commensurately with the child.

### Local Epidemiology of Congenital Plastic Surgery Anomalies
* **Polydactyly**: Most common congenital hand anomaly in Asian populations (esp. pre-axial/thumb duplication).
* **Microtia**: Incidence ~1 in 8,000 to 10,000 births. Right side > Left side. Males > Females.
* **Infantile Haemangioma**: Most common benign tumor of infancy (~4-5%). Female > Male (3:1). Premature infants at ↑ risk.

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Embryology of the External Ear
* Pinna develops from 6 **Hillocks of His** at 5-6 weeks gestation.
* Derived from 1st (mandibular) and 2nd (hyoid) branchial arches.
* Errors in migration/fusion → Microtia, preauricular tags, ear sinus.

### Embryology of Congenital Hand Anomalies
* Limb buds form at 4-8 weeks gestation.
* **Syndactyly**: Failure of programmed cell death (apoptosis) in the interdigital necrotic zones.
* **Polydactyly**: Errors in the Zone of Polarizing Activity (ZPA) or apical ectodermal ridge (AER).

### Pathophysiology of Vascular Anomalies (ISSVA Classification)
* **Haemangioma**: True neoplasm. Endothelial cell hyperplasia. **GLUT-1 +ve** marker.
* **Vascular Malformations**: Dysmorphogenesis (inborn error of vascular morphogenesis). Normal endothelial cell turnover. **GLUT-1 -ve**.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Key Signs of Microtia & Prominent Ears
* **Microtia Grading (Marx Classification)**:
  * Grade I: Smaller than normal, but identifiable anatomy.
  * Grade II: Partial pinna (usually lower half present), closed canal.
  * Grade III: Peanut-shaped rudimentary cartilaginous mass, lobule present, absent canal (Most common).
  * Grade IV: Anotia (complete absence).
* **Prominent Ears (Bat Ears / 招風耳)**: Absence of antihelical fold, deep conchal bowl, conchoscaphal angle >90°.

### Key Signs of Congenital Hand Anomalies
* **Syndactyly**: Most common between 3rd & 4th digits. Shortened web space.
* **Polydactyly**:
  * **Pre-axial**: Thumb duplication (Wassel classification).
  * **Post-axial**: Little finger duplication (common in African descent).
  * **Central**: Duplication of index, middle, or ring fingers.

### Key Signs of Vascular Anomalies
* **Infantile Haemangioma**: "Strawberry mark". Bright red, raised, compressible, completely blanches initially.
* **Venous Malformation**: Soft, compressible, bluish hue. Swells with dependency or Valsalva maneuver.
* **Lymphatic Malformation (Cystic Hygroma)**: Transilluminates +ve, often cervicofacial, compressible macrocystic masses.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Vascular Tumor vs Vascular Malformation Discriminatory Features
| Feature | Infantile Haemangioma | Vascular Malformation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Onset** | Usually weeks post-partum | Present at birth |
| **Growth Pattern** | Rapid proliferation (months 1-6) | Grows commensurately with child |
| **Involution** | Yes (spontaneous over years) | No |
| **Histology / IHC** | Endothelial hyperplasia, **GLUT-1 +ve** | Normal endothelium, **GLUT-1 -ve** |
| **First-line Treatment** | Observation or **Propranolol (普萘洛爾)** | Laser, Sclerotherapy, Surgery |

### Syndactyly Classifications
| Classification | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Simple** | Soft tissue union only |
| **Complex** | Side-to-side bony fusion |
| **Complicated** | Interposed accessory bones, abnormal tendons/vessels |
| **Incomplete** | Webbing does not extend to the fingertip |
| **Complete** | Webbing extends to the distal tip of digits |

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)

### Audiological Workup for Microtia
* **Gold Standard Investigation**: Hearing assessment is absolutely critical.
* **Auditory Brainstem Response (ABR)**: For infants/uncooperative children.
* **Pure Tone Audiometry (PTA)**: For older children.
* Identify presence of contralateral hearing (to prioritize interventions).

### Imaging Workup for Microtia & Craniofacial Anomalies
* **High-Resolution CT Temporal Bone**: Assess middle ear anatomy, ossicular chain, facial nerve path, and cochlea pre-operatively (usually delayed until age 6-8 before surgery).

### Imaging Workup for Hand Anomalies
* **Plain Radiographs (X-rays)**: AP & Lateral views of affected limb. Gold standard to assess bony anatomy, identify complex/complicated syndactyly, and classify thumb duplications.

### Imaging Workup for Vascular Anomalies
* **Doppler USS**: 1st line. Differentiates high-flow (AVM, Haemangioma) vs low-flow (Venous, Lymphatic) lesions.
* **MRI with Contrast**: Gold standard for delineating tissue extent, deep structures, and planning surgical excision or sclerotherapy.

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)

### HA Management Algorithm for Infantile Haemangiomas
* **Conservative**: Observation for uncomplicated, asymptomatic lesions (90% spontaneously involute by age 9).
* **Medical Indications**: Prescribe oral **Propranolol (普萘洛爾)** (non-selective β-blocker) if:
  * Vision-threatening (peri-orbital).
  * Airway-compromising (beard distribution/subglottic).
  * High risk of ulceration/bleeding (perineal/lip).
  * Significant cosmetic disfigurement.
* **Pre-Propranolol Workup**: Requires ECG (rule out heart block) and strict blood glucose monitoring (risk of hypoglycemia).

### Management Algorithm for Vascular Malformations
* **Venous/Lymphatic (Low Flow)**: Percutaneous Sclerotherapy (e.g., Bleomycin, Sodium Tetradecyl Sulfate).
* **AVM (High Flow)**: Angiographic embolization followed within 24-48 hours by surgical resection.

### Surgical Indications & Timing for Ear Anomalies
* **Prominent Ears**: Pinnaplasty/Otoplasty performed at age **5-6 years** (ear is ~85% adult size, prevents school bullying).
* **Microtia**: Autologous rib cartilage reconstruction delayed until **age 8-10 years** (chest circumference >60cm ensures sufficient rib cartilage volume).

### Surgical Indications & Timing for Hand Anomalies
* **Syndactyly Release**: Typically at age **12-18 months**. Early release (6 months) mandatory for border digits (Thumb-Index or Ring-Little) to prevent tethering/growth deviation.
* **Polydactyly Excision**: Typically at age **12 months** (before development of fine motor pinch grasp).

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Operative Principles of Microtia Reconstruction (Autologous)
* Typically a 2-stage procedure (Nagata or Brent technique).
* **Stage 1**: Harvest 6th, 7th, 8th costal cartilages → carve and assemble framework → insert into subcutaneous pocket at microtia site.
* **Stage 2**: Ear elevation with skin grafting and fascial flap (creates auriculocephalic sulcus).
* **Consent Issues**: Chest wall deformity, pneumothorax (pleural tear during rib harvest), permanent chest scar, skin flap necrosis, infection.

### Operative Principles of Syndactyly Release
* **Incisions**: Zig-zag (Bruner) incisions on palmar/dorsal aspects. Avoid straight longitudinal incisions (prevents scar contracture).
* **Web Reconstruction**: Use dorsal rectangular or triangular fascial flaps.
* **Skin Coverage**: Always requires **Full-thickness skin graft (FTSG) (全層植皮)** (typically harvested from groin or hypothenar eminence) to cover raw areas.
* **Strict Vascular Principle**: Never release both sides of a single digit concurrently → isolates bilateral digital arteries → catastrophic ischemic necrosis.

### Operative Principles of Prominent Ear Correction (Otoplasty)
* **Cartilage Sparing**: Mustarde sutures (creates antihelical fold), Furnas sutures (pins conchal bowl to mastoid fascia).
* **Cartilage Cutting**: Anterior cartilage scoring (Chongchet/Stenstrom technique) to cause cartilage to warp backwards (Gibson's principle).

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Post-operative Care for Hand Surgery
* **Immobilization**: Bulky soft dressings and above-elbow cast (prevents cast slipping in infants).
* **Elevation**: Strict elevation to ↓ edema.
* **Occupational Therapy (OT)**: Post-cast removal for scar massage, silicone gel sheeting, and functional mobilization/splinting.

### Post-operative Care for Ear Reconstruction
* **Drainage**: Small suction drains (e.g., Blake/Jackson-Pratt) placed strictly to coapt skin tightly to cartilage framework and prevent hematoma.
* **Protection**: Rigid ear shield worn at night/during play to prevent traumatic framework fracture.
* **Suture Removal**: Non-absorbable sutures removed at 1-2 weeks.

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Complications of Microtia Reconstruction
* **Early**: Hematoma, flap ischemia/necrosis, Pleural tear (Pneumothorax) from donor site.
* **Intermediate**: **Infection** (devastating → leads to complete cartilage framework melting/loss; requires urgent IV antibiotics/debridement).
* **Late**: Wire/suture extrusion, framework resorption, hypertrophic scarring.

### Complications of Syndactyly Release
* **Early**: Digital ischemia/gangrene (from tension or bilateral release), graft loss/infection.
* **Late**: **Web creep** (distal migration of the web space with growth; common indication for revision surgery), flexion contractures.

### Prognosis of Infantile Haemangiomas
* 50% involute by age 5; 90% by age 9.
* May leave a residual fibrofatty mass, telangiectasia, or redundant skin requiring secondary aesthetic excision.

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Pitfall: Performing cosmetic otoplasty too early. Wait until the ear is ~85% adult size (around age 5-6) and patient is cooperative, which also mitigates schoolyard bullying.
* Trap: Releasing both sides of the middle finger simultaneously in complex syndactyly to "save time". Pearl: Staged release is mandatory (wait at least 6 months between sides) to avoid catastrophic digital ischemia.
* Viva Question: What is the molecular immunohistochemical marker for Infantile Haemangioma? Answer: GLUT-1 positivity.
* Viva Question: When should autologous rib cartilage be harvested for microtia reconstruction and why? Answer: Age 8-10 (chest circumference >60cm), to ensure adequate cartilage yield without causing severe chest wall deformities.
* Pitfall: Applying a short-arm cast in a toddler post-syndactyly release. The conical shape of a child's forearm causes short casts to slide off; always use a long-arm (above-elbow) cast bent at 90 degrees for secure immobilization.
* Trap: Discharging an infant with an airway/beard distribution haemangioma without intervention. Pearl: Mandibular/beard haemangiomas have a high association with subglottic haemangiomas and risk acute airway obstruction; aggressive Propranolol and ENT scoping are required.
* Viva Question: What kind of skin graft is used for syndactyly release and why? Answer: Full-Thickness Skin Graft (FTSG) to minimize secondary wound contracture and accommodate digital growth.
