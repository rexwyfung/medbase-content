# Peripheral Arterial Disease (周邊動脈疾病)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Definition of Peripheral Arterial Disease
* **Pathological Definition:** Progressive stenosis/occlusion of systemic arteries (excluding coronary/intracranial) primarily due to **Atherosclerosis (動脈粥樣硬化)**.
* **Haemodynamic Effect:** ↓ Arterial blood flow → mismatch between O2 supply & demand in lower limbs → **Ischaemia (缺血)**.
* **Spectrum of Disease:** Ranges from asymptomatic → **Intermittent Claudication (間歇性跛行)** → **Critical Limb Ischaemia (嚴重肢體缺血)**.

### Hong Kong Local Epidemiology
* **Prevalence:** ↑ in HK ageing population; highly prevalent in Hospital Authority (HA) vascular clinics.
* **Key Demographics:** ♂ > ♀; typically age >65.
* **Major HK Risk Factors:** **Diabetes Mellitus (糖尿病)** (endemic in HK, causes distal tibial vessel disease), smoking, hypertension, hyperlipidaemia.
* **Mortality Link:** PAD is a marker of systemic atherosclerosis; 5-yr CV mortality is ↑↑ (many die from MI/CVA before limb loss).

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Key Lower Limb Arterial Anatomy
* **Aortoiliac Segment:** Abdominal Aorta → Common Iliac Artery (CIA) → External Iliac Artery (EIA) & Internal Iliac Artery (IIA).
* **Femoropopliteal Segment:** EIA crosses inguinal ligament → Common Femoral Artery (CFA) → Profunda Femoris (deep, crucial collateral source) & **Superficial Femoral Artery (SFA) (股淺動脈)** (most common site of occlusion at Hunter’s/Adductor canal) → Popliteal Artery.
* **Crural (Infrapopliteal) Segment:** Popliteal → Anterior Tibial (ATA), Posterior Tibial (PTA), Peroneal arteries (highly diseased in DM patients).

### Atherosclerotic Pathophysiology
* **Vessel Wall Injury:** Endothelial dysfunction (shear stress/toxins) → ↑ permeability to LDL.
* **Plaque Formation:** Macrophages engulf oxidized LDL → Foam cells → Fatty streak → Fibroatheroma (lipid core + fibrous cap).
* **Haemodynamic Consequence:** Stenosis >50% diameter (75% cross-sectional area) → critical pressure drop across lesion.
* **Collateralization:** Chronic ↓ flow → opening of pre-existing collateral networks (compensates initially, fails as disease progresses).

### Pathophysiology of Critical Limb Ischaemia (CLI)
* **Definition:** End-stage PAD with inadequate resting perfusion to maintain tissue viability.
* **Criteria:** Ischaemic rest pain >2 weeks OR tissue loss (ulcers/gangrene).
* **Consequence:** Rest pain often occurs at night (↓ cardiac output + loss of gravity assistance) → patient hangs foot off bed to ↑ hydrostatic pressure.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Intermittent Claudication (間歇性跛行) Symptoms
* **Classic Presentation:** Reproducible, cramp-like muscle pain (usually calf/buttock) induced by specific exercise distance (claudication distance).
* **Relief:** Relieved entirely by rest within 5-10 mins (standing still, no need to sit).
* **Localization:** 
  * Buttock/Thigh claudication → Aortoiliac disease (Leriche syndrome if +ve erectile dysfunction).
  * Calf claudication → SFA/Popliteal disease.
  * Foot claudication → Tibial/Pedal disease (rare, mostly in DM).

### Critical Limb Ischaemia (嚴重肢體缺血) Symptoms
* **Ischaemic Rest Pain:** Severe, unremitting burning pain in distal foot/toes.
* **Nocturnal Exacerbation:** Wakes patient at night; temporary relief by dangling leg over bed edge.
* **Tissue Loss:** Non-healing **Arterial Ulcers (動脈性潰瘍)** (punched out, painful, lateral malleolus/toes) or frank **Gangrene (壞疽)**.

### Clinical Signs & Buerger's Test
* **Inspection:** Hair loss, thin/shiny skin, brittle nails, muscle atrophy.
* **Palpation:** ↓ or absent pulses (Femoral, Popliteal, Dorsalis Pedis, Posterior Tibial), ↓ skin temperature.
* **Auscultation:** Bruits over CFA or abdominal aorta.
* **Buerger’s Test (伯格氏試驗):**
  * Elevate legs → observe for **Pallor (蒼白)**. Buerger's angle <20° indicates severe ischaemia.
  * Hang legs down → observe for **Reactive Hyperaemia (反應性充血)** (dusky red/rubor).
* **Capillary Refill Time (CRT):** >2 seconds is abnormal.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Vascular vs Neurogenic Claudication Table

| Feature                   | Vascular Claudication (PAD)           | Neurogenic Claudication (Spinal Stenosis)   |
| :------------------------ | :------------------------------------ | :------------------------------------------ |
| **Pain Character**        | Cramping, tight, aching               | Shooting, electric, "heavy legs"            |
| **Onset**                 | Fixed walking distance                | Variable distance, worse standing/extension |
| **Relief**                | Standing still (fast relief, <5 mins) | Sitting or bending forward (slow relief)    |
| **Pulses**                | ↓ or Absent                           | Normal                                      |
| **Leg Weakness**          | Rare                                  | Common (+ve numbness/tingling)              |
| **Shopping Trolley Sign** | -ve (leaning forward doesn't help)    | +ve (flexion ↑ spinal canal space)          |

### Other Key Differential Diagnoses
* **Venous Claudication:** Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) history, "bursting" pain, relieved by leg elevation.
* **Osteoarthritis (OA):** Pain on weight-bearing/joint movement, morning stiffness, local joint signs.
* **Diabetic Neuropathy:** Burning/glove-and-stocking numbness, present at rest, normal pulses (if pure neuropathy).
* **Buerger’s Disease (Thromboangiitis Obliterans):** Young male heavy smokers, upper limb involvement, distal vessel corkscrew collaterals.

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)

### Bedside & Laboratory Workup
* **Ankle-Brachial Pressure Index (ABPI) (踝肱血壓指數):**
  * Normal: 1.0 - 1.2
  * Claudication: 0.5 - 0.9
  * CLI/Rest Pain: <0.5
  * Falsely ↑ (>1.3): Calcified incompressible vessels (common in HA DM patients) → Need Toe-Brachial Index (TBI).
* **Bloods:** Fasting glucose/HbA1c, Lipid profile (Total Chol, LDL), RFT (Cr/eGFR prior to contrast), CBC (exclude polycythaemia/anaemia).
* **ECG:** Exclude silent MI/AF (high cardiovascular risk profile).

### Vascular Imaging Modalities
* **Arterial Duplex Ultrasound (多普勒超聲波):** 1st line structural imaging. Non-invasive, assesses stenosis velocity/turbulent flow. Operator dependent.
* **CT Angiography (CTA):** Gold standard for pre-op planning. Provides 3D anatomy, vessel calcification. Cons: Contrast nephropathy risk, ionizing radiation.
* **MR Angiography (MRA):** Alternative if poor renal function (avoid Gadolinium if eGFR <30 due to Nephrogenic Systemic Fibrosis). Overestimates stenosis.
* **Digital Subtraction Angiography (DSA):** Invasive gold standard. Reserved for concomitant endovascular intervention.

### Disease Staging Classifications

| Stage/Grade | Fontaine Classification | Rutherford Classification |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Asymptomatic** | Stage I | Grade 0, Category 0 |
| **Mild Claudication**| Stage IIa (>200m) | Grade I, Category 1 |
| **Severe Claudication**| Stage IIb (<200m) | Grade I, Category 2-3 |
| **Rest Pain** | Stage III | Grade II, Category 4 |
| **Minor Tissue Loss**| Stage IV | Grade III, Category 5 |
| **Major Tissue Loss**| Stage IV | Grade III, Category 6 |

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)

### First-Line Conservative Management
* **Strict Smoking Cessation:** Absolute priority. Refer to HA Smoking Cessation clinics.
* **Supervised Exercise Programme:** 1st line for claudicants. Walk to point of near-maximal pain, rest, repeat. 30-45 mins/session, 3x/week for 3-6 months. Promotes collateralization.
* **Foot Care:** Podiatry referral, avoid barefoot walking, well-fitting footwear.

### Hospital Authority (HA) Medical Optimization
* **Antiplatelet Therapy:** **Aspirin (阿士匹靈)** 80mg daily OR **Clopidogrel (氯吡格雷)** 75mg daily.
* **Lipid Modification:** High-dose statin therapy (e.g., **Atorvastatin (阿托伐他汀)** 40-80mg daily) regardless of baseline cholesterol (pleiotropic plaque stabilization).
* **Vasodilator/Haemorheological Agents:** Cilostazol (PDE3 inhibitor) rarely used in HA due to cost/side effects, but ↑ claudication distance.
* **Comorbidity Control:** Strict BP control (<130/80), strict DM control (HbA1c <7%).

### Indications for Surgical/Endovascular Intervention
* **Critical Limb Ischaemia (CLI):** Absolute indication to save limb (rest pain, tissue loss, gangrene).
* **Disabling Claudication:** Lifestyle/profession-limiting claudication unresponsive to 6 months of conservative/medical therapy.

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Endovascular Revascularization Principles
* **Techniques:** Percutaneous Transluminal Angioplasty (PTA) +/- Stenting. 
* **Access:** Retrograde CFA (for iliac/SFA lesions) or Antegrade CFA (for distal infra-inguinal lesions).
* **Indications:** Short, focal stenoses (TASC II A/B lesions); patients unfit for open surgery.
* **Pros/Cons:** Minimally invasive, local anaesthesia; but ↓ long-term patency vs open bypass.

### Open Surgical Bypass Principles
* **Anatomical Bypass:** 
  * Aortobifemoral bypass (gold standard for severe aortoiliac disease).
  * Femoropopliteal bypass (above or below knee).
  * Femorodistal bypass (CFA to tibial vessels).
* **Conduit Choice:** **Autologous Great Saphenous Vein (GSV)** is superior (resists infection, better patency). Synthetic PTFE/Dacron used if GSV unavailable (acceptable above knee, poor below knee).
* **Endarterectomy:** Physical removal of plaque + intimomedial layer (often CFA endarterectomy).

### Pre-operative Surgical Consent
* **Specific Risks:** Bleeding, wound infection/dehiscence, graft thrombosis/failure, distal embolization.
* **Systemic Risks:** Myocardial Infarction (MI), Stroke (CVA), mortality.
* **Ultimate Risk:** Major amputation (Below-knee/Above-knee) if revascularization fails.

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Immediate Post-operative Care
* **Vascular Monitoring:** Hourly pulse checks (pedal/popliteal) + continuous wave Doppler signals. Assess CRT, colour, warmth.
* **Haemodynamic Control:** Strict BP management. Hypotension → graft thrombosis. Hypertension → anastomotic bleeding.
* **Medications:** Dual antiplatelets or systemic anticoagulation (Heparin → Warfarin/DOAC) depending on graft type/HA protocol.
* **Wound Care:** Monitor groin incisions for haematoma or seroma/lymphocoele.

### Post-operative Rehabilitation
* **Mobilization:** Early ambulation to ↓ DVT/chest infection risk.
* **Amputee Rehabilitation:** If primary amputation performed → OT/PT for wheelchair/prosthesis fitting, stump shaping/compression.
* **Surveillance:** Regular Duplex ultrasound surveillance of vein grafts (at 6 wks, 3, 6, 12 months) to detect early intimal hyperplasia/stenosis.

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Early Surgical Complications (<30 days)
* **Graft Thrombosis:** Usually due to technical error (kink, retained valve, anastomotic stricture) or poor outflow tract.
* **Bleeding/Haematoma:** Requires urgent exploration if expanding or compromising skin.
* **Compartment Syndrome:** Reperfusion injury after prolonged ischaemia → swelling in closed fascial space → requires urgent **Fasciotomy (筋膜切開術)**.

### Late Surgical Complications (>30 days)
* **Intimal Hyperplasia:** Most common cause of intermediate graft failure (1-18 months), typically at anastomoses.
* **Graft Infection:** Severe complication (esp. synthetic grafts) → requires total excision of infected graft + extra-anatomic bypass.
* **Disease Progression:** Atherosclerosis worsens in proximal inflow or distal outflow arteries.
* **False Aneurysm (Pseudoaneurysm):** Breakdown of anastomosis (often due to infection or structural failure).

### Long-Term Prognosis
* **Claudicants:** 20% progress to surgery/CLI; 5% require amputation at 5 years. However, 30% die within 5 years from CV events (MI/CVA).
* **CLI Patients:** Without intervention, >50% require major amputation within 1 year. 1-year mortality is ~25%.

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: What is the most important initial management step for a patient with new-onset intermittent claudication? Answer: Strict smoking cessation and enrolment in a supervised exercise programme.
* Trap: Prescribing compression stockings for a patient with leg ulcers. Pearl: Always measure ABPI before applying compression therapy; compression is strictly contraindicated in arterial ulcers (ABPI <0.8) as it worsens ischaemia.
* Pitfall: Reassuring a diabetic patient with an ABPI of 1.3 that their peripheral circulation is excellent. Falsely elevated ABPI (>1.3) in diabetics reflects heavily calcified, incompressible vessels (Mönckeberg's arteriosclerosis), and you must proceed to perform a Toe-Brachial Index (TBI).
* Viva Question: What are the indications for primary amputation over revascularization? Answer: Dead limb (fixed mottling, rigor), unsalvageable foot (extensive gangrene extending into heel/midfoot), or a bedbound patient with severe flexion contractures where a bypass would not restore mobility.
* Trap: Assuming rest pain is relieved by walking. Pearl: Vascular rest pain is classically relieved by dependency (hanging the foot off the bed) to increase gravity-assisted perfusion, whereas walking would increase oxygen demand and worsen ischaemic pain.
* Pitfall: Performing isolated above-knee amputation without assessing vascular supply. Ensure the selected amputation stump level has adequate perfusion to heal, otherwise the wound will break down, necessitating a higher revision amputation.
