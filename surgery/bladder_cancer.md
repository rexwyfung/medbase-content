# Carcinoma of the Bladder (膀胱癌)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Bladder Cancer
* **Malignancy** originating from the **urothelium** (Urothelial Carcinoma / Transitional Cell Carcinoma) lining the urinary bladder.
* Characterized by a high propensity for multi-focal recurrence and progression.
* Divided clinically into **Non-Muscle-Invasive Bladder Cancer (NMIBC)** and **Muscle-Invasive Bladder Cancer (MIBC)**.

### Local Epidemiology in Hong Kong
* Ranked among the top 10 most common cancers in Hong Kong males.
* **Male-to-female ratio** is approximately 3:1 to 4:1.
* Peak incidence occurs in patients aged **60–80 years**.
* High local prevalence is strongly associated with a history of **cigarette smoking** and specific occupational exposures.


## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Gross and Histological Layers of the Bladder Wall
* **Urothelium (Transitional Epithelium)**: Epithelial lining, site of **Carcinoma in Situ (CIS)** and papillary tumors.
* **Lamina Propria (Submucosa)**: Connective tissue layer containing rich vasculature; defines **T1** disease.
* **Muscularis Propria (Detrusor Muscle)**: Thick smooth muscle layer; invasion defines **T2** disease and guides major therapeutic shifts.
* **Perivesical Fat**: Outer adventitial layer; invasion defines **T3** disease.

### Key Anatomical Landmarks & Relations
* **Bladder Trigone (膀胱三角)**: Triangular region at the bladder base bounded by the two **ureteric orifices** and the **internal urethral meatus**; common site for malignant lesions.
* **Pelvic Lymph Nodes**: Primary lymphatic drainage basin includes **obturator**, **internal iliac**, **external iliac**, and **common iliac** lymph node stations.
* **Peritoneal Reflections**: **Rectovesical pouch** in males and **vesicouterine pouch** in females; routes for potential direct transperitoneal tumor seeding.

### Pathophysiology & Etiological Risk Factors
* **Cigarette Smoking**: The most significant risk factor; accounts for >50% of cases via aromatic amines and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons excreted in urine.
* **Occupational Exposures**: Industrial dyes, rubber, leather, and paint industries handling **β-naphthylamine** or **benzidine**.
* **Chronic Irritation**: **Schistosomiasis** infection (linked to **Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC)**, rare in HK but high in endemic regions); long-term indwelling catheters; recurrent urinary tract infections.
* **Iatrogenic Carcinogens**: Prior pelvic radiation; systemic exposure to **cyclophosphamide** chemotherapy.

### Molecular Pathways of Carcinogenesis
* **Papillary Pathway**: Often driven by **FGFR3** mutations and **PIK3CA** alterations; leads to low-grade, recurrent non-invasive papillary tumors.
* **Non-Papillary / Solid Pathway**: Driven by **TP53** and **RB1** tumor suppressor gene mutations; leads to high-grade **CIS** or rapid **MIBC**.
* **Field Cancerization Effect**: The entire urothelium is exposed to excreted carcinogens, causing widespread genetic alterations and a high rate of asynchronous recurrence throughout the urinary tract.


## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Storage Lower Urinary Tract Symptoms
* **Painless Gross Visible Haematuria (無痛性肉眼血尿)**: The cardinal and most common presenting symptom (>80% of patients); characteristically intermittent and present throughout the entire urinary stream.
* **Urgency & Frequency**: Irritative **Storage Lower Urinary Tract Symptoms (LUTS)**; highly suspicious for **Carcinoma in Situ (CIS)** or extensive trigonal lesions.
* **Dysuria**: Painful micturition mimicking a urinary tract infection; often signals advanced or inflammatory neoplastic infiltration.

### Advanced Signs of Local Extent & Metastasis
* **Palpable Suprapubic Mass**: Indicates a large, advanced T3/T4 tumor fixing the bladder wall; assessed via **bimanual examination** under anesthesia.
* **Unilateral Flank Pain**: Secondary to **ureteric orifice obstruction** leading to hydronephrosis.
* **Lower Limb Oedema**: Secondary to extensive **pelvic lymphadenopathy** causing lymphatic or venous outflow obstruction.
* **Constitutional Symptoms**: Anorexia, cachexia, and bone pain indicating distant metastatic dissemination.


## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Table of Differential Diagnoses for Visible Haematuria
| Differential Diagnosis | Discriminatory Clinical Features | Diagnostic Workup |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Urolithiasis (Urinary Calculi)** | Acute, severe **ureteric colic** radiating to the groin; accompanied by microscopic or visible haematuria. | Non-contrast **CT KUB** |
| **Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)** | Dysuria, pyuria, nitrites on dipstick, and systemic fever; haematuria resolves completely post-antibiotics. | **Urine Midstream Culture (MSU)** |
| **Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH)** | Exclusively elderly males; presentation is predominantly **voiding LUTS** (hesitancy, weak stream); terminal haematuria due to ruptured engorged surface veins. | **Digital Rectal Examination (DRE)**, **PSA** |
| **Renal Cell Carcinoma (RCC)** | Classic triad (<10%): flank pain, palpable flank mass, visible haematuria; can cause a left-sided varicocele. | Triple-phase **Contrast-enhanced CT Abdomen/Pelvis** |
| **Radiation Cystitis** | Explicit history of prior **pelvic radiotherapy** for cervical, prostate, or colorectal malignancies; bladder mucosa appears pale with telangiectasia. | **Flexible Cystoscopy** + tissue biopsy |


## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup

### Initial Bedside & Laboratory Testing
* **Urine Dipstick**: Confirms **Visible Haematuria (VH)** or **Non-Visible Haematuria (NVH)**; excludes pseudo-haematuria.
* **Urine Midstream Specimen (MSU)**: Excludes concurrent active bacterial infection.
* **Voided Urine Cytology**: Microscopic assessment for shed malignant urothelial cells; high specificity for **high-grade tumors** and **CIS**, but low sensitivity for low-grade lesions.
* **Complete Blood Count (CBC)**: Assesses the severity of chronic anemia secondary to ongoing haematuria.
* **Renal Function Tests (Urea & Creatinine)**: Baseline assessment critical before contrast imaging or systemic cisplatin-based chemotherapy.

### Gold Standard Diagnostic Imaging & Endoscopy
* **Flexible Cystoscopy (膀胱鏡檢查)**: Performed under local anesthesia in the **Hong Kong HA Haematuria Clinic** setting; allows direct visualization of tumor location, size, morphology (papillary vs. solid), and number.
* **Computed Tomography Urogram (CTU)**: Multi-phase contrast CT evaluating the entire upper urinary tract (kidneys and ureters) to rule out synchronous upper tract urothelial carcinoma (UTUC).

### Surgical Staging & Assessment
* **Transurethral Resection of Bladder Tumor (TURBT)**: Both diagnostic and therapeutic; must capture the underlying **detrusor muscle** to accurately differentiate T1 from T2 disease.
* **Bimanual Examination Under Anesthesia (EUA)**: Performed immediately before and after TURBT to assess for a fixed pelvic mass.
* **Chest/Abdomen/Pelvis CT**: Performed for confirmed MIBC to evaluate for pelvic lymphadenopathy and visceral metastases.

### TNM Staging System Matrix
| T Stage | Pathological Extent of Invasion |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Ta** | Non-invasive papillary carcinoma |
| **Tis** | Carcinoma in situ (CIS): flat, high-grade intraepithelial malignancy |
| **T1** | Tumor invades the subepithelial connective tissue (**lamina propria**) |
| **T2a** | Tumor invades superficial **muscularis propria** (inner half) |
| **T2b** | Tumor invades deep **muscularis propria** (outer half) |
| **T3** | Tumor invades **perivesical tissue** microscopically (T3a) or macroscopically (T3b) |
| **T4** | Tumor invades adjacent organs: prostate, uterus, vagina (T4a) or pelvic wall (T4b) |


## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications

### Management of Non-Muscle-Invasive Bladder Cancer (NMIBC)
* **Complete TURBT**: Primary management for all Ta and T1 tumors.
* **Immediate Post-op Intravesical Chemotherapy**: Single instillations of **Mitomycin C (MMC)** given within **24 hours** post-TURBT to minimize tumor cell implantation.
* **Risk Stratification Matrix**:
  * Low Risk (Solitary, Ta, Low Grade): Regular surveillance flexible cystoscopy.
  * Intermediate Risk (Multiple/Recurrent, Ta/T1, Low Grade): Maintenance intravesical MMC.
  * High Risk (Any T1, High Grade, or **CIS**): Induction and maintenance **Intravesical Bacillus Calmette-Guérin (BCG)** immunotherapy.

### Intravesical BCG Therapy Protocol
* **Induction Course**: 6 weekly intravesical instillations of live attenuated *Mycobacterium bovis*.
* **Mechanism**: Triggers a local **T-cell mediated immune response** to eliminate residual microscopic disease.
* **Contraindications**: Visible gross haematuria; traumatic catheterization; active systemic tuberculosis; immunosuppression.

### Management of Muscle-Invasive Bladder Cancer (MIBC)
* **Neoadjuvant Chemotherapy (NAC)**: Cisplatin-based combination regimens (e.g., **gemcitabine + cisplatin**) administered prior to radical surgery; improves overall survival by addressing micrometastatic disease.
* **Radical Cystectomy (根治性膀胱切除術)**: Gold standard surgical option for localized T2–T4a, N0, M0 disease.
* **Trimodality Bladder-Sparing Therapy**: Alternative for patients medically unfit for major surgery or refusing cystectomy; combines maximal **TURBT** followed by concurrent **radiotherapy** and **chemotherapy**.


## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Transurethral Resection of Bladder Tumor (TURBT) Technique
* Patient positioned in the **lithotomy position** under spinal or general anesthesia.
* Complete visual mapping of the entire bladder cavity and identification of the **ureteric orifices**.
* Systematic resection of the exophytic tumor portion using a resectoscope loop.
* Deep resection of the tumor base to capture the **muscularis propria** layer.
* Hemostasis achieved via electrocoagulation.

### Radical Cystectomy Excision Extent
* **Male Patient**: Removal of the bladder, prostate, seminal vesicles, distal ureters, and regional pelvic lymph nodes.
* **Female Patient**: Removal of the bladder, urethra, anterior vaginal wall, uterus, fallopian tubes, ovaries, distal ureters, and regional pelvic lymph nodes.
* **Pelvic Lymph Node Dissection (PLND)**: Mandatory template boundaries include the common iliac bifurcation, genitofemoral nerve, inguinal ligament, and obturator fossa.

### Urinary Diversion Options Post-Cystectomy
* **Ileal Conduit (Bricker's Diversion)**: Incontinent diversion; an isolated segment of terminal ileum is used as a conduit to anastomose the ureters to a cutaneous urostomy.
* **Orthotopic Neobladder (Studer Pouch)**: Continent diversion; a detubularized ileal segment is fashioned into a reservoir and anastomosed directly to the remaining native urethra; requires strict patient selection and intact urethral sphincter function.

### Informed Consent & Major Risks
* **TURBT Risks**: **Bladder perforation** (risk of extraperitoneal or intraperitoneal extravasation); severe secondary hemorrhage; **obturator nerve reflex** causing accidental deep thermal injury.
* **Radical Cystectomy Risks**: Massive intraoperative hemorrhage; bowel injury; prolonged postoperative ileus; urinary leak from ureteroenteric anastomoses; **erectile dysfunction** and infertility.


## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Enhanced Recovery After Surgery (ERAS) Protocols
* **Pre-operative Optimization**: Carbohydrate loading; avoiding prolonged fasting; thorough stoma-site marking by a dedicated stoma nurse.
* **Intra-operative Strategies**: Goal-directed fluid therapy; maintaining normothermia; avoiding routine use of abdominal drains unless necessary.
* **Early Mobilization**: Out of bed within **24 hours** post-surgery to minimize venous thromboembolism and accelerate bowel function recovery.

### Immediate Post-operative Monitoring
* **Fluid Balance & Electrolytes**: Meticulous tracking of input and output; monitoring for high-output fluid shifts common after bowel mobilization.
* **Stoma Care & Viability**: Frequent inspections of the **ileal conduit stoma** color; a dusky or black appearance indicates acute vascular ischemia requiring urgent surgical re-exploration.
* **Catheter/Drain Management**: Maintaining patency of pelvic drains and pelvic/urethral stents to prevent urine collection or leakage.

### Long-term Surveillance & Rehabilitation
* **NMIBC Surveillance Protocol**: Repeat flexible cystoscopy and urine cytology every **3 months** for the first 2 years, then every **6 months** up to 5 years, and annually thereafter.
* **Metabolic Monitoring**: Annual evaluation for **hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis** secondary to chronic ammonium and chloride reabsorption by the intestinal mucosa used in the urinary diversion.
* **Upper Tract Surveillance**: Annual **CT Urogram** or renal ultrasound to check for synchronous or metachronous upper tract tumor development.


## 9. Surgical Complications & Prognosis

### Early Complications (<30 Days)
* **Post-TURBT Hemorrhage**: Manifests as severe clot retention; requires continuous bladder irrigation (CBI) or urgent return to theatre for endoscopic clot evacuation and fulguration.
* **Intraperitoneal Bladder Perforation**: Presents with acute peritonitis and abdominal distension; requires emergency open or laparoscopic primary laparorrhaphy repair.
* **Ureteroenteric Anastomotic Leak**: Extravasation of urine into the pelvis causing pelvic collections or localized peritonitis; managed via percutaneous drainage and retrograde or antegrade stenting.

### Intermediate to Late Complications
* **Ureteroenteric Stricture**: Fibrotic narrowing at the site of the ureteral anastomosis leading to unilateral **hydronephrosis** and a progressive decline in renal function.
* **Stomal Stenosis or Parastomal Hernia**: Late complications of an **ileal conduit** requiring surgical revision or stoma appliance adjustments.
* **Vitamin B12 Deficiency**: Occurs years later due to the resection of the distal ileum for urinary diversion construction; requires lifelong intramuscular B12 supplementation.

### Prognosis Factors
* **5-Year Overall Survival**:
  * NMIBC (Ta/T1): >85–90%, but carries a high recurrence rate (50–70%).
  * Localized MIBC (T2): ~60–70% following radical cystectomy and neoadjuvant chemotherapy.
  * Lymph Node Positive (N+): ↓ sharply to 25–35%.
  * Distant Metastatic Disease (M1): Poor prognosis; 5-year survival is <10%.


## 10. Examiner's Pearls

* Viva Question: What is the single most critical histological requirement when a pathologist reviews a TURBT specimen? Answer: The presence of the detrusor muscle (muscularis propria) in the biopsy chip must be explicitly commented on to confidently distinguish T1 disease from muscle-invasive T2 disease.
* Viva Question: Why must intravesical BCG immunotherapy never be administered in a patient experiencing active gross visible haematuria? Answer: Active hemorrhage indicates open vascular channels, which allows the live attenuated *Mycobacterium bovis* to enter the systemic circulation, leading to life-threatening BCG sepsis or mycobacterial dissemination.
* Trap: Assuming that a completely normal renal ultrasound excludes a bladder tumor. Pearl: Ultrasound has low sensitivity for small flat tumors or carcinoma in situ (CIS); flexible cystoscopy remains mandatory for any adult presenting with unexplained visible haematuria.
* Trap: Treating an elderly male smoker for a presumed urinary tract infection when he presents with new-onset irritative storage LUTS and negative urine cultures. Pearl: These symptoms are classic for flat Carcinoma in Situ (CIS) and warrant urgent cystoscopic assessment rather than repeated courses of empirical antibiotics.
* Pitfall: Forgetting to pause continuous bladder irrigation if a patient reports sudden severe lower abdominal pain and abdominal distension post-TURBT. This mistake can lead to massive bladder distension and rupture through a small occult perforation site; immediately stop the irrigation, check catheter patency, and arrange a cystogram if a perforation is suspected.
