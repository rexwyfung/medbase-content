# Chest Trauma (胸部創傷)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Definition of Chest Trauma
* **Chest Trauma (胸部創傷)**: Any physical injury to the thoracic cage, pleura, lungs, heart, or mediastinal structures.
* Categorized primarily into **Blunt Trauma (鈍性創傷)** (most common) and **Penetrating Trauma (穿透性創傷)**.
* Follows the **ATLS (Advanced Trauma Life Support)** paradigm for immediate identification of lethal injuries (the "Deadly Dozen").

### Epidemiology of Chest Trauma in Hong Kong
* **Mechanism**: In HK, **Blunt Trauma** accounts for >90% of cases.
* **Top Causes**: Falls from height (particularly elderly population), Motor Vehicle Collisions (MVCs/RTAs), and industrial accidents (construction).
* **Penetrating Trauma**: Rare in HK compared to Western centers; usually intentional stabbings rather than gunshot wounds.
* **Mortality**: High morbidity/mortality if associated with multi-trauma; isolated rib fractures in elderly carry high risk of **Pneumonia (肺炎)**.

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Surgical Anatomy of the Thoracic Cavity (胸腔解剖)
* **Bony Thorax**: 12 pairs of ribs, sternum, thoracic vertebrae.
* **Intercostal Space (肋間隙)**: Vein, Artery, Nerve (**VAN**) run under the inferior margin of each rib. → *Procedural safety*: Always insert drains superior to the rib.
* **Pleural Cavity**: Potential space between visceral and parietal pleura; normal pressure is negative (-4 to -8 cmH2O).
* **Mediastinum (縱隔)**: Heart, great vessels (aorta, SVC/IVC), trachea, esophagus.

### Pathophysiology of Tension Pneumothorax (張力性氣胸)
* **Mechanism**: "One-way valve" air leak from lung/chest wall into pleural space.
* **Hemodynamics**: ↑ Intrathoracic pressure → Mediastinal shift to contralateral side → Compression of SVC/IVC → ↓ Venous return → ↓ Cardiac output → **Obstructive Shock (阻塞性休克)**.

### Pathophysiology of Massive Haemothorax (大量血胸)
* **Mechanism**: Rapid accumulation of blood (>1500ml) in pleural space.
* **Sources**: Often torn intercostal artery, internal mammary artery, or lung parenchyma.
* **Effects**: Dual insult of **Hypovolaemic Shock (低血容量休克)** (blood loss) + **Respiratory Compromise** (lung compression).

### Pathophysiology of Flail Chest (連枷胸)
* **Mechanism**: ≥2 contiguous ribs fractured in ≥2 places.
* **Kinematics**: Segment loses continuity with chest wall → **Paradoxical movement (反常呼吸運動)** (sucks in during inspiration, pushes out during expiration).
* **Core Pathology**: Underlying **Pulmonary Contusion (肺挫傷)** drives hypoxia, *not* just the mechanical flail segment.

### Pathophysiology of Cardiac Tamponade (心包填塞)
* **Mechanism**: Blood accumulation in rigid pericardial sac (often penetrating injury).
* **Hemodynamics**: ↑ Pericardial pressure → restricts right ventricular filling → ↓ Stroke volume → **Obstructive Shock**.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Clinical Signs of Tension Pneumothorax
* Severe **Respiratory Distress (呼吸困難)** & Tachypnoea (↑ RR).
* Tracheal deviation AWAY from the affected side (late sign).
* **Hyper-resonant percussion (叩診呈過清音)** on affected side.
* Absent or ↓ breath sounds on affected side.
* **Distended neck veins (頸靜脈怒張)** (↑ JVP due to ↓ venous return).
* Hypotension & Tachycardia (Shock).

### Clinical Signs of Massive Haemothorax
* Shock (Hypotension, Tachycardia, Pallor).
* **Dullness to percussion (叩診呈濁音)** on affected side.
* Absent or ↓ breath sounds on affected side.
* Flat neck veins (due to profound hypovolaemia, unlike tension PTX).

### Clinical Signs of Flail Chest & Pulmonary Contusion
* Visible **Paradoxical chest wall movement (反常胸壁運動)**.
* Severe localized pain (prevents deep inspiration → ↑ risk of atelectasis).
* Crepitus over rib fractures.
* Hypoxia (↓ SpO2) and haemoptysis (if severe contusion).

### Clinical Signs of Cardiac Tamponade (Beck's Triad)
* **Beck's Triad (貝克三聯徵)**:
  * 1. Hypotension.
  * 2. Muffled heart sounds.
  * 3. Distended neck veins (↑ JVP).
* **Pulsus Paradoxus (奇脈)**: Drop in SBP >10mmHg during inspiration.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Differential Diagnosis of Traumatic Shock & Respiratory Distress
| Condition | Breath Sounds | Percussion Note | Tracheal Deviation | JVP | Shock Type |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Tension Pneumothorax** | ↓ or Absent | Hyper-resonant | Away | ↑ (Distended) | Obstructive |
| **Massive Haemothorax** | ↓ or Absent | Dull | Midline (usually) | ↓ (Flat) | Hypovolaemic |
| **Cardiac Tamponade** | Normal | Normal | Midline | ↑ (Distended) | Obstructive |
| **Simple Pneumothorax** | ↓ | Hyper-resonant | Midline | Normal | None (Usually) |
| **Tracheobronchial Injury** | Variable | Variable | Midline | Normal | Hypoxic |

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)

### Bedside Investigations in Trauma Resuscitation (ATLS Protocol)
* **eFAST Scan (延伸重點創傷超聲波)**: Gold standard adjunct in primary survey. Checks for pneumothorax (absence of lung sliding), haemothorax (fluid in pleural spaces), and tamponade (pericardial fluid).
* **ABG (動脈血氣分析)**: Assess hypoxia, hypercapnia, and base deficit (surrogate for tissue perfusion/shock).
* **ECG (心電圖)**: Check for arrhythmias (blunt cardiac injury).

### Radiological Imaging for Chest Trauma
* **Portable CXR (Trauma series)**: Rapid assessment for gross PTX, large HTX, widened mediastinum (aortic injury), multi-rib fractures.
* **Contrast CT Thorax/Abdomen/Pelvis (Trauma Pan-scan)**:
  * *Indication*: Haemodynamically STABLE patients.
  * *Findings*: Identifies occult pneumothorax, active extravasation (blush), aortic dissection/transection, pulmonary contusion extent, and precise rib fracture mapping.

### Laboratory Investigations for Major Trauma
* **Type and Screen / Crossmatch (血型及交叉配血)**: Prepare for massive transfusion (O-ve blood if uncrossmatched emergency).
* **CBC**: Baseline Hb (may not drop immediately due to hemoconcentration).
* **Coagulation Profile**: PT, APTT, Fibrinogen (monitor for trauma-induced coagulopathy).
* **Troponin I**: Rule out blunt myocardial injury.

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)

### ATLS Primary Survey Approach (ABCDE)
* **Airway**: Intubation if GCS ≤8 or severe hypoxia. Maintain C-spine precautions.
* **Breathing**: Identify and treat immediately life-threatening injuries (Tension PTX, Open PTX, Massive HTX).
* **Circulation**: 2x large bore IV (16G/14G), 1L crystalloid bolus, trigger Massive Transfusion Protocol (MTP) if responding poorly. Activate HA trauma call.

### Immediate Management of Tension Pneumothorax
* **Do NOT wait for CXR.**
* **Immediate Decompression**:
  * *Adults*: **Finger Thoracostomy (手指胸腔造口術)** at 5th Intercostal Space (ICS), mid-axillary line (preferred over needle per latest ATLS).
  * *Alternative/Pre-hospital*: Needle thoracentesis (14G angiocath) at 5th ICS mid-axillary line or 2nd ICS mid-clavicular line.
* **Definitive**: Followed immediately by **Chest Tube Insertion (胸腔引流管)**.

### Immediate Management of Massive Haemothorax
* **Chest Tube Insertion**: Large bore (28-32 Fr) drain to evacuate blood, allow lung expansion, and monitor output.
* Volume resuscitation (Blood products >> Crystalloids).

### Indications for Emergency Thoracotomy (緊急開胸手術)
* **Massive Haemothorax Guidelines**:
  * >1500 ml of blood immediately upon chest tube insertion.
  * >200 ml/hour continuous bleeding for 2-4 consecutive hours.
  * Rapidly worsening physiological status with continuous hemorrhage.
* **Other Surgical Indications**:
  * Tracheobronchial tree injury (massive air leak).
  * Massive esophageal leak.
  * Radiologically confirmed great vessel injury.

### Resuscitative Thoracotomy (EDT) Indications (HA Trauma Guidelines)
* Penetrating thoracic trauma + cardiac arrest in ED, or witnessed arrest <15 mins prior.
* Blunt thoracic trauma + cardiac arrest in ED, or witnessed arrest <10 mins prior (highly controversial, low survival, selective use in HA).
* *Goal*: Release tamponade, control cardiac/hilar bleeding, cross-clamp descending aorta.

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Operative Principles of Chest Drain Insertion (胸腔造口術)
* **Triangle of Safety (安全三角)**: Anterior border of latissimus dorsi, lateral border of pectoralis major, a line superior to the horizontal level of the nipple, and an apex below the axilla (usually 5th ICS).
* **Technique**:
  * Local anaesthetic infiltration (including periosteum).
  * Incision, blunt dissection with forceps over superior edge of the lower rib (avoid VAN).
  * Digital exploration (sweep finger to clear adhesions and confirm intrapleural placement).
  * Direct tube posteriorly and apically. Secure with heavy silk suture.

### Operative Principles of Trauma Thoracotomy
* **Incision**: Anterolateral thoracotomy (often left-sided) via 4th or 5th ICS. Clamshell incision if bilateral access needed.
* **Exposure**: Retract ribs, open pericardium *anterior* to phrenic nerve to release tamponade.
* **Aortic Control**: Cross-clamp descending thoracic aorta to prioritize flow to brain/heart.

### Consent Issues in Emergency Chest Surgery
* **Life-Saving Interventions**: Chest drains, EDT, or emergency OR thoracotomy for unstable patients operate under **Implied Consent (默示同意)** (Doctrine of Necessity).
* Two senior doctors should ideally document the necessity in HA notes if patient is unconscious/unaccompanied.

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Post-Chest Drain Ward Management
* **System Setup**: Connect to underwater seal drain.
* **Monitoring**:
  * Observe for **Swinging (波動)** (confirms patency).
  * Observe for **Bubbling (氣泡)** (indicates ongoing air leak).
  * Record hourly fluid drainage volume.
* **Removal Criteria**: Drainage <100-150ml/24hr (serous), lung fully expanded on CXR, and no bubbling (air leak resolved).

### Multi-modal Analgesia & Pulmonary Rehabilitation
* *Crucial for rib fractures/flail chest* to prevent atelectasis and pneumonia.
* **Analgesia Step-ladder**: Paracetamol + NSAIDs (if no CI) → Tramadol/Opioids → **Thoracic Epidural (胸椎硬膜外麻醉)** or Paravertebral Block (Gold standard for severe multi-rib #).
* **Physiotherapy**: Incentive spirometry, active coughing, early mobilization.

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Early Complications (<24 Hours)
* Exsanguination / Hypovolaemic shock.
* Tension pneumothorax (if drain blocks or kinks).
* Myocardial contusion → Arrhythmias / Heart failure.

### Intermediate Complications (Days to Weeks)
* **Retained Haemothorax (滯留性血胸)**: Clotted blood cannot be drained → Risk of infection. (May require early VATS evacuation within 3-7 days).
* **Empyema Thoracis (膿胸)**: Infection of pleural space.
* **Pneumonia (肺炎)**: Secondary to splinting from rib fracture pain.
* **ARDS (急性呼吸窘迫綜合徵)**: Triggered by massive transfusion or severe pulmonary contusion.

### Late Complications & Prognosis
* **Fibrothorax (纖維胸)**: Organized haematoma leading to lung trapping and restrictive lung disease. Requires decortication.
* **Chronic Pain**: Post-thoracotomy pain syndrome (intercostal neuralgia).

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: What is the most important initial management step for a patient presenting with tracheal deviation, distended neck veins, and absent breath sounds following an RTA? Answer: Immediate needle or finger thoracostomy to decompress the tension pneumothorax, BEFORE getting a CXR.
* Viva Question: What are the absolute indications for emergency thoracotomy in a patient with a haemothorax? Answer: Initial drainage of >1500ml of blood, or continuous bleeding of >200ml/hr for 2 to 4 hours.
* Viva Question: How do you differentiate a massive haemothorax from a tension pneumothorax clinically? Answer: Both have absent breath sounds, but haemothorax has dullness to percussion and flat neck veins, whereas tension pneumothorax has hyper-resonance and distended neck veins.
* Trap: Believing that paradoxical movement of the flail segment is the primary cause of hypoxia in flail chest. Pearl: The underlying pulmonary contusion and resulting V/Q mismatch is the primary driver of hypoxia, not just the mechanical flail segment.
* Trap: Inserting a chest drain directly inferior to a rib. Pearl: The intercostal neurovascular bundle (VAN) runs along the inferior groove of the rib. Always pass instruments strictly over the superior margin of the lower rib to avoid catastrophic bleeding.
* Pitfall: Waiting for a chest X-ray to confirm a tension pneumothorax in an unstable patient. Tension pneumothorax is a clinical diagnosis; delaying decompression for imaging can lead to fatal cardiac arrest.
* Pitfall: Under-dosing analgesia in elderly patients with rib fractures. Poor pain control prevents deep breathing and coughing, inevitably leading to atelectasis, retention of secretions, and potentially fatal pneumonia. Always consider epidural or PCA early.
