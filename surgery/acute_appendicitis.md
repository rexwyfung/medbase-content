# Acute Appendicitis (急性闌尾炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Acute Appendicitis
* **Acute Appendicitis** (急性闌尾炎): Acute inflammation of the **vermiform appendix** (闌尾). 
* Most common general surgical emergency globally.

### Local Epidemiology of Acute Appendicitis in Hong Kong
* **Peak Incidence**: 10–30 years old.
* **Lifetime Risk**: ~7-8%.
* **Gender**: Slight ♂ predominance (1.4:1).
* **Mortality**: <1% in uncomplicated; up to 5% if perforated in elderly.

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Surgical Anatomy of the Vermiform Appendix
* **Base location**: Convergence of the 3 **taeniae coli** (結腸帶) at the posteromedial caecum (McBurney’s point/麥氏點).
* **Blood Supply**: **Appendicular artery** (闌尾動脈) → branch of ileocolic artery. Crucially an **end artery** (prone to thrombosis/ischemia).
* **Positional Variants**:
	* **Retrocaecal** (盲腸後) (74% - most common).
	* **Pelvic** (骨盆) (21%).
	* Paracaecal, Subcaecal, Pre-ileal, Post-ileal (5%).

### Pathophysiology of Appendiceal Obstruction
* **Primary Mechanism**: Luminal obstruction.
* **Adults** (成人): **Faecolith** (糞石) / Appendicolith.
* **Children** (兒童): **Lymphoid hyperplasia** (淋巴組織增生) (often post-viral URTI).
* **Rare causes**: Tumours (carcinoid, caecal Ca), foreign bodies, parasites (*Enterobius vermicularis*).
* **Consequence**: Obstruction → continued mucus secretion → ↑ intraluminal pressure → venous & lymphatic engorgement (edema).

### Pathophysiology of Appendiceal Ischemia and Perforation
* **Progressive Inflammation**: ↑ Intraluminal pressure > capillary perfusion pressure → arterial compromise.
* **Tissue Necrosis**: Hypoxia + bacterial overgrowth (mixed flora, *E. coli*, *B. fragilis*) → **Gangrenous appendicitis** (壞疽性闌尾炎).
* **Rupture**: Wall transmural necrosis → **Perforation** (穿孔).
* **Spill**: → Localized abscess (膿腫) OR generalized **Peritonitis** (腹膜炎).

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Classic Symptoms of Acute Appendicitis
* **Migratory Pain** (轉移性痛楚): Visceral pain (vague, periumbilical) → Parietal peritoneum irritation → Somatic pain (sharp, localized to RIF).
* **Anorexia** (厭食): Highly sensitive; hunger questions appendicitis.
* **Nausea & Vomiting** (噁心及嘔吐): Typically *follows* the onset of pain (if vomiting precedes pain, suspect gastroenteritis).

### Key Abdominal Signs in Acute Appendicitis
* **McBurney’s Point Tenderness**: Maximal tenderness 1/3 distance from ASIS to umbilicus.
* **Guarding** (肌衛): Involuntary abdominal muscle spasm.
* **Rebound Tenderness** (反跳痛): +ve **Blumberg's sign** → denotes peritonitis.

### Eponymous Signs in Acute Appendicitis
* **Rovsing’s sign**: Deep palpation in LIF → causes pain in RIF (gas pushed back to caecum).
* **Psoas sign**: Pain on R hip extension (suggests retrocaecal appendix).
* **Obturator sign**: Pain on R hip internal rotation (suggests pelvic appendix).
* **Dunphy’s sign**: Increased pain in RIF on coughing.

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)

### Laboratory Investigations for Acute Appendicitis Workup
* **CBC**: ↑ WBC (Leukocytosis), ↑ Neutrophils (left shift).
* **CRP**: ↑ (Inflammatory marker).
* **Urinalysis (Dipstick)**: Rule out UTI/stones. *Note:* Trace WBC/RBC possible due to inflamed appendix abutting ureter/bladder.
* **Pregnancy Test (UPT/Serum β-hCG)**: Mandatory in ALL females of childbearing age to rule out ectopic pregnancy.

### Alvarado Scoring System for Acute Appendicitis
| Mnemonic | Feature                            | Score |
| :------- | :--------------------------------- | :---- |
| **M**    | **M**igratory RIF pain             | 1     |
| **A**    | **A**norexia                       | 1     |
| **N**    | **N**ausea / Vomiting              | 1     |
| **T**    | **T**enderness in RIF              | 2     |
| **R**    | **R**ebound tenderness             | 1     |
| **E**    | **E**levated temperature (>37.3°C) | 1     |
| **L**    | **L**eukocytosis                   | 2     |
| **S**    | **S**hift to left (Neutrophils)    | 1     |
* **<5**: Appendicitis unlikely.
* **5-6**: Equivocal (Observe or CT/USG).
* **≥7**: Probable appendicitis (Surgical consult).

### First-line Imaging for Right Iliac Fossa Pain
* **USG Abdomen & Pelvis** (腹部超聲波):
	* **Target population**: Children, pregnant women, thin adults.
	* **Findings**: Non-compressible, blind-ended tubular structure >6mm diameter, target sign, periappendiceal fluid.

### Gold Standard Imaging for Acute Appendicitis
* **CT Abdomen & Pelvis with IV Contrast** (電腦掃描):
	* **Target population**: Atypical presentation, elderly (rule out Caecum Ca), diagnostic uncertainty.
	* **Findings**: Dilated appendix (>6mm), wall thickening/enhancement, periappendiceal fat stranding, appendicolith, abscess/phlegmon.

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)

### Initial Medical Management and Resuscitation
* **NPO** (禁食): Keep nil by mouth for potential surgery.
* **IV Fluid Resuscitation**: Crystalloids (Normal Saline or Plasmalyte).
* **Analgesia**: IV Paracetamol, short-acting opioids (Fentanyl). *Does not obscure clinical signs.*
* **IV Antibiotics**: Pre-operative prophylaxis within 60 mins of skin incision.
	* **HA Protocol**: Typically IV Amoxicillin/Clavulanate (Augmentin) OR IV Cefuroxime + Metronidazole (covers Gram -ve and anaerobes).

### Indications for Emergency Appendectomy
* Uncomplicated acute appendicitis.
* Generalized peritonitis (ruptured appendix).
* Patient presenting <48 hours of symptom onset.

### Management of Appendiceal Mass or Abscess (Ochsner-Sherren Regimen)
* **Presentation**: Patient presents late (>48-72 hours) with palpable RIF mass (phlegmon/omentum wrapped around inflamed appendix).
* **Conservative Management (Ochsner-Sherren Regimen)**:
	* NPO, strict bed rest, IV fluids.
	* IV broad-spectrum antibiotics.
	* Vitals + mass size monitoring (mark borders on abdomen).
* **Percutaneous Drainage**: Under CT/USG guidance if distinct abscess >3cm.
* **Interval Follow-up**: Interval appendectomy at 6-8 weeks (traditional). *Modern HA practice:* Increasingly adopting early laparoscopic approach if advanced expertise available.
* **Mandatory Follow-up in >40 y/o**: Colonoscopy (大腸鏡) at 6-8 weeks to rule out underlying **Caecal Carcinoma** mimicking appendicitis.

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Operative Steps for Laparoscopic Appendectomy (腹腔鏡闌尾切除術)
* **Pneumoperitoneum**: Hassan or Veress needle technique (12mmHg).
* **Port Placement**: Umbilical (10mm, optical), LIF (5mm), Suprapubic/RIF (5mm).
* **Exploration**: Inspect whole abdomen (exclude Meckel's, ovarian cysts, PID).
* **Identify Appendix**: Locate caecum and trace taeniae coli to the base.
* **Mobilize**: Divide mesoappendix (using bipolar/Ligasure/harmonic scalpel).
* **Ligate Base**: Double endoloop (Roeder knot) or surgical stapler.
* **Transect & Retrieve**: Cut appendix and place in endobag (prevents port-site infection).
* **Washout**: Copious suction-irrigation if pus/peritonitis present.

### Surgical Consent and Risks for Appendectomy
* **General Risks**: General anesthesia risks, bleeding, Surgical Site Infection (SSI), DVT/PE.
* **Procedure-Specific Risks**:
	* Intra-abdominal abscess collection.
	* Visceral injury (bowel, bladder, vessels).
* **Right Hemicolectomy** (右半結腸切除術): If appendiceal base is severely necrotic or caecal tumor suspected.
* **Conversion to Open Surgery** (轉開腹手術): 5-10% rate (due to dense adhesions, unclear anatomy, severe bleeding).

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Immediate Post-operative Care Routine
* **Vitals**: Regular monitoring for early sepsis/bleeding.
* **Analgesia**: Multimodal (PO Paracetamol, NSAIDs, weak opioids like Tramadol).
* **Mobilization**: Early ambulation on Day 0 to prevent DVT and atelectasis.

### Post-operative Diet and Antibiotic Protocol
* **Diet**: Diet As Tolerated (DAT) - clear fluids → soft diet → normal diet once +ve bowel sounds or +ve flatus.
* **Antibiotic Duration**:
	* **Uncomplicated (Non-perforated)**: Discontinue antibiotics within 24 hours post-op.
	* **Complicated (Perforated/Gangrenous)**: Continue therapeutic IV antibiotics for 3-5 days depending on clinical response (fever resolution, normalizing WBC).

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Early Surgical Complications of Appendectomy (< 1 week)
* **Surgical Site Infection (SSI)** (傷口感染): Most common complication, especially in open/perforated cases.
* **Hemorrhage**: Slipped appendicular artery ligature.
* **Paralytic Ileus**: Bowel stasis due to handling/inflammation.

### Intermediate Surgical Complications of Appendectomy (1 - 3 weeks)
* **Intra-abdominal Abscess**: Pelvic or subphrenic collection. Suspect if persistent swinging pyrexia and ↑ WBC on post-op day 5-7. Requires CT scan and possible percutaneous drainage.
* **Fistula**: Faecal fistula from slipped appendiceal stump ligature or unrecognised bowel injury.

### Late Surgical Complications of Appendectomy (> 3 weeks)
* **Adhesive Small Bowel Obstruction (SBO)** (腸梗阻): Common late complication of any open/laparoscopic abdominal surgery.
* **Incisional/Right Inguinal Hernia**: Specifically in open appendectomy (Gridiron incision) due to injury to the **iliohypogastric nerve**, causing weakness in the conjoint tendon.
* **Stump Appendicitis**: Inflammation of residual appendix if amputated too far from the base (>5mm).

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: What is the most important anatomical landmark for locating the appendix during surgery? Answer: The convergence of the taeniae coli at the caecum.
* Viva Question: Why do you mandate a colonoscopy 6 weeks after conservative management of an appendiceal mass in a 45-year-old patient? Answer: To rule out an underlying caecal neoplasm obstructing the appendiceal orifice.
* Trap: Assuming a normal leukocyte count definitively rules out acute appendicitis. Pearl: Up to 10% of acute appendicitis cases present with a normal WBC count; rely on clinical signs and imaging if suspicious.
* Pitfall: Discharging a female patient of childbearing age with RIF pain without a pregnancy test. You must always rule out ectopic pregnancy; failure to do so is a classic fatal error in emergency medicine.
* Trap: Using strong opioids in A&E masks abdominal signs and delays diagnosis. Pearl: Modern evidence shows early administration of IV opioid analgesia provides comfort and does NOT obscure the physical exam or delay surgical consultation.
* Viva Question: During a diagnostic laparoscopy for suspected appendicitis, the appendix appears completely normal. What MUST you do before concluding the surgery? Answer: Examine the terminal ileum (at least 60cm) to rule out Meckel's diverticulitis and check the pelvic organs (ovaries/tubes) in females.
