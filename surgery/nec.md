# Necrotising Enterocolitis (NEC) (壞死性小腸結腸炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology
### Clinical Definition
**Definition:** Acquired, devastating inflammatory bowel disease (primarily affecting the terminal ileum & ascending colon) → ischaemia, necrosis, and potential perforation.

### Local Epidemiology & Risk Factors
* **Demographics:** Primarily affects **premature infants** in the **Neonatal Intensive Care Unit (NICU)**.
* **Incidence:** Inversely proportional to **gestational age** and birth weight (affects ~5% of VLBW infants).
* **Tertiary Care:** Managed in tertiary NICUs in Hong Kong (e.g., QMH, PWH, KWH, UCH).
* **Key Risk Factors:** **Intrauterine Growth Restriction (IUGR) (子宮內生長遲滯)** with antenatal reversed end-diastolic flow on Doppler, and **perinatal asphyxia (圍產期窒息)**.

## 2. Pathophysiology & Genetics
### Immature Gut & Immune System
* **Prematurity:** Immature gut barrier & mucosal immunity → ↑ susceptibility to injury.
* **Poor Motility:** Intestinal stasis allows bacterial overgrowth.

### Enteral Feeding & Microbiome
* **Formula Feeding (配方奶):** ↑↑ risk compared to **Breast Milk (母乳)**.
* **Altered Microbiome:** Abnormal colonization + substrate from feeds → excessive bacterial proliferation & gas production within the bowel wall (**Pneumatosis Intestinalis**).
* **Speed of Feeding:** Unduly rapid ↑ in enteral feeds triggers mucosal damage.

### Ischaemia/Hypoxia (缺血/缺氧)
* **Perinatal Stress:** Asphyxia, severe RDS, PDA, or hypotension → shunts blood away from the splanchnic bed → profound gut ischaemia.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs
### Presentation Timeline
* **Onset:** Typically in the 2nd-3rd week of life in very preterm infants (usually after establishment of enteral feeds).

### Gastrointestinal Signs (Early & Progressive)
* **Feed Intolerance:** ↑ gastric aspirates (often **bilious/green**).
* **Vomiting:** Often **bile-stained (膽汁性嘔吐)**.
* **Abdominal Distension:** Earliest & most consistent sign; abdomen becomes tense & shiny.
* **Abdominal Wall Changes:** Erythema or **bluish discoloration (腹壁變藍)** → indicates underlying peritonitis/necrosis.
* **Stools:** Fresh **blood in stool (血便)**.

### Systemic Signs (Late/Severe)
* **Instability:** Temperature instability, apnoea, bradycardia.
* **Shock:** Lethargy, ↓ perfusion, hypotension → rapid progression to **septic shock (感染性休克)**.
* **Respiratory:** May require **mechanical ventilation (機械通氣)** due to splinting from abdominal distension & pain.

## 4. Discriminatory Features (How to distinguish from similar conditions)
### NEC vs. Neonatal Sepsis
* **Sepsis:** Presents identically with systemic signs (apnoea, bradycardia) & feed intolerance.
* **NEC Discriminator:** Specific abdominal signs (discoloration, massive distension) + pathognomonic AXR findings (**Pneumatosis Intestinalis**).

### NEC vs. Spontaneous Intestinal Perforation (SIP)
* **SIP:** Occurs earlier (typically 1st week of life).
* **SIP Discriminator:** Often associated with **Indomethacin/Dexamethasone** use; lacks the widespread inflammation & pneumatosis seen in NEC.

## 5. Differential Diagnosis
### Diagnostic Table
| Diagnosis | Key Clinical Features | Diagnostic Clues |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **NEC (壞死性小腸結腸炎)** | Preterm, bilious aspirates, bloody stools, abdo distension. | AXR: Pneumatosis Intestinalis, Portal venous gas. |
| **Neonatal Sepsis (新生兒敗血症)** | Temp instability, lethargy, apnoea, ↓ feeds. | +ve Blood cultures, generalized symptoms, normal AXR. |
| **Spontaneous Intestinal Perforation (SIP)** | ELBW infant, sudden deterioration, isolated perforation. | AXR: Pneumoperitoneum *without* Pneumatosis. |
| **Malrotation with Volvulus (腸扭轉)** | Sudden bilious vomiting, shock, often term infant. | Upper GI Contrast: Corkscrew appearance, misplaced DJ flexure. |
| **Hirschsprung Disease (赫什斯普林病)** | Delayed meconium >24h, abdominal distension. | Contrast enema: Transition zone. Rectal biopsy diagnostic. |

## 6. Tiered Investigations
### Bedside Imaging (Gold Standard)
* **Abdominal X-ray (AXR):** AP & Lateral Decubitus views (Crucial: Serial AXR every 6-12h).
* **Early AXR:** Dilated bowel loops, bowel wall thickening.
* **Pathognomonic AXR:** **Pneumatosis Intestinalis (腸壁積氣)** → gas in bowel wall (looks like bubbles/train-tracks).
* **Advanced AXR:** **Portal Venous Gas (門靜脈積氣)** → branching lucencies over the liver.
* **Perforation AXR:** **Pneumoperitoneum (氣腹)** → free air under diaphragm (erect) or anteriorly (lateral decubitus).

### Blood Investigations
* **CBC:** ↓ Plt (Thrombocytopenia = poor prognostic sign), ↓ Neutrophils.
* **CRP:** ↑ (Inflammatory marker).
* **Blood Gas (VBG/ABG):** Metabolic acidosis, ↑ Lactate (indicates gut ischaemia).
* **Blood Cultures:** Frequently +ve (bacterial translocation).

## 7. Management (Acute, Chronic, HA Protocols)
### Acute Medical Management (Bell's Stage I & II)
* **NBM (Nil By Mouth):** STOP all enteral feeds IMMEDIATELY.
* **Gastric Decompression:** Insert large-bore Replogle tube on free drainage/low suction.
* **Nutrition:** Commence **Total Parenteral Nutrition (TPN) (全靜脈營養)**.
* **IV Antibiotics:** Broad-spectrum to cover aerobes + anaerobes (e.g., Ampicillin, Gentamicin, Metronidazole) per HA NICU protocols.
* **Cardiorespiratory Support:** Mechanical ventilation & inotropes if shocked.

### Surgical Management (Bell's Stage III)
* **Indications:** Bowel perforation (Pneumoperitoneum), failure of medical therapy, rapid clinical deterioration.
* **Laparotomy (剖腹手術):** Resection of necrotic bowel + formation of **Stoma (ileostomy/colostomy)**. Primary anastomosis is rarely performed acutely.
* **Peritoneal Drain:** Temporising measure in extremely unstable ELBW infants unable to tolerate general anaesthesia.

## 8. MDT & Psychosocial Care
### Prevention Strategies
* **Breast Milk (母乳):** Strongly protective; mother's own milk or donor milk prioritized.
* **Standardised Protocols:** Slow, cautious advancement of enteral feeds in HA NICUs.
* **Probiotics/Prebiotics:** Emerging evidence suggests they improve the microbiome & ↓ NEC risk.

### Family Support
* **Medical Social Worker (MSW):** Support for parents dealing with prolonged NICU stays, emotional stress, and potential bereavement.

## 9. Safeguarding & NAI Red Flags
### Parental Communication
* **High Mortality:** Grave prognosis (~20% mortality rate).
* **Consent:** Requires frequent, sensitive communication regarding the potential for rapid deterioration & the urgent need for surgical consent.

## 10. Complications & Prognosis
### Acute Complications
* **Septic Shock & DIC:** Widespread necrosis → profound systemic inflammatory response.
* **Peritonitis:** Arising from frank bowel perforation.
* **Death:** ~20% mortality rate overall.

### Long-Term Complications
* **Bowel Strictures (腸道狹窄):** Common (10-20%); occurs weeks/months post-recovery → presents as bowel obstruction.
* **Short Bowel Syndrome (短腸綜合症):** Following massive bowel resection → malabsorption, long-term TPN dependence → high risk of TPN-associated liver disease.
* **Neurological:** ↑ risk of severe **neurodevelopmental impairment (神經發育障礙)**.

## 11. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Trap: Waiting for pneumoperitoneum to definitively diagnose NEC. Pearl: Pneumatosis intestinalis is the pathognomonic hallmark. If you see it, stop feeds and treat aggressively. Pneumoperitoneum means the bowel has already perforated.
* Viva Question: What is the single most effective intervention to prevent NEC in premature infants? Answer: The use of exclusive human milk (mother's own milk or donor breast milk).
* Pitfall: Assuming bilious vomiting in a neonate is always NEC. Always urgently rule out surgical emergencies like Malrotation with Volvulus, which requires an urgent Upper GI contrast study.
* Viva Question: What specific AXR view is best to detect small amounts of free air in a sick neonate who cannot sit upright? Answer: The Left Lateral Decubitus view (free air rises above the liver edge).
