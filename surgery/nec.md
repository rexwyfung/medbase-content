# Necrotising Enterocolitis (壞死性小腸結腸炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Necrotising Enterocolitis
* **Necrotising Enterocolitis (NEC)**: Devastating gastrointestinal medical-surgical emergency in neonates characterized by ischemic & inflammatory necrosis of the bowel mucosa → transmural necrosis → perforation.

### Local Epidemiology & Risk Factors in Hong Kong
* **Prematurity**: Inversely proportional to gestational age & birth weight. Heavily clustered in **Very Low Birth Weight (VLBW) (<1500g)** and **Extremely Low Birth Weight (ELBW) (<1000g)** neonates in HK HA NICUs.
* **Incidence**: ~5-10% of VLBW infants.
* **Onset Timing**: Inversely related to gestational age (e.g., ELBW infants develop NEC weeks after birth; near-term infants develop it within first few days).
* **Key Risk Factors**: Prematurity (早產), enteral formula feeding (formula > breast milk), hypoxia/ischemia (perinatal asphyxia), congenital heart disease (PDA, ↓ gut perfusion), indomethacin/ibuprofen use.

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Affected Surgical Anatomy
* **Most Frequent Sites**: **Terminal ileum (回腸末端)** & **proximal ascending colon (近端升結腸)**.
* **Vascular Watershed Areas**: Highly susceptible due to poor collateral circulation during hypotensive episodes.
* **Global Involvement**: Can manifest as isolated disease (single segment), multifocal segments, or *pan-necrosis* (entire bowel from Treitz to rectum).

### Pathophysiological Triad
* **Ischemic Injury**: Perinatal hypoxia/shock → diving reflex → preferential blood flow to brain/heart → mesenteric ischemia → mucosal barrier breakdown.
* **Enteral Substrate**: Introduction of enteral feeds (especially hyperosmolar formula) → provides substrate for bacterial fermentation.
* **Bacterial Colonization**: Altered microbiome + impaired mucosal barrier → pathogenic bacterial overgrowth & translocation (e.g., *Klebsiella*, *E. coli*, *Clostridium*).

### Inflammatory Cascade
* Influx of bacteria into intestinal wall → massive innate immune response (TLR4 activation) → release of PAF, TNF-α, IL-6 → progressive tissue destruction → **Pneumatosis intestinalis (腸壁積氣)** (gas within bowel wall) → transmural necrosis & perforation.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Systemic Signs of Sepsis in Neonates
* **Temperature Instability**: Hypothermia or fever.
* **Cardiorespiratory**: Apnea, bradycardia (心動過緩), lethargy, ↑ ventilator requirements.
* **Metabolic**: Poor peripheral perfusion, mottled skin, sudden shock.

### Gastrointestinal Symptoms
* **Feeding Intolerance**: Large gastric residuals, vomiting.
* **Bilious Aspirates (膽汁性抽出物)**: Cardinal sign of mechanical/functional obstruction.
* **Stool Changes**: Gross or occult **bloody stools (血便)**.

### Advanced Surgical Signs (Impending/Actual Perforation)
* **Abdominal Distension (腹脹)**: Tenseness, shiny skin.
* **Abdominal Wall Erythema (腹壁紅斑)**: Pathognomonic sign of underlying necrotic bowel & peritonitis.
* **Palpable Mass**: Fixed, inflammatory mass (usually RLQ) representing walled-off perforation.
* **Absent Bowel Sounds**: Paralytic ileus.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Discriminatory Differentials for Neonatal Abdominal Catastrophes
| Differential Diagnosis | Key Discriminatory Features vs NEC |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Spontaneous Intestinal Perforation (SIP)** | Usually isolated terminal ileum perforation. **No pneumatosis** on AXR. Early onset (<7 days). Assoc w/ early Indomethacin/Dexamethasone use. |
| **Midgut Volvulus / Malrotation (中腸扭轉)** | Sudden catastrophic bilious vomiting. **AXR shows gasless abdomen or double-bubble**, not pneumatosis. Upper GI contrast is diagnostic. |
| **Hirschsprung's Enterocolitis (先天性巨結腸併發腸炎)** | Usually term infants. History of delayed meconium passage (>48h). Contrast enema shows transition zone. |
| **Neonatal Sepsis (新生兒敗血症)** | Systemic signs mimic NEC, but isolated sepsis lacks extreme abdominal distension, localized erythema, or pneumatosis on AXR. |

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)

### Laboratory Investigations & Pre-operative Bloods
* **FBC**: ↓ Platelets (Thrombocytopenia is a reliable marker of severity/gangrene), ↓/↑ WBC (neutropenia predicts poor outcome).
* **ABG/VBG**: Severe uncompensated **metabolic acidosis** (lactic acidosis 2° to ischemia), hypoxia, hypercapnia.
* **Infection Markers**: ↑ CRP, ↑ Procalcitonin.
* **Electrolytes**: Hyponatremia (third-spacing), hyperkalemia (cell lysis).
* **Microbiology**: Blood, urine, and CSF cultures (exclude concomitant meningitis).

### Gold Standard Imaging Modalities
* **Abdominal X-Ray (AXR)**: AP & Left Lateral Decubitus views. 
* **AXR Key Findings**:
    * **Early/Nonspecific**: Asymmetrical bowel dilatation, thickened bowel walls, fixed loops.
    * **Pathognomonic**: **Pneumatosis intestinalis (腸壁積氣)** (linear/bubbly radiolucencies in bowel wall).
    * **Advanced**: **Portal venous gas (門靜脈積氣)** (branching radiolucencies over liver shadow), **Pneumoperitoneum (氣腹)** (Rigler's sign, Football sign, free gas under diaphragm on decubitus/cross-table lateral).
* **Bedside Ultrasound (USG)**: Increasing use in HK HA. Detects portal venous gas, mural thickening, absent peristalsis, and echogenic free fluid (indicating perforation).

### Modified Bell's Staging System for NEC
| Stage | Classification | Systemic Signs | Intestinal Signs | Radiographic Signs |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Stage I** | Suspected NEC | Apnea, bradycardia, lethargy | Mild distension, occult bloody stool | Normal or mild ileus |
| **Stage IIA** | Definite NEC (Mild) | Mild toxemia | Gross bloody stool, absent bowel sounds | **Pneumatosis intestinalis** |
| **Stage IIB** | Definite NEC (Mod) | Mild acidosis, thrombocytopenia | Definite tenderness, mild cellulitis | Pneumatosis + **Portal venous gas** |
| **Stage IIIA** | Advanced (Severe) | Severe shock, DIC, mixed acidosis | Severe peritonitis, **abdominal wall erythema** | Prominent pneumatosis, ascites |
| **Stage IIIB** | Advanced (Perforated) | Severe shock, severe apnea | Severe peritonitis | **Pneumoperitoneum (free air)** |

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)

### Initial Medical Resuscitation (Stage I & II)
* **Immediate NPO (禁食)**: Strict bowel rest.
* **Gastric Decompression**: Insert large-bore NGT/OGT on free drainage/low intermittent suction.
* **Total Parenteral Nutrition (TPN)**: Central line insertion (PICC/UVC) for nutritional support.
* **Aggressive Fluid Resuscitation**: Correct hypovolemia (NS boluses), maintain MAP for renal/gut perfusion.
* **Broad-Spectrum IV Antibiotics**: HK HA Protocol typically empirical Ampicillin + Gentamicin/Cefotaxime + Metronidazole (to cover anaerobes, gram -ves, and Enterococcus). Tailor to local NICU antibiogram.

### Absolute Surgical Indications
* **Pneumoperitoneum (氣腹)**: Free intra-abdominal air on AXR (Bell's Stage IIIB).
* **Paracentesis**: Brown/green foul-smelling fluid or +ve Gram stain for bacteria on peritoneal tap (indicating microscopic perforation).

### Relative Surgical Indications (Failure of Medical Therapy)
* **Clinical Deterioration**: Refractory shock, profound metabolic acidosis, worsening thrombocytopenia despite maximal medical therapy.
* **Radiological Worsening**: Persistent fixed loop on serial AXR (suggests full-thickness gangrene), advancing portal venous gas.
* **Clinical Signs of Gangrene**: Palpable abdominal mass, advancing **abdominal wall erythema**.

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Pre-operative Preparation & Optimization
* Intubation and maximal ventilator support.
* Inotropic support (Dopamine/Dobutamine/Adrenaline).
* Correction of coagulopathy (DIC is common): Transfuse Platelets (aim >50k), FFP, Cryoprecipitate.

### Definitive Operative Approaches
* **Exploratory Laparotomy (Standard of Care)**:
    * Right transverse supraumbilical incision.
    * **Resection + Primary Enterostomy (Stoma)**: Resect frankly necrotic bowel, bring viable ends out as stomas (e.g., terminal ileostomy + mucous fistula). Safest option.
    * **Resection + Primary Anastomosis**: Only if patient is hemodynamically stable without profound peritonitis, and disease is localized. Rarely performed in acute severe NEC.
* **Primary Peritoneal Drainage (PPD)**:
    * Indicated for extremely unstable, VLBW/ELBW micro-preemies who cannot tolerate laparotomy.
    * Local anesthetic, incision in RLQ/LLQ, insertion of Penrose drain.
    * Acts as a bridge to formal laparotomy or, occasionally, as definitive treatment.

### Principle of Bowel Preservation
* **Conserve Length**: Resect ONLY frankly necrotic/perforated bowel.
* **"Second Look" Laparotomy**: Leave bowel of questionable viability inside. Pack and plan a re-look operation at 24-48 hours. Aggressive primary resection guarantees Short Bowel Syndrome.
* **Pan-necrosis Management**: If massive necrosis (>75%), options are closure without resection (palliative care/comfort care) vs proximal high jejunostomy. High mortality.

### Pre-operative Consent & Communication Issues
* **High Mortality Risk**: Explicitly counsel parents on intra-operative death risk.
* **Stoma Creation**: Almost guaranteed need for stoma(s) (造口).
* **Long-term Morbidity**: High risk of **Short Bowel Syndrome (短腸綜合症)** and long-term TPN dependence.

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Immediate NICU Care
* Continue invasive ventilation, inotropes, broad-spectrum IV ABx (usually 10-14 days).
* Strict strict fluid/electrolyte balance (high stoma output replacements).
* Aggressive TPN via central line to ensure growth.

### Enteral Feeding Reintroduction
* **Delayed Restart**: Typically withhold feeds for 7-14 days post-op or after resolution of ileus/sepsis.
* **Trophic Feeding**: Start micro-enteral feeds (10-20 ml/kg/day) to stimulate mucosal trophism.
* **Substrate Choice**: **Expressed breast milk (母乳)** or donor breast milk is strictly preferred. Contains IgA, lactoferrin, and macrophages. Avoid formula.

### Stoma Care & Reversal Planning
* Monitor output for high-output stoma state (requires sodium & fluid replacement).
* **Timing of Closure**: Usually performed when infant reaches 2.5kg - 3.0kg, or 6-8 weeks post-op, provided nutrition is optimized.
* **Mandatory Pre-reversal Study**: Distal contrast enema (loopogram) MUST be performed to rule out distal colonic stricture before closure.

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Early Complications (< 2 weeks)
* Overwhelming sepsis, DIC, multi-organ failure.
* Wound dehiscence, surgical site infection.
* Stoma necrosis, stoma retraction, high-output stoma leading to severe electrolyte derangement.

### Intermediate Complications (Weeks to Months)
* **Intestinal Strictures (腸道狹窄)**: Occurs in 10-20% of cases (both medical & surgically managed NEC).
* **Stricture Location**: Most commonly in the **colon** (areas of prior ischemia that healed with fibrosis).
* **Presentation**: Recurrent feeding intolerance, abdominal distension weeks after acute episode.

### Late Complications & Long-Term Morbidity
* **Short Bowel Syndrome (SBS)**: Due to massive resection. Leads to malabsorption, chronic diarrhea, failure to thrive.
* **TPN-Associated Cholestasis (TPNAC) / Intestinal Failure-Associated Liver Disease (IFALD)**: Cirrhosis and liver failure due to prolonged TPN use.
* **Neurodevelopmental Impairment**: ↑ risk of cerebral palsy, cognitive/motor delays 2° to prolonged inflammation and hypoxia.

### Overall Prognosis
* Mortality ranges from 15-30% globally (approaches 50% in surgical NEC requiring operation).
* Extent of bowel resected and residual functional bowel length dictate long-term quality of life.

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: What is the pathognomonic radiological sign of NEC on AXR? Answer: Pneumatosis intestinalis (gas within the bowel wall).
* Viva Question: Which segment of the bowel is most frequently affected in NEC? Answer: Terminal ileum and proximal ascending colon.
* Viva Question: What is the absolute indication for surgery in a neonate with NEC? Answer: Pneumoperitoneum (free air on AXR) or positive paracentesis indicating perforation.
* Viva Question: A premature infant medically treated for NEC develops feeding intolerance 4 weeks later. What is the most likely diagnosis? Answer: Post-NEC intestinal stricture (most commonly colonic).
* Trap: Creating a primary anastomosis in a critically ill neonate with profound peritonitis and DIC. Pearl: The standard of care in hemodynamically unstable NEC is resection with creation of a primary enterostomy (stoma) and mucous fistula.
* Trap: Attempting to resect all bowel of questionable viability to "cure" the infection during the primary operation. Pearl: Resect ONLY frankly necrotic and perforated bowel to avoid Short Bowel Syndrome. Leave questionable bowel and perform a "second-look" laparotomy in 24-48 hours.
* Pitfall: Reversing the enterostomy without investigating the distal bowel limb. Contrast enema (loopogram) MUST be done before stoma closure to rule out downstream strictures.
* Pitfall: Rushing to restart formula feeds early to aid weight gain post-NEC. Aggressive enteral feeding, especially with hyperosmolar formula, increases the risk of recurrent NEC. Restart with slow trophic feeds using purely expressed breast milk.
