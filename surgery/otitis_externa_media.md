# Otitis Media & Externa (中耳炎與外耳炎)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Otitis Externa (OE)
* **Otitis Externa (外耳炎)**: Diffuse inflammation of external auditory canal (EAC) skin +/- pinna/tympanic membrane (TM).
* **Malignant Otitis Externa (MOE) (惡性外耳炎)**: Invasive, life-threatening necrotizing osteomyelitis of temporal bone, primarily affecting immunocompromised/diabetic patients.

### Clinical Definition of Otitis Media (OM)
* **Acute Otitis Media (AOM) (急性中耳炎)**: Rapid-onset acute inflammation/infection of middle ear cleft with purulent effusion.
* **Otitis Media with Effusion (OME) (滲出性中耳炎)**: Presence of non-purulent fluid in middle ear without acute signs of infection (also known as "glue ear").
* **Chronic Suppurative Otitis Media (CSOM) (慢性化膿性中耳炎)**: Chronic inflammation of middle ear/mastoid cavity presenting with recurrent/persistent otorrhoea (>2-6 weeks) through a perforated **Tympanic Membrane (耳膜)**.

### Hong Kong Local Epidemiology & High-Yield Demographics
* **OE**: Incidence ↑ during hot, humid HK summers (May-Sept). High rates of fungal OE (Otomycosis) locally.
* **AOM/OME**: Highly prevalent in HK pediatric population (daycare centers). 
* **Adult Unilateral OME**: Endemic HK risk factor → **Nasopharyngeal Carcinoma (NPC) (鼻咽癌)** until proven otherwise due to high regional EBV prevalence.

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Surgical Anatomy of External & Middle Ear
* **EAC**: Outer 1/3 cartilaginous (contains cerumen glands, hair follicles); Inner 2/3 bony (thin skin directly over periosteum → highly sensitive, lacks subcutaneous tissue).
* **Eustachian Tube (ET) (耳咽管)**: Connects middle ear to nasopharynx. In children: Shorter, wider, more horizontal → ↑ risk of reflux of nasopharyngeal pathogens.
* **Sensory Innervation**: V3 (Auriculotemporal), VII, IX (Jacobson's), X (Arnold's). Causes referred **Otalgia (耳痛)** from teeth, tonsils, larynx.

### Pathophysiology of Otitis Externa
* Loss of protective cerumen (acidic pH) + epithelial microtrauma (cotton buds) + moisture (swimming) → ↑ pH → bacterial/fungal invasion.
* **Pathogens**: *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* (綠膿桿菌) (most common), *Staphylococcus aureus* (金黃色葡萄球菌). 
* **Otomycosis**: *Aspergillus niger* (black spores), *Candida albicans*.

### Pathophysiology of Acute Otitis Media & OME
* Viral URTI / allergic rhinitis → ET mucosal edema & dysfunction → -ve middle ear pressure → transudation of fluid (OME) → secondary bacterial colonization → purulent acute infection (AOM).
* **AOM Pathogens**: *Streptococcus pneumoniae* (肺炎鏈球菌), *Haemophilus influenzae* (流感嗜血桿菌), *Moraxella catarrhalis*.

### Pathophysiology of Malignant Otitis Externa (MOE)
* *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* infection spreads from EAC → fissures of Santorini → temporal bone osteomyelitis → cranial nerve involvement (VII, IX, X, XI, XII) → fatal intracranial spread.
* **Risk factors**: Poorly controlled Diabetes Mellitus (DM), immunosuppression, advanced age.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Clinical Presentation of Otitis Externa
* **Symptoms**: Severe **Otalgia (耳痛)** (worse with chewing), pruritus, blocked ear, scant thick **Otorrhoea (耳漏)**.
* **Signs**: +ve Tragal tenderness (pathognomonic), +ve pinna pull pain, EAC erythema/edema, obliterating debris. Normal TM (if visible).

### Clinical Presentation of Malignant Otitis Externa
* **Symptoms**: Deep, unrelenting, out-of-proportion, nocturnal otalgia in diabetic elderly.
* **Signs**: Granulation tissue at EAC bony-cartilaginous junction (floor), purulent otorrhoea, +/- facial nerve (CN VII) palsy.

### Clinical Presentation of Acute Otitis Media
* **Symptoms**: Rapid onset throbbing pain, fever, irritability (children), sudden relief of pain + copious discharge if TM ruptures.
* **Signs**: Bulging, hyperaemic, opaque TM with loss of light reflex. ↓ TM mobility.

### Clinical Presentation of Otitis Media with Effusion
* **Symptoms**: Painless **Conductive Hearing Loss (傳導性聽力受損)**, aural fullness, delayed speech in children.
* **Signs**: Dull, retracted TM, visible air-fluid levels or bubbles.

### Clinical Presentation of Chronic Suppurative Otitis Media
* **Symptoms**: Chronic painless mucopurulent otorrhoea, progressive hearing loss.
* **Signs**: Central or marginal TM perforation, inflamed middle ear mucosa.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Diagnostic Differentiation of Common Ear Pathologies
| Condition | Key Discriminatory Feature | TM Status | Otorrhoea | Pain Profile |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **OE** | Tragal tenderness, EAC edema | Usually normal | Scant, thick | Severe, worse on jaw movement |
| **AOM** | Fever, acute URTI history | Bulging, red | Copious only if perforated | Throbbing, sudden relief if perforated |
| **OME** | Painless, blocked ear | Retracted, fluid levels | None | None |
| **CSOM** | Chronic discharge >6 wks | Perforated | Copious, mucopurulent | Painless |
| **Cholesteatoma (膽脂瘤)** | Attic crust, foul-smelling debris | Marginal/Attic retraction | Scanty, very foul | Painless (unless infected) |

### Non-Otogenic Referred Otalgia Discriminators
* **TMJ Dysfunction**: Pain on chewing, palpable click, normal ear exam.
* **Dental Caries / Impacted Molar**: Mandibular/maxillary pain radiating to ear.
* **Tonsillitis / Pharyngitis**: Odynophagia, tonsillar exudates (referred via CN IX).

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)

### 1st Line Investigations for OM and OE
* **Otoscopy / Micro-otoscopy**: Gold standard for direct visualization & aural toilet.
* **Ear Swab for C&S**: Indicated for resistant OE, suspected MOE, or CSOM before starting targeted antibiotics.

### Audiological Assessment
* **Pure Tone Audiometry (PTA) (純音聽力測試)**: Essential for OME, CSOM, and Cholesteatoma to document baseline hearing loss (typically conductive).
* **Tympanometry**: 
  * Type A: Normal
  * Type B: Flat (indicates fluid in OME or perforation if high volume)
  * Type C: Negative pressure (ET dysfunction)

### Imaging & Advanced Investigations
* **CT Temporal Bone (Fine Cut)**: Indicated for suspected MOE, Mastoiditis, Cholesteatoma, or surgical planning. Shows bone erosion/opacification.
* **Endoscopic Nasopharyngoscopy**: MANDATORY for any Chinese adult presenting with new-onset unilateral OME to rule out NPC.

### Pre-operative Workup for MOE
* **Inflammatory Markers**: ↑ ESR, ↑ CRP (used to monitor treatment response).
* **Diabetic Control**: Fasting glucose, HbA1c.
* **Nuclear Scans (HA Protocol)**: 
  * Tech-99m bone scan: Localizes osteomyelitis.
  * Gallium-67 scan: Tracks active inflammation/resolution (normalizes upon cure).

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)

### 1st Line Management of Otitis Externa
* **Aural Toilet (耳部清潔)**: Microsuction to remove debris.
* **Topical Therapy**: Antibiotic + Steroid drops (e.g., Sofradex, Tarivid, or Ciproxin HC).
* **Ear Wick (Pope Wick)**: Inserted if EAC severe edema prevents drop penetration. Apply drops to wick.
* **Prevention**: Keep ears dry (water precautions), avoid cotton buds.

### Management Protocol for Malignant Otitis Externa (MOE)
* **Strict Admission & Multi-Disciplinary Team**: ENT + Endocrinologist.
* **Aggressive Diabetic Control**: Strict glycemic management (sliding scale insulin).
* **Prolonged IV Antibiotics**: High-dose IV Ciprofloxacin (anti-Pseudomonal) for 6-8 weeks until Gallium scan normalizes.
* **Surgical Debridement**: Reserved for dead bone/abscess; radical surgery generally avoided.

### Management of Acute Otitis Media (HK HA Protocol)
* **Analgesia & Conservative**: NSAIDs/Paracetamol. Watchful waiting in mild cases >2 yrs old.
* **1st Line Medical**: PO Amoxicillin (5-7 days).
* **2nd Line Medical**: PO Amoxicillin-Clavulanate (Augmentin) if severe, recent Abx use, or failure to respond in 48-72h.

### Management Algorithm for OME
* **Watchful Waiting**: 3 months (most resolve spontaneously). Auto-inflation (Valsalva).
* **Surgical Indications**: Persistent >3 months + bilateral hearing loss >25-30dB, speech delay, or structural TM damage.
* **Surgery**: **Myringotomy and Grommet insertion (耳膜穿刺及導管置入術)** +/- Adenoidectomy (if hypertrophic).

### Management of Chronic Suppurative Otitis Media (CSOM)
* **Conservative**: Strict water precautions, regular microsuction.
* **Medical**: Topical fluoroquinolones (Ofloxacin/Tarivid drops). Avoid ototoxic aminoglycosides if TM perforated.
* **Surgical Indications**: Failure of medical therapy, desire to swim, prevention of recurrent infection.
* **Surgery**: **Myringoplasty (耳膜修補術)** / Tympanoplasty.

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Operative Principles of Myringotomy & Grommet Insertion
* **Anesthesia**: GA (children), LA (adults).
* **Incision**: Antero-inferior quadrant of TM (avoids ossicles/chorda tympani).
* **Aspiration**: Thick mucoid fluid ("glue") suctioned.
* **Insertion**: Bobbin-shaped ventilation tube (Grommet) placed to equalize pressure.

### Operative Principles of Myringoplasty (Tympanoplasty Type 1)
* **Graft Material**: Temporalis fascia or tragal cartilage/perichondrium.
* **Approach**: Transcanal, Endaural, or Post-auricular.
* **Technique**: Underlay (graft placed medial to TM annulus) vs Overlay (lateral).

### Surgical Consent & Specific Risks (Grommet & Myringoplasty)
* **Grommet Complications**: Premature extrusion, retained tube, persistent TM perforation (1-2%), otorrhoea, tympanosclerosis (chalk patches).
* **Myringoplasty Complications**: Graft failure (re-perforation), conductive/sensorineural hearing loss, taste disturbance (Chorda tympani injury), facial nerve palsy (rare but critical to mention).

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Immediate Post-operative Care (Ear Surgery)
* **Head Elevation**: Semi-upright position to ↓ venous congestion and bleeding.
* **Dressings**: Mastoid bandage (removed day 1), BIPP/medicated ear packing (removed in clinic at 2-3 weeks).

### Ward & Discharge Protocols
* **Water Precautions**: STRICT avoidance of water entry. Use cotton wool coated with Vaseline during showers. NO swimming.
* **Valsalva Precaution**: Avoid forceful nose blowing, open mouth when sneezing to prevent graft displacement.
* **Medications**: Prescribe PO antibiotics (1 week) and topical drops (once packing removed).

### Rehabilitation & Follow-up
* **Audiology Review**: Repeat PTA at 6-8 weeks post-op to assess hearing improvement.
* **Grommet Follow-up**: Check every 6 months. Extrudes spontaneously in 6-12 months.

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Extracranial Intratemporal Complications of OM
* **Acute Mastoiditis (急性乳突炎)**: Post-auricular erythema, fluctuance, loss of post-auricular sulcus, forward protrusion of pinna. Requires IV Abx + cortical mastoidectomy.
* **Facial Nerve Palsy (面神經麻痺)**: Due to bony dehiscence over CN VII.
* **Labyrinthitis**: Vertigo, sensorineural hearing loss, nystagmus.

### Intracranial Complications of OM
* **Meningitis (腦膜炎)**: Most common intracranial complication.
* **Intracranial Abscess**: Extradural, subdural, or temporal lobe/cerebellar brain abscess.
* **Sigmoid Sinus Thrombosis**: Spiking 'picket-fence' fevers, positive Griesinger's sign (edema over mastoid via emissary veins).

### Prognosis
* **Uncomplicated AOM/OE**: Excellent, near 100% resolution with primary care.
* **MOE**: Mortality historically >50%, now <10-20% with prompt IV Ciprofloxacin, but cranial neuropathies may be permanent.
* **CSOM Post-Myringoplasty**: 80-90% graft take success rate, substantial improvement in conductive hearing loss.

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: What is the most critical differential diagnosis to rule out in a 50-year-old local Hong Kong man presenting with new-onset, painless, unilateral otitis media with effusion? Answer: Nasopharyngeal Carcinoma (NPC) causing Eustachian tube obstruction. Endoscopic evaluation of the post-nasal space is mandatory.
* Viva Question: Which anatomical quadrant of the tympanic membrane is the safest for a myringotomy incision, and why? Answer: The antero-inferior quadrant. It avoids the ossicular chain and chorda tympani located superiorly and posteriorly.
* Trap: Prescribing Neomycin/Gentamicin ear drops for a patient with Chronic Suppurative Otitis Media (CSOM). Pearl: Aminoglycosides are ototoxic. If the tympanic membrane is perforated, they can enter the middle ear/round window and cause permanent sensorineural hearing loss. Use topical fluoroquinolones (e.g., Ofloxacin) instead.
* Trap: Discharging an elderly diabetic patient with severe, deep ear pain, normal-looking TM, and granulation tissue in the external canal with a simple script for oral Amoxicillin. Pearl: This is Malignant Otitis Externa until proven otherwise. It requires urgent admission, IV Ciprofloxacin, strict glycemic control, and a CT temporal bone.
* Pitfall: Diagnosing AOM solely based on a "red" tympanic membrane in a crying child. Erythema alone can be caused by crying/vascular engorgement. AOM requires bulging of the TM, loss of mobility, and clinical signs of acute infection (fever, purulence).
