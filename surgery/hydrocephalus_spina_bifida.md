# Hydrocephalus & Spina Bifida (腦積水與脊柱裂)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Hydrocephalus
* **Hydrocephalus (腦積水)**: Active distension of ventricular system due to inadequate passage of CSF from point of production (ventricular choroid plexus) to point of absorption (arachnoid granulations).
* Classically divided into **Communicating (交通性)** (failure of absorption) and **Non-communicating/Obstructive (非交通性/阻塞性)** (blockage within ventricular system).

### Clinical Definition of Spina Bifida
* **Spina Bifida (脊柱裂)**: Incomplete closure of embryonic neural tube (Neural Tube Defect, NTD) at week 3-4 of gestation.
* **Spina Bifida Occulta (隱性脊柱裂)**: Bony defect only, intact skin.
* **Meningocele (腦膜膨出)**: Herniation of meninges + CSF, no neural tissue.
* **Myelomeningocele (脊髓脊膜膨出)**: Herniation of meninges + CSF + spinal cord/nerve roots (most severe, ↑ neurological deficit).

### Hong Kong Epidemiology & HA Context
* Incidence of NTDs in HK ↓ significantly (currently ~0.1-0.5 per 1000 live births) due to widespread maternal peri-conceptional **Folic Acid (葉酸)** supplementation via Department of Health Maternal and Child Health Centres (MCHCs).
* Hydrocephalus often presents concomitantly with NTDs (esp. **Chiari II Malformation**).
* Multidisciplinary management usually centralized in HA tertiary pediatric surgery/neurosurgery centers (e.g., Queen Mary Hospital, Hong Kong Children's Hospital).

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Normal Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF) Circulation Pathway
* **Production**: Choroid plexus (側腦室脈絡叢) → ~500mL/day (rate = 20mL/hr).
* **Flow**: Lateral ventricles → **Foramen of Monro (室間孔)** → 3rd ventricle → **Aqueduct of Sylvius (大腦導水管)** → 4th ventricle → **Foramina of Luschka & Magendie (側孔與正中孔)** → Subarachnoid space.
* **Absorption**: Arachnoid granulations (蛛網膜顆粒) → Superior sagittal sinus.

### Pathophysiology of Hydrocephalus
* **Obstructive**: Stenosis of aqueduct (congenital), posterior fossa tumors (medulloblastoma), intraventricular hemorrhage (IVH) clot.
* **Communicating**: Post-meningitis/post-subarachnoid hemorrhage (SAH) → scarring of arachnoid granulations → ↓ CSF absorption. ↑ ICP → ventricular dilation → compression of cerebral mantle.

### Embryology & Pathophysiology of Spina Bifida
* Failure of primary neurulation (neuropore closure).
* **Two-Hit Hypothesis**: 1st hit (developmental failure) + 2nd hit (amniotic fluid toxicity to exposed neural elements).
* Leads to lower limb paralysis, sensory loss, and **Neuropathic Bladder (神經性膀胱)**.

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Infant Hydrocephalus Presentation
* ↑ **Head Circumference (頭圍)** (crosses percentiles on growth chart).
* Bulging, tense **Anterior Fontanelle (前囟門)**.
* Splayed cranial sutures.
* **Macewen Sign (破壺音)**: "Cracked pot" sound on cranial percussion.
* **Sunsetting Eyes (落日眼)**: Upward gaze paresis due to pressure on dorsal midbrain (Parinaud syndrome).
* Prominent scalp veins, irritability, poor feeding, vomiting.

### Older Child/Adult Hydrocephalus Presentation
* Cranium fused → cannot expand → rapid ↑ ICP.
* **Classic Triad**: Headache (worse in AM/supine), Vomiting, Papilledema.
* 6th cranial nerve palsy (false localizing sign).

### Spina Bifida Clinical Signs
* **Lumbar/Sacral Midline Defect**: Visible cystic mass (**Myelomeningocele**), or skin stigmata in **Occulta** (hairy patch, dimple, lipoma).
* **Motor/Sensory**: Flaccid paraplegia, absent deep tendon reflexes (DTRs), clubfeet (talipes equinovarus).
* **Urological**: Constant urinary dribbling, recurrent UTIs, patulous anal sphincter.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Differential Diagnosis of Pediatric Macrocephaly Table
| Differential Diagnosis | Discriminatory Clinical & Radiological Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Hydrocephalus (腦積水)** | ↑ Ventricular size, bulging fontanelle, sunsetting eyes, ↑ ICP signs. |
| **Benign Familial Macrocephaly** | +ve Family history, normal milestones, normal ventricles, no ↑ ICP. |
| **Chronic Subdural Hematoma** | Hx of trauma/birth injury, bridging vein rupture, fluid outside brain parenchyma on USG/MRI. |
| **Skeletal Dysplasia (Achondroplasia)** | Frontal bossing, short-limbed dwarfism, trident hands. |

### Differential Diagnosis of Midline Spinal Swelling Table
| Differential Diagnosis | Discriminatory Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Myelomeningocele** | Contains neural tissue, +ve transillumination deficit, profound neurological deficits. |
| **Meningocele** | Contains only CSF, brilliant transillumination, usually normal neurological exam. |
| **Sacrococcygeal Teratoma** | Solid/cystic mass, highly vascular, can be malignant, normal spinal cord. |
| **Lipomyelomeningocele** | Subcutaneous lipoma connected to neural elements, late-onset tethered cord syndrome. |

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)

### Antenatal Diagnostic Workup
* **Maternal Serum**: ↑ **Alpha-fetoprotein (MSAFP)** at 16-18 weeks (highly suggestive of open NTD).
* **Fetal USG**: 
  * **Lemon Sign**: Scalloping of frontal bones.
  * **Banana Sign**: Downward displacement of cerebellum (Chiari II).
  * Visible spinal defect.

### Postnatal Cranial & Spinal Imaging
* **Cranial Ultrasound (頭顱超聲波)**: 1st line in infants (via open anterior fontanelle). Bedside, no radiation. Identifies ventricular dilation/IVH.
* **MRI Brain & Whole Spine (磁力共振掃描)**: **Gold Standard**. Delineates exact anatomy, aqueductal stenosis, Chiari malformations, tethered cord.
* **CT Brain**: Used in emergencies for acute ↑ ICP/shunt blockage if MRI unavailable.

### Urological Assessment for Neuropathic Bladder (HA Protocols)
* *Ref: Local protocols for neuropathic bladder workup in pediatric patients.*
* **USS KUB (泌尿系統超聲波)**: 1st line baseline to rule out hydronephrosis.
* **Urodynamics (尿動力學檢查)**: Mandatory to assess bladder compliance, storage LUTS vs voiding LUTS, detrusor-sphincter dyssynergia (DSD).
* **VCUG (排尿期膀胱尿道造影)**: Rules out vesicoureteral reflux (VUR).
* **Renal Function**: Serum Creatinine, eGFR monitoring.
* **Urine**: MSU/Urine culture to rule out active UTI before interventions.

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)

### Medical Management of Hydrocephalus (Temporary)
* **Acetazolamide (乙酰唑胺)**: Carbonic anhydrase inhibitor → ↓ CSF production.
* **Osmotic Agents**: IV Mannitol/Hypertonic saline for acute life-threatening ↑ ICP (temporizing measure).

### Surgical Indications for Hydrocephalus
* Progressive ventricular dilation on imaging.
* Clinical signs of ↑ ICP (headache, vomiting, papilledema).
* Arrested development/deteriorating cognitive milestones.

### Management Options for Hydrocephalus Table
| Surgical Option | Indications & Mechanism | HA Context / Notes |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **VP Shunt (腦室腹腔引流術)** | Most common. Diverts CSF from lateral ventricle to peritoneal cavity. | Lifelong dependency. Revisions common. |
| **ETV (內視鏡第三腦室造口術)** | For Obstructive hydrocephalus. Perforates floor of 3rd ventricle → skips block. | Eliminates foreign body. Success depends on age/anatomy. |
| **External Ventricular Drain (EVD)** | Acute crisis with infection/hemorrhage. Temporary CSF diversion. | High risk of infection > 10 days. |

### Surgical Indications for Spina Bifida
* **Open Myelomeningocele**: Surgical closure within 24-48 hours of birth is strictly mandated to prevent CNS infection (meningitis) and preserve existing neural function.
* HA Pediatric Urology Protocol: Early introduction of **Clean Intermittent Catheterization (CIC, 清潔間歇導尿)** + Anticholinergics (e.g., Oxybutynin) to maintain low-pressure bladder and protect kidneys.

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Principles of Ventriculoperitoneal (VP) Shunt Insertion
* **Positioning & Access**: Supine with head turned. Incision at **Kocher's Point** (frontal) or Keen's/Frazier's point (parieto-occipital).
* **Catheter Placement**: Proximal catheter placed in lateral ventricle.
* **Valve Mechanism**: Connected to a programmable/fixed pressure valve (placed subgaleally).
* **Tunnelling & Distal Placement**: Subcutaneous tunnelling to abdomen. Mini-laparotomy/laparoscopic placement of distal catheter into peritoneal cavity.

### Principles of Endoscopic Third Ventriculostomy (ETV)
* Rigid neuroendoscope introduced via frontal burr hole.
* Navigation: Through Foramen of Monro into 3rd ventricle.
* **Stoma Creation**: Blunt perforation of the tuber cinereum (floor of 3rd ventricle) anterior to mammillary bodies.
* Balloon dilation of stoma to allow CSF to reach basal cisterns.

### Principles of Myelomeningocele Closure
* Prone position. Excision of abnormal sac (placode).
* Meticulous untethering of the neural placode from surrounding skin/dura.
* **Multilayer Closure**: Pia mater → Dura mater (watertight closure to prevent CSF leak) → Myofascial flaps → Skin.
* Simultaneous VP shunt placement may be needed if hydrocephalus exacerbates post-closure.

### Informed Consent & Risks
* **VP Shunt**: Mechanical failure (blockage/disconnection), infection, over/under-drainage, lifelong need for follow-up.
* **Spina Bifida Repair**: No improvement in existing paralysis, risk of CSF leak, meningitis, tethered cord in future.

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Immediate Post-operative Ward Care
* **Positioning**: Keep flat initially, gradually elevate head of bed (HOB) to avoid sudden over-drainage (subdural hematoma risk).
* **Neurological Obs**: Hourly GCS, pupil size/reactivity, fontanelle tension.
* **Infection Control**: Strict aseptic handling of wounds. Routine IV antibiotics rarely >24h unless infected.
* **Wound Care**: Monitor for CSF leak (halo sign on dressing) or erythema along shunt tract.

### Long-term Multidisciplinary Rehabilitation (HA Protocol)
* **Physiotherapy & Orthopedics**: Orthotics (AFOs), wheelchair training, management of scoliosis/hip dislocations. (e.g., Duchess of Kent Children's Hospital).
* **Urology**: Lifelong surveillance of renal tracts (annual USS KUB). Optimization of CIC regimen.
* **Neurosurgery**: Regular head circumference tracking, monitor for shunt failure.
* **Developmental Pediatrics**: Special educational needs (SEN) assessment.

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Shunt-related Complications
* **Blockage (Most Common)**: Proximal (choroid plexus occlusion), Valve, or Distal (pseudocyst formation in peritoneum). Presents with recurrent ↑ ICP.
* **Infection (5-15%)**: Usually early (<6 months). Pathogen: *Staph. epidermidis* (coagulase-negative). Requires shunt removal + IV/intrathecal antibiotics + temporary EVD.
* **Over-drainage**: Slit-ventricle syndrome, subdural hematoma.
* **Mechanical**: Disconnection, fracture, migration.

### Spina Bifida Repair Complications
* **Early**: CSF leak, wound dehiscence, meningitis.
* **Late**: **Tethered Cord Syndrome (脊髓牽扯綜合症)** (progressive lower limb weakness, worsening gait, worsening urological symptoms due to scar tissue anchoring spinal cord). Requires surgical release.

### Long-term Prognosis
* Spina bifida survival into adulthood >85% in modern HA context.
* Renal failure (due to poorly managed neuropathic bladder) remains a major cause of late morbidity/mortality.

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: What is the most common organism responsible for VP shunt infections? Answer: Staphylococcus epidermidis (Coagulase-negative staphylococcus) adhering via biofilm.
* Trap: Assuming a patient with Spina Bifida and worsening urinary incontinence has a simple UTI. Pearl: Always consider Tethered Cord Syndrome or new-onset VP shunt failure as the primary cause of neurological deterioration.
* Pitfall: Performing a lumbar puncture in a patient with suspected obstructive hydrocephalus. Causes acute pressure gradient resulting in fatal tonsillar herniation (coning).
* Viva Question: What is the significance of the "lemon" and "banana" signs on fetal USG? Answer: The lemon sign indicates frontal bone scalloping (spina bifida), and the banana sign indicates downward cerebellar herniation (Chiari II malformation).
* Viva Question: How do you differentiate a meningocele from a myelomeningocele clinically? Answer: A meningocele transilluminates brilliantly and usually has a normal neurological exam; myelomeningocele contains neural tissue (opaque) and is associated with profound paralysis below the lesion.
* Trap: Relying solely on clinical examination to rule out hydrocephalus in an infant. Pearl: Always perform serial head circumference measurements and plot them on standard growth charts; crossing percentiles is the earliest indicator.
* Pitfall: Treating a pediatric patient's neuropathic bladder solely based on UTI symptoms without assessing upper tracts. Routine monitoring via USS KUB and Urodynamics is mandatory to prevent silent renal failure.
