# Microtia (小耳症)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Microtia
* **Microtia (小耳症)**: Congenital underdevelopment of the external ear (pinna).
* Often accompanied by **Aural Atresia (耳道閉鎖)** (narrowing/absence of external auditory canal) → **Conductive Hearing Loss (傳導性聽力受損)**.
* Spectrum ranges from mild structural abnormalities to complete absence (**Anotia (無耳症)**).

### Hong Kong Epidemiology of Microtia
* **Incidence**: ~1–3 per 10,000 live births (higher in Asian populations).
* **Sex**: Male > Female (2:1).
* **Laterality**: Right-sided > Left-sided; Unilateral (90%) > Bilateral (10%).
* Mostly sporadic; ~5-15% inherited (autosomal dominant/recessive).

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Embryological Origins of the Auricle
* Develops during **6th–8th weeks of gestation**.
* Arises from 6 **Hillocks of His (希氏結節)**:
* **1st Branchial Arch (Mandibular)**: Hillocks 1-3 → Tragus, Helical root, Superior helix.
* **2nd Branchial Arch (Hyoid)**: Hillocks 4-6 → Antihelix, Antitragus, Lobule.

### Pathophysiology of Microtia
* Vascular accident (e.g., in utero stapedial artery ischaemia) OR neural crest cell migration failure.
* Teratogen exposure (Isotretinoin, Thalidomide, Mycophenolate) ↑ risk.
* Frequently syndromic (e.g., **Hemifacial Microsomia (半邊小臉症)**, **Treacher Collins Syndrome**, **Goldenhar Syndrome**).

### Marx Classification of Microtia Severity
| Grade | Description | Auditory Canal |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Grade I** | Mild deformity; all normal anatomical subunits present but smaller. | Patent |
| **Grade II** | Longitudinal remnant; missing some subunits (e.g., upper helix). | Often stenosed |
| **Grade III** | Classic "peanut ear"; only skin-vestige & misplaced lobule present. | **Atretic** |
| **Grade IV** | **Anotia (無耳症)**; Complete absence of external ear/tissue. | **Atretic** |

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Physical Signs of Microtia
* Deformed, malpositioned, or absent auricle.
* Low-set ear (often displaced anteriorly/inferiorly).
* **External Auditory Canal (外耳道)**: Stenotic or completely absent.
* Facial asymmetry (assess mandibular hypoplasia).

### Associated Syndromic Signs
* **Craniofacial**: Facial nerve (CN VII) weakness, hemifacial microsomia, macrostomia, cleft lip/palate.
* **Ophthalmological**: Epibulbar dermoids, coloboma (Goldenhar).
* **Systemic (VACTERL association)**: Vertebral, cardiac, renal anomalies.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Differential Diagnosis of Congenital Ear Anomalies
| Condition | Discriminatory Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Microtia (小耳症)** | Severe hypoplasia/absence of structural subunits; mostly cartilaginous defect. |
| **Prominent Ear (招風耳)** | Normal size; absent antihelical fold and/or deep conchal bowl; >2cm from mastoid. |
| **Cryptotia (隱耳症)** | Upper pole of ear buried under temporal skin; cartilage is present but hidden. |
| **Stahl's Ear (史塔爾氏耳)** | Extra third crus in upper antihelix ("Spock ear"); normal size. |
| **Acquired Traumatic Deformity** | History of trauma/haematoma ("cauliflower ear"); older age of onset. |

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)

### Audiological Workup for Microtia
* **Neonatal Period**: **Otoacoustic Emissions (OAE)** & **Auditory Brainstem Response (ABR)** ASAP.
* Bilateral microtia → Urgent Bone Conduction Hearing Aid (BCHA) fitting by 3-6 months to prevent severe speech/language delay.
* Unilateral microtia → Test normal ear to ensure normal speech development.

### Radiological Imaging for Microtia
* **High-Resolution CT (HRCT) Temporal Bone**: 
* Delayed until age 6-8 (wait for temporal bone pneumatisation).
* Assesses middle ear ossicles, cochlea, facial nerve course (often aberrant), and feasibility of atresiaplasty.

### Genetic Workup & Multi-disciplinary Screening
* **Renal USG**: Rule out congenital renal anomalies (strongly associated).
* **Echocardiogram**: Rule out cardiac defects.
* **Clinical Genetics Referral**: If dysmorphic features or bilateral involvement +ve.

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)

### Timing of Surgical Intervention for Microtia
* **Rule of 10s (approximate)**: Wait until **age 9-10 years**, chest circumference >60cm.
* Rationale: Allows sufficient **Autologous Rib Cartilage (自體肋軟骨)** growth to match adult ear size; provides psychological maturity for compliance with post-op care.
* Exception: Medpor implant can be done earlier (age 3-5), but higher extrusion risk.

### Management Options for Microtia
| Modality | Advantages | Disadvantages |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Autologous Rib Cartilage** (Gold Standard in HK) | Biocompatible, grows with child, resistant to infection/extrusion. | Donor site morbidity (chest scar, pain), technically demanding, multiple stages. |
| **Alloplastic Implant (Medpor)** | Earlier age (3-5), single stage, no chest morbidity. | ↑ Risk of extrusion, ↑ risk of catastrophic infection, requires temporoparietal fascia (TPF) flap. |
| **Prosthesis (Osseointegrated)** | Excellent aesthetics for severe trauma/salvage/failed reconstruction. | Requires daily maintenance, pin-site infections, unappealing to children. |

### Indications for Surgery in Microtia
* Psychosocial impact (bullying, low self-esteem).
* Desire for symmetry and capability to wear glasses/masks.
* Correction of associated **Conductive Hearing Loss (傳導性聽力受損)** (via Atresiaplasty or Bone Anchored Hearing Aid - BAHA).

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Autologous Costal Cartilage Reconstruction (Nagata/Brent Techniques)
* **Stage 1 (Nagata): Framework Fabrication & Insertion**
* Harvest 6th, 7th, 8th and 9th costal cartilages (contralateral side to utilize natural curvature).
* Sculpt framework (base plate + helical rim + antihelix) using wire/sutures.
* Excise vestigial cartilage; place framework under tight subcutaneous pocket with closed suction drain (crucial for skin coaptation).
* **Stage 2 (Nagata): Elevation & Sulcus Creation** (6 months later)
* Elevate constructed ear.
* Place wedge of cartilage behind framework to maintain projection.
* Cover defect with **Temporoparietal Fascia (TPF) Flap** and Full-Thickness Skin Graft (FTSG) from groin/scalp.

### Pre-operative Consent Issues for Microtia
* **Scarring**: Chest scar (rib harvest), scalp/groin scars (skin grafts), periauricular scars.
* **Chest Morbidity**: Pain, risk of pneumothorax during rib harvest, chest wall deformity.
* **Graft Issues**: Resorption of cartilage, loss of definition, hair growth on ear.
* **Expectation Management**: Constructed ear will be thicker, stiffer, and less defined than natural ear; color mismatch possible.

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Immediate Post-operative Care for Microtia Surgery
* **Suction Drains**: Keep on continuous negative pressure (vacutainer) for 3-5 days to ensure skin adheres perfectly to cartilage framework (prevents haematoma and loss of contour).
* **Positioning**: Strictly sleep on contralateral side; use donut pillow. Avoid direct pressure.
* **Wound Care**: Minimal dressing changes; apply antibiotic ointment to suture lines.
* **Analgesia**: PCA pump + oral NSAIDs (beware bleeding risk) for rib harvest pain.

### Long-term Rehabilitation and Hearing Management
* **Sports**: Avoid contact sports for 6-8 weeks; permanent protection recommended for combat sports.
* **Hearing Support**: Coordinate with ENT for atresiaplasty (must be done AFTER Stage 1 microtia repair to avoid scarring the skin pocket) or BAHA implantation.

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Early Complications of Microtia Surgery
* **Pneumothorax**: Intra-operative pleural tear during rib harvest (requires Valsalva check + potential chest tube).
* **Haematoma**: Results in skin necrosis and framework exposure.
* **Surgical Site Infection (SSI)**: Catastrophic if involving cartilage → requires aggressive IV Abx + washout.
* **Skin Necrosis**: Due to excessive tension over framework or poor vascularity.

### Late Complications of Microtia Surgery
* **Cartilage Resorption**: Loss of structural definition over years (10-20% volume loss).
* **Framework Exposure**: Requires salvage with local flaps (e.g., TPF flap).
* **Chest Wall Deformity**: Pectus excavatum-like deformity at donor site.
* **Hypertrophic Scarring/Keloid**: Common in Asian skin at chest and graft sites.

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Viva Question: Why do we typically wait until a child is 9-10 years old for autologous cartilage microtia repair? Answer: To ensure the rib cage has grown enough to provide sufficient cartilage for a framework matching the adult ear size, and to allow the child to be psychologically mature enough to comply with post-operative care.
* Viva Question: What is the embryological origin of the external ear? Answer: The six Hillocks of His, derived from the first (mandibular) and second (hyoid) branchial arches.
* Viva Question: Should aural atresia repair (canalplasty) by ENT be done before or after the plastic surgery microtia reconstruction? Answer: After. Any prior incisions for canalplasty will compromise the virgin, unscarred skin flap required to drape over the reconstructed cartilage framework.
* Trap: Assuming unilateral microtia requires immediate surgical hearing intervention. Pearl: In unilateral microtia with normal contralateral hearing, speech and language development will usually be normal; urgent intervention is only mandated for bilateral cases.
* Pitfall: Removing the suction drain too early post-op. The negative pressure must be maintained for several days to coapt the skin precisely into the crevices of the cartilage framework to achieve the anatomical contours of an ear.
