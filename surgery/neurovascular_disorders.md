# Subarachnoid Haemorrhage & Aneurysms (蛛網膜下腔出血與動脈瘤)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Subarachnoid Haemorrhage
* **Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)** (蛛網膜下腔出血): Extravasation of blood into the subarachnoid space between the arachnoid mater and pia mater.
* **Primary SAH**: 85% spontaneous, predominantly secondary to ruptured **saccular/berry aneurysms** (漿果動脈瘤).
* **Non-aneurysmal SAH**: 15% due to arteriovenous malformations (AVMs) (動靜脈畸形), trauma, or perimesencephalic bleeding.

### Epidemiology of SAH in Hong Kong
* **Incidence**: ~6-8 per 100,000 annually in Hong Kong.
* **Age & Gender**: Peak incidence 50-60 years; Female > Male (1.6:1).
* **Risk Factors (Local)**:
 * **Modifiable**: Hypertension (高血壓), smoking, heavy alcohol consumption.
 * **Non-modifiable**: Family history (+ve in first-degree relative), **Autosomal Dominant Polycystic Kidney Disease (ADPKD)** (常染色體顯性多囊腎病), Ehlers-Danlos syndrome, Marfan syndrome.

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Surgical Anatomy of the Circle of Willis (威利氏環)
* **Aneurysm Distribution**:
 * **Anterior Communicating Artery (AComA)** (前交通動脈): 30-40% (Most common).
 * **Internal Carotid Artery / Posterior Communicating Artery (PComA)** (後交通動脈): 20-30%.
 * **Middle Cerebral Artery (MCA)** (大腦中動脈): 20% (Often presents with intracerebral haematoma).
 * **Posterior Circulation**: 10% (Basilar tip, vertebral arteries).

### Pathophysiology of Berry Aneurysms (漿果動脈瘤)
* **Structural Defect**: Congenital absence/weakness of tunica media & internal elastic lamina at arterial bifurcations.
* **Haemodynamic Stress**: Constant pulsatile arterial pressure → out-pouching → wall thinning → rupture.
* **Rupture Mechanics**: Intracranial pressure (ICP) acutely ↑ to equal mean arterial pressure (MAP) → transient arrest of cerebral circulation → syncope.

### Pathophysiology of Vasospasm (血管痙攣) and Delayed Cerebral Ischaemia (DCI)
* **Oxyhaemoglobin Toxicity**: RBC lysis in subarachnoid space → release of oxyhaemoglobin → profound smooth muscle contraction.
* **Endothelial Dysfunction**: ↓ Nitric oxide (vasodilator), ↑ Endothelin-1 (vasoconstrictor).
* **Consequence**: Luminal narrowing → ↓ cerebral blood flow (CBF) → **Delayed Cerebral Ischaemia (DCI)** (延遲性腦缺血) (Peak days 4-14).

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Classic Clinical Presentation of SAH
* **Thunderclap headache** (雷擊性頭痛): Sudden onset, "worst headache of my life", reaching peak intensity within seconds to minutes.
* **Nausea & vomiting** (噁心及嘔吐): Secondary to ↑ ICP.
* **Transient Loss of Consciousness** (短暫失去意識): Occurs in ~50% at the moment of rupture.
* **Seizures** (癲癇發作): Focal or generalized, secondary to cortical irritation.

### Key Neurological Signs of SAH
* **Meningism** (腦膜刺激徵):
 * **Neck stiffness** (頸部僵直) (takes 6-12 hours to develop).
 * **Kernig's sign +ve**: Pain on knee extension with hip flexed.
 * **Brudzinski's sign +ve**: Involuntary hip/knee flexion upon passive neck flexion.
* **Focal Neurological Deficits**:
 * **PComA aneurysm**: Ipsilateral **CN III palsy** (第三對腦神經麻痺) (dilated pupil, "down and out" eye).
 * **AComA aneurysm**: Bilateral leg weakness, abulia.
 * **MCA aneurysm**: Contralateral hemiparesis, aphasia.
* **Ocular Signs**:
 * **Papilloedema** (視乳頭水腫): ↑ ICP.
 * **Terson's Syndrome**: Vitreous haemorrhage associated with SAH.

### Warning Signs (Sentinel Bleed)
* **Sentinel Headache**: Minor leak days/weeks before major rupture. Often misdiagnosed as tension headache; sudden onset is key discriminator.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Differential Diagnosis for Sudden Severe Headache
| Differential Diagnosis | Key Discriminatory Features |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Bacterial Meningitis** (細菌性腦膜炎) | Fever, gradual onset over hours/days, ↑ WCC, ↑ CRP. |
| **Migraine** (偏頭痛) | Gradual onset, aura, throbbing nature, prior history. |
| **Cervical Artery Dissection** | Neck trauma history, Horner's syndrome, unilateral neck/face pain. |
| **Cerebral Venous Sinus Thrombosis** | Hypercoagulable state, gradual headache, papilloedema, abnormal MRV. |
| **Intracerebral Haemorrhage (ICH)** | Dense focal deficits early, evident on non-contrast CT. |

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)

### First-Line Imaging for Suspected SAH
* **Non-contrast CT Brain** (平掃電腦掃描):
 * **Sensitivity**: >98% if done <6 hours of onset; drops to ~50% at 1 week.
 * **Findings**: Hyperdense blood in basal cisterns, sylvian fissures, or sulci.
 * **Utility**: Identifies SAH, hydrocephalus, and associated ICH.

### Diagnostic Lumbar Puncture (腰椎穿刺) Protocol
* **Indication**: Mandatory if classical history but CT Brain is -ve. Must wait >12 hours post-headache onset.
* **Key Finding**: **Xanthochromia** (黃變症) (yellowish tint due to bilirubin from RBC breakdown).
* **Spectrophotometry**: Confirms bilirubin; differentiates true SAH from traumatic tap.

### Vascular Imaging for Aneurysm Detection
* **CT Angiogram (CTA)** (電腦掃描血管造影): 1st line non-invasive gold standard in HA. Detects aneurysms >2-3mm.
* **Digital Subtraction Angiography (DSA)** (數碼減影血管造影): Gold standard. Invasive. Done if CTA -ve but high clinical suspicion, or concurrent with endovascular treatment.

### Pre-operative Laboratory Workup
* **Baseline Bloods**: CBC, LRFT (monitor for baseline Na+).
* **Coagulation Screen**: PT/APTT/INR (must reverse coagulopathy pre-op).
* **Cardiac Workup**: ECG, Troponin (SAH causes massive sympathetic surge → neurogenic stunned myocardium → ↑ Troponin, T-wave inversion).

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)

### Immediate Resuscitation and HA Transfer Protocol
* **Airway/Breathing**: Intubate if GCS ≤8 to protect airway.
* **Urgent Transfer**: Transfer to regional HA neurosurgical centre (e.g., QEH, PWH, QMH).
* **Bed Rest**: Strict bed rest, elevate head of bed 30° (↓ ICP), minimal stimulation, dark room.
* **Analgesia**: IV Paracetamol, PRN opioids (avoid over-sedation to monitor GCS).

### Medical Management & Neuroprotection
* **Blood Pressure Control**: Strict target Systolic BP < 160 mmHg to prevent rebleeding. Use IV Labetalol (拉貝洛爾) or Hydralazine.
* **Vasospasm Prophylaxis**: **Nimodipine** (尼莫地平) 60mg PO Q4H for 21 days (HA standard protocol).
* **Seizure Prophylaxis**: IV Levetiracetam (Keppra) if +ve seizures or severe ICH (routine prophylaxis controversial, often given per neurosurgeon preference).
* **Laxatives**: Lactulose/Senna to prevent Valsalva manoeuvre (↑ ICP).

### Indications for Aneurysm Securing
* **Goal**: Secure within 24-72 hours to eliminate rebleeding risk.
* **Indications**: Any ruptured aneurysm in a salvageable patient (WFNS I-IV). WFNS V patients may require external ventricular drain (EVD) first to reassess neurological salvageability.

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Endovascular Coiling (血管內導管栓塞術) Principles
* **1st Line Treatment** (Based on ISAT Trial): Preferred if anatomy allows (narrow neck aneurysm).
* **Procedure**: Microcatheter via femoral/radial artery → platinum coils packed into aneurysm sac → promotes thrombosis.
* **Advantages**: Minimally invasive, lower short-term morbidity/mortality vs clipping.
* **Disadvantages**: Higher recurrence rate requiring retreatment.

### Surgical Clipping (外科動脈瘤夾閉術) Principles
* **Indications**: Wide-neck aneurysms, MCA bifurcation aneurysms, large compressive haematoma requiring evacuation, failed coiling.
* **Procedure**: Craniotomy → arachnoid dissection → application of titanium clip across aneurysm neck.
* **Advantages**: Definitive, lower recurrence.
* **Disadvantages**: Highly invasive, brain retraction injury, longer recovery.

### Informed Consent and Operative Risks
* **General Risks**: Bleeding, infection, stroke, death.
* **Specific Risks**: Intra-operative rupture (catastrophic), branch vessel occlusion → permanent focal neurological deficit (e.g., hemiplegia, aphasia), vasospasm.

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Immediate Neurocritical Care
* **Monitoring**: ICU/HDU admission. Hourly GCS and pupil checks.
* **Euvolaemia**: Maintain strict euvolaemia (CVP 5-8 mmHg) with 0.9% Normal Saline. **Do NOT fluid restrict**, even if hyponatraemic (exacerbates vasospasm).
* **Normothermia**: Aggressive fever control (Paracetamol, cooling blankets) to ↓ cerebral metabolic demand.

### Vasospasm Monitoring & Management Protocol
* **Monitoring**: Daily Transcranial Doppler (TCD) to measure Middle Cerebral Artery (MCA) flow velocities.
* **Clinical Diagnosis**: New focal neurological deficit or ↓ GCS between day 4-14.
* **Management**: **Induced Hypertension** (maintain euvolaemia and use inotropes/vasopressors to drive cerebral perfusion pressure). *Note: 'Triple H' therapy (Hypervolaemia, Haemodilution, Hypertension) is historically taught but modern HA practice focuses solely on euvolaemia + induced hypertension.*

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Early Complications (< 72 hours)
* **Rebleeding**: Highest risk within first 24 hours. Mortality >70%. Prevented by early securing (coiling/clipping) and strict BP control.
* **Acute Hydrocephalus** (急性腦積水): Blood occludes arachnoid granulations or ventricular pathways. Treated via urgent **External Ventricular Drain (EVD)** (腦室外引流).

### Intermediate Complications (3 to 14 days)
* **Vasospasm & DCI**: Leading cause of delayed morbidity. Treated with Nimodipine and induced hypertension.
* **Hyponatraemia** (低血鈉): Common.
 * **Cerebral Salt Wasting Syndrome (CSWS)** (腦性鹽耗損症候群): True hypovolaemia + hyponatraemia. Requires NS fluid replacement.
 * **SIADH**: Euvolaemic/hypervolaemic hyponatraemia.

### Late Complications (> 14 days)
* **Chronic Hydrocephalus**: Requires long-term Ventriculoperitoneal (VP) Shunt (腦室腹腔分流術).
* **Epilepsy**: ~5-10% develop chronic seizure disorder.
* **Cognitive Impairment**: Executive dysfunction, memory deficits, depression.

### Prognosis & Mortality Rates
* **Overall Mortality**: ~40-50% overall. 10-15% die before reaching the hospital.
* **Morbidity**: ~30% of survivors have permanent neurological/cognitive deficits.

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)

* Viva Question: What is the most common cranial nerve palsy associated with a PComA aneurysm? Answer: Ipsilateral CN III palsy, classically with pupillary involvement (dilated pupil).
* Viva Question: Why do we test for xanthochromia 12 hours after symptom onset instead of immediately? Answer: It takes up to 12 hours for RBCs in the CSF to undergo enzymatic breakdown into bilirubin, which produces the yellow colour.
* Trap: Treating SAH-induced hyponatraemia with fluid restriction. Pearl: Fluid restriction exacerbates cerebral hypoperfusion and triggers fatal vasospasm. Always maintain euvolaemia with 0.9% Normal Saline and use hypertonic saline if needed for severe hyponatraemia.
* Trap: Attributing a sudden, severe headache to migraine without a prior history. Pearl: Any first-time thunderclap headache must be managed as a ruptured aneurysm until proven otherwise with CT and/or LP.
* Pitfall: Stopping Nimodipine if the patient's blood pressure drops slightly. Switch to smaller, more frequent dosing or IV infusion protocols, but never abruptly stop Nimodipine as it is critical for preventing vasospasm-induced delayed cerebral ischaemia.
* Viva Question: What was the landmark trial that shifted standard of care from surgical clipping to endovascular coiling for ruptured aneurysms? Answer: The International Subarachnoid Aneurysm Trial (ISAT).
* Pitfall: Assuming a normal non-contrast CT brain 3 days after onset rules out SAH. CT sensitivity drops significantly after 6-12 hours; a negative CT must be followed by a diagnostic LP.
