# Anal Fissure (肛裂)

## 1. Clinical Definition & Local Epidemiology

### Clinical Definition of Anal Fissure
* **Definition**: Linear tear or ulcer in the **anoderm** distal to the **Dentate Line (齒狀線)**.

### Local Epidemiology & Risk Factors
* **Peak incidence**: 20-40 years.
* **Sex distribution**: M = F overall (Anterior fissures F > M).
* **HK Context**: High prevalence in Hong Kong associated with low-fibre urban diets → chronic constipation and straining. 

## 2. Surgical Anatomy & Pathophysiology

### Surgical Anatomy of the Anal Canal
* **Internal Anal Sphincter (內括約肌)**: Smooth muscle (involuntary), continuation of circular muscle of rectum. 
* **Blood Supply**: **Inferior Rectal Artery**.
* **Anatomical Watershed**: Vessels traverse the sphincter radially; marked paucity of collateral flow at the **posterior midline** (most ischaemic zone).

### Pathophysiology of Primary Fissures
* Hard stool trauma → mechanical tear of sensitive anoderm.
* Pain → reflex **Internal Anal Sphincter Spasm** (↑ resting tone).
* Spasm → vascular compression → ↓ local mucosal blood flow → **Ischaemia** → non-healing chronic ulcer.

### Anatomical Distribution & Red Flags
* **Posterior Midline**: ~90% (due to poor perfusion/ischaemia).
* **Anterior Midline**: ~10% (mainly females due to deficient anterior perineal support post-vaginal delivery).
* **Atypical Locations** (Lateral/Multiple): Strongly suspect underlying systemic disease (Crohn's disease, TB, HIV, Syphilis, Anal SCC, Leukaemia).

## 3. Key Symptoms & Signs

### Cardinal Patient Symptoms
* **Pain (肛門痛)**: Sharp, tearing, "passing glass" sensation.
* **Timing**: Exacerbated *during* defaecation, persists for minutes to hours post-defaecation.
* **Bright Red Blood Per Rectum (鮮血便)**: Small volume, noted *strictly* on toilet paper or streaking the surface of hard stool.
* Pruritus ani (due to discharge/poor hygiene secondary to pain).

### Acute vs Chronic Physical Signs
* **Acute (<6 weeks)**: Superficial tear, marked spasm, acutely tender.
* **Chronic (>6 weeks)**:
* **Sentinel Pile (前哨痔)**: Distal hypertrophied skin tag.
* **Hypertrophied Anal Papilla (肥大肛乳頭)**: Proximal mucosal swelling at dentate line.
* Fibrotic induration and exposed horizontal circular fibres of the IAS at the ulcer base.
* **Contraindication**: Digital Rectal Examination (DRE) is fiercely painful and unnecessary in acute classical presentation.

## 4. Differential Diagnosis & Discriminatory Features

### Table of Anorectal Pathologies
| Condition | Pain Profile | Bleeding Profile | Discriminatory Physical Findings |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Anal Fissure (肛裂)** | Severe, sharp, post-defaecation | Scanty, on toilet paper | Sentinel pile, IAS spasm, midline tear |
| **Haemorrhoids (痔瘡)** | Painless (unless thrombosed) | Dripping BRBPR post-defaecation | Prolapsing venous cushions, painless DRE |
| **Fistula-in-ano (肛瘻)** | Dull ache, constant | Purulent discharge, ± blood | External opening, indurated tract |
| **Anal SCC (肛門癌)** | Constant pain, tenesmus | Scanty, mixed with stool | Hard, irregular lateral ulcer, inguinal lymphadenopathy |
| **Crohn's Disease** | Variable | Mixed with stool/mucus | Multiple, lateral, painless, indurated tags |

## 5. Tiered Investigations & Pre-operative Workup (Labs, Imaging, Staging)

### Primary Diagnostic Workup
* **Clinical Diagnosis**: Visual inspection via gentle traction of buttocks.
* **DRE/Proctoscopy**: Defer until pain resolves OR perform Examination Under Anaesthesia (EUA).

### Endoscopic Evaluation (HA Guidelines)
* **Flexible Sigmoidoscopy / Colonoscopy (大腸鏡)** indications:
* Age >50 (HK Colorectal Cancer screening criteria).
* Positive family Hx of CRC.
* Altered bowel habit or weight loss.
* Unexplained iron deficiency anaemia.
* Refractory bleeding after fissure healing.

### Advanced Investigations for Atypical Disease
* **Examination Under Anaesthesia (EUA) + Biopsy**: Mandatory for laterally located or non-healing ulcers to rule out malignancy/Crohn's.
* **Endoanal Ultrasound (EAUS) / Pelvic MRI**: Indicated in multiparous women to rule out occult pre-existing sphincter defects before surgical sphincterotomy.

## 6. Management Algorithm & Surgical Indications (Conservative, Medical, HA Protocols)

### 1st-Line: Conservative & Dietary Management
* Goal: Soft, bulky stool to prevent recurrent trauma.
* ↑ fluid intake + high-fibre diet.
* **Osmotic / Bulk-forming Laxatives**: Lactulose, Ispaghula husk.
* **Sitz Baths (溫水坐浴)**: Warm water for 10-15 mins post-defaecation → relieves IAS spasm.

### 2nd-Line: Pharmacological Sphincterotomy
* **Topical 2% Diltiazem ointment**: Calcium channel blocker. (Preferred 1st-line medical in many HK HA clusters due to better side-effect profile).
* **Topical 0.2% Glyceryl Trinitrate (GTN)**: Nitric oxide donor. 
* Side effect: Throbbing headache (~20-30% of patients), hypotension.
* Application: Pea-sized amount applied gently into distal anal canal twice daily for 6-8 weeks.

### Indications for Surgical Intervention
* Failure of conservative/medical therapy (>6-8 weeks).
* Severe, intractable pain affecting activities of daily living.
* Relapsing/recurrent chronic fissures with fibrotic changes.

## 7. Key Operative Principles & Consent Issues

### Gold Standard: Lateral Internal Sphincterotomy (LIS)
* **Objective**: Permanently divide a portion of the IAS to ↓ resting pressure and restore mucosal blood flow.
* **Technique**: Open or Closed approach.
* **Anatomical landmark**: Division of IAS laterally (3 or 9 o'clock) up to the **Dentate Line** or the apex of the fissure (whichever is distal). Never divide the External Anal Sphincter.

### Alternative / Adjunctive Procedures
* **Botulinum Toxin (Botox) Injection**: Chemical denervation. Lasts 3-4 months. Ideal for elderly, high-risk patients, or baseline weak sphincter tone.
* **Anal Advancement Flap (V-Y plasty)**: Used when LIS is contraindicated (e.g., pre-existing low resting tone, post-partum perineal tear). Covers the defect without sacrificing sphincter muscle.

### Strict Pre-operative Consent (LIS)
* **Incontinence (失禁)**: ~3-5% long-term risk of flatus or liquid stool incontinence. (Crucial medicolegal point in HK).
* Perianal haematoma / bleeding.
* Surgical site infection / Fistula-in-ano.
* Recurrence (<5%).

## 8. Post-operative Care (ERAS) & Rehabilitation

### Immediate Post-operative Care
* Day-surgery case; same-day discharge.
* Analgesia: Paracetamol + NSAIDs. Avoid opiates (e.g., Codeine) → strictly prevents constipation.

### Post-discharge Bowel Regimen
* Maintain high-fibre diet and generous hydration.
* Continue regular laxatives for 2-4 weeks post-op to ensure soft passage.
* Daily Sitz baths to maintain hygiene and soothe the surgical site.

### Outpatient Follow-up Protocol
* Surgical Outpatient Department (SOPD) review at 4-6 weeks to evaluate wound healing and assess continence status.

## 9. Surgical Complications (Early, Intermediate, Late) & Prognosis

### Early Complications (<30 Days)
* Acute urinary retention (secondary to pain/reflex spasm or spinal anaesthesia).
* Perianal haematoma (inadequate haemostasis at LIS site).
* Local sepsis / abscess formation.

### Late Complications (>30 Days)
* **Permanent Fecal Incontinence**: Most feared complication. ↑ risk in multiparous women and elderly.
* **Iatrogenic Fistula-in-ano**: From infected LIS wound.
* Keyhole Deformity: Rare, occurs if fissurectomy is excessively deep or posterior sphincterotomy is mistakenly performed.

### Long-Term Prognosis
* Medical therapy (GTN/Diltiazem): ~60% healing rate, but ~30% recurrence rate.
* LIS: >95% healing rate, <5% recurrence. Definitive cure.

## 10. Examiner's Pearls (High-yield viva questions and common pitfalls)
* Viva Question: What is the classic triad of a chronic anal fissure? Answer: Sentinel pile, deep ulcer with exposed IAS fibres, and hypertrophied anal papilla.
* Trap: Treating a laterally placed anal fissure with standard LIS without biopsy. Pearl: Lateral fissures are highly suspicious for secondary causes like Crohn's, TB, or malignancy; a biopsy and EUA must be performed first.
* Pitfall: Performing a DRE in the outpatient clinic on a patient presenting with classic tearing pain and minor BRBPR. You will cause excruciating pain and lose patient trust; visual inspection is sufficient for initial diagnosis.
* Viva Question: Why do anal fissures predominantly occur at the posterior midline? Answer: The posterior midline is the watershed area of the internal anal sphincter's blood supply, making it highly susceptible to ischaemia when resting tone increases.
* Trap: Prescribing codeine for post-operative pain relief after fissure surgery. Pearl: Opiates cause severe constipation, which will immediately undo the surgery and cause recurrent mucosal tearing; strictly use NSAIDs and Paracetamol.
